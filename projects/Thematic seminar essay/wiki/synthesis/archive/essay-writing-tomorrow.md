In November 2024, Indonesia's Special Presidential Envoy for Climate and Energy stood at the COP29 pavilion in Baku and announced that the country had 557 million verified tonnes of CO₂ credits ready to sell. He attracted EUR 1.2 billion in green financing. What he did not mention was that his own family conglomerate holds a licensed carbon-trading concession in the very market he was designing. This is not an isolated irony — it is the operating logic of Indonesia's carbon-pricing architecture. Read carefully, the system is not a decarbonisation mechanism. It is a credit-export infrastructure: an ETS that runs free allowances to the politically connected industries that matter; a carbon tax legislated in 2021 and postponed every year since; and a voluntary market now formally decoupled from Indonesia's own climate targets so that credits can be sold internationally without counting against the national carbon budget. Indonesia has built a sophisticated carbon accounting system while ensuring the price signal never reaches the economy that needs to change.

The sequencing is the puzzle. Indonesia's Tax Harmonization Law (Law 7/2021) legislated both a carbon tax and an Emissions Trading Scheme in the same legislation. The ETS launched in February 2023, covering coal-fired power plants on the state utility's grid. The carbon tax, originally due in April 2022, was postponed four times and as of early 2026 has still not been enforced (ICAP, 2025). The ETS, for its part, generated approximately USD 5 million in total transactions since launch — barely a functioning market (IEEFA, 2025). The academic literature broadly recommends a carbon tax over an ETS for developing-country contexts (van den Bergh & Drews, 2026; Jia & Lin, 2020), yet Indonesia has done the opposite of what the literature recommends, and what it has done is not working. Why?

This essay argues that Indonesia's carbon-pricing sequencing reflects deliberate political-economy choices rather than economic-design optimality. The ETS protects key industrial coalitions through free allowances; the perpetually postponed carbon tax ensures the price signal does not reach the broader economy; and the re-opened Article 6 international market turns potential emissions reductions into an export revenue stream. The result is a system that is simultaneously among the world's most developed in legal architecture and among the least effective in practice. The essay proceeds in three sections: Section 1 explains the political-economy logic of the ETS-first sequencing; Section 2 describes how the ETS has actually operated; Section 3 applies the developing-country policy literature to argue what Indonesia should — and credibly can — do next.

## SECTION 1: WHY AN ETS FIRST? (800 words)

Four moves. Each move is roughly one paragraph.

### Move 1 — Document the sequencing [~150 words, 1 paragraph]

Just facts. State:
1. Law 7/2021 legislated BOTH a carbon tax and an ETS in the same legislation
2. ETS launch: February 2023 (coal power sector); Phase 2 (2025) added captive coal + gas (563 installations total)
3. Carbon tax schedule: April 2022 → postponed → July 2022 → postponed → 2025 → still not enforced as of early 2026
4. Presidential Regulation 110/2025 reaffirmed the "cap-tax-and-trade" hybrid but tied the carbon levy to the ETS market price rather than fixing it independently

End the paragraph: "So Indonesia has not simply chosen ETS over tax — it has legislated a full hybrid, implemented only the ETS side, and repeatedly deferred the tax half."

**Sources:** ICAP 2025 factsheet §"Regulatory Framework" and §"Year in Review"; Sunanda et al. (2025) Table 4.

---

### Move 2 — Why the literature prefers a tax [~150 words, 1 paragraph]

Brief. Explain the puzzle by showing what the literature says:
- Carbon tax is simpler to administer (builds on existing fuel-tax infrastructure): van den Bergh & Drews (2026)
- Tax gives a certain price signal for long-term investment decisions (vs. ETS price volatility)
- In Indonesia specifically: CGE modelling shows a revenue-recycled carbon tax raises both GDP and employment (Hartono et al., 2023)
- Jia & Lin (2020) on China: tax alone outperforms ETS alone on emissions reduction

End with the question: "If this is what the literature recommends, why did Indonesia launch the ETS and delay the tax?"

**Sources:** van den Bergh & Drews (2026); Hartono et al. (2023); Jia & Lin (2020).

---

### Move 3 — The political-economy explanation [~350 words, 3–4 paragraphs]

This is the core of the essay. Cover these three reasons (pick the three strongest for your word count — Reasons 1–3 are most important):

**Reason 1 — Burden visibility.** A carbon tax raises fuel and electricity prices for 270 million people; the ETS concentrates costs on ~563 regulated power plants, mostly state-owned utility PLN. Political costs that are concentrated in a small set of firms whose executives can be managed through industry dialogue are far easier to handle than a visible price rise on household energy bills. Stevens (in Sunanda et al., 2025, reference [87]) states directly: "Political resistance and lack of coordination hinder carbon tax implementation in Indonesia, despite its potential to reduce emissions and fund climate programs." The "resistance" is from industries and coalition partners, not from voters.

**Reason 2 — Free allocation protects industrial policy priorities.** The ETS grants 100% free allowances in Phase 1 (2023–24), then 75–85% free in Phase 2 — a de facto subsidy to nickel processing, steel, cement, and captive coal. These are exactly the sectors the Jokowi and Prabowo governments have prioritised for "downstreaming" (domestic processing of raw commodities before export). A carbon tax would have taxed these same industries from day one with no equivalent sweetener.

**Reason 3 — Revenue without a new tax.** The re-opened Article 6 international credit market (moratorium lifted January 2025) generates foreign exchange from credit exports without requiring a visible new domestic tax. The government has stated a target of USD 65 billion from carbon projects by 2028 (Tempo/Hashim Djojohadikusumo at COP29, November 2024). This creates an incentive to maximise the credit-export machine rather than enforce a domestic price.

Source: Andriansyah & Hong (2022); ICAP 2025 factsheet.

## SECTION 2: HOW THE ETS ACTUALLY WORKS (500 words)

**Goal:** factual grounding + the hollow-reality punchline. Keep this tighter than the original essay outline — description serves the argument, it doesn't replace it.

### Paragraph 1 — Coverage and phases [~150 words]

Facts to land:
- Phase 1 (2023–24): 99 coal power plants ≥25 MW on PLN grid; ~42 compliance entities
- Phase 2 (2025–27): expanded to 563 installations covering captive coal + gas power
- Phase 3 (2028–30): all fossil-fuel power
- Total ETS coverage today: ~21% of national GHG emissions (ETS sector: 225.3 MtCO2e vs. national total: 1,053.4 MtCO2e per ICAP)
- Cap design: **intensity-based** (tCO2 per MWh of electricity), NOT an absolute emissions cap; the 2025 NDC introduced an absolute 1.35 GtCO2e economy-wide cap that future phases are meant to align toward

**Source:** ICAP 2025 factsheet (almost all of this section comes from here).

### Paragraph 2 — Allocation and Article 6 [~100 words]

- All allowances free in Phase 1; 75–85% free in Phase 2; **no auctions have taken place as of early 2026**
- Article 6 moratorium lifted January 2025; ~1.78 MtCO2e PLN-affiliated credits already authorised for international transfer
- Critical PR 110/2025 provision: voluntary carbon market **decoupled from NDC timeline**; Corresponding Adjustments (the double-counting prevention mechanism) are **optional, not mandatory**, for international transfers — meaning credits can be exported without being deducted from Indonesia's own climate account

**Sources:** ICAP 2025 factsheet; see `raw/Chain task 9 search/DONE_Gap2` for PR 110/2025 analysis.

### Paragraph 3 — The hollow reality [~200 words] — the punchline

Write this sharply:
- 2025 allowance allocation was **postponed** due to regulatory revision — Phase 2 exists on paper but has not issued Phase 2 allowances
- No auctions; no allowance trading in 2025
- Total IDX Carbon exchange transactions since launch (September 2023): IDR 78 billion (~USD 4.9 million)
- Compare: Indonesia spent **IDR 713.5 trillion (~USD 44 billion)** on energy subsidies in 2024, of which 89% supported fossil fuels (IISD/GSI, 2025). The carbon market is roughly **8,000 times** smaller than the subsidy system it is supposed to offset.

- Climate Action Tracker (December 2024) rates Indonesia: **"Critically Insufficient"**

**Sources:** ICAP 2025 factsheet; IEEFA (2025) — online; IISD/GSI press release 2025 — see `raw/Chain task 9 search/DONE_Gap1`; OECD ECR 2025; Climate Action Tracker.

## SECTION 3: WHAT INDONESIA SHOULD DO NOW (1,000 words)

**Opening frame [~50 words]:** "The developing-country carbon-pricing literature converges on five recommendations. Crucially, Indonesia has already committed to all five on paper — in Presidential Regulation 110/2025 and the October 2025 NDC. The binding constraint is not policy design but political-economy implementation."

*Source: Sunanda et al. (2025) §4.2; van den Bergh & Drews (2026).*

---

### Recommendation 1 — Enforce the levy and set a graduated escalation path [~200 words]

- The carbon levy in Law 7/2021 (IDR 30/kg CO2 ≈ USD 2/tCO2) exists but has been enforced approximately zero times
- Recommendation: commit to a credible **graduated escalation path** — e.g. USD 2 (now) → USD 10 (2028) → USD 30 (2032). The level matters less than the trajectory (Sunanda et al., 2025 §4.2; UNDP Carbon Tax report, 2025)
- But note: Indonesia's **net effective carbon rate is likely negative** once fossil-fuel subsidies (IDR 713.5 trillion/year) are netted out (OECD ECR 2025). The coal price cap alone (IDR 58.5 trillion/year) implicitly subsidises the very industry the ETS prices. Step one is not raising the carbon price — it is removing the subsidy that cancels it.
- The good news: Indonesia has already successfully reformed fuel subsidies before (Jokowi cut the Rp 6,500/litre petrol subsidy in his first year, 2014, saving ~IDR 200 trillion/year). The "untouchable third rail" framing is partly dated (IISD/GSI analysis, 2025).

**Sources:** Sunanda et al. (2025); OECD ECR 2025; IISD/GSI (`raw/Chain task 9 search/DONE_Gap1`); ICAP 2025.

---

### Recommendation 2 — Revenue recycling [~150 words]

- Hartono et al. (2023): CGE modelling specific to Indonesia shows carbon tax revenue recycled into household transfers raises both GDP and employment — this is the **double dividend** (Goulder, 1995)
- Indonesia's PKH conditional cash-transfer program is the ready-made vehicle; it already reaches the households most vulnerable to energy-price increases
- The 3-kg LPG subsidy (IDR 80.2 trillion/year) is actually **regressive** — the richest 10% consume nearly as much subsidised LPG as the poorest 10% (IISD/GSI, 2025). Replacing it with targeted cash transfers would be simultaneously more progressive AND free up fiscal space for revenue recycling.
- Frame this for the reader: "The political obstacle to a carbon tax is not that it harms the poor — it is that the beneficiaries of the *current* subsidy system have no interest in changing the system."

**Sources:** Hartono et al. (2023); Rahma et al. (2025); IISD/GSI; Goulder (1995).

---

### Recommendation 3 — Fix the ETS before expanding it [~100 words]

Before expanding to industry, transport, and forestry, the core ETS needs:
1. **Auctions** — none have happened; all allocation is still free
2. **A functioning price floor** — the embedded carbon levy should kick in when the ETS price falls below a floor level
3. **Stable rules** — the 2025 postponement was caused by regulatory revision mid-year; investors and compliance entities cannot plan under rules that change annually

*Source: ICAP 2025; IEEFA (2025).*

---

### Recommendations 4–5 [~100 words, brief]

**Recommendation 4 — Expand to AFOLU/peatland:** The power sector covers only ~21% of national GHG emissions. Indonesia's largest emissions sources are deforestation and peat fires, entirely outside the current ETS. Peatland restoration is among the cheapest, highest-impact climate interventions available (Tan et al., 2022).

**Recommendation 5 — ASEAN coordination:** Indonesia is the first SE Asian country with an operational ETS. If Indonesia and Singapore (carbon tax at ~USD 25/tCO2) align on price levels, the "competitiveness leakage" argument for keeping Indonesia's price low weakens (Andriansyah & Hong, 2022).

---

### The CBAM clincher [~250 words] — this is the essay's sharpest recommendation

Structure as: problem → fiscal perversity → prescription.

**The problem:** The EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) entered its definitive application regime on **1 January 2026**. It applies to Indonesia's most carbon-intensive exports to the EU: steel (USD 726 million exported to EU in 2023), aluminium (USD 29 million), and fertilisers. Indonesia's domestic carbon price (~USD 2/tCO2) is roughly 97% below the EU ETS price (~€70–75/tCO2), which means Indonesian exporters face near-full CBAM liability on covered products.

**The fiscal perversity:** Indonesia's stated reason for keeping the domestic carbon price low is to protect industrial competitiveness. But CBAM means the opposite is happening: by keeping the price near zero, Indonesia is handing carbon tax revenue to the European Union. Every tonne of CO₂ embedded in Indonesian steel exported to the EU is being priced — just by Brussels, not Jakarta. A domestic price of USD 30/tCO2 would reduce CBAM liability while generating Indonesian state revenue. The current "low price = competitive advantage" logic no longer holds for EU-facing sectors.

**The prescription:** Raise the domestic price to the Tony Blair Institute's benchmark of USD 12–13/tCO2 — the marginal abatement cost for Indonesian coal retirement (TBI, 2025). At that level, Indonesia would simultaneously unlock its cheapest domestic abatement opportunity AND reduce CBAM exposure for steel and aluminium exporters. This is the argument that converts climate ethics into fiscal self-interest.

**Sources:** `raw/Chain task 9 search/DONE_Gap3`; Tony Blair Institute (2025) at `raw/expert/`; ICAP 2025; Argus Media reporting on ETS/CBAM linkage.

## CONCLUSION (300 words)

### Paragraph 1 — Restate the thesis [~100 words]

- ETS-first sequencing is a political-economy choice, not an economics-of-design choice
- The implementation-gap pattern (carbon tax postponed four times; ETS generating USD 5 million in trades against USD 44 billion in fossil-fuel subsidies) is not accidental — it reflects the interests of industrial coalitions in free allocation and of state actors in credit-export revenue
- The Hashim/Arsari story is the human face of a structural argument: the rule-setter and the market beneficiary are the same family

### Paragraph 2 — The constructive close [~100 words]

- Indonesia does not need new policy — it needs to enforce what is already on paper
- Presidential Regulation 110/2025 and the October 2025 NDC already contain the hybrid design (cap-tax-and-trade) that the literature recommends (Lin & Jia, 2020; Sunanda et al., 2025)
- The five recommendations in Section 3 are not proposals — they are already Indonesia's stated commitments. The binding constraint is political will.

### Paragraph 3 — Forward gesture [~100 words]

- CBAM is the external pressure that may finally align fiscal self-interest with climate action — the "keep prices low to protect competitiveness" argument collapses when low prices mean EU collects the revenue instead
- For future research (optional — if word count allows): whether the "policy on paper, hollow in practice" pattern is a generalizable feature of carbon pricing in middle-income countries, and whether external mechanisms (CBAM, conditional climate finance, Article 6 corresponding-adjustment requirements) are the only durable way to shift this equilibrium

### INTRODUCTION (~400 words, 3 paragraphs)

1. **Hook [~160 words]:** The COP29 story — Indonesia's Special Presidential Envoy announced 557 million tonnes of credits ready to sell and attracted EUR 1.2 billion in green financing while not disclosing that his own family conglomerate holds a carbon-concession in the market he was designing — is not an isolated irony but the operating logic of Indonesia's carbon-pricing architecture. *(Tempo/Pulitzer "Forest for Tycoons", June 2025; see `raw/Chain task 9 search/DONE_Gap2`)*

2. **The puzzle [~120 words]:** Indonesia's Tax Harmonization Law (Law 7/2021) legislated both a carbon tax and an ETS in the same legislation, the ETS launched in February 2023, but the carbon tax has been postponed four times and remains unenforced as of early 2026 — despite a developing-country literature that broadly recommends the tax first, and despite total ETS trades amounting to approximately USD 5 million. *(ICAP, 2025; IEEFA, 2025; van den Bergh & Drews, 2026)*

3. **Thesis and roadmap [~120 words]:** This essay argues that ETS-first sequencing reflects deliberate political-economy choices — concentrated burden on a small set of facilities, free allocation for industrial-policy priorities, and a credit-export revenue motive — rather than economic-design optimality, and that Indonesia's own Presidential Regulation 110/2025 already contains the hybrid design the literature recommends, making the binding constraint political will rather than policy design. *(Sunanda et al., 2025; ICAP, 2025)*

---

### SECTION 1: WHY AN ETS FIRST? (~800 words, 5 paragraphs)

1. **Sequencing facts [~150 words]:** Law 7/2021 legislated both carbon tax and ETS simultaneously; the ETS launched February 2023 (Phase 1: ~99 coal plants; Phase 2: 563 installations from 2025) while the carbon tax, due April 2022, has been postponed four times and remains unenforced as of early 2026, meaning Indonesia has not simply chosen ETS over tax but has implemented only the ETS side of a legislated hybrid. *(ICAP, 2025; Sunanda et al., 2025, Table 4)*

2. **What the literature prefers — the puzzle [~150 words]:** Carbon tax dominates the developing-country literature for its administrative simplicity, price certainty for long-term investment, and revenue-recycling potential — and Indonesia-specific CGE modelling shows a revenue-recycled carbon tax raises both GDP and employment — yet Indonesia launched the ETS first, raising the question the essay must answer. *(van den Bergh & Drews, 2026; Hartono et al., 2023; Jia & Lin, 2020)*

3. **Political-economy reason 1 — burden visibility [~150 words]:** The ETS concentrates compliance costs on ~563 regulated installations (mostly state-owned utility PLN), while a carbon tax would visibly raise energy prices for 270 million people; industry representatives have explicitly cited "political resistance" as the barrier to tax enforcement, confirming the ETS is the politically safer option for a government managing industrial coalitions. *(Sunanda et al., 2025 [Stevens §87]; Finon, 2019)*

4. **Political-economy reason 2 — free allocation + credit-export revenue [~200 words]:** Indonesia's 100% free allocation in Phase 1 functions as a de facto industrial subsidy for the downstreaming sectors (nickel, steel, cement, captive coal) that the Jokowi and Prabowo governments prioritise, while the January 2025 lift of the Article 6 moratorium creates a credit-export revenue stream without a domestic tax — the USD 65 billion government target from carbon projects by 2028 confirms the revenue motive, and PR 110/2025's decoupling of international credits from the NDC timeline is the regulatory architecture that makes this possible. *(ICAP, 2025; `raw/Chain task 9 search/DONE_Gap2`; Pierucci et al., 2025)*

5. **Counter-evidence — the "tax is political poison" framing overstates the case [~150 words]:** The carbon tax is not regressive at the national level in Indonesia — it is progressive among lower-income rural households and regressive only in urban areas — and over 70% of Southeast Asian respondents support a national carbon tax, indicating the resistance is elite-level rather than mass-level, a distinction that matters for the essay's prescription in Section 3. *(Yusuf & Resosudarmo, 2015; Rahma et al., 2025; East Asia Forum, Feb 2025)*

### SECTION 2: HOW THE ETS ACTUALLY WORKS (~500 words, 3 paragraphs)

1. **Coverage and phases [~150 words]:** Indonesia's ETS operates in three phases expanding across the electricity sector — Phase 1 (2023–24): ~99 PLN-grid coal plants; Phase 2 (2025–27): 563 installations covering captive coal and gas; Phase 3 (2028–30): all fossil-fuel power — but even at full power-sector completion the ETS covers only ~21% of national emissions (225.3 MtCO2e out of 1,053.4 MtCO2e), leaving forestry, transport, and most industry entirely outside its scope. *(ICAP, 2025)*

2. **Allocation and Article 6 [~100 words]:** The ETS has held no auctions in any phase, continues to allocate allowances at 75–100% free, and under PR 110/2025 has decoupled international credit trading from the NDC timeline while making Corresponding Adjustments — the double-counting prevention mechanism — optional rather than mandatory for international transfers, structurally prioritising credit-export revenue over domestic climate accountability. *(ICAP, 2025; `raw/Chain task 9 search/DONE_Gap2`)*

3. **The hollow reality [~200 words]:** Total IDX Carbon market transactions since the September 2023 launch amount to approximately USD 5 million (IDR ~78 billion), against IDR 713.5 trillion (~USD 44 billion) in annual fossil-fuel subsidies — a ratio of roughly 1:8,000 — while Indonesia's net effective carbon rate is likely negative once those subsidies are netted out, rendering the nominal ETS price signal economically irrelevant and earning Indonesia a "Critically Insufficient" rating from Climate Action Tracker. *(IEEFA, 2025; IISD/GSI, 2025; OECD ECR, 2025; Climate Action Tracker, Dec 2024)*

### SECTION 3: WHAT INDONESIA SHOULD DO NOW (~1,000 words, 6 paragraphs)

1. **Opening frame — "already legislated, not yet enforced" [~50 words]:** Presidential Regulation 110/2025 and the October 2025 NDC already commit Indonesia on paper to all five recommendations the developing-country literature makes, making political will rather than policy design the binding constraint. *(Sunanda et al., 2025; van den Bergh & Drews, 2026)*

2. **Recommendation 1 — Enforce the levy; remove the subsidy that cancels it [~200 words]:** Indonesia should commit to a graduated levy escalation path (e.g. USD 2 now → USD 10 by 2028 → USD 30 by 2032), but since fossil-fuel subsidies create a net-negative effective carbon rate, the prior step is removing the IDR 58.5 trillion coal price cap that implicitly subsidises the very sector the ETS prices — a reform Indonesia has precedent for, having cut the petrol subsidy in 2014. *(Sunanda et al., 2025; OECD ECR, 2025; IISD/GSI, 2025)*

3. **Recommendation 2 — Revenue recycling via PKH [~150 words]:** CGE modelling specific to Indonesia shows that recycling carbon tax revenue into household transfers (the "double dividend") raises both GDP and employment, the existing PKH conditional cash-transfer program is the ready-made vehicle, and replacing the regressive 3-kg LPG subsidy with targeted cash transfers would simultaneously be more progressive and free up fiscal space. *(Hartono et al., 2023; Goulder, 1995; IISD/GSI, 2025)*

4. **Recommendations 3–5 — Fix the ETS, expand to peatland, coordinate regionally [~100 words]:** Before expanding the ETS to industry and forestry, auctions, a price floor, and stable annual rules are prerequisites; peatland restoration offers Indonesia's cheapest large-scale abatement opportunity; and ASEAN price coordination would weaken the competitiveness-leakage argument that keeps Indonesia's price low. *(ICAP, 2025; Tan et al., 2022; Andriansyah & Hong, 2022)*

5. **The CBAM clincher — problem and fiscal perversity [~150 words]:** With CBAM entering definitive application on 1 January 2026, Indonesian steel (USD 726 million exported to EU in 2023) and aluminium face near-full CBAM certificate costs at ~€70–75/tCO2 because Indonesia's domestic price is ~USD 2/tCO2 — meaning Indonesia's "low price protects competitiveness" argument has inverted: by keeping the price near zero, Indonesia is handing carbon tax revenue to Brussels rather than collecting it itself. *(`raw/Chain task 9 search/DONE_Gap3`; ICAP, 2025)*

6. **The CBAM prescription [~150 words]:** Raising the domestic carbon price to the Tony Blair Institute's USD 12–13/tCO2 benchmark — the marginal abatement cost for Indonesian coal retirement — would simultaneously reduce CBAM liability for steel and aluminium exporters and unlock Indonesia's largest and cheapest domestic abatement opportunity, converting climate ethics into fiscal self-interest and eliminating the core tension between the "downstreaming" industrial agenda and credible decarbonisation. *(Fursman, Dace & Macgeoch, 2025 (TBI); `raw/Chain task 9 search/DONE_Gap3`)*

### CONCLUSION (~300 words, 3 paragraphs)

1. **Restate the thesis [~100 words]:** The ETS-first sequencing and perpetually deferred carbon tax are not policy failures — they are deliberate political-economy choices: free allocation protects industrial coalitions, credit exports generate state revenue without a domestic tax, and the price signal is calibrated below the level that would threaten the downstreaming agenda; the Hashim/Arsari conflict of interest is the personal face of a structural argument. *(Sunanda et al., 2025; ICAP, 2025; `raw/Chain task 9 search/DONE_Gap2`)*

2. **Constructive close [~100 words]:** Indonesia already has the right hybrid design on paper — PR 110/2025 and the 2025 NDC contain the cap-tax-and-trade combination that the literature confirms outperforms either instrument alone — making the five Section 3 recommendations not proposals but Indonesia's own stated commitments waiting for enforcement. *(Lin & Jia, 2020; Sunanda et al., 2025)*

3. **Forward gesture [~100 words]:** CBAM is the external lever that may finally align fiscal self-interest with domestic decarbonisation — and the deeper question, whether "policy on paper, hollow in practice" is a generalizable middle-income country pattern and whether external mechanisms are the only durable way to shift this equilibrium, remains open for future research. *(`raw/Chain task 9 search/DONE_Gap3`; Jakob & Steckel, 2014)*
