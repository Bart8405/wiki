Summary 
AENOR has carried out the verification of the Sumatra Merang Peatland Project (SMPP) under the VCS 
and CCB Programs. The project is a combination of Afforestation, Reforestation and Revegetation (ARR) 
project  and  Wetlands  Restoration  and  Conservation  (WRC) project  implemented  in South  Sumatra 
province,  Sumatra,  Republic  of  Indonesia. The  project  to aims  to  protect  and  restore  22,922  ha  of 
peatland  ecosystem,  while  building  strong  and  supportive  relationships  with  the  broad  range  of 
stakeholders  within  the  landscape.  Project  activities  include  forest  patrolling,  fire prevention  and 
monitoring, canal blocking and hydrology management, reforestation and assisted natural regeneration, 
as well as development activities for communities neighboring the project area, such as education and 
health support or provision of access to clean water. 
The project start date is 1 January 2016 and will be operational until 31 December 2062. The estimated 
net  GHG emissions  reductions  and  removals at   validation for  the 47-year  project  span were 
62,912,755 tCO
2

2

monitoring period (January 1st, 2021 – December 31st, 2021) is 1,619,710 tCO
2

project. 
The purpose of the verification was to determine the conformance of the project with respect to the VCS 
Version 4 and CCB Third Edition and the validated PD, and the assessment of the ex-post monitored 
anthropogenic GHG emissions reductions and/or removals that have occurred as a result of the project’s 
activities.  The  scope  of  the  verification  was  to  assess  the  conformance  of  validated  project,  once 
implemented, with the VCS and CCB requirements and requirements in the validated PD. The process 
was  performed  through  a  combination  of  desk  review,  interviews,  and  communications  with  relevant 
personnel. This is the fourth verification event, corresponding to the monitoring period from 1 January 
2021 to 31 December 2021. 
During the verification 5 CLs and 7 CARs were raised.  All these issues where appropriately closed by 
means of corrections, more clear explanations, and other supporting documents. 
Once all issued detected were appropriate resolved, AENOR carried out this final verification report and 
deems with reasonable  level of assurance that  the  project complies with  all of the verification criteria. 
The assessment team has no restrictions or uncertainties with respect to the compliance of the project 
with  the  verification  criteria,  hence,  the  audit  team  concludes  that  the  cumulative  net  GHG  emissions 
reductions or removals of  1,619,710 tCO
2
e over the monitoring period has been quantified in accordance 
with  VCS  rules.  A  buffer  discount  rate  of  14%  was  applied,  resulting 1,392,951 VCUs  eligible  for 
issuance. AENOR  confirms  that  the  project  has  achieved  the Climate  Gold  and Biodiversity  Gold 
distinctions for the verified monitoring period in accordance with the Third Edition CCB Standards.

1 INTRODUCTION 
1.1 Objective 
The  objective  of  the  verification  audit  was  to  conduct  an  independent  assessment  of  the  project  to 
determine:  
• The  extent  to  which  methods  and  procedures,  including  monitoring  procedures,  have  been 
implemented in accordance with the validated project description, including the monitoring plan.  
• The extent to which GHG emission reductions and removals reported in the monitoring report are 
materially accurate. 
1.2 Scope and Criteria 
The  scope  of  the  verification  included  the  review  of  the  GHG  project  and  implementation;  physical 
infrastructure,  activities, technologies, and  processes  of  the  GHG  project;  GHG  sources,  sinks  and/or 
reservoirs; types of GHG’s; and time periods covered. The project follows the framework an Agriculture, 
Forestry,  and  Other  Land-Use  (AFOLU)  project.  Project  activities  are  categorized  as  a  combination  of 
Afforestation, Reforestation and Revegetation (ARR) and Wetlands Restoration and Conservation (WRC). 
Under the WRC component, the project activities relate to both the Conservation of Undrained and Partially 
Drained Peatland (CUPP) and Rewetting of Drained Peatland (RDP). The geographic verification scope is 
defined by the project boundary, the carbon reservoir types, management activities, inventory program, and 
contract periods. 
The monitoring period for this verification is from 01 January 2021 to 31 December 2021. 
The scope of this audit included a verification of the projects calculated reductions and removals with the 
Verified  Carbon  Standard  requirements.  In  addition,  the  audit  assessed  the  project  with  respect  to  the 
validated  baseline  scenarios  presented  in  the  PD  and  the  fulfilment  of  the climate, community, and 
biodiversity criteria against the CCB Standard. 
The scope was defined as follows:  
• The project and its baseline scenarios; 
• The physical infrastructure, activities, technologies and processes of the project; 
• The GHG sources, sinks and/or reservoirs those are applicable to the project; 
• The types of GHGs that are applicable to the project; and 
• The project monitoring period 
• The climate, community, and biodiversity benefits, including gold distinctions

• VCS Standard, v4.3  
• Program Definitions, v4.2  
• AFOLU Non-Permanence Risk Tool, v4.0  
• Climate, Community & Biodiversity Standards, v3.1

1.3 Level of Assurance 
The assessment was conducted to provide a reasonable level of assurance of conformance against the 
defined  audit  criteria  and  materiality  thresholds  within  the  audit  scope.  Based  on  the  audit  findings,  a 
positive evaluation statement reasonably assures that the project GHG assertion is materially correct and 
is a fair representation of the GHG data and information.  
The  threshold  for  materiality  with  respect  to  the  aggregate  of  errors,  omissions,  and  misrepresentations 
relative to the total reported GHG emission reductions/removals was one percent (1%), as established for 
large projects by the VCS Standard. 
All  the  revisions  of  the  verification  report  before  being  submitted  to  the  client  were  subjected  to  an 
independent internal technical review to confirm that all verification activities had been completed according 
to  the  pertinent  AENOR  instructions  required.  The  technical  review  was  performed  by  a  technical 
reviewer(s)  qualified  in  accordance  with  AENOR ́s  qualification  scheme  for  CDM/VCS  validation  and 
verification. 
1.4 Summary Description of the Project 
The  project  is  an ARR  and  WRC project  implemented  in  the implemented  in  South  Sumatra  province, 
Sumatra,  Republic  of  Indonesia. It aims to  protect  and  restore 22,922  ha  of  peatland  ecosystem,  while 
building  strong  and  supportive  relationships  with  the  broad  range  of  stakeholders  within  the  landscape. 
Project  activities  include  forest  patrolling,  fire  prevention  and  monitoring,  canal  blocking  and  hydrology 
management,  reforestation  and  assisted  natural  regeneration,  as  well  as  community  development  sub-
activities. The Project Objective is to restore the ecological functions of the Merang Peatland through the 
use of sustainable land-use models that benefit critically endangered biodiversity and provide benefits for 
local communities.  Throughout this period, the project has supported a daily forest patrol system, and with 
daily weather monitoring. The project continued to implement community development activities and other 
programs achieving positive impacts for stakeholders. During the monitoring period an additional 15 dams 
were  successfully  constructed,  bringing  the  total  to  203  permanent  canal  blocking  dams. The  specific 
objectives are: 
• Climate Objectives 
o Credibly avoid peatland emissions through the protection and rehabilitation of the project 
site.  
o Restore the variety, integrity, and extent of forest types to their original pre-2007 fire state 
to rejuvenate the presence of valuable ecosystem services and peatland habitats.  
o Restore the hydrological integrity of the peatlands.

2.2 Method and Criteria 
The  verification  was  performed  through  a  combination  of  document  review  and  interviews  with  relevant 
personnel, as discussed in Sections 2.3 through 2.5 of this report. At all times, the project was assessed 
for conformance to the criteria described in Section 1.2 of this report. As discussed in Section 2.6, findings 
were issued to ensure that the project was in full conformance to all requirements. 
A project specific Verification and Sampling Plan was developed to guide the verification auditing process 
to ensure efficiency and effectiveness. The purpose of the Verification and Sampling Plan was to present 
a risk assessment for determining the nature and extent of verification procedures necessary to ensure the 
risk of  auditing error  was reduced to a reasonable level. The  Verification  &  Sampling  Plan  methodology 
was derived from all items in our verification process stated above. Specifically, the sampling plan utilized 
the VCS guidance documents and ISO 14064-3. Any modifications applied to the Verification and Sampling 
plan were made based upon the conditions observed for monitoring in order to detect the processes with 
highest risk of material discrepancy. 
The verification activities in which risks were assessed were the evaluations of the monitoring system (data 
flow,  data  control  procedures,  etc.)  but  mainly  the  quality  of  raw  data  as  well  as  sources  and  the 
spreadsheet     calculations.     AENOR     reproduced     and     verified 100%     of     sheets     in     the 
MR_Tables_2021_20240119 and    the    other    spreadsheets /36-47/ for    the    monitoring    period  
01 January 2021 – 31 December 2021 for the project area. The project boundary and land cover changes 
in the project area for the monitoring period were 100% checked using the GIS database /18-24/. 
The  carbon  stock  changes,  and  the  land  used  classes  in  the  project  area  were  also  100%  verified  and 
crosschecked with validated values. For data provided for the reference region, AENOR carried out onsite 
samples of at least 5% of data since they had already been previously validated and posed a lower risk to 
the emissions reductions achieved by the project. 
AENOR  carried  out  a  deep  and  meticulous  review  of  the  spreadsheets  in  order  to  verify  the  correct 
application  of the methodology (formulae,  equations.)  and checked that data required for calculating the 
GHG removals and reductions were appropriately provided. Based on the assessment carried out, AENOR 
confirms with a reasonable level of assurance that the claimed emission reductions and removals are free 
from material errors, omissions, or misstatements. 
AENOR confirms that sufficient evidence was presented for the reported net anthropogenic GHG emission 
reductions and removals and that there  is a clear audit trail that contains the evidence  and records that 
validate the stated figure in this verification report since: 
• Sufficient evidence available: The project participant has provided the 100%  of  data used  in the 
calculations to achieve the final amount of GHG emission reductions and removals reported. 
• Nature of evidence: The raw data were collected from reliable sources. They are detailed in the 
project  documents  and  have  been  provided  to  the  verification  team  and  the  most  relevant  are 
appropriately detailed in the Appendix 1.

2.3 Document Review

processes with highest risk of material discrepancy.

documentation and confirmed that the Project adheres to the requirements set out in the VCS AFOLU Non-
Permanence Risk Tool. Each risk factor  was thoroughly assessed for conformance. The final score was 
calculated to be 14%.

displacement of pre-project agricultural activities and iii) emissions from ecological leakage (LK-ECO).

of the PP described in the MR demonstrates that there was no leakage throughout the monitoring period

increase  in  GHG  emissions  attributable  to  displacement  of  pre-project  agricultural  activities  in  A/R  CDM

4.4.5 Optional Gold Level: Climate Change Adaptation Measures (GL1.3) 
According to the reports and studies cited in the validated PD, the geographic region of the project zone is 
likely  to  exhibit  various  effects  of  climate  change  with  greater  weather  anomalies.  Temperatures  will 
increase consistently with increased duration of heat waves and dry spells. There will be a considerable 
shift in precipitation patterns. There will be increased frequency and intensity of heavy rainfall events. 
The region is vulnerable to other weather-related disasters and climate hazards such as forest and land 
fires,  storms,  and  drought  that  damage  infrastructure  and  degrade  ecosystems,  leading  to  loss  of  life, 
property, ecosystem services, and  livelihoods.  Accordingly,  longer  dry season  and  a  more intense rainy 
season will result in prolonged drought and more intense flooding. 
Changes in land-use, temperature, and precipitation may cause widespread wildfires in the project zone. 
Altered  biophysical  features,  as  well  as  vegetation  composition  and  structure  impact  biodiversity  and 
ecosystem services functioning. Climate change would exacerbate this problem. Climate change has food 
security and economic growth implications. Changing precipitation patterns, evaporation, run-off, and soil 
moisture would all impact agricultural production levels. In addition, reduced availability of freshwater will 
have implications for health and economic production processes. 
The PP has design and is implementing a set of project activities that will contribute directly or indirectly to 
climate change adaptation. Section 3.3 of the MR lists these activities, their details, the achievements during 
the monitoring period and the adaptation benefits. 
Due to the specific nature of the activities, their direct impacts and results are obvious. Furthermore, all the 
stakeholders asked by the audit team during the interviews were well aware of the adverse effects of climate 
change and the contribution of project activities to reduce these effects. All interviews mentioned primarily 
the effectiveness of fire prevention and how the adverse effects of fire (destruction of the forest and harmful 
smoke  clouds)  have  been minimized since  the  project  started,  reporting  no  fires  during  the  monitoring 
period. 
Based on the evidence and testimonies, it is the opinion of the audit team that the activities implemented 
by the project deliver the intended impacts on climate change adaptation. Hence, AENOR deems that the 
measures that the project proponent has implemented to mitigate or get adaptation to the climate change 
are reliable, effective and are consistent according to the information provided on the MR.

4.5.2 Negative Community Impact Mitigation (CM2.2) 
The PP identified the potential negative impacts listed below and took measures to mitigate these impacts 
so that the project has had a net positive impact on communities. 
As biodiversity returns to the project area as result of project activities, it is possible that daily activities of 
fishermen and farmer communities may be impacted by large wildlife such as sun bears or tigers. The PP

taken place for the moment.

inconvenience  caused  by  higher  water  levels.  The  biggest  risk  to  smallholder  farming  assets  is  from 
wildfires,  which  could  completely  wipe  out  their  investment.  This  has  been  confirmed  by interviewing 
community members. 
Regular engagement and communication with fishermen and farmers have been confirmed by the evidence 
provided /239-250/ and the interviews performed during the onsite visit.  
In  accordance  with  the  reported  information,  the  project does  not result  in  net  negative  impacts  on  the 
wellbeing  of  the  community groups.  Assessment  by  the  audit  team  concluded  that  the  likelihood  of  net 
negative  impacts  on  the  well-being  of  the  community groups is  adequately  addressed  in  the  monitoring 
report. Mitigation actions are implemented, and negative impacts are reduced to the minimum.

4.6.3 Net Positive Biodiversity Impacts (B2.2) 
The  demonstration  of  a  net-positive  biodiversity  impact  over  the  project  lifetime  has  been  done  by 
comparing the biodiversity without-project scenario, an Acacia plantation, with further degradation of the 
peatland, the remaining vegetation and the forest, with the project’s current biodiversity conditions: further 
habitat degradation and homogenization, continued use of the canals for drainage which heavily impacts 
hydrology of the  peatland,  potential unchecked  hunting and illegal  logging practices, continued  poor fire 
management practices and exacerbated drought and increased fire incidence in the area 
The main project  activities  that  produce  biodiversity  impacts are  i)  canal  blocking,  ii)  reforestation  and 
assisted natural regeneration, iii) fire management and iv) increased monitoring and enforcement. Many of 
these project activities that are effectively maintaining and supporting biodiversity in the project area are 
bringing about climate and community benefits as well. 
Specifically, during the 2021 period, the PP constructed 15 canal dams, provided a consistent monitoring 
of fires with the total absence of fires, increased the planting with 2.950 nursery seedlings, improved the 
biodiversity  monitoring  as  described  in  sections  above  and  continued  with  daily  patrols.  The  PP  has 
provided further  explanations of the consequences of these activities and how they mitigate the impacts 
that could occur in the without-project scenario.

4.6.9 Negative Offsite Biodiversity Impacts (B3.1) and Mitigation Actions (B3.2) 
The PP has identified a potential negative offsite biodiversity impact during the current monitoring period. 
Certain  populations  of  fish  now  living  in  man-made  canals  may  have  their  lifecycles,  feeding  strategies, 
mating strategies negatively affected, and their canal habitats fragmented by canal dam construction. The 
return  of  riparian  vegetation  to  canals  should  gradually  improve  the  health  of  the  wetland  ecosystem. 
Furthermore, the dams are constructed to create natural spillways that allow for fish passage between dams 
when water levels are high.. Any potential net negative impact of the canal-living fish species is more than 
offset  by  the  benefits  of  blocking  the  canals  for  the  wider  peatland  ecosystem  (particularly  terrestrial 
species),  meaning  that  project  activities  should  result  in  clear  net  biodiversity  benefits,  both  onsite  and 
offsite. Verification team assessed the information provided on the MR through interviews during the onsite 
visit and deems that the information stated on the MR is reliable and right. Complementary, AENOR has 
reviewed the dams to verify the spillways mentioned above.

4.6.13 Optional Gold Level: Trigger Species Population Trends (GL3.3) 
The project area and project zone have a number of endangered and critically endangered trigger species 
within it that qualify this project for exceptional biodiversity benefits under the CCB Standard version 3. The 
project area qualifies as providing exceptional biodiversity benefits by meeting the vulnerability criteria (a), 
which requires the regular  occurrence of at least a single individual critically endangered or endangered 
species.  127 trigger  species have been actively surveyed and identified as being present  in  the  project 
area during  the  current  monitoring  period,  including mammals  (21),  birds  (39),  plants  (32),  reptiles  (7), 
amphibians (5), fish (18), arthropods (5). 
Without the presence of the project, it is highly likely that species diversity would be negatively affected by 
increasing development and land-use change within the project area. Even though currently it is unlikely 
that the project area holds resident individuals. Rather, it is more likely that the project area is being utilized 
as a corridor between other land-uses in the landscape. As project activities are developed further on, the 
ability  for  these  areas  to  support  biodiversity  will  be  strengthened  and  these  areas  will  be  protected. 
Increased forest cover, planting of tree species that are important in the lifecycle of these species, and the 
rewetting of drained peat areas will likely encourage population growth. 
The  PP has provided the  name of all the  trigger species in the area as  well as their  population  trend in 
different situations: the without project scenario, the baseline scenario, and the project scenario. The PP 
has explained the measures used to enhance the conservation of these species. AENOR has reviewed the 
sources such as IUCN red list, to verify that the species mentioned are really threatened. Also, the VVB 
during the onsite visit had the opportunity to directly sight some of threatened and trigger species such as 
dragonflies or birds among others. Also, the PP has shown proof of these animals’ population by results of 
the camera trap.  Also, the audit team has reviewed the papers provided by the PP where there is evidence 
of  the  presence  of  these  species  within  the  project  area.  Additionally,  different  interviews  have  been 
performed and information about the presence of these species and their increasing, have been obtained. 
The verification team is able to confirm that the presence of these species under the project area is reliable 
and accurate.
