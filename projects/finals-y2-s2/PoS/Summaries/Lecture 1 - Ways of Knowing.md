# Lecture 1 — Ways of Knowing

*The opening lecture. Three groups of sciences (natural, human, social) and the foundational distinction between the **nomothetic** and **idiographic** approaches to knowledge.*

**Date:** 4 February 2026 · **Lecturer:** Prof. James W. McAllister · **Key thinker:** ==**Wilhelm Windelband**== (1848–1915)

---

## Key concepts — definitions

| Term                     | Definition                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                     |
| ------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Nomothetic approach**  | ==Identifying regularities in the world==, formulating generalisations or laws to describe them, and deriving explanations from those laws. ==Typical of the natural sciences.== Strengths: sweeping, economical knowledge. Weaknesses: erases specificity, can be reductive/mechanistic.                                                                                                                                                                                                                      |
| **Idiographic approach** | ==Understanding the meaning of *contingent, unique, often subjective* outcomes. Typical of the human sciences==. %%Question - what is the difference or connection between nomothetic and idiographic and deduction/induction? Feels quite similar%% Strengths: context-sensitive, reveals differences between apparently similar cases. Weaknesses: blind to general factors that constrain outcomes. (Windelband's term for the disciplines that use it: *Geisteswissenschaften*, "sciences of the spirit".) |
| **Natural sciences**     | Physics, chemistry, biology, astronomy — assume the physical universe is uniform and simple; focus on universals and regularities; use mathematisation, abstraction, idealisation.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                             |
| **Human sciences**       | History, art history, linguistics, literary studies, philosophy, religious studies. ==Focus on historical particularity; main output is *interpretations* of acts, texts, artworks; mistrust generalisation.==                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                 |
| **Social sciences**      | Sociology, political science, economics, psychology, anthropology. The youngest group; feel the pull of *both* natural sciences (econ, demography — largely nomothetic) and human sciences (cultural anthropology, political theory — largely idiographic).                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    |
| **Multidisciplinarity**  | Integration of knowledge and methods from multiple disciplines to address complex problems. Lecture's example: ==the Dutch *stikstofcrisis*== needs chemistry + ecology + political science + economics + history.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                             |
|                          |                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                |

## Lecture walkthrough

The course has **5 themes** across 11 lectures:
- I. Basics (lectures 1–2)
- II. Scientific devices (3–4)
- III. Models of scientific practice (5–6)
- IV. Methodological toolbox (7–8)
- V. Rationality and culture (9–11)

==**Three groups of sciences:** Natural, human, social — each with different objects, methods, and forms of "highest knowledge":==
- ==Natural sciences: highest form = **scientific laws** (e.g. Newton's $F = G m_1 m_2 / r^2$).==
- ==Human sciences: highest form = **intimate knowledge of particulars** (interpretations, hermeneutics).==
- ==Social sciences: split — economics & demography lean nomothetic; cultural anthropology & political theory lean idiographic.====

==**The Windelband distinction (around 1894):** Conceptualises the natural-vs-human divide as a choice of *approach*, not just a choice of object. The same object (a society, a psyche) can be studied either nomothetically or idiographically.==

**Diversity within disciplines:** Even psychology splits — psychometric/trait psychology is nomothetic; Freud's 150-page clinical study of "little Hans" (1909) is idiographic.

## Key thinker — Wilhelm Windelband

German neo-Kantian philosopher, 1848–1915. Coined the **nomothetic/idiographic** distinction around 1894 in his rectoral address at Strasbourg. The terms are still the standard vocabulary for the natural-vs-human-science divide and recur throughout the rest of the course.

---

> **Explained easily — the whole lecture:**
> Different fields of study don't just have different *topics* — they have different *ideas of what counts as knowing well*. A physicist wants laws (general rules that work for everyone, everywhere). A historian wants to capture what made *this* event, *this* person, *this* moment unique. ==Windelband's two words for those two ambitions: **nomothetic** ("law-making" — generalising) and **idiographic** ("own-writing" — specifying).== The whole semester of Philosophy of Science is basically a tour of how these two ambitions play out and where they clash. Most of the time, social sciences are sitting awkwardly between them.

> **Explained easily — the nitrogen-crisis example:**
> The Netherlands' stikstofcrisis (since 2019) needs *chemistry* (what is ammonia doing in the soil?), *ecology* (which species are affected?), *political science* (which laws and which coalitions can do something?), *economics* (what happens to farmers?), and *history* (why is land use the way it is?). That's the point of multidisciplinarity — and why you need to *recognise* what kind of knowledge each discipline gives you before you can combine them.

## Likely exam angles

- **MCQ:** "Which best characterises the nomothetic approach?" → answer: typical of the natural sciences (per Lecture 7 review).
- **Define:** nomothetic vs idiographic, with one example of each.
- **Apply:** which of the three groups (natural / human / social) does a given example discipline belong to, and what are the strengths/weaknesses of treating it nomothetically vs idiographically?
- **Cross-lecture:** Windelband's term *Geisteswissenschaften* sets up Lecture 10 (Dilthey's *Verstehen*) directly.
