# Lecture 1 — Ways of Knowing

*The opening lecture. Three groups of sciences (natural, human, social) and the foundational distinction between the **nomothetic** and **idiographic** approaches to knowledge.*

**Date:** 4 February 2026 · **Lecturer:** Prof. James W. McAllister · **Key thinker:** ==**Wilhelm Windelband**== (1848–1915)

---

## Key concepts — definitions

| Term                     | Definition                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                     |
| ------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Nomothetic approach**  | ==Identifying regularities in the world==, formulating generalisations or laws to describe them, and deriving explanations from those laws. ==Typical of the natural sciences.== Strengths: sweeping, economical knowledge. Weaknesses: erases specificity, can be reductive/mechanistic.                                                                                                                                                                                                                      |
| **Idiographic approach** | ==Understanding the meaning of *contingent, unique, often subjective* outcomes. Typical of the human sciences==. %%Question - what is the difference or connection between nomothetic and idiographic and deduction/induction? Feels quite similar%% Strengths: context-sensitive, reveals differences between apparently similar cases. Weaknesses: blind to general factors that constrain outcomes. (Windelband's term for the disciplines that use it: *Geisteswissenschaften*, "sciences of the spirit".) |
| **Natural sciences**     | Physics, chemistry, biology, astronomy — assume the physical universe is uniform and simple; focus on universals and regularities; use mathematisation, abstraction, idealisation.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                             |
| **Human sciences**       | History, art history, linguistics, literary studies, philosophy, religious studies. ==Focus on historical particularity; main output is *interpretations* of acts, texts, artworks; mistrust generalisation.==                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                 |
| **Social sciences**      | Sociology, political science, economics, psychology, anthropology. The youngest group; feel the pull of *both* natural sciences (econ, demography — largely nomothetic) and human sciences (cultural anthropology, political theory — largely idiographic).                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    |
| **Multidisciplinarity**  | Integration of knowledge and methods from multiple disciplines to address complex problems. Lecture's example: ==the Dutch *stikstofcrisis*== needs chemistry + ecology + political science + economics + history.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                             |
|                          |                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                |

## Lecture walkthrough

The course has **5 themes** across 11 lectures:
- I. Basics (lectures 1–2)
- II. Scientific devices (3–4)
- III. Models of scientific practice (5–6)
- IV. Methodological toolbox (7–8)
- V. Rationality and culture (9–11)

==**Three groups of sciences:** Natural, human, social — each with different objects, methods, and forms of "highest knowledge":==
- ==Natural sciences: highest form = **scientific laws** (e.g. Newton's $F = G m_1 m_2 / r^2$).==
- ==Human sciences: highest form = **intimate knowledge of particulars** (interpretations, hermeneutics).==
- ==Social sciences: split — economics & demography lean nomothetic; cultural anthropology & political theory lean idiographic.====

==**The Windelband distinction (around 1894):** Conceptualises the natural-vs-human divide as a choice of *approach*, not just a choice of object. The same object (a society, a psyche) can be studied either nomothetically or idiographically.==

**Diversity within disciplines:** Even psychology splits — psychometric/trait psychology is nomothetic; Freud's 150-page clinical study of "little Hans" (1909) is idiographic.

## Key thinker — Wilhelm Windelband

German neo-Kantian philosopher, 1848–1915. Coined the **nomothetic/idiographic** distinction around 1894 in his rectoral address at Strasbourg. The terms are still the standard vocabulary for the natural-vs-human-science divide and recur throughout the rest of the course.

---

> **Explained easily — the whole lecture:**
> Different fields of study don't just have different *topics* — they have different *ideas of what counts as knowing well*. A physicist wants laws (general rules that work for everyone, everywhere). A historian wants to capture what made *this* event, *this* person, *this* moment unique. ==Windelband's two words for those two ambitions: **nomothetic** ("law-making" — generalising) and **idiographic** ("own-writing" — specifying).== The whole semester of Philosophy of Science is basically a tour of how these two ambitions play out and where they clash. Most of the time, social sciences are sitting awkwardly between them.

> **Explained easily — the nitrogen-crisis example:**
> The Netherlands' stikstofcrisis (since 2019) needs *chemistry* (what is ammonia doing in the soil?), *ecology* (which species are affected?), *political science* (which laws and which coalitions can do something?), *economics* (what happens to farmers?), and *history* (why is land use the way it is?). That's the point of multidisciplinarity — and why you need to *recognise* what kind of knowledge each discipline gives you before you can combine them.

### Multiple Choice Questions

**Q1.** According to Windelband, the nomothetic approach is primarily characterised by:
- A) Interpreting the meaning of unique, contingent events
- B) Identifying regularities and formulating general laws
- C) Using qualitative fieldwork and thick description
- D) Studying the biography of individual historical figures

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B) Identifying regularities and formulating general laws**

The nomothetic approach (from Greek *nomos* = law) aims at discovering universal regularities, which is the hallmark of the natural sciences. Option A describes the *idiographic* approach. Options C and D are associated with the human sciences, not the nomothetic ideal.

</details>

**Q4.** The Dutch *stikstofcrisis* is used in the lecture as an example of:
- A) Why the idiographic approach is superior to the nomothetic
- B) How quantitative methods outperform qualitative ones
- C) Why complex problems require multidisciplinary knowledge
- D) The limits of scientific laws in political contexts

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C) Why complex problems require multidisciplinary knowledge**

The nitrogen crisis requires chemistry, ecology, political science, economics, and history simultaneously — none alone is sufficient. The example makes the point that before combining disciplines you need to *recognise* what kind of knowledge each provides. It is not a comparison of nomothetic vs idiographic superiority.

</details>

**Q5.** Freud's 150-page clinical study of "Little Hans" (1909) is mentioned in the lecture as an example of:
- A) A nomothetic study within psychology
- B) An idiographic study within psychology
- C) A multidisciplinary study combining psychology and history
- D) An argument against the idiographic approach

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B) An idiographic study within psychology**

The "Little Hans" case study focuses on one child's particular psychological experience in depth — the hallmark of the idiographic approach. It contrasts with psychometric/trait psychology, which is nomothetic. This example shows that even within *one* discipline (psychology) both approaches are used.

</details>

**Q6.** Which of the following is *not* listed in the lecture as a feature of the natural sciences?
- A) Focus on universals and regularities
- B) Mathematisation and abstraction
- C) Intimate knowledge of particulars
- D) Assumption that the physical universe is uniform

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C) Intimate knowledge of particulars**

Intimate knowledge of particulars is the *highest form of knowledge* in the **human sciences**, not the natural sciences. The natural sciences aim for laws, mathematisation, and universality — the other three options.

### Short Essay / Open Questions

**Essay Q1.** Define Windelband's nomothetic and idiographic distinction and explain why the social sciences occupy an intermediate position between the natural and human sciences. Illustrate with at least one concrete example from each end of the spectrum.

<details>
<summary>Model answer outline</summary>

**Key points to hit:**
- Define nomothetic: law-seeking, generalising, abstracts away individual differences; typical of natural sciences; highest form of knowledge = scientific law.
- Define idiographic: meaning-seeking, focuses on the unique and contingent; typical of human sciences; highest form = intimate knowledge of a particular event, person, or text.
- Explain the intermediate position of social sciences: economics and demography lean nomothetic (Tinbergen's gravity model, Duverger's law); cultural anthropology and political theory lean idiographic.
- Note that Windelband frames this as a choice of *approach*, not just subject matter — the same object can be studied either way (e.g. psychology: trait psychology vs Freud's Little Hans).
- The distinction matters because it shapes what counts as a "good answer" in each field.

**Good example to use:** Psychology splits — psychometric/trait psychology is nomothetic (seeks general laws of personality); Freud's 150-page Little Hans study is idiographic (seeks to understand *this* child's unique experience). Or: economics (nomothetic — gravity model) vs. cultural anthropology (idiographic — thick description of a particular society).

**Common mistake to avoid:** Confusing nomothetic/idiographic with quantitative/qualitative. The lecture is explicit that these are *not* the same distinction — a discipline's *aim* (generalise vs. interpret particulars) is not the same as its *method* (numbers vs. words). This overlap comes up again in Lecture 8.

</details>

---

**Essay Q2.** "The same object can be studied either nomothetically or idiographically." What does Windelband mean by this? Use the example of psychology to illustrate and assess the implications for how we evaluate knowledge claims in the social sciences.

<details>
<summary>Model answer outline</summary>

**Key points to hit:**
- Windelband's key move: the nomothetic/idiographic divide is about *approach*, not about the inherent nature of the subject matter. A society, a psyche, a historical event can each be approached through either lens.
- Nomothetic psychology: looks for general laws of behaviour and personality applicable to all humans.
- Idiographic psychology: seeks to understand the unique meaning-structure of one person's experience (Freud's clinical work).
- Implication 1: what counts as a "valid result" differs — a nomothetic study must generalise; an idiographic study must capture particularity.
- Implication 2: strengths and weaknesses are flip sides — nomothetic psychology is powerful but erases individuality; idiographic case studies are rich but hard to generalise.
- Implication 3: social sciences are therefore permanently caught between two different *standards of success*, which is why methodology debates in the social sciences are so persistent.

**Good example to use:** The Little Hans case versus trait psychology / the Big Five personality model.

**Common mistake to avoid:** Treating one approach as simply "better." The lecture presents both as having genuine strengths and genuine weaknesses — a good answer weighs them rather than declaring a winner.

</details>

---

**Essay Q3.** "Natural sciences, human sciences, and social sciences each have a different idea of what counts as the highest form of knowledge." Explain and assess this claim, drawing on Windelband's framework and at least two examples from different disciplines.

<details>
<summary>Model answer outline</summary>

**Key points to hit:**
- Natural sciences: highest form = scientific law (mathematical, universal, elegant — e.g. Newton's law of gravitation).
- Human sciences: highest form = intimate, interpretive knowledge of particulars (a historian's deep knowledge of a specific event, a literary scholar's interpretation of a text).
- Social sciences: contested — some lean toward laws (Duverger's law in political science; Tinbergen's gravity model in economics) while others lean toward interpretation (cultural anthropology, political theory).
- Assess: this plurality of epistemic ideals explains methodological conflicts *within* disciplines, not just *between* them.
- The Dutch stikstofcrisis shows that complex real-world problems require *combining* these different standards — knowing what each kind of knowledge contributes.

**Good example to use:** Duverger's law vs. a detailed historical account of how a particular electoral system emerged in a specific country — both are "political science" but aim at completely different targets.

**Common mistake to avoid:** Implying that human or social science knowledge is "softer" or "lesser" than natural science knowledge. Windelband's point is precisely that idiographic knowledge is not inferior — it has a *different* ideal, not a lower one.

</details>
