2) Community 
The project is expected to increase community welfare through the establishment 
of  economic  institutions  (cooperatives)  in  24  villages.  Through  cooperatives,  the 
community  will  have  the  ability  to  organize  and  increase  their  capacity  and 
capability.   In   their   business,   the   cooperatives   will   involve   illegal   loggers, 
marginalized  groups  and  women's  groups.  The  involvement  of  illegal  loggers  is 
expected to reduce illegal activities due to the preservation of forest activities. This 
is  because  the  community  has  realized  that  the  forest  is  their  main  source  of 
income  through  optimizing  Non-Timber  Forest  Products  (NTFPs)  in  the  form  of 
certified  rattan,  forest  honey  farming,  deer  breeding,  and  conservation  tourism. 
Meanwhile,  the  involvement  of  women's  groups  will  optimize  the  role  of  women 
through agroforestry and Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises (MSMEs), so as to 
improve welfare quality indicators. 
The project highly respects the existence of indigenous or customary peoples. The 
project will increase the strength and influence of “adat” on the interaction of social 
life so that the preservation and sacredness of customs will be stronger. 
Apart from that, through the project, it is hoped that there will be a change in the 
pattern of land management from conventional to sustainable. Farmers will have 
the opportunity to produce organic fertilizer independently so that they can increase 
the  selling  price  of  agricultural/plantation  products  that  are  nowadays  more 
acceptable to the market.

The project will protect the habitat of endangered wildlife such as Manis javanica

Cynogale  bennettii (Water  Weasel).  Wildlife  protection  is  carried  out  through 
education and field practice for the community so that there will be no human-fauna 
conflicts, regular patrols involving the community, and expanding habitat through 
landscape connectivity and habitat restoration. 
The project will also protect and enrich various endangered species of flora such as 
Shorea sumatrana (Slooten) Desch, Shorea pachyphylla Ridl. ex Symington, Shorea 
pauciflora  King, Cinnamomum  parthenoxylon (Jack) Meisn., Shorea  ovata  Dyer,

ferrugineag  Parijis.  Local  community  will  be  involved  in  all  project  activities 
including   periodic   standing   inventory,   making   observation   plots,   procuring 
generative seeds, trial development research, planting vegetative seedlings trials, 
and planting/enrichment to increase species abundance.

2.1 Project Goals, Design and Long-Term Viability  
2.1.1 Summary Description of the Project (VCS, 3.2, 3.6, 3.10, 3.11, 3.13, 3.14; CCB, 
G1.2) 
Gunung Lumut Landscape Project (“GLLP”), located in Paser Regency, East Kalimantan Province, 
Indonesia, is one of the world's remaining tropical forests (See Figure 1). Paser Regency is directly 
adjacent to North Penajam Paser Regency, where the Capital City of Nusantara (New State Capital 
of Indonesia/”IKN”) is located. The regency will  be  a  buffer  zone  for  the  IKN  environment
12 
and 
since GLLP is located approximately + 75.60 km from IKN, its location is important for  IKN (Figure 
2). IKN has been proclaimed to replace DKI Jakarta as the capital of the Republic of Indonesia with 
the concept of Forest City. It is designed to have high  natural conditions (creating 75 % of green 
area) with low carbon emission
13
 
GLLP aims to protect and restore + 116,001 Ha of concession consisting of predominantly lowland 
forest with fragmented forest condition, and the main Watersheds (DAS), namely the Telake River 
in the North-Western part of the project area and the Kendilo River in the West, South, and Eastern 
part  of  the  project  area.  GLLP  consists  of  concession  areas  for  production  forests  (Unit  I)  and 
protected  forests  (Unit  II).  The  Unit  I  project  area  has  obtained  a  Forest  Management  Business 
License  for  Production  Forest (“PBPH-HP”) which for Business As Usual (BAU) is given permit to 
harvest  timber.  The  Unit  II  project  area  has  obtained  principal  approval  for  a  Forest  Utilization 
Business Permit for Protected Forest (“PBPH-HL”). The protected forest in the project area is the 
Gunung Lumut Protected Forest (“HL Gunung Lumut”) which is the sacred land
14
 and  cultural 
center  of  the  Dayak  Paser  indigenous  or  customary  people.  HL  Gunung  Lumut  is  located  in  the 
middle  of  the  production  forest  concession  areas  (Unit  I)  which  also  functions  as  the  utilization 
block  of  the  Gunung  Lumut-Gunung  Ketam  landscape  (see Figure  1).  Within  the project  zone  of 
GLLP, there is a customary forest area where the Mului indigenous or customary people live, which 
has  received  recognition  from  the  Government  of  the  Republic  of  Indonesia  (the  Ministry  of 
Environment and Forestry (“KLHK”) as the Mului Customary Forest.

Before the project started, the areas for this project were designated to be production forests and 
protected forests. For the Gunung Lumut Protected Forest area (Unit II), the provincial government 
carried out the site management, followed by a policy change where the management is then taken 
over  by  a  site-level  forest  management  unit  known  as  Kesatuan  Pengelolaan  Hutan  (“KPH”). 
Currently,  the  HL  Gunung  Lumut  is  managed  under  the  Telake  forest  management  unit  (KPH 
Telake) authority, where Unit II is included in the utilization Zone.          
Aligning  with  the  multi-business  forestry  concept  regulated  by  the  Ministry  of  Environment  and 
Forestry (Regulation No. 8 of 2021), the authority to manage the protected forests is now allowed 
to  be  managed  by  private  parties.  GLLP  has  initiated  a  change  in  business  activities  in  the 
production forest area (Unit I) by including the ecosystem services for carbon sequestration and 
storage  while also  placing the HL Gunung  Lumut  area (Unit  II)  in a strategic  position as a  buffer 
zone for Unit I to form a compact landscape to prevent fragmentation of wildlife habitat. With the 
project’s intervention, the production forest area will be converted into a protected area while 
management of the protected forest area will be strengthened to ensure undisturbed ecological 
functions of the project area. Project interventions to increase productivity in protected forest areas 
are also carried out through ecosystem services produced by forests (e.g., carbon) and is conducted 
with community participation to ensure achievement in sustainability. In a global challenge such 
as climate change, biodiversity loss, and socioeconomic disparities, "strong sustainability" offers a 
clear concept for a sustainable future. Natural capital, including biodiversity, ecosystems, and non-
renewable resources, has an inherent value that cannot be entirely replaced or duplicated by man-
made  capital  that  demonstrates  the  interconnectedness  and  interdependence  of  natural  and 
social  systems,  emphasizing  the  need  to  preserve  ecological  integrity
15
.  Achieving  "strong 
sustainability"  requires  a  collective  effort,  transformative  change,  and  a  solid  commitment  to 
preserving ecosystems and natural resources for future generations. 
GLLP Unit II is considered one of the centers for plant and wildlife biodiversity within Kalimantan 
Island (Tropenbos, 2009). Based on the survey results conducted by PT TMK in 2022, at least 1 
(one)  floral  species  were  categorized  as  critically  endangered,  8  (eight)  as  endangered,  and  15 
(fifteen)  as  vulnerable.  The  ecosystem  of  Gunung  Lumut-Gunung  Ketam  and  unit  I  areas  were 
considered  priority  areas  for  species  of  Rangkong/Hornbils,  which  is  categorized  as  critically 
endangered species in Indonesia. In terms of fauna species, there were 118 species of birds, 20 
mammals,  and  7  (seven)  herpetofauna  found  in  the  project  area.  Among  them,  at  least  2  (two) 
species  were  categorized  as  critically  endangered,  7  (seven)  as  endangered,  and  10  (ten)  as 
vulnerable. Tropenbos, (2009) stated that the protected forest of Gunung Lumut is considered a 
forest ecosystem with a richer biodiversity than any other forests within Kalimantan Island. 
In  addition  to  its  rich  biodiversity,  East  Kalimantan  is  the  largest  coal  producer  in  Indonesia 
according to the Ministry of Energy and Mineral Resources, 2020. The quality and calorie value of 
coal from East Kalimantan is considered above average, causing more coal from this province to

The potential for coal deposits under the forest stands in East Kalimantan poses a distinct threat 
of converting forests into mining. Deforestation caused by mining is a significant problem in East 
Kalimantan, Indonesia
18.
 Coal mining, in particular, has been identified as a substantial cause of 
mining-related deforestation in the country
19
. Mining-related deforestation in East Kalimantan has 
significantly impacted local communities and the environment, including damage to livelihoods and 
forest ecosystems
20.
 This threat also occurs in the GLLP project area, especially Unit I, a production 
forest;  where  legally,  the  prevailing  laws allow coal mining  permits  to  be  submitted  on  the area. 
Article 91 of Government Regulation of the Republic of Indonesia No. 23 of 2021 states that using 
forest areas for development purposes other than forestry activities is possible, including mining. 
The  maximum  quota  that  can  be  considered  is  10%  (ten  percent)  of  the  effective  area  of each 
Forest Management Business License (“PBPH”) so that there is an area of ± 6,861.8 Ha in GLLP 
Unit I that can be converted into mining (Figure 3). Based on this allocation, currently, there are 
areas for which a decree on the approval on the use of forest areas has been issued for mining 
production operations and its supporting facilities. 
The GLLP project area also has other challenges and threats. Illegal activities such as illegal logging 
have  occurred  within  the  last  few  decades. Furthermore,  there are  illegal  conversions  of  forests 
into  palm  and  rubber  plantations  by  the  local  communities.  In  fact,  between  2000-2005,  illegal 
logging in GLLP Unit II was intensive even though a ministerial decree (Decree of the Ministry of 
Forestry No. 24/Kpts/Um/1983) have passed declaring the Gunung Lumut forest to be a protected 
forest area prohibiting all logging activities (TBI, 2004).

3. Soil Types and Land System 
Land System 
The land system is a combined information based on ecological principles connected with the types 
of rocks, hydroclimate, landform, soil types, and organisms, where the same environmental factor 
combinations will generate the same land system. Based on the analyzed results, the project area 
has 16 land systems where the largest area occupies  the Maput Land System category22 located 
on rocky hills with moderate to slow soil permeability
23
. 
Soil Types 
Soil  as  one  of  the  biophysical  elements  that  make  up  an  area  consists  of  several  components, 
namely solid material (minerals), organic matter, water, and air. The soil types within the project 
area are categorized into two types, alluvial and podzolic soil types. The land area with alluvial soil 
type is 51,269 Ha and with podzolic soil type is 64,732 Ha. Alluvial soil type  is usually found in 
lands that experience recent or frequent flooding, this soil type is considered young and has not 
experienced  horizon  differentiation24.  Most  of  the  properties  of  alluvial  soil  type  are  from 
transported  or  deposited  materials,  therefore  the  properties  vary  depending  on  the  parent 
materials deposited. Also, the distribution of alluvial soils is not affected by altitude or climate25. 
Because alluvial soil properties are directly influenced by the original parent material, its fertility is 
also  determined  by  the  nature  of  the  original  material  (Arabia  et  al.  2015).  As  regards  podzolic 
soils, the soil type is formed due to heavy rainfall and very low temperatures. This soil type is also 
considered old mineral soil generally with a yellowish and reddish color. The color of the podzolic 
soil indicates a relatively low soil fertility (Agricultural Research and Development) (Refer to Table 
4 and Figure 7).

2.1.17 Project Activities and Theory of Change (VCS, 3.6; CCB, G1.8) 
GLLP aims to optimize the business permits given through multiple business schemes. One of the 
business  schemes  being  developed  is  carbon  project,  where  it  will  prevent  GHG  emissions  by 
stopping  logging activities, restoring  land ecosystems,  and  improving the economic value  that  is 
currently  in  a  degraded  state  caused  by  previous  fire  and  logging  activities.  The  activities  are 
designed  to  maximize  its  capability  to  restore  the  project  area's  ecological  functions  and  build 
relationships with stakeholders within the landscape. To achieve this, the project activities to be 
implemented are:  
Climate and Environment Biodiversity 
The project activities listed aim to avoid emissions and leakage from activities and events such as 
fires, deforestation, mining and illegal activities through activities such as land management and 
education on the issues caused by illegal activities. These project activities will also aim to protect 
various endangered species while providing new habitats and protecting the habitats of the wildlife 
through monitoring activities, education and establishing captive breeding. 
Community 
The project activities listed is committed to improve the economic, quality and the well-being of the 
people  through  activities  such  as  development  of  community  forest,  utilizing  natural  medicinal 
plants, education, and NTFP alternative solutions. The project also considers sustainability goals 
by  considering  the  customary  roles,  such  as  through  strengthening  customary  roles  within  the 
community as well as traditional schools to enrich the knowledge of the younger generations in the 
sustainability of customary institutions.

2.2 Without-project Land Use Scenario and Additionality  
2.2.1 Conditions Prior to Project Initiation and Land Use Scenarios without the Project 
(VCS, 3.13; CCB, G2.1) 
Before the GLLP, the project area was designated for production forest and protected forest area. 
The production forest area (Unit I) has been logged since the mid-1990s. As a result, it is a complex 
mosaic forest area with full until very degraded state (see figure 14) The supposed land use without 
project  scenario  that  is  most  possible  for  Unit  I  area  is  forest  management  activities  for 
logging/wood production. With the project, this area will be converted from a production forest area 
to a protected area. This shows that the project will not generate GHG emissions, yet rather it will 
reduce or further eliminate emissions. In addition, according to the applicable laws and regulations, 
the Ministry of Environment and Forestry (“KLHK”) has the right to grant lease-to-use forest area 
(“PPKH”) permits for legal mining companies to conduct operational mining activities up to 10% of 
the total forest area. The PPKH issuance triggers legal mining operation activities. However, it is 
also known that the people within and surrounding the project area have also carried out illegal 
mining activities within the project area. With the GLLP project activities, a review on and further 
rejection  proposal  to  the  submission  of  PPKH  proposals  can  be  submitted  to  KLHK,  as  well  as 
prevention  measures  regarding  illegal  mining  activities  can  be  implemented,  therefore  mining 
activities in the project area can be monitored and reduced in intensity.
