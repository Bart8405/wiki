# Week 10 — Nation, Race, and Gender

*What is a nation, where does nationalism come from, and how do race and gender structure the nationalist project? The lecture combines theory (primordialist vs modernist), IR-theoretic readings, two crisis cases (Korea + Ukraine), and the structural roles of race and gender in nation-building.*

## Lecture walkthrough

### What is a nation? Three dimensions

Heywood & Whitham (Ch. 8): a nation is a complex phenomenon with three dimensions:
- **Cultural** — common language, religion, history, traditions. (All nations are culturally heterogeneous to some degree.)
- **Political** — a group that regards itself as a *natural political community*, usually expressed through the desire for sovereignty.
- **Psychological** — shared loyalty / affection (patriotism). One can recognise membership without national pride.

**Words that get conflated** — nation ≠ state ≠ country ≠ ethnic group ≠ nation-state. Be precise on the exam.

### Anderson's imagined community

> *"An imagined political community — imagined as both inherently limited and sovereign."* — Anderson (1983), p. 6

Four features:
- **Imagined** — you'll never meet most fellow members; communion lives in the mind.
- **Limited** — every nation has finite (if elastic) boundaries; beyond them lie other nations.
- **Sovereign** — the nation is the legitimate site of political authority, replacing the divine right of kings.
- **Community** — regardless of internal inequality, conceived as horizontal *comradeship*.

Combined with *objective markers* (language, religion, territory) AND *subjective markers* (loyalty, sense of common destiny, willingness to sacrifice). Switzerland holds together with three languages; Germany unifies across Catholic-Protestant lines. No single objective marker is sufficient.

### Where does nationalism come from? The big debate

**Primordialists (Herder, Smith, Connor):** nations are ancient — extensions of ethno-religious / cultural communities. Nationalism *awakens* what was there. Strong for cases with deep ethno-religious roots.

**Modernists (Gellner, Hobsbawm, Anderson):** the nation is a *late-18th / 19th-century* political construction. Nationalism *creates* nations, not the other way around. Strong for the timing puzzle — *why* does nationalism appear when it does?

**Gellner's account (the load-bearing modernist argument):**
- Pre-industrial society was vertical and segmented (peasants, clergy, nobility — each with its own culture and dialect).
- Industrial economies need a mobile workforce literate in a *single high culture*.
- The state becomes the producer and protector of that culture via mass schooling.
- Nationalism = "the political and national unit should be congruent" (Gellner 1983, p.1).
- Without industrial modernity, nationalism as we know it does not appear → why nationalism is rooted in late-18th-century Europe and spreads outward from there.

**Anderson's print-capitalism addition:**
- Vernacular printing displaced Latin → capitalist publishers needed mass audiences.
- The novel and the newspaper taught readers to imagine many strangers *simultaneously* moving through the same time.
- The nation became thinkable as a horizontal community of readers.

> *"The convergence of capitalism and print technology on the fatal diversity of human language created the possibility of a new form of imagined community."* — Anderson (1983), p. 46

### Nationalism in IR theory

Griffiths & Sullivan (1997) point: IR has trouble with nationalism because it takes the *state* as the unit of analysis — but the nation is not the same as the state.

| Lens | What it sees |
|---|---|
| **Realist** | Nationalism = fuel for state power (mobilization, taxation, territorial claims). Useful, sometimes dangerous |
| **Liberal** | Nationalism (in its civic form) underpins self-determination and democratic citizenship. Wilsonian inheritance |
| **Constructivist** | Nationalism = socially shared ideas about who "we" are. Identity is constructed and contested |
| **Marxist** | Nationalism = ideology papering over class conflict; binds workers and capitalists into shared imagined community. Anderson sat partly here |

### Civic vs. ethnic nationalism — two faces

- **Civic** — membership via political institutions. Anyone accepting the institutions can join. France (revolutionary tradition), US in self-image.
- **Ethnic** — membership via descent, language, religion. Harder to acquire / lose. 19th-century German romanticism, post-colonial cases.

*These are ideal types, not mutually exclusive.* All nationalisms mix the two; the empirical question is *which axis is doing the heavier work right now*.

**Wilsonian vs reactionary nationalism:** same political vocabulary (nation, sovereignty, the people) → opposite projects. Wilsonian ties to self-determination + popular sovereignty; reactionary ties to recovered glory + narrow membership + foreigner-as-threat.

### Two cases on solidarity in crisis — Korea + Ukraine

The lecture's pedagogical move: challenge the "ethnic = bad, civic = good" reflex.

**South Korea (1910–1945 and after) — ethnic nationalism without a state:**
- Japanese colonial rule abolished the Korean state. No institutions to anchor civic identity.
- What survived: ethno-cultural narrative — shared blood (*tanil minjok*), Korean language, hangul, dynastic memory. Spine of anti-colonial resistance.
- Shin (2006): modern South Korean nationalism is fundamentally *ethnic in origin* — a response to the absence of statehood, not its expression.
- **ISSP 2023 data:** ethnic items ("born in your country," "ancestry," "dominant religion") sit much higher in South Korea (88% / 76% / 54%) than in NL (58% / 36% / 13%) or US (50% / 33% / 35%).
- Today: collides with rising migrant stock, international marriages, multicultural children.

**Ukraine (2014–present) — civic, not ethnic:**
- Panel surveys before / during / after the 2022 invasion show *civic attachment* rising — not ethno-linguistic exclusion.
- Russian-speaking Ukrainians and front-line respondents identify with Ukraine *as a political community*.
- Kremlin's appeals to ethnic-Russian solidarity inside Ukraine have largely *failed*: where civic institutions function, they cohere the polity.
- Sources: Kulyk (2023), Onuch (2023). The "rising ethnic nationalism in Ukraine" framing is NOT what the survey evidence shows.

**Why the divergence:**

| | Korea 1910–45 | Ukraine 2014– |
|---|---|---|
| Sovereign state available? | No — abolished by Japan | Yes — contested but functioning |
| Civic infrastructure? | None of one's own | Constitution, elections, institutions |
| Inclusion of linguistic minorities? | Not at issue (largely monolingual) | Russian speakers fully included |
| Primary axis of solidarity in crisis | Ethnic-cultural (blood, language) | Civic-territorial (state, institutions) |

**Take-home:** whether self-preservation runs through ethnic or civic channels depends on the *institutional context*, not on something fixed in "the people." When civic infrastructure exists, civic does the work; when it doesn't, ethnic fills the gap.

### Race, identity, and the politics of difference

Heywood & Whitham (Ch. 9): identity is two-way — also *identification by* others (racialization, ethnic labelling). People are racialized whether they like it or not. Identity is a relation of *power*: which identity gets to count as default, which gets marked as different?

**Othering:** nations are defined as much by who is *outside* as inside. The boundary work is constitutive. Anti-immigrant politics, civilizational rhetoric, "us-versus-them" framings all do the same operation — they convert difference into hierarchy. This is why nationalism and racism so often appear together.

**Hindutva (live case):** BJP under Modi redefines Indian identity along religious lines. Muslims, Christians, minorities sit awkwardly. Citizenship-amendment laws, lynching campaigns, rhetorical exclusion all push the same way. The ethnic-civic line can shift quickly inside one state.

### Gender and nationalism — Nagel's argument

Joane Nagel (1998), *Masculinity and Nationalism*:

> *"Nationalism is a project of nation-building, but also a project of gender-building."* — after Nagel (1998), p.113

- The classics of nation/state-building (Lipset, Marshall, Deutsch, Moore) are stories about men. Literature is structured around *political man*.
- Nagel: this is *by design*, not oversight. The nation itself is an ideologically masculine project — organised around honor, sacrifice, defense, conquest.
- Bringing women back in is necessary but *not* sufficient. The harder question: what has the masculinist framing been doing all along?

**Three gendered roles in nationalism:**
1. **Masculinist heroism** — men do war and rule (Delacroix's *Liberty Leading the People* — Marianne leads a column of *armed men*).
2. **Patriotic motherhood** — women carry, raise, embody the nation. Reproductive duty (biological + cultural). Bodies become national property. Why fertility, marriage, immigration laws appear in nationalist programs.
3. **Sexualized militarism** — adversary sexualized: enemy men as predatory + emasculated, enemy women as licentious + dishonorable. Defends the masculine + heterosexual character of military and political institutions. Gender nonconformity becomes coded as *national betrayal*.

Three puzzles this helps explain: why men defend masculine institutional preserves so fiercely; why men go to war; why men and women hold systematically different priorities for "the nation."

**Where the threads tie together (immigration politics):** contemporary nationalism frames immigrant men as threats to "our" women. The 2015–16 European refugee debate ran on this template; Trumpism on the US southern border runs on it now. Women get put in dual positions — victims needing national protection AND reproducers whose own choices must be policed.

### Nationalism today — resurgence

Brexit (2016), first Trump term, AfD in Germany, Le Pen in France, Orbán in Hungary, Meloni in Italy, Modi's Hindutva, second Trump administration: a wave of explicitly nationalist politics across very different polities.

**Shared features:** anti-globalist economic rhetoric, exclusionary identity claims, recovered-glory historical narrative, hostility to international institutions.

**Real differences:** religious nationalism (India) · economic nationalism (Trump) · civilizational nationalism (Orbán, Putin) · revanchist nationalism (Russia).

**Bridge to Week 11:** the first wave of post-Cold War IR theory assumed globalization would erode the nation-state. Half right (flows intensified, institutions expanded), half wrong (the nation reasserted itself, often *because of* those changes). Nationalism in 2026 is a 21st-century *response* to the post-1989 order — not a 19th-century holdover.

## Key thinker — Benedict Anderson (1936–2015)

Anglo-Irish political scientist and historian (Cornell), specialist in Southeast Asia (especially Indonesia, Thailand, Philippines). His *Imagined Communities* (1983) is the canonical modernist account of nationalism.

His core move: the nation is *imagined* (you'll never meet most members) but *consequential* (people die for it). The mechanism that made nations thinkable: *print capitalism* — vernacular novels and newspapers that taught millions of strangers to imagine themselves moving simultaneously through the same time. The nation is a creature of capitalism + technology + the collapse of Latin's universal authority.

Anderson is a *modernist* (against primordialism) and partly *Marxist* (nationalism as ideology that binds class divisions), though he resists reducing nationalism to ideology — he takes its emotional pull seriously.

*(Joane Nagel: American sociologist (University of Kansas), specialist on race, ethnicity, gender, and nationalism. Her 1998 article is the canonical statement of nationalism as a masculinist project — building on but pushing past feminist IR's earlier "where are the women?" question.)*

*(Other modernists: Ernest Gellner — nationalism as industrial-society requirement; Eric Hobsbawm — invented traditions. Primordialists: Walker Connor, Anthony D. Smith — ethno-symbolism.)*

---

> **Explained easily — imagined communities:**
> You have probably never met most Dutch people — yet you feel something when the Dutch football team plays, you'd rather not see Dutch soldiers die abroad, you'd pay taxes for "your" public services. That feeling is the *imagined community*. Anderson's point: this didn't exist for ordinary people in 1500 (they identified with their village, their lord, their church). What changed: cheap printing in vernacular languages let millions of strangers read the same news on the same morning and imagine themselves as one collective. The nation is a *side effect of capitalism + technology + a particular printing infrastructure*. Not natural, but real.

> **Explained easily — civic vs ethnic nationalism:**
> Civic nationalism is *like a club*: you join by signing up to the rules. Anyone who respects the institutions, speaks the language (sort of), and pays in is a member — your blood doesn't matter. Ethnic nationalism is *like a family*: you're either born into it or you're not. You can live somewhere your whole life and still be "the foreigner." Most real countries mix both, but in different proportions — Ukraine is mostly civic, Korea is mostly ethnic, the US tries to be civic but has strong ethnic undercurrents.

> **Explained easily — the Korea / Ukraine paradox:**
> The reflex is "ethnic nationalism = bad, civic nationalism = good." But Korea shows ethnic nationalism can do *self-preservation* work when there's no state to be civic *about* (1910–45: Japan abolished Korea, so all that survived was blood + language + memory). Ukraine shows the reverse — even under existential threat, when there ARE working civic institutions, *civic* identity rises (Russian-speaking Ukrainians identify with the *state*, not with ethnicity). The lesson: which form nationalism takes depends on the *institutional context*, not on something deep in "the people."

> **Explained easily — Nagel's masculinist nationalism:**
> Notice how war movies, national anthems, statues, founding myths all run on similar imagery — brave male soldiers, strong fathers of the nation, women either as mothers giving birth to citizens or as the *honor* being defended. Nagel's point: this isn't an accident or a fixable PR problem. The nation as a *concept* was built with men as the actors and women as the symbolic background. So when nationalism today talks about "protecting our women" from migrants, it's not deviation — it's the *original logic* surfacing again.

**Q1.** Anderson's *imagined community* (1983) refers to the nation as:
- A) A fictional construct that does not really exist
- B) An imagined political community whose members will never meet most fellow members, made thinkable by print capitalism
- C) A community held together purely by shared blood and ancestry
- D) An ancient community awakened by 19th-century nationalist movements

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B) An imagined political community whose members will never meet most fellow members, made thinkable by print capitalism**

Anderson explicitly says "imagined" does NOT mean "fake" — it means the communion lives in the mind because direct knowledge is impossible at scale. Print capitalism (newspapers, novels in vernacular) is the mechanism that made this imagining possible. Option D is the primordialist position Anderson rejects.

### Short Essay / Open Questions

**Essay Q1.** Compare the primordialist and modernist accounts of nationalism. Which better explains contemporary nationalism (e.g. Hindutva, Ukrainian civic nationalism, Korean ethnic nationalism)?

<details>
<summary>Model answer outline</summary>

**Key points:**
- **Primordialists** (Herder, Smith, Connor): nations are ancient ethnic / cultural communities. Nationalism *awakens* what was already there. Inherited bonds of kinship, language, faith. Strong for cases with deep ethno-religious roots.
- **Modernists** (Gellner, Hobsbawm, Anderson): nations are late-18th / 19th-century political constructions. Nationalism *creates* nations. Gellner: industrial society requires cultural homogeneity, produced via mass schooling. Anderson: print capitalism + simultaneity. Strong for the *timing* puzzle.
- **Application:**
  - **Hindutva:** mixes both. Primordialist *claim* (Hindu civilization as ancient) but modernist *mechanism* (mass communication, party organisation, schooling under BJP).
  - **Ukrainian civic nationalism (2014–):** strongly modernist — built on contemporary political institutions; mobilises Russian-speakers as Ukrainians via civic identification.
  - **Korean ethnic nationalism:** primordialist *narrative* (tanil minjok, dynastic memory) but modernist *function* (Shin 2006: it emerged precisely because the modern Korean state had been destroyed and had to be reimagined).
- **Verdict:** modernism wins on the timing question (nationalism is a 19th-century phenomenon globally) but primordialist *content* is real — modern nationalisms construct themselves by claiming primordial roots. Best framework: modernist about mechanism, ethno-symbolist about content (Anthony D. Smith).

**Common mistake:** Treating these as either/or. The most useful answer recognises that they explain different things (timing vs. emotional content).

</details>
