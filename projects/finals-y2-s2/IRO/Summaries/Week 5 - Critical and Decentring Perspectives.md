## 1. What the Study Guide tells you to know — with definitions

Most of the relevant glossary terms sit under "Critical and non-Western IR" — virtually all are testable for Week 5. Definitions are drawn from the Heywood chapter, Seth, the Wolff lecture, or — where the readings don't define a term — from standard reference sources.

| Term | Definition | Source |
|---|---|---|
| **Problem-solving theory** | Theory that takes the world as it is and asks how to make existing institutions and power relations work better. Implicitly conservative; serves policymakers within the existing system. | Cox 1981 (in Heywood) |
| **Critical theory** | Theory that asks how the existing order came into being, whose interests it serves, and how it might be changed. Emancipatory, post-positivist, transformative. *"Theory is always for someone and for some purpose."* | Cox 1981 (in Heywood) |
| **World-systems theory** | Wallerstein's neo-Marxist account of the world economy as one interlocking capitalist system, divided into **core** (developed North — high wages, advanced manufacturing), **periphery** (less-developed South — raw materials, subsistence wages, weak states), and **semi-periphery** (subordinate to core, extractive over periphery). Structural inequality is built in. | Heywood Ch. 4 |
| **Core–periphery** | The structural inequality at the heart of world-systems theory: core countries accumulate wealth and technology by **exploiting** peripheral countries (raw materials, cheap labour). The semi-periphery acts as a buffer. Also used by dependency theorists more broadly. | Heywood Ch. 4 |
| **Gramscian hegemony** | Domination achieved not just through coercion (force) but through **consent**: the dominant class's ideas become the **"common sense" of the age**, displacing rivals and seeming natural. Hegemony operates through institutions, civil society, and the state — not only the army or the police. | Gramsci, *Prison Notebooks*; Heywood Ch. 4 |
| **Subaltern** | Those **excluded from hegemonic structures** and dominant-class discourse — the marginalised, voiceless, dependent groups whose perspectives don't make it into mainstream knowledge. Gramsci's term, picked up by postcolonial theory (Gayatri Spivak's "Can the Subaltern Speak?"). | Gramsci / Lecture |
| **Discourse / power-knowledge** | Foucault's claim that **knowledge is power**: what counts as "truth" is produced by discourses (organised systems of statements, categories, practices) that simultaneously enable and constrain what can be said and done. There is no neutral knowledge separate from power relations. | Foucault, via Heywood + lecture |
| **Biopolitics** | Foucault's term for how **the modern state regulates life itself** — populations, health, sexuality, behaviour — through "humane" practices of **discipline and surveillance** (prisons, asylums, schools, factories, workhouses, hospitals). Power becomes productive, not just repressive. | Foucault; lecture |
| **Orientalism** | Edward Said's term for the West's systematic **construction of "the East"** as a coherent, exotic, irrational, inferior Other. This knowledge-production justified imperial domination and still shapes how Western media, scholarship, and politics depict the Middle East, North Africa, and Asia. | Said 1978 (referenced in lecture / Seth) |
| **Postcolonialism** | A critical lens that highlights **colonial legacies and racial hierarchies** in contemporary IR. Argues mainstream IR is Eurocentric and treats the international order as hierarchical, not anarchic. The "post" doesn't mean "after colonialism" — it means the colonial encounter **constituted** the modern world. | Lecture; Seth |
| **Decolonizing IR** | A scholarly project to **dismantle the Eurocentric foundations** of IR — its concepts (sovereignty, anarchy, balance of power), its canon (Thucydides → Hobbes → Kant), its archives (European diplomatic history) — and rebuild the discipline from a wider base of voices, histories, and experiences. | General usage; links to Acharya Week 4 |
| **Feminist IR** | A family of approaches challenging IR's **gender-blindness**: empirical feminism (women have been excluded from analysis), analytical feminism (mainstream concepts like "power politics" are built on masculinist assumptions of rivalry/competition), and normative feminism (changing gender hierarchies in world politics). Includes liberal, Marxist, postcolonial, and eco-feminist strands. | Heywood; lecture |
| **Intersectionality** | Kimberlé Crenshaw's 1991 framework: multiple axes of identity (**gender × race × class × sexuality × ability × culture × geography × religion × age**) intersect to shape oppression and experience. A Black working-class woman doesn't experience "racism + sexism + classism" as three separable layers — the intersection itself is distinct. | Crenshaw 1991; lecture |
| **Standpoint theory** | The idea that **knowledge is situated** — that the perspective of marginalised people offers **epistemic privilege** because they understand both the dominant culture (they have to, in order to survive in it) and their own. J. Ann Tickner is the IR exemplar of standpoint feminism. | Heywood; lecture |
| **Decentring** | Fisher-Onar & Nicolaïdis's three-part framework for EU foreign policy: **provincialise** (recognise Europe is not the universal centre), **engage** (listen to and learn from non-European perspectives), **reconstruct** ("a renewal of EU external action based on humility, empowerment, and mutuality"). | Fisher-Onar & Nicolaïdis 2013; lecture |
| **Non-Western IR / Global IR** | The Acharya-led project (Week 4) of broadening IR's foundations to include non-Western histories and concepts. **Global IR** is the constructive version — not replacing Western IRT but enriching it. Week 5 treats this as a **sister critique** to postcolonial theory. | Acharya 2014; Wolff lecture connects to this |
| **Post-positivism** | The meta-theoretical position that **rejects positivism's claim** to objective, neutral, value-free knowledge. Post-positivists (constructivists, critical theorists, postcolonialists, feminists, poststructuralists) argue knowledge is situated, shaped by power, and partly constitutes the world it describes. | Heywood (foundational meta-theory) |
| **Frankfurt School** | The Marxist-influenced Institute of Social Research (Frankfurt 1923 → US in 1930s → back to Frankfurt 1950s → dissolved 1969). First generation: **Adorno, Horkheimer, Marcuse**. Second generation: **Habermas**. Applied to IR by **Cox** and **Linklater**. Core theme: extend critique to all social practices, link substantive research to philosophy. | Heywood Ch. 4 |
| **Theoretical reflexivity** | An awareness of the impact of the **values and presuppositions** that a theorist brings to analysis, plus understanding of the historical dynamics that helped form them. The basic move critical theory demands of all theorists. | Heywood Ch. 4 |
| **Deconstruction** | Derrida's method — close reading of texts to expose their **blindspots, internal contradictions, and the binary oppositions they rely on**. The classic poststructuralist technique for showing that any text means something other (and more) than it appears. | Heywood Ch. 4 |
| **Provincialise Europe** | Dipesh Chakrabarty's phrase (used by Seth) — strip Europe of its assumed universal status, treat European history as **one regional history** among many rather than as the template for all human history. | Seth 2011 |
| **Ubiquity ≠ universality** | Seth's killer line: just because a practice (international law, the nation-state, the rational individual) is **globally widespread** does not make it **universal**. Widespread adoption tells you nothing about origins or the circumstances of its imposition. | Seth 2011 |

Likely short-answer prompt: *"How do critical theories challenge mainstream IR? Use an example."* Or: *"What does postcolonial theory bring to the study of IR that mainstream theories miss?"*

> **Explained easily — the whole week in one paragraph:**
> Mainstream IR (realism, liberalism, even much of constructivism) pretends to be *neutral and scientific*. Critical theorists say: no theory is neutral — **theory is always for someone and for some purpose** (Cox). Mainstream IR was built by Western men, for Western states, drawing on European history, asking the questions Western policymakers cared about. The critical theories (Marxism, Frankfurt School, feminism, poststructuralism, postcolonialism) all attack this from different angles. **Seth's job** in the required reading is to do the postcolonial version of this attack: he shows that IR sanitises the history of colonialism, falsely claims its concepts (sovereignty, the nation-state, international law) are universal when they are European exports, and **naturalises** (treats as obvious) things that are actually historically produced. The whole week is about that single meta-move: *the discipline is part of the power it claims to merely describe.*

## 2. The lecture — Wolff's framing (LEC5)

### 2.1 The three driving questions

1. How do critical theories challenge dominant paradigms (realism, liberalism)?
2. In what ways does postcolonial theory highlight the limits of traditional IR's claims to universality?
3. What role does power play in the construction and dissemination of IR knowledge?

### 2.2 Recap of traditional IR (so you can attack it)

| Theory | One-line core | Key thinkers |
|---|---|---|
| **Classical realism** | Power politics flows from human selfishness/egoism | Thucydides, Machiavelli, Hobbes, Morgenthau (1948 *Politics Among Nations*) |
| **Neorealism (structural)** | Power politics is forced on states by anarchic structure of the system | Waltz (defensive); Mearsheimer (offensive) |
| **Neoclassical realism** | Foreign policy = international structure × domestic factors | Rose 1998, Zakaria 1998 |
| **Liberalism** | Anarchy can be overcome through cooperation, institutions, interdependence, democratic peace, soft power | Kant 1795 *Perpetual Peace*; complex interdependence (Keohane/Nye) |
| **Constructivism** | Ideas, norms, identities matter; interests are socially constructed; "anarchy is what states make of it" | Wendt 1992 |

**Realism vs. Liberalism contrast table (often a test point):**

| | Realism | Liberalism |
|---|---|---|
| Human nature | Pessimistic | Optimistic |
| Main actor | State-centric | State + non-state |
| State internals | "Black boxes" | Regime type shapes behaviour |
| Anarchy | Determines conflict (zero-sum) | Overcomeable through cooperation (positive-sum) |
| Institutions | Vehicles for great power interests | Important actors that shape behaviour |

### 2.3 What are critical theories?

- An **old tradition** — Plato, Socrates, Aristotle used "dialectics" to propose and criticise theories.
- **Purpose:** challenge the status quo / current global order. For **feminists**, gender is the blind spot; for **postcolonialists**, colonial legacies and racial hierarchies are ignored.
- **Positionality:** *not* positivist. They are **normative**: they reflect interests, expose injustice, and enable change.
- Targets: racism, white supremacy, sexism, patriarchy, capitalism, class exploitation.

### 2.4 Critical theories Wolff walks through

#### Post-colonialism
- Importance given to inequalities and power disparities.
- Brings race/ethnicity, gender and class into IR.
- Highlights **Eurocentrism** of IR.
- Traditionalists see the world as **anarchic** — postcolonialists see it as a **hierarchy**.

#### Feminist & Gender approaches
- Challenge the **absence of women** from decision-making and from IR theory's male-dominated focus.
- Gender as a **socially constructed** identity. Masculinity/femininity based on constructed assumptions.
- IR is "**gender-blind**" — even theorists who like Bourdieu have ignored his feminist critique.
- Four strands:
  - **Liberal feminism** (John Stuart Mill) — institutional inequalities, legal obstacles.
  - **Marxist feminism** (Margaret Benston) — capitalist production serves patriarchy.
  - **Post-colonial feminism** (bell hooks, *Ain't I a Woman?* 1981) — inequalities between Western and non-Western women; cultural, racial, ethnic context.
  - **Eco-feminism** (Françoise d'Eaubonne, *Le Féminisme ou la Mort* 1974) — relationship to ecology.
- **Intersectionality** (Crenshaw 1991, critical legal race scholar): gender, race, class, sexuality, ability, culture, geography, religion, appearance, language, age — all intersect to shape position.
- Hutchings & Owens stat: across 22 canonical IR works, only 1.57% of references to historical figures are to women.

#### Marxism and Neo-Marxism
- World order is driven by powerful **socio-economic forces**; global **capitalism** is the main structure.
- IR reflects the mode of capitalist production and the power of the capitalist class.
- **Core–periphery** inequalities (Wallerstein's world-systems theory).

#### Gramsci and the Frankfurt School
- A **less economistic, more humanistic** critique of capitalism — social and cultural dimensions, alienation.
- **Power** is not only coercion — it also operates through **consent** and **ideological influence** (cannot be reduced to economic/military might).
- **Hegemony** = ideological and cultural leadership maintained through institutions, civil society, the state — **not just coercion**.
- **Subalterns** are those excluded from hegemonic structures and dominant-class discourse.

#### Poststructuralism — Power in Knowledge
- **Foucault's biopolitics** — French society reconfigured punishment through new "humane" practices of **discipline and surveillance** in prisons, asylums, schools, workhouses, factories.
- **Mouffe & Laclau** — discourse and knowledge as power. **Articulation (hegemony):** "any practice establishing a relation among elements such that their identity is modified… The structured totality resulting from the articulatory practice we will call discourse" (Hegemony and Socialist Strategy, p. 105).
- Hegemony / articulation → elements/moments → discourse.

#### Post-modern / post-human / ultrasocial futures
Open question raised in lecture — *what is post-modernism?, what is post-humanism?, is IR moving toward "ultrasociality"?* (M. Cross 2024).

### 2.5 The big payoff table — **Critical vs. Mainstream** (memorise)

| Dimension | Mainstream IR | Critical IR |
|---|---|---|
| **Epistemology** | Positivist: theories can be objective, neutral, value-free; seeks causal explanation and generalisable laws | Post-positivist: knowledge is situated, shaped by context, power, identity; emphasises **reflexivity** and uncovering bias |
| **Purpose of theory** | **Problem-solving**: explain/predict state behaviour; tools for policymakers within the existing system | **Emancipatory and transformative**: question and seek to change the global order; foreground justice and alternative futures |
| **View of power** | Primarily material (military, economic); sometimes soft power; power = ability to get others to do things | Multi-dimensional: **structural** (shaping the system), **ideological** (dominant ideas), **discursive** (defining truth and legitimacy) |
| **Actors** | State-centric; non-state and transnational often marginal | Broad: non-state actors, transnational movements, marginalised groups, social forces |
| **Geographical/intellectual focus** | Western-centric; Western experiences treated as universal | Critiques Western centrism; highlights colonial histories, Global South, situated knowledge |
| **Normative orientation** | Order and stability over justice | Global justice, inequality, emancipation alongside (or over) order |
| **Relation to status quo** | Works within and implicitly reproduces existing structures | Questions and challenges existing hierarchies |
| **Understanding of knowledge production** | Knowledge separate from politics; observer can be detached | Knowledge and power are intertwined; scholars are part of the structures they study |

This table is the **single most exam-likely thing** in the entire week. Memorise it.

### 2.6 The "Decentring Turn" in EU Foreign Policy

Fisher-Onar & Nicolaïdis (2013) — three principles. They map back to Acharya's deprovincialization in Week 4.

- **Provincialisation** — recognise Europe's particularity, drop universal claims.
- **Engagement** — listen to and learn from non-European perspectives.
- **Reconstruction** — *"a renewal of EU external action based on humility, empowerment and mutuality"* (Fisher-Onar & Nicolaïdis 2013). Most challenging of the three.

Question for the EU: *how can the EU remain a relevant actor in a multiplex world, under intense contestation?* (Wolff et al. 2022)

### 2.7 Two case studies — applying critical theory

#### Iran ("Epic Fury" — the recent US-Israeli strike scenario)
- **Postcolonialism:** frame from legacy of imperialism. 1953 coup against Mossadegh (US/UK); Shah's authoritarianism backed by the West; 1979 revolution as anti-imperial; US-Israeli strike and regime change as imperial move.
- **Feminist IR:** women's bodies regulated by the state to produce political order. Role of women in protests — September 2022 "Woman, Life, Freedom" slogan after Jina Mahsa Amini's death. Emphasise Kurdish women / working-class women at the centre. Analyse gendered impact of sanctions.
- **Marxist IR:** class struggles domestically; energy/oil politics as motivation for Trump; economic interests of US/West/region; link between Revolutionary Guards and oil companies.
- **Post-structuralist view:** what is the **dominant narrative** on "Epic Fury"? How does discourse shape perceptions? Who is the audience (domestic US, global)?

#### War in Ukraine
- **Postcolonialism:** Russia treats Ukraine as a colony; extracts minerals/raw materials; reunification of Slavic countries under the Russian empire.
- **Feminist IR:** (1) masculine armies create economic/social destabilisation and trauma; (2) occupied territories see more patriarchy and fewer rights; (3) vulnerabilities — sanitation, health, access to safety.
- **Marxist IR:** resources explain the invasion (gas, oil, grains); US-Ukraine agreement for 50% of natural minerals; core-periphery dynamics; Ukraine's refusal to sign customs union with Russia.
- **Post-structuralist view:** European elites frame this as "war against Europe"; labels matter — "Russia's war on Ukraine" not "Ukraine-Russia war."

> Wolff walks through Iran and Ukraine using **four different critical lenses** (postcolonial, feminist, Marxist, poststructuralist) to show you *how a critical theorist actually does analysis*. The same event looks completely different depending on the lens. ## 3. Required reading 1 — Heywood & Whitham, Chapter 4 "Critical Theories of Global Politics"

*The textbook chapter, presented through the 2nd-edition material in your folder — substantively identical to the 3rd-ed Ch. 4 on critical theories.*

### 3.1 Marxism, neo-Marxism and critical theory

Marxism is the principal critical/radical alternative to realist and liberal thinking, though its impact on **academic** IR theorising has always been limited. It has two strands:

1. **Economic strand** — exposes capitalism as a system of class oppression at national and international levels. Includes classical Marxism + most neo-Marxism.
2. **Cultural/ideological strand** — emphasises the ideological and cultural dimension of oppression; post-positivist, post-Marxist. Includes **critical theory** (Gramsci + Frankfurt School).

### 3.2 Classical Marxism to neo-Marxism

- Core of Marxism: a **philosophy of history** — capitalism is doomed; socialism then communism will replace it. Based on the **materialist conception of history** (economic factors are ultimately determining).
- History driven by **dialectics**: internal contradictions in each "mode of production" → class conflict → social revolution → new higher mode of production.
- Historical stages: slavery → feudalism → capitalism → classless communist society.
- Marx as early "hyperglobalist" — capitalism would "tear down every barrier to intercourse" and "conquer the whole earth for its market."
- **Lenin** (*Imperialism: The Highest Stage of Capitalism*, 1916) — imperialism is economic, driven by capitalism's quest to maintain profit through export of surplus capital. WWI = an **imperialist war** fought over colonies.

### 3.3 Neo-Marxism — revived in the 1970s

- **Dependency theory** — post-1945 imperialism gave way to **neo-colonialism** or "economic/dollar imperialism." Highlights structural imbalances that impose dependency and underdevelopment on poorer states/regions.
- **World-systems theory** (Wallerstein) — the world economy is one interlocking capitalist system:
  - **Core** — developed North; concentration of capital, high wages, high-skilled manufacturing; benefits from tech innovation and sustained investment.
  - **Periphery** — less-developed South; exploited via dependency on raw materials export, subsistence wages, weak state protection.
  - **Semi-periphery** — economically subordinate to the core, but extracts from the periphery; buffer between the two.
- These ideas influenced the **anti-globalisation/anti-capitalist movement** ("Battle of Seattle" 1999).

### 3.4 Critical theory (Frankfurt School)

- The Institute of Social Research, Frankfurt, founded 1923; relocated to US in 1930s; re-established in Frankfurt in early 1950s; dissolved 1969.
- **Defining theme:** extend the notion of critique to all social practices by linking substantive social research to philosophy.
- First generation: Adorno, Horkheimer, Marcuse. Second generation: Habermas.
- Applied to IR by **Robert Cox** (1981, 1987) and **Andrew Linklater** (1990, 1998) in three ways:
  1. Underlines the **linkage between knowledge and politics**: all theory is embedded in values and interests, all theorising is normative → call for greater **theoretical reflexivity**.
  2. Explicit commitment to **emancipatory politics**: uncover structures of oppression to advance individual or collective freedom.
  3. Questions the conventional **state = political community** assumption: opens space for more inclusive, even cosmopolitan, notions of political identity.

### 3.5 The Gramscian influence — hegemony

- **Antonio Gramsci** (1891–1937, imprisoned 1926, *Prison Notebooks* 1929–35).
- Argued the capitalist class system is upheld not just by unequal economic/political power but by the **"hegemony" of bourgeois ideas and theories**.
- **Hegemony** here = ideological dominance: bourgeois ideas displace rivals and become the **"common sense" of the age**.
- Bourgeois hegemony can only be challenged at the political/intellectual level through **"counter-hegemonic" struggle** carried out in the interests of the proletariat on the basis of socialist principles.
- Modern **neo-Gramscians** apply this: hegemony operates through a **mixture of coercion and consent**, interplaying economic, political, military and ideological forces.
- **Robert Cox** analysed US hegemonic power not just as military ascendancy but as **ability to generate broad consent** for the world order it represents.

### 3.6 The Cox distinction (you must know this)

> *"Theory is always for someone and for some purpose."* — Cox 1981

- **Problem-solving theory** — takes the world as it is and addresses how to make existing institutions work better. Implicitly conservative; serves the powerful.
- **Critical theory** — asks how the existing order came into being, whose interests it serves, and how it might be changed. Emancipatory.

This is the meta-distinction the entire Critical vs. Mainstream table rests on.

> **Explained easily — Cox in one example:**
> Imagine the UN Security Council is dysfunctional. A **problem-solving** theorist asks: "How can we reform the veto so the Council works better?" That accepts the Council as legitimate. A **critical** theorist asks: "Why does this body exist with these five permanent members in the first place? Whose interests does it serve? What alternative orders might be possible?" Cox's point is that mainstream IR almost always does the first kind of work — and pretends that's the only "serious" kind. Critical theorists insist the second kind is just as legitimate and politically necessary.

### 3.7 Constructivism (positioned as a bridge to critical theories)

- "Anarchy is what states make of it" (Wendt 1992).
- No objective social/political reality independent of our understanding of it. The social world is "inter-subjective" — not "out there" but "inside" awareness.
- **Beliefs, norms and values** are at least as important as material structures in shaping world affairs.
- Anarchy can be Hobbesian, Lockean, or Kantian — different "cultures of anarchy" (Wendt). An "anarchy of friends" is not the same as an "anarchy of enemies."
- Limitation: ideas do not "fall from the sky." They are products of complex social realities — and constructivism can underplay the material world.

### 3.8 Poststructuralism

- Emerged alongside postmodernism; sometimes used interchangeably.
- All ideas/concepts are expressed in language, which is enmeshed in complex relations of power.
- **Foucault**: power and systems of thought are linked through **discourse / "discourses of power"** — knowledge is power.
- No universal frame of reference — only **competing perspectives**, each a particular discourse of power.
- Derrida (*Of Grammatology* 1967): *"There is nothing outside the text."*
- Method: **deconstruction** — close reading to expose blindspots and contradictions.
- Critics charge poststructuralism with **relativism**: if all modes of knowing are equally valid, even science cannot distinguish truth from falsehood.

### 3.9 Feminism in IR — three strands

- **Empirical feminists** — challenge the "sexist" exclusion of women from conventional IR analysis. The roles of women (diplomats' wives, domestic workers, sex workers) are ignored; so are the global processes through which women are subordinated and exploited.
- **Analytical feminists** (e.g. **J. Ann Tickner**, exponent of **standpoint feminism**) — expose the **gender biases** that pervade IR's key theories and concepts. The "power politics" paradigm is built on **"masculinist" assumptions** (rivalry, competition, inevitable conflict). Champion alternative concepts (power as **collaboration**, not just conflict).
- **Standpoint feminism** — there is a distinct female perspective from which the world appears different; this perspective should be central to theorising.

> **Explained easily — what Heywood Ch. 4 is doing:**
> The chapter walks you through the family tree of critical theories — Marxism → neo-Marxism (world-systems) → critical theory proper (Frankfurt School + Gramsci) → constructivism → poststructuralism → feminism. Each is presented as a **layer of critique** of mainstream realism/liberalism. The key thing to take from this chapter is **Cox's distinction** between problem-solving theory (works inside the existing system) and critical theory (questions the system itself). That distinction structures the whole exam-relevant Mainstream vs. Critical table in §2.5 — it's the single most testable thing here.

## 4. Required reading 2 — Seth, "Postcolonial Theory and the Critique of International Relations" (2011)

*Millennium 40(1), pp. 167–183. The most cited piece for this week. A **three-part** postcolonial critique of mainstream IR.*

### 4.1 The aim

> *"What is distinctive about the critique offered here is that it seeks to systematically 'provincialise Europe' — in a threefold sense: it challenges the centrality accorded to Europe as the historical source and origin of the international order; it queries the universality accorded to moral and legal perspectives which reflect and reproduce the power relations characteristic of the colonial encounter…; and it questions the epistemological privilege accorded to an understanding of knowledge which is blind to the constitutive, and not merely representational, role of knowledge."*

Seth does **not** offer a better way of "doing" IR — he offers a **critique** of the discipline.

> **Explained easily — what Seth is doing:**
> Seth attacks mainstream IR in **three moves**: (1) **History is wrong** — IR's story of the "expansion of international society" sanitises slavery, conquest, and genocide, treating colonialism as a footnote when it's actually central. (2) **Universality is fake** — IR says its rules (sovereignty, international law) are universal because everyone uses them, but Seth's line is *"ubiquity is not universality"* — mini-skirts and burqas are also globally widespread; that doesn't make them universal features of womanhood. (3) **Knowledge constitutes reality** — IR doesn't just *describe* the international order, it *helps produce* it by treating the nation-state as natural when it's actually a recent and contingent historical creation. Bottom line: IR theory *naturalises what it should problematise.*

### 4.2 Part I — History: the "expansion of international society" narrative is wrong

#### The default IR view of history
- Realists/neorealists (Waltz): history is unnecessary because "the enduring anarchic character of international politics accounts for the striking sameness in the quality of international life through the millennia." History is reduced to an "ahistorical repetition" — even Thucydides, Hobbes, Kant get read as articulating eternal truths about an unchanging tragic reality.
- The **English School** (Bull, Watson, Buzan & Little) does engage with history — but offers a sanitised version of "expansion."

#### Watson's *Evolution of International Society*
- European international society emerged around the early 16th century, formalised at Westphalia.
- It "spread beyond its original home, in an uncoordinated but orderly fashion" — even mentioning "the first orderly partition of the transatlantic world between Spain and Portugal" as **orderly**.
- Europeans wanted to "Europeanize and modernize the non-European world, to bring 'progress' to it."
- Non-Europeans "found it difficult to resist what the Europeans had to offer" and sought to join the European society of states.
- Eventually Europe + US relented and admitted all states as equals; decolonisation completed this.

Seth's response: this elides the **bloody conquest of the Americas, the transatlantic slave trade, the expropriation and sometimes genocide of indigenous peoples, wars of conquest, land grabs, exploitation and oppression**. The struggles of decolonisation also disappear — there is only "one powerful actor, the white man, who eventually comes to see that the very principles of his club mandate inclusion rather than exclusion."

#### Buzan & Little
- Slightly more sophisticated, but still: "the European empires can be seen as the nursery or mechanism by which the political form of the modern state was transposed onto the rest of the world." Eurocentrism is **warranted** for the modern period because Europe did dominate.

#### Bull & Watson's killer line (which Seth attacks)
> *"It is not our perspective but the historical record itself that can be called Eurocentric."*

#### Seth's structural critique — borrowing from economic history
- The conventional story of capitalism's spread — first Europe, then the rest — has been challenged. **Andre Gunder Frank**: Europe did *not* pull itself up by bootstraps; it "used its American money to muscle in on and benefit from Asian production." Hobson, Goody, Blaut, Bhambra all argue similar.
- The "great divergence" (Pomeranz) between West and Rest happened much later than usually thought, and due to historical contingencies rather than European uniqueness.
- **Edward Keene**: at the same time the "Westphalian system" of equally sovereign states was taking shape, *quite different* colonial and imperial systems were being established beyond Europe — based not on equality and sovereignty but on **divided sovereignty for the purposes of commerce and "good government."**
- The events privileged by IR (peace of Augsburg, Westphalia) **coincide** with: subjugation of the Americas, rise of the slave trade, founding of British/Dutch East India Companies, Macartney's mission to China, 19th-century scramble for Africa, mandates, paramountcy, concessions, franchises, spheres of influence, protectorates. Could all this really not have shaped the international order?

#### The feminist / South African analogies
- Feminist scholars: denying rights to women was not just "exclusion" later "remedied" — it **shaped** modern political thought and modern polities.
- Apartheid white suburbs: their existence was tied to and shaped by the shanty towns whose inhabitants they barely acknowledged.
- Likewise: the international order was **shaped by** the interactions between Europe and those it colonised.

#### What "post" in postcolonialism means
- **Not** a periodisation. It does not mean "after colonialism."
- It signifies that conquest, colonialism and empire are **not a footnote or episode** in a larger story (capitalism, modernity, expansion of international society) — they are a **central, constitutive part** of that story.
- Stuart Hall: "Since the Sixteenth Century, these different temporalities and histories have been irrevocably and violently yoked together… No site, either 'here' or 'there', in its fantasied autonomy… could develop without taking into account its significant and/or abjected others."

### 4.3 Part II — Culture and Theory: the universality claim is false

Seth turns to **Robert Jackson's** *The Global Covenant* — sensitive to cultural diversity but still flawed.

- Jackson: the post-19th-century international order is "horizontal rather than hierarchical, inclusive rather than exclusive, based on pluralist ethics… the first bona fide normative discourse that communicates with and accommodates all the world's cultures and civilizations."
- The problem: how do diverse peoples interact? Jackson's answer = **equal state sovereignty, self-determination, non-intervention**. These are **procedural** rules — mere form, no content — so they can be accepted by all without sharing the same culture.
- This is the same move liberal political theory made in the 17th–18th centuries to handle religious/property diversity inside states: separate **public/procedural** from **private/substantive**. Marx's *On the Jewish Question* dissected this.

#### Seth's critique
- The "procedural" is **never neutral**. Critics of liberalism showed it presupposed Christian values over others, men over women. **Rawls** kept stripping his liberalism down (`Justice as Fairness: Political not Metaphysical', 1985) to avoid privileging even individualism/autonomy — but you can never reach pure neutrality. *"All presumptions, including (perhaps especially) ones about what it means to be human, to be rational and desiring, are historically and culturally produced."*
- Jackson concedes international law is "European in origin… now universal." But:
  - **Antony Anghie**: "Over the centuries, international law developed a sophisticated series of technologies, doctrines, and disciplines that borrowed in important ways from the broader justifications of colonialism to address the problem of the governance of non-European peoples."
  - **James Gathii**: this legacy continues to affect contemporary international law.
- Crucial Seth line: **"Ubiquity is not the same as universality."** That a practice is widespread tells us nothing about its origins or the circumstances of its adoption. Mini-skirts and burqas are widespread — that does not make them "universal features of womanhood."
- Jackson's fallback to "prudence" as a universal — Seth: prudence is no more universal than international law; it just sounds universal because it is vague.

#### The nation-state ≠ culture problem
- Postcolonial critique of liberalism does double duty against IR: liberalism naturalises the **rational individual**; IR naturalises the **nation-state** as the unit that supposedly carries cultural content.
- **Benedict Anderson** (*Imagined Communities*): nationality and nation are **artefacts**, not facts. Even the colonised resisted in the form of nation-states because *"our imagination has been dominated by the nation-state."*
- Nations never corresponded to actual "peoples" or cultures (Hindus, Muslims, Gujaratis, Bengalis all had to start "imagining themselves as Indians"). Imagining nations was both **creative** (re-imagining) and **coercive** (peasants forced to become Frenchmen, Uighers into Chinese, Catalans into Spaniards).
- Few scholars actually believe nation-states represent cultures — yet IR theory and the UN both upheld this fiction by defending territorial integrity against external challenges *and* "sub-nationalisms."

### 4.4 Part III — Knowing and Being: knowledge constitutes its objects

#### The big claim
- Mainstream IR assumes knowledge is a relation between a "cognising, representing subject and an object" — knowledge is *of* something out there.
- **Postcolonial theory's payoff:** knowledges **constitute** what they purport to merely represent. IR theory **naturalises** what is historically produced.

#### Examples
- The "free, rational, equal individual" of liberal political theory is **not a discovery** of social contract theorists — it is a **product** of pedagogical crafting, historical processes, and discourses. **Uday Mehta**: Locke's liberal individual was "something he thought had to be forged through careful and detailed pedagogical crafting."
- **Nietzsche**: in *Genealogy of Morals*, the individual capable of making promises and feeling guilt was "forged on the anvil of Greek philosophy, Christian morality and Roman law."
- **Foucault** (and Nikolas Rose, Roy Porter, etc.) — show how the individual was *produced* by knowledges that posited him, rather than *discovered* by knowledges that finally recognised him.

#### Pollock and Kaviraj (the non-Western evidence)
- **Sheldon Pollock**: the social sciences originated to make sense of "the behaviour of power and culture under Western capitalist modernity… The theory developed from that history fails to help us understand, and even impedes us from seeing, what did happen elsewhere."
- **Sudipta Kaviraj**: in the Third World we meet socialists, liberals, democrats, nationalists, federalists — but their actual behaviour differs from "the long-established meanings of these terms in Western political and social thought." "Not [just] single isolated ideas but entire languages seem to be composed of systematically misleading expressions."

#### The constitutive role of knowledge — Mitchell, Seth's own work
- **Timothy Mitchell**: the distinction between "real and representation" — central to modern Western ways of organising the world and to British/French colonisers in Egypt — did not make sense to the Egyptians, who did not inhabit a world organised around it.
- **Seth's own work on colonial Indian education**: complaints that Indian students learned by rote, or were torn between traditional beliefs and Western ideas, do **not** testify to "real problems" — they reveal that the foundational assumptions of modern knowledge (knowledge as subject-object relation; morality as "beliefs held in the mind") could not, in fact, be assumed in India.
- As colonial institutions transformed life-worlds in India and Egypt, "the social sciences became more adequate as tools" — i.e. the knowledges helped **produce the reality** they purported to describe.

#### Why this matters for IR specifically
- Liberal political theory has **succeeded** in naturalising the individual (now an "axiom").
- Mainstream IR theory has been **less successful** in naturalising the state, nation, and the international order — they remain contingent and contested, with the struggles of their making still inscribed on their bodies.
- This is what makes **"the international"** the most interesting sphere of investigation: few things have become so naturalised that they cannot be contested. *"In what is still one of the most illuminating texts on the subject, Leviathan, Hobbes shows that sovereignty is the name and form of a capacity to impose and stabilise meanings."*

### 4.5 Conclusion (Seth's bottom line)

Postcolonial theory shares affinity with critical, constructivist, feminist, poststructuralist critiques — but is *especially sensitive to*:
- The **role of knowledge** as a "potent force for shaping what is 'out there'" (not just a mirror).
- The many circumstances in which **knowledges born in Europe are inadequate to their non-European object**.

> *"In my readings within the discipline, I find a great deal of IR theory is an obstacle to a recognition and exploration of this, rather than a guide to it, for it seems content to naturalise what it could problematise, and to assume that which it should deconstruct."*

This is the **most quotable Seth line** for a short-answer question.

## 6. Likely exam questions and how to answer

### 6.1 Concept-level (MCQs / short definitions)

- **Problem-solving theory** — takes the existing world as given and asks how to make institutions function better. Cox 1981: "theory is always for someone and for some purpose." Implicitly conservative.
- **Critical theory** — asks how the existing order came about, whose interests it serves, how it might be changed. Emancipatory.
- **Gramscian hegemony** — ideological and cultural leadership through institutions, civil society, the state. Hegemony works through consent + coercion. Bourgeois ideas become "common sense."
- **Subaltern** — those excluded from hegemonic structures and dominant-class discourse.
- **Discourse / power-knowledge** (Foucault) — knowledge is power; truths are produced by discourses that organise what can be said.
- **Biopolitics** (Foucault) — the modern state regulates life: surveillance, discipline, normalisation in prisons, asylums, schools.
- **Orientalism** (Said) — the West's construction of "the East" as a coherent, exotic, inferior Other — knowledge production that justifies imperial domination.
- **Standpoint theory / feminism** — knowledge is situated; the perspective of the marginalised offers epistemic privilege.
- **Intersectionality** (Crenshaw 1991) — multiple axes of identity (gender × race × class × etc.) intersect to shape oppression and experience.
- **Decentring** (Fisher-Onar & Nicolaïdis 2013) — provincialise + engage + reconstruct.
- **Ubiquity ≠ universality** (Seth) — the global spread of a practice tells you nothing about whether it is genuinely universal vs. a contingent product of Western dominance.
- **Eurocentrism** — treating European history, concepts and experience as the default standard for all human history.
- **Provincialise Europe** (Chakrabarty / Seth) — strip Europe of its assumed universal status and treat its history as one among many.

### 6.2 Short-answer template — "Critical theory's challenge to mainstream IR"

**Define:** Critical theories (Marxism/Frankfurt School, postcolonialism, feminism, poststructuralism) reject the **positivist** assumption that IR theory can be objective, neutral, value-free. With Cox, they argue all theory is "for someone and for some purpose." They are **post-positivist** and **emancipatory**, foregrounding power-knowledge, structural and discursive power, and the situated, often Western-centric, character of mainstream IR's concepts.

**Apply (a case):** Take **postcolonial IR** as developed by Seth. The English School's account of the "expansion of international society" — European states extending sovereignty outward in an orderly fashion — elides the **constitutive role of colonialism**: the slave trade, conquest of the Americas, the scramble for Africa, the structures of mandates, concessions and protectorates. The same events conventionally credited as IR's origin story (Westphalia, the rise of European international law) **coincide with and depend on** the violent subjugation of non-European peoples. The implication: any satisfactory account of the international order must be postcolonial. International law is not "European in origin but now universal" — it is contingent on imperial power. **Ubiquity is not universality.**

**Assess (what it explains / overlooks):**
- *Explains:* the **Eurocentrism** of foundational IR concepts (sovereignty, the nation-state, anarchy); how IR's claims to neutrality reproduce **power-knowledge** relations (Foucault); how the colonised continue to be invisible in mainstream accounts; how **the nation-state ≠ culture** (Anderson's *Imagined Communities*) — yet IR and the UN both uphold this fiction.
- *Overlooks / weaknesses:* (1) critical theory rarely offers an alternative way of "doing" IR (Seth himself admits this); (2) risk of relativism (poststructuralism); (3) can be excessively focused on critique at the expense of explanation/prediction; (4) postcolonial IR sometimes treats the West as monolithic and "the rest" as homogenous victims, recreating the very essentialism it criticises.

### 6.3 Cross-week threads

- **Week 4 (NWIRT/Global IR)** is the most natural pair. Critical theories + postcolonialism support Acharya's deprovincialization project. Both are sister critiques of mainstream Western IR.
- **Week 6 (migration)**: Huysmans uses **securitisation theory** — a critical/constructivist framework — to expose how migration becomes a "security issue" not because of inherent threat but because of **discourse**, professional practices, and political construction. Postcolonial migration scholars (El Qadim) likewise show how European migration governance is shaped by colonial legacies.

## 7. One-page revision sheet

**The meta-distinction (memorise):**
- **Problem-solving theory** (Cox 1981): takes the world as given; helps the powerful manage it. Mainstream IR.
- **Critical theory**: asks how the world came to be this way, whose interests it serves, how it might be changed. Emancipatory, post-positivist.

**The five major critical lineages:**
1. **Marxism / world-systems** — capitalism as global structure; core/periphery/semi-periphery; uneven development.
2. **Frankfurt School / Gramsci** — power as consent + coercion; hegemony of bourgeois ideas as "common sense"; counter-hegemony.
3. **Feminism** — IR is gender-blind; "power politics" is built on masculinist assumptions; standpoint theory; intersectionality (Crenshaw).
4. **Poststructuralism / Foucault** — discourse and power-knowledge; biopolitics; "there is nothing outside the text" (Derrida); deconstruction.
5. **Postcolonialism** — Orientalism (Said); provincialise Europe (Chakrabarty / Seth); ubiquity ≠ universality; colonial encounters constitute the modern international order.

**Seth's three-part critique:**
- **History** — the "expansion of international society" narrative sanitises slavery, conquest, genocide. Postcolonial accounts treat colonialism as **constitutive**, not a footnote.
- **Culture/theory** — the "procedural" universality of international law is never neutral. *Ubiquity is not universality.*
- **Knowing/being** — knowledges **constitute** what they purport to represent. IR naturalises what is historically produced. The "free, rational individual," the nation-state, sovereignty — all are products of historical processes and discourses, not discoveries.

**Decentring (Fisher-Onar & Nicolaïdis 2013):** provincialise → engage → reconstruct.
