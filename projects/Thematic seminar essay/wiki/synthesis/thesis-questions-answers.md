# Thesis Questions — Direct Answers

*Synthesis page answering two core essay questions based on the full bibliography. Written 2026-05-17 drawing on the existing wiki sources plus the Overnight Search finds. Treat this as a thinking aid for drafting the essay — cite from the original sources, not this page.*

---

## Question 1: Why does Indonesia pursue an ETS first rather than a carbon tax?

**Short answer:** Indonesia chose the ETS first for political-economy reasons, not because of superior economic design. The ETS hides costs in a way the tax does not, protects politically connected industries through free allocation, and generates revenue without a visible new tax on the population. A secondary reason is regulatory sequencing — the ETS was designed to produce a market price that the embedded carbon levy will eventually track.

### The five reasons, in order of explanatory weight

**1. Burden visibility (most important)**

A carbon tax makes fuel and electricity prices go up visibly for 270 million people. An ETS imposes costs on ~563 regulated power plants — mostly the state-owned utility PLN and a small number of private energy-intensive firms (ICAP 2025 factsheet, §Phase 2 coverage). The political cost is concentrated in a small set of firms whose executives can be managed through industry dialogue. It does not appear on anyone's electricity bill as a line item.

This is the core of the political-economy argument. Stevens [87] in Sunanda et al. 2025 states explicitly: "Political resistance and lack of coordination hinder carbon tax implementation in Indonesia, despite its potential to reduce emissions and fund climate programs." The "resistance" is from industries and from coalition partners in government who would face visible voter backlash from a price hike. Finon 2019 provides the theoretical anchor: developing economies face political-economy constraints that make standard carbon-pricing prescriptions a poor fit.

**2. Free allocation as protection for favoured industries**

Indonesia's ETS grants 100% free allowances in Phase 1 (2023–24), then 75–85% free thereafter, with non-compliance triggering a reduction (ICAP 2025 factsheet, §Allocation). A carbon tax offers no equivalent sweetener. The industries receiving free allowances — nickel processing, steel, cement, captive coal — are precisely the sectors the Jokowi and Prabowo governments have prioritised for "downstreaming" (processing raw materials domestically rather than exporting them). Free allocation is a subsidy to politically connected industrial transformation. A carbon tax would tax those same industries at a flat rate from day one.

**3. Revenue without a new tax**

The ETS plus Article 6 international credit sales generate government revenue (from eventual auctioning) and foreign exchange (from credit exports) without introducing a politically visible new tax. The re-opening of Article 6 in January 2025 — allowing PLN-affiliated credits to be exported internationally — adds an international revenue stream (~1.78 MtCO2e already authorised as of January 2026 per ICAP factsheet). This is consistent with Indonesia's desire to be a carbon-credit exporter at scale; the government has stated an ambition to sell 13.4 billion tonnes of credits to global buyers (carboncredits.com, 2025).

Risk: Jakob & Steckel 2014 warn of a "climate finance curse" parallel to resource curse — if Indonesia becomes a credit-export economy rather than a genuinely decarbonising one, the revenue motive may crowd out domestic action.

**4. Regulatory sequencing ("cap first, then tax")**

AMRO's analysis ("Cap First, and Then Tax: Carbon-Pricing in Indonesia") argues Indonesia's sequencing is internally coherent: the ETS is designed to generate a market carbon price, and the carbon levy under Presidential Regulation 110/2025 is explicitly structured to *float with* the ETS market price as a backstop. This means Indonesia has deferred setting the carbon tax rate to let the market determine it. The logic: set a market price first, then anchor the tax to it once that price has credibility. No carbon tax before the ETS = no valid price benchmark for the tax rate. (Note: this is an unconventional design; most jurisdictions set the tax and build the ETS alongside it, but Indonesia's approach is internally coherent as a sequencing choice.)

**5. Regulatory and MRV readiness**

The ETS can start with the power sector, where PLN already has grid-level emissions data. A carbon tax applied economy-wide would require national MRV infrastructure across all sectors — industry, transport, agriculture, forestry — where data quality is far lower. Starting the ETS in one data-rich sector and expanding incrementally allows MRV infrastructure to be built as the market grows. The voluntary ETS pilot in 2021 (32 coal-fired power plants, 28 transactions) generated the institutional knowledge base for the mandatory 2023 launch (AMRO source). The carbon tax had no equivalent pilot phase.

### The counter-evidence (for a balanced essay)

The "tax is political poison" framing is partly a *perception* problem, not a hard distributional fact:

- **Yusuf & Resosudarmo 2015** found the carbon tax is *not* regressive at the national level in Indonesia — it is progressive among lower-income rural households (who use less fossil energy) and regressive only in urban areas.
- **Rahma, Hartono & Hastuti 2025** reach similar conclusions via a social accounting matrix analysis.
- **ADB climate perception survey 2024** (cited in East Asia Forum, Feb 2025): over 70% of Southeast Asian respondents *support* a national carbon tax, and 93% of that segment are willing to bear the personal costs.

So the political resistance to a carbon tax in Indonesia appears to be primarily from the *industry* lobby and *political coalition partners*, not from voters who — when surveyed — are broadly supportive. This gap between political reality and public opinion is worth foregrounding in the essay.

## Question 2: What are the most crucial things Indonesia should do now to enhance the environmental and economic framework of carbon pricing?

**Short answer:** Indonesia needs to do five things. The most urgent is to actually enforce the carbon levy that is already legislated. The rest are extensions of that first step. Crucially, Indonesia has already *committed* to all five on paper (in PR 110/2025 and the October 2025 NDC) — the binding constraint is implementation, not policy design.

### The five moves, in priority order

**1. Enforce the carbon levy and commit to a graduated escalation roadmap (most urgent)**

The carbon levy under Law 7/2021 (IDR 30/kg CO2 ≈ $2/tCO2) has existed since 2021 and been enforced approximately zero times. PR 110/2025 links the levy to the ETS market price — but the ETS market is illiquid (IDR 1 billion total transactions in the first half of 2025; IEEFA 2025), so the levy is effectively inactive.

The recommendation literature converges on a graduated tax escalation path:
- **Sunanda et al. 2025 §4.2:** explicit recommendation for a graduated roadmap starting at the current $2 and rising predictably.
- **UNDP 2025:** "progressive carbon tax trajectory with initial low tax rates, temporal rebates, adequate transition periods, with incremental tax adjustments built in upfront." The predictability is as important as the level.
- **van den Bergh & Drews 2026:** the same graduated-escalation model recommended for developing-country carbon pricing.
- **OECD Effective Carbon Rates 2025:** Indonesia's net effective carbon rate (once fuel subsidies are netted out) is likely *negative*, meaning fuel users are still being implicitly subsidised. The first task is to eliminate the net subsidy before even getting to a positive price.

A realistic escalation path for the essay: $2/t (now) → $10/t (2028) → $30/t (2032). Even $30/t is below the literature-recommended $30–60 for emerging economies (Parry et al. 2021, cited in Stastny lit review), but a credible trajectory matters more than the starting level.

**2. Revenue recycling — make it visible and pro-poor**

Hartono et al. 2023 is the single most important source for this recommendation: CGE modelling shows that in Indonesia specifically, recycling carbon tax revenue into household transfers and clean energy investment raises *both* GDP and employment. This is the double-dividend argument (Goulder 1995) applied to the Indonesian context with actual data.

The key mechanism: Indonesia's PKH cash-transfer program (conditional cash transfers to the poorest households) already exists and could be used as the distribution vehicle. A politically framed "carbon dividend" would:
- Neutralise the distributional concern (counter the urban-regressive finding from Rahma et al. 2025)
- Make the carbon price "legible" to voters as a transfer rather than just a new cost
- Mirror successful designs elsewhere: British Columbia's carbon tax + dividend; Switzerland's carbon tax + per-capita redistribution

The World Bank's State and Trends 2025 reports that globally, over half of carbon pricing revenue is earmarked for environment, infrastructure, and development — Indonesia's design should target the same.

**3. Fix the ETS's market liquidity before expanding coverage**

The current ETS is operationally hollow: total IDX Carbon exchange transactions of ~$5 million since launch (IEEFA 2025); no allowances issued and no trading in 2025 due to regulatory revisions (ICAP 2025). Before expanding to industry, transport, and forestry (which is the right long-term goal), the core ETS needs:
- **Auctions** (none have happened; all allocation is still free)
- **A functioning price floor** (the levy should kick in as a market maker of last resort)
- **Stable MRV and allocation rules** (the 2025 postponement was caused by regulatory revision — predictability is essential for compliance investment)

The IISD Southeast Asia carbon pricing brief (2025) recommends as a priority: "improve systems to track and verify emissions, finalize sector roadmaps." This is prerequisite to everything else.

**4. Expand ETS coverage to forestry and AFOLU**

Power sector ETS coverage = only ~21% of national GHG emissions (225.3 MtCO2e verified vs 1,053.4 MtCO2e national total; ICAP 2025 factsheet). Indonesia's largest emissions sources are **deforestation and peat fires** (AFOLU sector), which are entirely outside the current ETS. This is where the biggest cheap abatement lies:
- Tan et al. 2022: peatland restoration is among the lowest-cost, highest-impact climate solutions available to Indonesia.
- The coal retirement abatement opportunity ($12–13/tCO2, TBI 2025) is valuable, but peatland and forest protection is potentially larger in volume.
- The SRUK (Carbon Unit Registry System) introduced by PR 110/2025 provides the legal infrastructure for non-power-sector credits. The policy framework exists; institutional build-out (Ministry of Agriculture, Ministry of Environment MRV) is what is missing.

**5. ASEAN regional coordination**

Indonesia is the first Southeast Asian country with an operational ETS (IISD 2025). The Philippines is drafting legislation; Vietnam postponed its ETS to 2026; Malaysia, Thailand are still at policy-debate stage. If Indonesia and Singapore (which has a functioning carbon tax at ~$25/t) move toward price-level coordination, the competitive leakage argument against Indonesia's low carbon price ($2/t) becomes weaker. Andriansyah & Hong 2022 (AMRO) maps the ASEAN+3 landscape; Nurdianto & Resosudarmo 2016's modelling of a uniform ASEAN carbon tax found Indonesia would *gain* (its heavy energy subsidies mean a uniform ASEAN tax is less distortionary for Indonesia than it sounds).

The East Asia Forum piece (Feb 2025) makes the public-opinion case: 70%+ SEA public supports a national carbon tax. This is the political foundation for a regional price alignment conversation.

## Connecting the two answers

The essay's punchline should be that these two answers tell a coherent story:

- Indonesia chose ETS-first because of **political-economy constraints** on the carbon tax (burden visibility, free-allocation incentives, revenue-without-new-tax logic).
- But those same political-economy constraints are **the main obstacle** to the five recommendations above.
- The essay's contribution is to show that the constraints are not about public opinion (which broadly supports a carbon tax) but about **industry coalitions and government revenue incentives** — and that the right policy design (graduated escalation + PKH dividend + stable ETS) can address those constraints directly.
- Indonesia has already committed to all five recommendations on paper in PR 110/2025 and the October 2025 NDC. **The binding constraint is political will on implementation, not policy design.**
