# Lecture 11 — Absolutism and Relativism

*Final substantive lecture. How to think clearly about relativism: never "in general," always about a specific property F. Relativism about *truth* doesn't work (self-undermining). Relativism about *rationality* does work and offers a non-judgemental way to think about cultural difference.*

**Date:** 13 May 2026 · **Lecturer:** Prof. James W. McAllister · **Key thinkers:** **Peter Winch** (radical relativism) and **Steven Lukes** (intermediate view) — for the Azande debate; **Maria Baghramian** (reading author).

---

## Key concepts — definitions

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Absolutism about property F** | The thesis that entities have or lack F **intrinsically** — perspective plays no role in determining whether an entity has F. |
| **Relativism about property F** | The thesis that it is *not* the case that entities have or lack F intrinsically — entities have or lack F only **relative to a perspective S**. |
| **Perspective S** | The "viewpoint from which" something is judged. Can range over: standpoints, cultures, world views, styles, conceptual schemes, practices, disciplines. |
| **Important precision** | Absolutism/relativism is *not* a single view. **Only absolutism/relativism *about* a specified property F is a specific view.** Don't argue "I'm a relativist" in general; argue "I'm a relativist about X." |
| **Relativism about truth** | The thesis that a proposition's truth-value depends on perspective. Same proposition can be true from my viewpoint and false from yours. *Verdict in analytic philosophy: unrestricted relativism about truth is self-subverting and incoherent.* |
| **Self-subverting** | A claim that, when applied to itself, undermines itself. *Example:* "All generalisations have exceptions" — itself a generalisation, must have an exception, so isn't a fully reliable generalisation. Relativism about truth, applied to its own statement, has this problem. |
| **Relativism about rationality** | The thesis that a reasoning style has or lacks rationality only relative to perspective (cultural or conceptual setting). Different reasoning styles can be **on a par** in their own settings. Implication: no single reasoning style has privileged access to reality. |
| **Absolutism about rationality** | One reasoning style intrinsically *is* rational (usually claimed on behalf of Western logic/science); all others are intrinsically irrational. Fosters a judgemental stance toward different cultures' reasoning. |
| **Indexical** | A term whose reference shifts with the speaker, time, or place: *I, this, here, now.* A common source of fake relativism — "*I* teach philosophy of science" seems true if McAllister says it and false if you say it, but the two utterances are *different claims*, not the same claim with shifting truth-value. |
| **Forms of life** (Winch) | Wittgensteinian phrase used by Peter Winch — different cultures have different practices, languages, and frameworks; "accepting or rejecting appeals to witchcraft is the result of different forms of life." Used to argue rationality is culture-bound. |

## Lecture walkthrough

### Start with precision

A common move in public discourse is to dismiss "relativism" globally — to associate it with no-standards, "anything goes," postmodernism. **The lecture rejects this** as imprecise. The useful question is always: relativism *about what property*?

Schema:
- **Absolutism about F:** entities have F intrinsically.
- **Relativism about F:** entities have F only relative to perspective S.

*Trivial example to lock in the form:* relativism about **utility**. Is a tin-opener useful? It depends — on your aims. Utility *is* perspective-relative — depending on S (aim or purpose). That's relativism about utility. No one finds this controversial.

### Application 1 — relativism about truth

**Truth is a property of propositions only** (recall Lecture 2). So "relativism about truth" is a view about how propositions possess truth.

- **Absolutist:** A proposition is true or false based on how the world is. Perspective is irrelevant.
- **Relativist:** A proposition can be true from my perspective and false from yours.

⚠ **Caution about indexicals:** "I teach philosophy of science" seems to be true when McAllister says it and false when you say it — but the two utterances are *different propositions* (different *I*s referring to different people), not the same one with shifting truth.

**Refutation of relativism about truth — by self-reference:**

> Step 1: State relativism about truth: *"Assertion P has or lacks truth not absolutely, but only relative to perspective."*
> Step 2: Apply this claim *to itself* (replace P with the claim of relativism about truth).
> Result: We cannot assert relativism about truth *regardless of perspective*. The view undermines its own assertion. **Incoherent.**

This is the same logical pattern as: *"The penultimate sentence on sheet 17 is not true"* or *"For every generalisation, there is an exception."*

**Possible defences (and why they fail):**
- *Defence 1:* introduce "subjective truth" as a new property. Response: subjective truth ≠ truth. You've changed the subject.
- *Defence 2:* allocate the statement of relativism to a *metalanguage* to avoid self-reference. Response: requires a piecemeal theory of truth — fragile.

**Implication:** we should acknowledge alternative perspectives, but *not* by adopting relativism about truth. Better routes: reconstruct claims more fully (so different people are putting forward different claims); acknowledge that *justifications* for untrue claims can be genuine; consider relativism about *rationality* instead.

### Application 2 — relativism about rationality

Here "rationality" is a property of a **reasoning style** (the *style itself* may or may not be rational).

**Absolutism about rationality:**
- Each reasoning style intrinsically is or is not rational.
- Usually paired with: only *one* reasoning style is rational, all others are not.
- Motivation: to be rational is to reason in accord with the world; the world is unique; therefore only one style fits.
- Often claimed on behalf of Western logic and science.
- Fosters a **judgemental stance**: "my style R is rational; deviations from R are irrational."

**Relativism about rationality:**
- A reasoning style has or lacks rationality only relative to a perspective.
- Different reasoning styles can be **on a par** in their own settings.
- Implication: no single style has privileged access to reality.
- Motivation: empirical evidence of *plurality* of reasoning styles and their *effectiveness in their own settings* — diversity in time (Kuhn) and in place (anthropology).

### Two domains where this plays out

#### History of science — early Kuhn

Each paradigm has its own incommensurable form of rationality, effective within itself. No grounds for ranking. Early Kuhn (Lecture 6) is essentially a relativist about rationality.

#### Cultural anthropology — the Azande debate

Tension between universalist and relativist approaches to human reason.

The **Azande** are an agricultural people of northern Central Africa who consult oracles, believe in witchcraft, practise magic. *Three takes:*

| Scholar | Position | Verdict on Azande |
|---|---|---|
| **Classical Western anthropology** | Extreme absolutism | Azande reasoning is "irrational" and "primitive" |
| **E. E. Evans-Pritchard** (1902–1973), *Oracles, Witchcraft, and Magic Among the Azande* (1937) | More nuanced absolutism | Azande reasoning genuinely helps them make sense of misfortunes; but Western frameworks show it is flawed (witchcraft doesn't exist) |
| **Peter Winch** (1926–1997), *"Understanding a Primitive Society"* (1964) | **Far-reaching relativism** | Accepting/rejecting witchcraft results from different *forms of life*. Azande reasoning is no less objective than ours. Anthropologists cannot ascertain any culture is "wrong" |
| **Steven Lukes** (1941–), *"Different Cultures, Different Rationalities?"* (2000) | **Intermediate position** | Our attempts to get the Azande right must respect their attempts to get the world right — but assessing rationality across cultures is *both possible and necessary*. There exist universal norms |

## Key thinkers — Peter Winch and Steven Lukes

**Peter Winch** (1926–1997) — British philosopher of social science. His 1964 essay "Understanding a Primitive Society" is *the* classic defence of far-reaching relativism about rationality. Drew on Wittgenstein's idea of "forms of life."

**Steven Lukes** (1941–) — British social theorist. His 2000 essay "Different Cultures, Different Rationalities?" articulates the moderate, intermediate position — respecting cultural difference *without* abandoning the possibility of cross-cultural assessment.

*(The lecture's reading author **Maria Baghramian** — Aristotelian Society 2019 — defends "the virtues of relativism" as a *clarifying* rather than dissolving stance.)*

---

> **Explained easily — the precision move:**
> "Are you a relativist?" is a bad question. The answer should be: "About what?" Relativism about *utility* — sure (a tin-opener's usefulness depends on your aim). Relativism about *fashion* — sure (what looks good depends on era and place). Relativism about *truth* — probably incoherent. Relativism about *rationality* — interesting and possibly defensible. The whole lecture's discipline: don't argue in general, argue about a specific property.

> **Explained easily — why relativism about truth shoots itself in the foot:**
> Take the claim: "all truth is relative to perspective." Now ask: is *that* claim absolutely true, or only relative to perspective? If absolutely true, it contradicts itself. If only relative to perspective, then from some other perspective it's false — so it can't be a general philosophical thesis. Either way, you can't *assert it consistently*. Self-reference does the killing.

> **Explained easily — relativism about rationality (the survivable one):**
> Different cultures and different historical eras use very different reasoning styles — and within each style, things "make sense." Quantum-mechanical reasoning would look bizarre to a 17th-century scientist; their reasoning would look bizarre to us. Azande consulting an oracle uses logic that works in their context. Relativism about rationality says: there's no single neutral "above all of these" view from which we can rank them. This avoids the colonial reflex of calling other cultures "irrational" without sliding into "anything goes" — because each style still has internal standards.

> **Explained easily — the Azande debate, three positions:**
> Imagine you're an anthropologist studying a culture that uses witchcraft to explain misfortune. *Old colonial answer:* they're primitive and wrong. *Evans-Pritchard:* they have internal coherence, but they're objectively wrong (no witches). *Winch:* there's no neutral standpoint to call them "objectively wrong" — they're playing a different game. *Lukes:* respect them, but don't pretend we can't sometimes judge — some norms (e.g. against torturing children) are universal. The lecture seems sympathetic to Lukes-style middle ground.

## Likely exam angles

- **Apply:** Use the schema to formulate (a) absolutism vs relativism about utility (easy warm-up), (b) about truth, (c) about rationality.
- **Critical:** Explain why unrestricted relativism about truth is self-subverting. Walk through the self-reference argument.
- **Compare:** Three positions on Azande rationality — classical anthropology, Winch, Lukes.
