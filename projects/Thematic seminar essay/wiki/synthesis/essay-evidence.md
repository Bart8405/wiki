## Evidence 1 — IISD/GSI — Indonesian Fossil-Fuel Subsidies

*Supports Objection 5 in [essay-master.md §5](essay-master.md#5-stress-test--counter-evidence) and the §3 hollow-reality punchline.*

### Key findings in one sentence

Indonesia spent **IDR 713.5 trillion (~USD 45 billion)** on energy subsidies in 2024 (88.9% supporting fossil fuels) — and IISD/OECD now quantify the net effect: **fossil-fuel subsidies produced a net effective carbon price of *negative* USD 7.8/tCO₂ in 2023**, more than fully cancelling the carbon-pricing scheme's signal.

> **The single most important number for the essay:** the OECD 2023 net effective carbon rate is **IDR −118,000 / USD −7.8 per tCO₂** — explicit pricing (IDR 165 / USD 0.01 nominal) overwhelmed by implicit fossil-fuel subsidies. ([IISD/OECD 2026](../../raw/New%20sources/IISD_Indonesia%E2%80%99s%20Energy%20Support%20Measures%20-%202026.md), Box 1)

The ratio is not a rounding error — it is the structural argument. Indonesia simultaneously operates one of the world's largest fossil-fuel subsidy systems relative to its carbon market *and* claims to price carbon.

**The Box-1 finding (IISD 2026 citing OECD):** *"In 2023, fossil fuel subsidies in Indonesia more than fully counteracted the impact of the national carbon pricing scheme, creating a net negative carbon price of USD 7.8 per tonne of carbon."* The carbon-pricing scheme's nominal explicit rate of IDR 165 (USD 0.01) per tCO₂ is overwhelmed by implicit fossil-fuel subsidies. This is **the single sharpest empirical anchor for Framing 2** — the system is not a decarbonisation mechanism by any plausible reading; the net signal is in the wrong direction.

### Who benefits — distributional finding (IISD 2026 update)

IISD's 2026 update sharpens the regressivity argument:

- **More than 68% of households in *every* income group consume subsidised 3-kg LPG.** "Hanya untuk masyarakat miskin" (for the poor only) labels do not bind in practice.
- **The richest 10% consume almost as much subsidised LPG as the poorest 10%** — confirmed.
- **Indonesia imports three-quarters of its LPG** (~IDR 74 trillion / USD 4.7B annually), making the subsidy regressive AND import-dependent — a fiscal and geopolitical exposure as well as a climate one.
- **Induction-stove alternative:** IISD's modelling shows induction is **17% cheaper to run than unsubsidised LPG**. Targeting the poorest 40–60% to transition could save IDR 7–12 trillion/year and free fiscal space.
- The World Bank CCDR 2023 recommendation still holds: replace the LPG subsidy with targeted cash transfers (per PKH/BPNT design). The Indonesian conditional-cash-transfer infrastructure already exists.

**Implication for the argument:** The political impossibility of subsidy reform is overstated for the household component. The genuinely hard-to-reform part is the **industrial component** (PLN compensation + Pertamina industrial fuel allocation), which benefits politically connected utilities and the national oil company, not the urban poor.

### Reform history (the third-rail framing is dated)

Indonesia has reformed fuel subsidies before:
- **2014 (Jokowi, first year):** Removed Rp 6,500/litre premium petrol subsidy, saving ~IDR 200 trillion/year. Redirected to infrastructure and social transfers. Public backlash manageable.
- **2022:** Cut some fuel subsidies as global prices spiked. Pertalite rose from IDR 7,650 → 10,000/litre.
- **Regression (2023–24):** Under Prabowo, some subsidy protections expanded, particularly for PLN compensation.

The 1998 riots were an extreme case (Suharto crisis context, 70%+ price rises, no cash-transfer cushion). Modern targeted-compensation programs can blunt household-level impact. **The binding constraint is industrial energy subsidies captured by PLN, Pertamina, and captive-coal operators** — these are harder to reform because the beneficiaries are large corporations with political connections, not vulnerable households.

### Fiscal arithmetic for the essay

- Fossil-fuel subsidy bill 2024: ~IDR 634 trillion
- Tony Blair Institute's MAC for coal retirement in Indonesia: USD 12–13/tCO2
- Annual coal retirement cost at ~50 Mt abatement: ~USD 600–650 million
- **Savings from even partial subsidy reform would dwarf the cost of coal-retirement abatement** — the fiscal logic runs directly against the "Indonesia can't afford to decarbonise" argument

The coal price cap alone (IDR 58.5T = ~USD 3.6B annually) subsidises the coal sector the ETS is supposed to price. Removing the coal price cap would immediately raise the effective carbon rate on power generation more than any realistic ETS escalation could.

## Evidence 2 — Hashim Djojohadikusumo / Arsari Group

*Supports Pattern D (credit-export logic) in [essay-master.md §1.3](essay-master.md#pattern-d--credit-export-economy-not-decarbonisation-economy) and the §3 hollow-reality + introduction hook.*

### Why this matters for the essay

Framing 2 argues Indonesia is building a "credit-export economy, not a decarbonisation economy." The Hashim/Arsari network is the **single clearest piece of evidence** that this is not accidental policy drift — it is purposeful design. The person who designed the rules benefits from the rules.

### The core conflict of interest — one paragraph

**Hashim Djojohadikusumo** (Prabowo Subianto's younger brother) was appointed **Special Presidential Envoy for Climate and Energy** at COP29 in Baku (November 2024). He publicly claimed Indonesia had **557 million tonnes of CO2e verified and ready for trade** and announced a government target of **USD 65 billion from carbon projects by 2028**. At the same COP29 pavilion, he attracted EUR 1.2 billion in green financing for Indonesia's electricity sector. He then recruited **ex-World Bank Managing Director Mari Elka Pangestu** to design the carbon trading mechanism for Indonesia. Meanwhile, a subsidiary of **Arsari Group** (his family conglomerate) — **ITCI Kartika Utama → ITCI Kayan Hutani** — is listed in government documents as a participant in the carbon trading rollout, with a forest concession in **Bulungan Regency, North Kalimantan**. The conflict of interest is structural: the rule-setter and the market participant are the same person's family.

### Timeline of events

| Date | Event |
|---|---|
| Oct 2024 | Prabowo takes office; appoints Hashim as Special Presidential Envoy for Climate/Energy |
| Nov 11, 2024 | Hashim at COP29 Baku: 557 Mt ready for trade; USD 65 billion by 2028 target; EUR 1.2B green-finance announcement |
| Nov 2024 onward | Hashim recruits Mari Elka Pangestu (ex-WB MD) to design carbon market mechanism |
| Early 2025 | MRA with Verra, Gold Standard, Plan Vivo, ART being finalised |
| Apr 9, 2025 | Carbon market launch meetings at The Alana Hotel, Sentul City — 50+ companies invited, including Austral Byna (owned by a corruption convict) |
| Apr 16, 2025 | Environment Minister confirms Hashim is "personally overseeing" VCM opening (Tempo interview) |
| May 2025 | Arsari Group's ITCI Kayan Hutani confirmed in government documents as carbon-market participant (Bulungan, N. Kalimantan) |
| Oct 10, 2025 | PR 110/2025 issued: decouples VCM from NDC timeline; makes corresponding adjustments **optional** for international transfers |
| Nov 2025 | Indonesia uses carbon market as showcase at COP30; critics warn of "hot air" (Mongabay) |

### PR 110/2025 — the regulatory consequence

The regulation that Hashim's process produced contains a critical provision serving the credit-export logic:

> **PR 110/2025 explicitly decouples voluntary carbon trading from the NDC timeline**, allowing projects to generate and sell credits without waiting for national target fulfilment. Corresponding Adjustments (CAs) — the mechanism preventing double-counting by removing a credit from Indonesia's NDC accounting — are made **optional, not mandatory**, for international transfers.

**Translation for the essay:** under the new rules, Indonesia can export forest carbon credits internationally *without* those credits counting against Indonesia's own NDC commitment. This means the same tonne of avoided deforestation can simultaneously (a) be sold to a Japanese buyer who counts it toward their net-zero target, and (b) *not* be deducted from Indonesia's national emissions account. This is structurally a credit-export mechanism, not a domestic decarbonisation mechanism.

### The 13.4 billion tonne ambition

The Indonesian government has stated an ambition of selling **13.4 billion tonnes** of carbon credits to global buyers (timing unspecified; some sources reference 2060; others reference a 2028 horizon for early tranches). The Prabowo target is USD 65 billion from credits by 2028.

For context: Indonesia's total annual CO2 emissions are approximately 1.2 Gt/year. Selling 13.4 Bt of credits implies either credits spanning several decades, OR credits far exceeding any realistic domestic emissions reduction (relying on land-use accounting that critics — Pierucci et al. 2025; Rizzi 2025 — say overstates genuine sequestration).

### Caveats for academic use

1. Tempo/Pulitzer reporting is investigative journalism, not peer-reviewed research. For an academic essay, use it as evidence for claims about actor networks, not as proof of illegality.
2. Hashim's conflict of interest is alleged by civil society (Walhi, Mongabay, Tempo); the government has not acknowledged it. Frame as "documented conflict of interest" not "confirmed corruption."
3. The Arsari Group/ITCI participation in the carbon market is **confirmed by government documents** reviewed by Tempo — not just an allegation.

### Recommended essay use

Use Hashim/Arsari in **one focused paragraph** in the section that establishes Pattern D (credit-export economy). It is the named face of an otherwise abstract structural argument. The paragraph should:
1. Name Hashim and his dual role (envoy + beneficiary)
2. Name the regulatory consequence (PR 110/2025 decoupling)
3. Connect to the broader pattern of conglomerate entry into the carbon market (Arsari, Adaro, RGE, Melchor — documented in Political_Economy_Notes §7–8)
4. Cite Tempo/Pulitzer for sourcing

**Do NOT** let this become the essay's main argument — it is one actor in a systemic story.

## Evidence 3 — EU CBAM Exposure for Indonesia

*Supports Recommendation 6 (the CBAM clincher) in [essay-master.md §4.3](essay-master.md#43-section-3-what-indonesia-should-do-now-1000-words).*

### Why CBAM matters for the essay — one paragraph

The EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) enters its **definitive application regime on 1 January 2026**. It creates an external revenue incentive that the essay should name explicitly: if Indonesia's domestic carbon price remains near zero, **EU importers of Indonesian steel and aluminium will pay the full CBAM certificate cost** — and that money goes to the EU budget, not to Indonesia. A genuine domestic carbon price allows Indonesian exporters to deduct it from CBAM obligations. Every dollar of domestic carbon revenue Indonesia fails to capture from these export sectors will instead be collected by Brussels. This is the sharpest "what Indonesia should do now" argument, and it requires no appeal to abstract climate ethics — it is pure fiscal self-interest.

### What CBAM covers (from 2026)

- Cement
- Iron and steel (**biggest exposure for Indonesia**)
- Aluminium (significant exposure)
- Fertilisers (highest tariff rate exposure per tonne)
- Electricity
- Hydrogen

### Indonesia's quantified exposure

| Sector | 2023 Indonesian exports to EU | CBAM tariff exposure |
|---|---|---|
| Steel (iron & steel) | USD 726.84 million | High — carbon-intensive production methods |
| Aluminium | USD 29.17 million | 12.14% tariff rate cited |
| Fertilisers | USD 0.29 million | 138.72% tariff rate cited (highest) |

- EU ranks **5th** among Indonesia's export destinations (~7.4% of total exports)
- Approximately **6.2% of these exports** fall under CBAM coverage
- Indonesia faces higher CBAM costs than Japan and South Korea due to more carbon-intensive production processes

**Important caveat on nickel:** Indonesia is the world's largest nickel producer and is developing a downstream nickel-to-steel (and nickel-to-EV battery) sector under Jokowi/Prabowo's "downstreaming" policy. Steel from nickel-based Indonesian integrated mills could face significant CBAM exposure as EU imports grow. Not yet captured in 2023 trade data but strategically important.

### The carbon-price gap and its CBAM consequence

| Price | 2025 |
|---|---|
| EU ETS allowance (EUA) price | ~€70–75/tCO2 |
| Indonesia domestic carbon price (ETS) | ~USD 2/tCO2 |
| Gap | ~€68–73/tCO2 |

At current EU EUA prices, Indonesian steel exporters to the EU face CBAM certificate cost close to the full EU carbon price (~€70/tonne of CO2 embedded in their products), because Indonesia's domestic price credits are near-zero.

**Rough fiscal consequence for Indonesian steel sector:**
- If Indonesian steel to EU embeds ~1.8 tCO2/tonne (typical for coal-based production), and USD 726M of steel is exported annually:
- CBAM cost ≈ USD 726M × (1.8 tCO2/tonne steel) × (~€70–75/tCO2) — significant competitiveness cost.

### The perverse incentive structure — the essay's gift

CBAM creates an irony the essay can exploit:
- Indonesia has resisted raising its domestic carbon price citing competitiveness ("ETS should not disadvantage Indonesian industry")
- CBAM means that *by not raising* its domestic price, Indonesia is actually **making its export industries less competitive** in EU markets — and transferring potential domestic fiscal revenue to Brussels
- A domestic price of €30/tCO2 would reduce CBAM liability *while generating* Indonesian state revenue — the opposite of what the "keep prices low to protect competitiveness" logic predicts

This is the cleanest "what should Indonesia do now" recommendation: raise the domestic carbon price to the level required to neutralise CBAM exposure, using the revenue to compensate affected workers and communities. The Tony Blair Institute's USD 12–13/tCO2 coal-retirement MAC is the right domestic benchmark; CBAM is the external pressure lever.

### Indonesia's current policy response

- Indonesia is "working closely with the European Commission to develop an ETS to offset any potential tariffs and duties imposed under CBAM" (per Argus Media / ICAP sources)
- This confirms CBAM is explicitly motivating Indonesia's ETS development — but the ETS at current prices (~USD 2/tCO2) still leaves ~95% of CBAM exposure uncovered
- Ministry of Industry published an Industrial Decarbonisation Roadmap in 2025; Ministerial Regulation on Industrial Decarbonisation planned for 2026 — operationally behind

### Recommended essay use

Use CBAM in **one paragraph in the recommendation section** (Pattern E / "what Indonesia should do now" close):
1. State CBAM starts 2026 and covers Indonesia's key export sectors
2. Name the fiscal arithmetic: low domestic price → EU collects Indonesian carbon revenue
3. Connect to the Tony Blair Institute finding (USD 12–13 MAC for coal retirement) — raising the domestic price to that level serves both CBAM exposure management AND coal-retirement incentivisation
4. This is the "external pressure" argument that converts critique into concrete policy recommendation
