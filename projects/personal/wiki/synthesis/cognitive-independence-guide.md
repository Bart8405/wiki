# What NOT to Use Claude Code For — A Cognitive Independence Guide

*How to stay intellectually sharp while using AI: the tasks that belong in your head, not in a prompt.*

---

## Why this guide exists

There is a real risk with AI tools: convenience can quietly replace capability. Every time you ask Claude to form a view for you rather than helping you refine your own, you miss a small piece of intellectual exercise. Over months and years, those missed exercises add up.

This guide is not about using Claude less. It's about using it in the right places — so that the skills that define a great researcher and thinker stay sharp in *you*, not just in your AI assistant.

The working principle throughout: **Claude should be the gym equipment, not the athlete.**

---

## Part 1 — The cognitive tasks that belong entirely to you

These are the tasks where the process of doing them *is* the point. Outsourcing them to Claude means skipping the workout that builds the muscle.

### 1. Forming your research question

The single most important intellectual act in any research project is deciding *what you want to know* and *why it matters*. This is where original scholarship begins.

When you ask Claude "what's a good research question about the Indonesian carbon market?", you get a question — but it won't be yours. It won't reflect your specific reading of the literature, your intuitions formed over months of reading, your frustrations with what existing scholarship has missed.

**The rule**: Before any session about a paper or project, write down your research question yourself in one sentence. Then discuss it with Claude. Never let Claude produce the question first.

### 2. Reading a source for the first time

Reading is thinking. When you read a paper and have to wrestle with a dense paragraph, your brain is building a mental model of the argument. That struggle is the learning. If you ask Claude to summarise before you read, you skip the struggle — and you remember almost nothing.

**The rule**: Read first, Claude second. Use Claude to check your understanding, fill gaps, or find connections after you've read. Never use it as a substitute for reading.

Exception: for sources that are truly peripheral (you just need one specific fact from a 200-page report), a targeted summary is fine. But for anything central to your argument, read it yourself.

### 3. Writing your first attempt at an argument

Writing is thinking made visible. The act of trying to express an idea in words forces you to notice when your thinking is vague. If Claude writes your argument and you edit it, you never encounter that friction — and your writing and thinking development slows.

**The rule**: Write a rough version yourself first, always. It can be bad. It can be a bullet-point sketch. Then bring it to Claude for feedback. The sequence must be: you think → you write → Claude helps → you revise.

Never start a writing session by asking Claude "write me a section about X." Start by writing something yourself, then say "here's my rough attempt — help me sharpen it."

### 4. Making connections between ideas

In humanities and social science, the most valuable thing you can do is see a connection that nobody else has seen — linking a concept from one theory to a case study in another field, or noticing that two seemingly different phenomena have the same underlying structure.

This is the core of original scholarship. It happens in your mind through reading, note-making, and reflection. Claude can help you *test* a connection once you've spotted it ("does this parallel between rent-seeking theory and Indonesian forestry licensing hold up?") but it should not *find* the connection for you.

**The rule**: When you have an "aha" moment — a connection, an analogy, an unexpected parallel — write it down before you ask Claude about it. Get your intuition on paper first. Then explore it with Claude.

### 5. Deciding what is credible and what is not

Critical source evaluation is one of the most important skills in academic work. Deciding who funded a study, what the author's ideological positioning is, whether the methodology is sound, whether the conclusion follows from the evidence — this requires your judgement, not Claude's.

Claude can describe a source's methodology or summarise its claims. But it has no consistent ability to judge credibility, detect motivated reasoning, or flag ideological bias. It will often be too credulous about authoritative-sounding sources.

**The rule**: Always form your own view on a source's credibility before asking Claude about it. Ask "is this source reliable for my purpose?" of yourself first. Claude can help you check specific methodological questions, but never delegate the trust decision.

### 6. Evaluating your own argument

When you're deciding whether your argument is good enough — whether your evidence actually supports your claim, whether the counterargument is fatal or manageable — don't outsource that judgement.

Ask Claude to steelman the counterargument (i.e. put the strongest possible version of the objection), then evaluate it yourself. The evaluation is yours. The decision to revise or defend is yours.

**The rule**: Use Claude as a sparring partner, not a judge. "Construct the strongest objection to my argument" is a good use. "Is my argument good?" is not — because your judgement of that answer is what you're training.

## Part 2 — Skills that atrophy fastest if you delegate them

These skills take longer to atrophy than the ones in Part 1, but they erode quietly over a year or two of heavy AI use.

### Language learning (Bahasa Indonesia)

If Claude always translates for you, you will plateau. The discomfort of not immediately understanding something in Indonesian is the signal that learning is happening. Sitting with uncertainty, guessing from context, being wrong and correcting yourself — that is how you build intuition in a language.

**The rule**: When reading Indonesian text, try to read it yourself for at least 30 seconds before asking for help. Guess at unknown words from context. Only then check. When Claude translates, read the translation back against the original yourself to see what you missed.

### Summarising things you've just learned

After a lecture or seminar, the instinct to immediately dump your notes into Claude and ask for a summary is strong. Resist it. The act of trying to recall and condense what you just learned — even imperfectly — is one of the most effective memory-building techniques known to science (it's called retrieval practice).

**The rule**: After lectures and seminars, write a one-paragraph summary yourself from memory before opening your notes. Then use Claude to check and extend it. This takes five extra minutes and improves retention dramatically.

### Forming opinions on events and news

You want to be an expert on Indonesia, Southeast Asia, and geopolitics. That means having opinions — real, argued opinions, not just "here's what the experts say." If you always ask Claude what to think about something before forming your own view, you skip the habit of opinion-formation.

**The rule**: When you read a news article or geopolitical development, write one or two sentences of your own reaction before asking Claude anything. What do you think is happening? Why? Then discuss it.

### Mathematical and logical reasoning (if relevant)

Whenever you encounter a graph, a statistic, or a logical chain of reasoning, try to parse it yourself before asking for an explanation. The ability to interpret data and follow arguments is fundamental to political economy research and weakens with disuse.

## Part 3 — Warning signs you're becoming dependent

These are signals worth watching for. None of them are catastrophic on their own, but together they indicate drift.

- **You feel anxious without Claude.** If you can't start an essay or reading session without a Claude window open, that's a sign the tool has become a crutch rather than equipment.
- **You can't summarise what you read without notes.** If you finish a paper and can't say in two sentences what it argued, something went wrong in the reading.
- **You don't have opinions before you ask Claude.** If your first instinct when you hear about a policy or event is to ask Claude rather than think, you've outsourced your intellectual first response.
- **Your writing sounds like Claude.** If a friend or supervisor says your writing has changed — become smoother but less distinctively *you* — you may be accepting Claude's phrasing too readily.
- **You find sustained reading harder than before.** If reading a long paper now feels more uncomfortable than it used to, your tolerance for the friction of difficult reading may be decreasing.

None of these are irreversible. The remedy is simple: impose the "you first" rule more consistently for a few weeks.

## Part 4 — The "you first" rule in practice

A single rule covers most of the ground above:

> **Before asking Claude anything, take two minutes and form your own view, draft, or question first. Write it down. Then bring it to Claude.**

This applies to:
- Starting a paper → write your thesis in one sentence first
- Reading a source → read it before asking for a summary
- News or events → form an opinion before asking for analysis
- An argument in an essay → write it yourself before asking for help
- Understanding a concept → try to explain it in your own words first

The two minutes spent forming your own thought make every subsequent Claude session more useful, because you're testing and extending your thinking rather than replacing it.

## Part 5 — What Claude is genuinely for

This guide is not anti-Claude. It's about channelling Claude correctly. The tasks where Claude adds the most value without any cognitive cost:

| Good use | Why it's safe |
|----------|---------------|
| Finding and retrieving sources | No thinking substituted — you evaluate them yourself |
| Formatting and citation checking | Mechanical, not intellectual |
| Translating a document you've already tried to read | The real learning already happened |
| Checking your argument for logical gaps | You wrote the argument first |
| Generating Anki cards from readings you've already done | The reading and synthesis was yours |
| Running the wiki: ingesting, indexing, cross-referencing | Administrative, not analytical |
| Testing your understanding by asking you questions | Reverses the dynamic — Claude quizzes, you answer |

The pattern: Claude handles the mechanical and administrative; you handle the analytical and generative.

---

## The one principle

**Do not use Claude to skip the friction.** Intellectual growth comes from the discomfort of not immediately understanding something, the struggle of putting a half-formed idea into words, the effort of holding two competing arguments in your head and judging between them. That friction is the point. Use Claude to amplify what you've already done, not to do the hard parts for you.
