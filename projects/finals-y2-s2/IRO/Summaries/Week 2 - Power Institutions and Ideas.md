# Week 2 — Power, Institutions, and Ideas: Traditional IR Theories

*The three traditional schools of IR theory — realism, liberalism, constructivism — what they assume, what they explain well, and how they read the same events (Ukraine-Russia, US-China, NATO) differently.*

## Lecture walkthrough

### The setup — three lenses on the same events

Denney opens with three recent geopolitical stories:
1. Trump threatens tariffs over Greenland (coercive diplomacy? Power politics? Institutional crisis?)
2. Western leaders visit Beijing amid US uncertainty (balancing? Interdependence? Identity shift?)
3. NATO sets 5% GDP defense benchmark as US deprioritizes Europe (burden-sharing? Institutional adaptation? Evolving security identities?)

**Goal:** by the end, you should be able to explain each event through realist, liberal, AND constructivist lenses.

### Realism

**Core assumptions:**
- **Power and self-interest** drive global politics.
- **Anarchy** — no higher authority; states must self-help.
- **Power politics** — states act primarily in pursuit of power.
- States prioritize **survival and security** above all.
- Not all realists agree on the details — multiple sub-traditions.

**Classical realism (Morgenthau, Carr):**
- Power politics rooted in pessimistic view of HUMAN NATURE.
- "The social world is but a projection of human nature onto the collective plane" (Morgenthau, 1962).
- Human egoism → state egoism. Conflict is enduring.

**Neorealism (Waltz, 1979):**
- State behaviour shaped by the STRUCTURE of the international system, not human nature.
- Anarchy + distribution of capabilities = the key explanatory variables.
- Different sub-variants disagree on HOW states respond to structural pressure.

**Offensive realism (Mearsheimer):**
- Anarchy + uncertainty → states seek to MAXIMIZE relative power.
- Great powers aim for *regional hegemony* (the highest feasible goal).
- Non-great powers face strong constraints; they often bandwagon with dominant powers or pursue opportunistic gains.
- *Example:* the rise of China as a challenge to US primacy in East Asia.

**Defensive realism (Waltz, Walt):**
- States are SECURITY maximizers, not power maximizers.
- Excessive power accumulation → counter-balancing → reduces your security.
- States favour BALANCING: internal (build capabilities) or external (alliances), plus restraint to avoid provoking counter-balancing.
- *Example:* states accommodating US power after the Cold War.

**Neoclassical realism (Rose, Schweller, Zakaria, Sterling-Folker):**
- Structure matters BUT state behaviour is also conditioned by DOMESTIC factors: state capacity, ideology, leadership.
- *Example:* Nazi Germany pursued expansion not solely due to systemic pressures but because of domestic ideology + leadership ambitions.

### Liberalism

**Core assumptions:**
- States can rationally COOPERATE under anarchy.
- War is possible but NOT inevitable.
- INSTITUTIONS, INTERDEPENDENCE, and DOMESTIC POLITICS matter alongside power.
- **Progress is possible** — international politics can become more cooperative over time.

**Key contrast with realism:** liberals argue that realists underestimate how institutions, interdependence, and domestic politics interact with anarchy.

**Three major variants:**

| Variant | Core mechanism | Thinkers |
|---|---|---|
| **Interdependence liberalism** | Economic exchange creates mutual dependence; conflict gets costlier | Ricardo, Cobden, Bright |
| **Republican / democratic liberalism** | Domestic institutions constrain leaders; democracies resolve disputes peacefully with each other | Kant, Doyle |
| **Neoliberal institutionalism** | Institutions reduce uncertainty, info problems, transaction costs → enable cooperation | Keohane, Nye |

**The Liberal International Order (LIO):**

The LIO is **not a separate variant** of liberalism — it's a *theory of international order* explaining the post-1945 US-led system. It combines all three liberal variants:
- From neoliberal institutionalism: rules + institutions stabilise cooperation.
- From republican liberalism: liberal states are more rule-bound and legitimate.
- From interdependence liberalism: open markets raise the cost of conflict.

**What the LIO adds:** explains why leading states (especially the US) ACCEPT rules and constraints. Order depends on **legitimacy + restraint**, not force alone. (Ikenberry, Deudney, Lissner.)

### Constructivism

**Core assumptions:**
- **Social construction of reality:** states act not on material power alone but on shared ideas and identities.
- **Identity matters:** state interests are shaped by historical, social, political contexts.
- **Norms and ideas shape behaviour** as much as material capabilities.
- **Anarchy is not fixed:** the system is shaped by how states interact and interpret their relationships.

**Key contrast with realism + liberalism:** both treat state interests as given. Constructivists ask: ==**where do those interests come from?**==

**Wendt's "Anarchy is what states make of it" (1992):**

Wendt's three cultures of anarchy:

| Culture | Logic | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Hobbesian** | States as existential threats; kill or be killed | Early 20th century Europe |
| **Lockean** | States as rivals who recognise sovereignty | Cold War (1947–1991) |
| **Kantian** | States as partners with shared norms | EU and NATO |

Which culture characterises US-China today? US-Europe? Russia-NATO? The answer matters — and it's contestable, which is exactly the constructivist point.

**Norms, identity, and discourse (Finnemore, Sikkink, Onuf):** shared norms (human rights, sovereignty, R2P) shape what states *can* do legitimately, even when material power would allow otherwise.

### The trio compared

| | **Realism** | **Liberalism** | **Constructivism** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Philosophical roots** | Thucydides, Machiavelli, Hobbes | Locke, Kant, Smith | — (post-positivist) |
| **View of anarchy** | Inherent; leads to conflict | Mitigated by institutions | Socially constructed |
| **State interests** | Fixed: power + security | Fixed: cooperation | Shaped by identity |
| **Key actors** | States | States + institutions | States + norms |
| **Epistemology** | Positivist | Positivist | Post-positivist |

### Applying the theories — Mearsheimer's "Bound to Fail"

Mearsheimer (2019) — a leading realist's critique of the LIO:

**The argument:**
- After the Cold War, the US built a liberal international order premised on spreading democracy, open markets, strong institutions.
- The LIO required ==**liberal hegemony**== — US dominance to enforce liberal norms.
- This provoked **nationalist backlash** and **great-power resistance**.
- It led to **costly interventions** (Iraq, Libya).

**The implication:**
- The LIO was a product of UNIPOLARITY that cannot survive the return of great-power competition.
- This is a *realist critique of liberalism*.

**Responses:**

*Liberal response:*
- Institutions are RESILIENT — they have adapted before and can again.
- Economic interdependence creates strong incentives against conflict.
- The LIO's problems were about IMPLEMENTATION, not inherent flaws.

*Constructivist response:*
- The "failure" narrative is itself a product of REALIST FRAMING.
- Norms of human rights, sovereignty, multilateralism are deeply embedded.
- Whether the order "fails" depends on HOW states choose to act.

**Connection to Wendt:** if anarchy is what states make of it, then the collapse of the liberal order is NOT inevitable — it is a choice shaped by discourse and identity.

### Ukraine-Russia through three lenses

| Lens | What it sees |
|---|---|
| **Realist** | Security dilemma: Russia perceives NATO expansion as intolerable threat. Ukraine = strategic buffer. Western miscalculation about Russian red lines. |
| **Constructivist** | Russia's actions shaped by IDENTITY: imperial narrative, great-power self-image. Framing of Ukraine as historically "Russian." Western engagement rooted in norms of sovereignty and self-determination. |
| **Liberal** | Failure of institutions (UN veto) BUT evidence that economic sanctions can impose real costs — interdependence as leverage. |

### Summary — what each theory does best (and worst)

| Theory | Explains well | Struggles with |
|---|---|---|
| **Realism** | Power competition, security dilemmas, alliance formation | Cooperation, norm change, why democracies rarely fight each other |
| **Liberalism** | Institutional cooperation, trade peace, democratic peace | Why institutions sometimes fail, why liberal orders collapse |
| **Constructivism** | Identity politics, norm diffusion, how perceptions shape conflict | Predicting specific outcomes, material constraints on state action |

**Knowledge-check highlights (correct-answer rates):**

| Q | Concept | Correct |
|---|---|---|
| Realism: international politics driven by ... | Power and pursuit of national interest | **85%** |
| Classical realism (Morgenthau): drive for power stems from ... | Human nature | 55% |
| Neorealism (Waltz): state behaviour shaped by ... | Anarchic structure of the international system | 62% |
| Key difference realism vs liberalism | Realists see cooperation as difficult; liberals say institutions and interdependence make it possible | **83%** |
| Positivist epistemology means ... | World can be studied objectively; patterns observable and measurable | 61% |
| Core concern of liberalism | Role of institutions and economic interdependence in promoting cooperation | **78%** |
| Core concern of constructivism | How ideas, identities, and norms shape state behaviour | 72% |
| Wendt's "anarchy is what states make of it" means ... | Consequences of anarchy depend on how states interact and perceive each other | **78%** |
| Mearsheimer (2019): the liberal international order ... | Was destined to fail because it required liberal hegemony, provoking nationalist backlash | 74% |

*The weakest answer was on classical realism's human-nature root (only 55%) — worth nailing this for the exam.*

**The framing experiment — does discourse shape perceptions?**

Each respondent was randomly assigned one of three short analyst briefs about US-China relations before answering 5 Likert items about likelihood of cooperation:

- **Hobbesian brief** (~33%): emphasised rivalry, military competition, historical precedent for great-power conflict.
- **Lockean brief** (~33%): emphasised diplomatic channels, economic interdependence, cooperation.
- **Control** (~33%): no brief — straight to questions.

**Cooperation Perception Score** (1 = least cooperative, 5 = most cooperative):

| Condition | Mean | N |
|---|---|---|
| Lockean | **2.77** | 55 |
| Control | 2.73 | 43 |
| Hobbesian | **2.65** | 45 |

*Direction matches the constructivist prediction* (Lockean framing → higher cooperation perception; Hobbesian → lower) but the Hobbesian-vs-Lockean difference did NOT reach statistical significance after Bonferroni correction (p = 0.71, Cohen's d = 0.24, small effect).

**The lecture's takeaway for the exam:** even with weak statistical effects, the *direction* is consistent with Wendt — a short paragraph can shift how people perceive the likelihood of cooperation between two states. That's the constructivist insight in miniature: *"if anarchy is what states make of it,"* discourse and framing are doing real work in international politics. Denney's framing for class discussion: *"Is anarchy really what states make of it?"*

## Key thinker — Alexander Wendt

American IR theorist (Ohio State), the canonical constructivist. His 1992 *International Organization* article *"Anarchy Is What States Make of It: The Social Construction of Power Politics"* is the founding text of mainstream constructivism in IR.

**His central move:** challenge the assumption (shared by realists and liberals) that anarchy has a *fixed* meaning. Anarchy is a SOCIAL relationship whose content is shaped by how states interact and interpret each other. The same anarchy can produce Hobbesian, Lockean, or Kantian cultures — depending on identity and practice.

**Why this matters:** if interests are socially constructed (not given by material power), then *politics is possible*. Realism's pessimism (conflict is enduring) and liberalism's optimism (institutions will solve it) both presume too much about what states "must" do. Wendt opens space for AGENCY — and for the role of ideas, norms, and identity in international politics.

Wendt's later work (*Social Theory of International Politics*, 1999) further developed this into a comprehensive constructivist theory. He has since moved toward ever more philosophical territory (quantum mind, panpsychism) that the mainstream IR field has been less sure what to do with.

*(John Mearsheimer: University of Chicago. Leading offensive realist. The Tragedy of Great Power Politics (2001) is the canonical statement. His "Bound to Fail" (2019) extends the argument to a realist obituary for the liberal international order.)*

---

> **Explained easily — the three traditions in one paragraph each:**
> **REALISM:** the world is a dark forest. Every state is a hunter. There's no police, so you'd better be the strongest or have allies who are. Power and survival are everything; ideas and institutions are mostly decoration.
>
> **LIBERALISM:** the world is a marketplace. States that trade together, share institutions, and run democratic governments don't shoot each other — too much to lose. Build institutions, spread democracy, increase economic ties, and war becomes irrational.
>
> **CONSTRUCTIVISM:** the world is a story we keep retelling. There's no "fixed" anarchy or "fixed" interests — there's whatever story states have agreed (often without realising it) to live inside. NATO members don't fear each other because they've built an identity where war is unthinkable. The story can change.

> **Explained easily — Wendt's three cultures of anarchy:**
> Picture a high-school cafeteria. Same building, same rules, but three possible cultures:
> - **Hobbesian:** Mean Girls. Everyone's a potential enemy, you watch your back, betrayals are normal.
> - **Lockean:** sports rivalries. You compete fiercely, but you respect that the other team has the right to exist; you don't bring weapons.
> - **Kantian:** close-knit study group. You actively help each other; "fighting" isn't even an option people consider.
> Same anarchy (no teacher running things), three completely different politics. Wendt: that's the international system too. NATO is Kantian, US-China is Lockean drifting toward Hobbesian, Russia-NATO is Hobbesian.

> **Explained easily — Mearsheimer's "Bound to Fail":**
> Mearsheimer says: the post-1991 US-led liberal order only worked because the US was the only game in town. The moment China and Russia recovered enough to push back, the order was always going to crack. Trying to spread democracy by force (Iraq, Libya) wasn't a deviation from the LIO — it was inscribed in its DNA. So we're not in a temporary crisis; we're watching the structural collapse of a unipolar arrangement. (Liberals respond: the order has reformed before; institutions are sticky. Constructivists respond: "failure" is itself a story you're choosing to tell.)

> **Explained easily — applying all three to Ukraine:**
> A REALIST sees a security dilemma: NATO crept east, Russia hit back. A LIBERAL sees a failure of institutions (the UN couldn't stop it) but also a vindication of sanctions and interdependence as leverage. A CONSTRUCTIVIST sees an identity story: Putin reaching for "Holy Russia," Ukrainians reaching for "Europe," with the war as a contest over whose narrative defines the region. None is wrong; all illuminate different parts of the same event.

### Multiple Choice Questions

**Q1.** Which of the following is the central claim of offensive realism (Mearsheimer)?
- A) States seek to maximise security and prefer restraint over expansion
- B) Anarchy + uncertainty about intentions incentivize states to maximise relative power; great powers seek regional hegemony
- C) State behaviour is shaped by domestic ideology and leadership perceptions
- D) Power politics is rooted in a pessimistic view of human nature

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B) Anarchy + uncertainty about intentions incentivize states to maximise relative power; great powers seek regional hegemony**

Option A is defensive realism (Waltz, Walt). Option C is neoclassical realism (Rose, Schweller). Option D is classical realism (Morgenthau, Carr). Each is a distinct realist sub-tradition.

- A) Was a successful adaptation of liberal norms to a multipolar world

### Short Essay / Open Questions

**Essay Q1.** Apply realism, liberalism, and constructivism to ONE contemporary case (Ukraine-Russia, US-China rivalry, or NATO's burden-sharing debate). Which lens best captures the case?

<details>
<summary>Model answer outline</summary>

**Key structure:**
- Pick the case and set up the empirical puzzle (2 sentences).
- Apply each lens:
  - **Realist reading:** What does power, security, and the structure of anarchy explain here?
  - **Liberal reading:** What do institutions, interdependence, and domestic politics explain?
  - **Constructivist reading:** What do shared ideas, identities, and norms explain?
- Discuss what each lens MISSES.
- Take a position on which best captures the case (or argue that combination is needed).

**Worked example — Ukraine-Russia:**
- Realist: security dilemma (NATO expansion → Russian counter-response); Ukraine as buffer; offensive realist logic of regional hegemony.
- Liberal: institutional failure (UN veto blocks SC action); BUT sanctions show interdependence-as-leverage; democratic peace logic struggles with how Russia (an electoral authoritarianism) makes decisions.
- Constructivist: identity is doing massive explanatory work — Putin's "Holy Russia" / Slavic civilisational rhetoric; Ukraine's "European" identity (Onuch 2023, Kulyk 2023 showed civic rather than ethnic mobilisation). Wendt would say: this is a HOBBESIAN re-construction of what could have been a LOCKEAN relationship.
- Verdict: constructivism captures the *content* (what Russia and Ukraine think they're fighting for); realism captures the *form* (security dilemma, alliance dynamics); liberalism captures the *consequences* (sanctions as leverage). All three needed.

**Common mistake:** Treating the theories as predicting different *facts*. They often agree on facts; what differs is the *causal story*.

</details>

---

**Essay Q2.** Is the Liberal International Order "bound to fail" (Mearsheimer) or capable of adaptation? Discuss using ALL THREE traditions.

<details>
<summary>Model answer outline</summary>

**Key structure:**
- State Mearsheimer's argument: LIO required US liberal hegemony → provoked backlash + costly interventions → cannot survive return of multipolar great-power competition.
- **Realist support (Mearsheimer):** LIO depended on unipolarity. The 2003 Iraq war and 2011 Libya intervention are *features*, not bugs — they were what liberal hegemony required, and they produced the backlash. The rise of China and re-empowerment of Russia removes the structural foundation.
- **Liberal counter (Ikenberry, Deudney, Keohane):** institutions have adapted before (Bretton Woods → floating exchange rates; GATT → WTO; EU through multiple crises). Interdependence creates strong incentives against conflict (US-China trade is mutually destructive to sever). The LIO's problems are about IMPLEMENTATION not inherent design. The order can evolve toward more inclusive forms.
- **Constructivist counter (Wendt, Finnemore):** "failure" is a NARRATIVE that itself shapes outcomes — if states (and analysts) keep telling the failure story, they make it self-fulfilling. Norms of sovereignty, human rights, multilateralism are deeply institutionalised; even violators feel the need to justify themselves in those terms. Whether the order persists depends on what states CHOOSE to do.
- **Synthesis:** Mearsheimer captures the structural pressure honestly — unipolarity IS ending. But liberalism is right that the institutional architecture is sticky, and constructivism is right that the order's fate depends on identity / discourse / choice. The order is in *partial rupture* (Carney 2026) but not collapse.
- **Bridge to Acharya's multiplex world (Week 1):** the third option — diverse coexistence beyond a single dominant order — may be where the structural pressure plus institutional stickiness leads.

**Common mistake:** Treating Mearsheimer as making an *empirical prediction* about a date by which the LIO will collapse. His argument is structural — about what FORCES the order can or cannot withstand.

</details>
