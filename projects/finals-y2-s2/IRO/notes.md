The two tricks to remember:
- **Classical realism = HUMAN NATURE (Morgenthau)**. Easy to confuse with neorealism. Classical = pessimistic view of humans → state egoism. Neorealism = STRUCTURE / ANARCHY (Waltz) drives behaviour, regardless of human nature.
- **Positivism = objective study, observable patterns.** Constructivism is **post-positivist** (knowledge is socially constructed). Don't pick "knowledge is socially constructed" for positivism even though it sounds intellectually sophisticated — that's the constructivist answer.

### Bonus: the framing experiment as exam material

Denney's class also ran a **constructivism-in-action experiment**: students were randomly shown a Hobbesian, Lockean, or Control brief about US–China relations before being asked how much cooperation they expected. The Lockean group rated cooperation highest (M = 2.77), the Hobbesian group lowest (M = 2.65). The effect was directional but not statistically significant after Bonferroni correction (p = 0.71, Cohen's d = 0.24, "small").

Why this could be a short-answer or MCQ: it is the perfect illustration of Wendt's claim. The key question Denney posed:

> *If a short paragraph can shift how you perceive the likelihood of cooperation between two states, what does that tell us about the role of discourse and framing in international relations? Is anarchy really what states make of it?*

Expected answer pattern: yes, framing matters → consistent with constructivism → but the effect was small and noisy → suggesting material constraints (realism) and institutions (liberalism) also matter → mature analysts use all three lenses.

## Week 1 (Wolff) — Old Debates in a New World

### The big question

After the Cold War ended in 1991, three thinkers tried to predict the trajectory of global politics. They disagreed sharply. Week 1 is about whether any of them got it right.

### IR vs. International Studies — a likely MCQ

| | International Relations | International Studies |
|---|---|---|
| Focus | Interstate relations, the international system | Global issues, cultural/social dimensions |
| Themes | Power, security, war | Globalization, development, human rights, migration, environment |
| Theory | Realism, liberalism, constructivism | Critical theory, post-colonial, cultural/identity, feminist/queer |
| Units | Mainly states + state actors + IOs | Wide range of actors |
| Roots | Political science, economics | Interdisciplinary |

### The three trajectories

#### 1. Fukuyama — *The End of History?* (1989)

- **Thesis:** Liberal democracy is the final stage of human ideological evolution. Fascism was defeated in 1945; communism in 1991. Liberalism is the last ideology standing, and all states will eventually gravitate toward it.
- **Implication for IR theory:** Revives **democratic peace theory** (democracies don't fight each other), **liberal institutionalism**, **economic interdependence**.
- **Image of the post-Cold War world:** Convergence toward liberal democracy. American-led order continues.
- **Theoretical affinity:** Liberalism / Hegelian idealism.
- **Main critique:** Underestimates nationalism, religion, and power. Also: the 2008 financial crisis, the rise of China, and the 2016 populist wave (Brexit, Trump) all challenge the convergence thesis.

soo, all counties are bound to become liberal democracies as autocratic regimes have been defeated

#### 2. Huntington — *The Clash of Civilizations?* (1993)

- **Thesis:** Future conflict will be driven not by ideology or economics but by **culture and civilization**. Identity becomes the main fault line.
- **The 7/8 civilizations** (textbook p. 267): Western, Confucian (Sinic), Japanese, Islamic, Hindu, Slavic-Orthodox, Latin American, and "possibly" African.
- **Image of the post-Cold War world:** Fragmentation into civilizational blocs.
- **Theoretical affinity:** Constructivism (culturalist) with realist undertones — culture as the source of state interests.
- **Main critique:**
  - **Intra-civilizational conflict** is everywhere (Sunni–Shia, Russia–Ukraine, intra-"Confucian" tensions) — undermines the inter-civilizational clash thesis
  - **Essentializes and racializes** entire populations
  - **Self-fulfilling prophecy** — if Western policymakers act as if Islam is the enemy, they help create that enemy
  - Counter-example: strong US–India strategic ties despite different civilizational labels (Acharya 2025: 360)

conflict might occur due to clash of civilizations not due to economic and ideological reasons

#### 3. Kagan — *The End of Dreams, Return of History* (2007, Policy Review)

- **Thesis:** The post-Cold War "end of history" optimism was an illusion. **Traditional great-power competition is back.** Ideology is back too — but as **liberalism vs. autocracy** (US/EU vs. China/Russia), not as Huntington's culture clash.
- **Image of the post-Cold War world:** Return of great-power competition; liberal order vs. revisionist powers.
- **Three claims that may need to be balanced on the exam:**
  1. Traditional power struggles between nations, driven by ambition and competition, still shape global politics
  2. The ideological struggle between liberalism and autocracy has (re)emerged
  3. But great powers also lack any common political principles beyond *power itself* — even tensions inside the "democratic camp" (US–EU) and inside the "authoritarian camp" (China–Russia) may matter as much as those across the divide (textbook p. 342)
- **Theoretical affinity:** Realism / Neoconservatism.
- **Role of the West:** Order-enforcer, especially the US.
- **Main critique:** Normalizes militarism; underplays norms; risks self-fulfilling prophecy about US–China rivalry.


critique of end of history and clash of civilization, soo liberalists vs autocracies and multifaceted reasoning behind conflict 

#### Comparison table (memorise this — perfect MCQ material)

| | **Fukuyama** | **Huntington** | **Kagan** |
|---|---|---|---|
| Main driver | Ideology & political systems | Culture & civilization | Power, force, geopolitical rivalry |
| Post-Cold War image | Convergence to liberal democracy | Fragmentation into civilizational blocs | Return of great-power competition |
| Main argument | Liberal democracy vs residual authoritarianism | West vs Islam/Sinic/etc. | Liberal order vs revisionist powers |
| Role of the West | Vanguard of human development | One civilization among others | Order-enforcer (esp. US) |
| Theoretical affinity | Liberalism / Hegelian idealism | Constructivism (culturalist) + realist undertones | Realism / Neoconservatism |
| Main critique | Underestimates nationalism, religion, power | Essentializes cultures; self-fulfilling prophecy | Normalizes militarism; underplays norms |

#### 4. Acharya — *Beyond the West* / multiplex world

A counter to all three:
- Future world order won't be dominated by one nation or civilization
- Neither West nor East will dictate
- A **multiplex world**: "where there are multiple shows on offer, giving the audience a choice of producers, directors, actors and plots" (2025, 19). Like a multiplex cinema, not a single screen.
- Implication: more cultural and political diversity, new forms of connectedness, but stability still possible.

### Current-events anchors Wolff used in lecture

These showed up to motivate the theoretical material and could appear as application examples:
- **V-Dem 2025:** 72% of the world lives in autocracies; democracy for the average citizen is back to 1985 levels; 88 democracies vs 91 autocracies (full reversal from year before); liberal democracies are now the rarest regime type (29 in 2024)
- **The end of arms control:** New START expiring; US/Russia/China all projected to grow nuclear arsenals to 2035
- **Carney's Davos speech (Jan 2026):** "We are in the midst of a rupture, not a transition." Hegemons can't keep "monetizing relationships"; middle powers must hedge; "if we're not at the table, we're on the menu."

## Week 2 (Denney) — Power, Institutions, and Ideas: Traditional IR Theories

### Realism

**Core assumptions** (the "atoms" of realism):
- Global politics is fundamentally about **power and self-interest**
- **Anarchy**: no higher authority above states → **self-help**
- Humans (and states) are inherently selfish/competitive (**egoism**)
- States prioritise **survival and security**
- **Realpolitik / power politics**

**Four variants — know who said what:**

| Variant | Key feature | Major thinkers |
|---------|-------------|----------------|
| **Classical realism** | Power politics rooted in **human nature** (egoism, fear, ambition). "The social world is but a projection of human nature onto the collective plane" (Morgenthau 1962) | E.H. Carr, Hans Morgenthau |
| **Defensive realism** | States are **security maximizers**, not power maximizers. Overexpansion provokes counter-balancing. Favours balancing strategies (internal: capabilities; external: alliances; restraint) | Kenneth Waltz, Stephen Walt |
| **Offensive realism** | States are **power maximizers**. Great powers seek **regional hegemony**. Smaller states bandwagon or grab limited gains | John Mearsheimer |
| **Neoclassical realism** | Structure matters BUT domestic factors mediate it (state capacity, ideology, leader perceptions). Nazi Germany's expansion = systemic pressure + ideology + Hitler's ambition | Gideon Rose, Randall Schweller, Fareed Zakaria, Keren Yarhi-Milo, Jennifer Sterling-Folker |

> **Confusion trap (Q6.3 vs Q6.4):** Classical = HUMAN NATURE. Neorealism (Waltz) = STRUCTURE / ANARCHY. They are *different* answers on two different questions. Both can appear on the same exam paper. Don't merge them.

### Liberalism

**Core assumptions:**
- States can **rationally cooperate** under anarchy
- **Conflict is not inevitable**
- **Institutions and norms** shape behaviour
- **Domestic politics matter** (regime type, societal preferences)
- **Progress is possible** over time
- Key contrast with realism: liberals say realists underestimate how institutions, interdependence, and domestic politics interact with anarchy

**Variants:**

| Variant | Key claim | Major thinkers |
|---------|-----------|----------------|
| **Interdependence liberalism** | Economic ties raise the costs of conflict | David Ricardo, Richard Cobden, John Bright |
| **Republican (democratic) liberalism** | Democracies don't fight each other (democratic peace) | Immanuel Kant, Michael Doyle |
| **Neoliberal institutionalism** | Institutions enable cooperation by reducing uncertainty, information problems, transaction costs (focus on **absolute gains**) | Robert Keohane, Joseph Nye |
| **Liberal International Order (LIO)** | Not really a separate variant — a *theory of order*. Post-1945 rule-based system sustained by US hegemony + institutions + legitimacy + open markets + hegemonic self-restraint | John Ikenberry, Daniel Deudney, Rebecca Lissner |

### Constructivism

**Core assumptions:**
- **Social construction of reality**: states act on shared ideas and identities, not on material power alone
- **Identity matters**: state interests are shaped by historical, social, political context
- **Norms and ideas** shape behaviour as much as material capabilities
- **Anarchy is not fixed** — the system depends on how states interact and interpret each other

**Key contrast with realism + liberalism:** Realists and liberals take state interests as *given*. Constructivists ask: **where do those interests come from?**

| Concept | Key feature | Major thinkers |
|---------|-------------|----------------|
| Social construction | Identities and interests are socially produced | Alexander Wendt, Nicholas Onuf |
| Norms & ideas | Shared norms and discourse shape behaviour | Martha Finnemore, Kathryn Sikkink |
| Cultures of anarchy | Anarchy → conflict, competition, or cooperation, depending on perceptions | Alexander Wendt |

#### Wendt (1992) — "Anarchy is what states make of it"

This is *the* constructivist text and almost certainly an exam item. Memorise the three **cultures of anarchy**:

| Culture | States as… | Historical example |
|---------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Hobbesian** (conflictual) | Existential threats to each other | Early 20th-century Europe |
| **Lockean** (competitive) | Rivals who recognise each other's sovereignty | The Cold War (1947–1991) |
| **Kantian** (cooperative) | Partners with shared norms | The EU and NATO |

Discussion prompt Denney used: which culture characterises US–China relations today? US–Europe?

#### Mearsheimer (2019) — "Bound to Fail"

A realist critique of the LIO, very likely to appear as MCQ + short answer.

**Argument:**
- The LIO required **liberal hegemony** — US dominance to enforce liberal norms
- It provoked **nationalist backlash** (in target states) and **great-power resistance** (China, Russia)
- It led to costly interventions (Iraq, Libya)
- It was a **product of unipolarity**, not a self-sustaining order

**Implication:** LIO could not survive the return of great-power competition.

**Liberal response:** institutions are resilient; interdependence raises conflict costs; the problems were about implementation, not design.

**Constructivist response:** the "failure" narrative is itself a product of realist framing; norms of human rights, sovereignty, multilateralism are deeply embedded; whether the order "fails" depends on how states *choose* to act (this is Wendt all over again).

### The trio side-by-side (high-yield comparison)

| | **Realism** | **Liberalism** | **Constructivism** |
|---|---|---|---|
| Philosophical roots | Thucydides, Machiavelli, Hobbes | Locke, Kant, Smith | — |
| View of anarchy | Inherent; leads to conflict | Mitigated by institutions | Socially constructed |
| State interests | Fixed: power & security | Fixed: cooperation | **Shaped by identity** |
| Key actors | States | States + institutions | States + norms |
| Epistemology | **Positivist** | **Positivist** | **Post-positivist** |

### What each theory explains well — and struggles with (for short-answer "assess" sections)

| Theory | Explains well | Struggles with |
|--------|---------------|----------------|
| Realism | Power competition, security dilemmas, alliance formation | Cooperation, norm change, why democracies rarely fight each other |
| Liberalism | Institutional cooperation, trade peace, democratic peace | Why institutions sometimes fail, why liberal orders collapse |
| Constructivism | Identity politics, norm diffusion, how perceptions shape conflict | Predicting specific outcomes, material constraints on state action |

### Applying the trio — Denney's worked example: Russia–Ukraine

| Lens | Reading of the war |
|------|---------------------|
| **Realist** | Security dilemma. Russia perceives NATO expansion as intolerable threat. Ukraine is a buffer zone. Western miscalculation about Russian red lines. |
| **Liberal** | Failure of institutions (UN veto) — *but* sanctions show economic interdependence cuts both ways and can be used as leverage |
| **Constructivist** | Russia's actions shaped by identity (imperial narrative, great-power self-image). Framing of Ukraine as historically "Russian." Western engagement rooted in norms of sovereignty and self-determination |

You should be able to do the same three-lens reading for any major contemporary case (US-China rivalry, Trump tariffs over Greenland, NATO 5% GDP defense benchmark).

## Flashcard set — Weeks 1–2 glossary terms

From the official glossary (Foundations of IR theory section). Cover the right column; recall from the left.

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Realism | School of IR holding that international politics is driven by power, state self-interest, and competition under anarchy |
| Classical realism | Realism rooted in pessimistic view of **human nature**; Morgenthau, E.H. Carr |
| Structural realism (neorealism) | Realism rooted in the **structure of the international system** (anarchy); Waltz |
| Offensive realism | Neorealism in which great powers maximize relative power and seek regional hegemony; Mearsheimer |
| Defensive realism | Neorealism in which states maximize security and prefer balancing/restraint; Waltz, Walt |
| Neoclassical realism | Structure + domestic factors (state capacity, ideology, leader perception); Rose, Schweller, Zakaria |
| Anarchy | Absence of a higher authority above states in the international system |
| Self-help | The logic that under anarchy each state must provide for its own security |
| Balance of power | Distribution of capabilities such that no single state can dominate; can be a deliberate strategy or systemic outcome |
| Relative vs absolute gains | Realists prioritise relative (am I gaining more than my rival?); liberals tolerate absolute (am I gaining at all?) |
| Security dilemma | Defensive measures by one state appear threatening to others, prompting counter-measures and reducing everyone's security |
| Liberalism | School of IR holding that cooperation under anarchy is possible via institutions, interdependence, and shared norms |
| Interdependence liberalism | Economic ties raise the costs of conflict |
| Complex interdependence | Keohane & Nye: multiple channels link societies; military force is less useful; no clear hierarchy of issues |
| Democratic peace theory | Democracies rarely or never fight each other |
| Neoliberal institutionalism | Institutions reduce uncertainty/transaction costs and enable cooperation under anarchy; Keohane |
| Liberal international order (LIO) | Post-1945 US-led rule-based system: open markets + multilateral institutions + liberal norms + US hegemony with self-restraint |
| Liberal hegemony | The U.S. strategy of using its dominant position to spread liberal democracy and markets; Mearsheimer says this provokes backlash |
| Soft power | The ability to attract and persuade rather than coerce; Joseph Nye |
| Constructivism | School holding that state identities and interests are socially constructed, not given |
| Norms / norm cascade / norm entrepreneurs | A norm = standard of appropriate behaviour for actors with a given identity; norm cascade = rapid international diffusion; entrepreneurs = actors who promote new norms (Finnemore & Sikkink) |
| Cultures of anarchy (Hobbesian, Lockean, Kantian) | Wendt's three modes of state-to-state perception: existential threats / rivals recognising sovereignty / partners with shared norms |
| Positivism vs post-positivism | Positivism: the social world can be studied objectively with observable patterns. Post-positivism: knowledge is mediated by concepts, language, and power; constructivism is post-positivist |
| Multiplex world | Acharya's metaphor for the emerging order: multiple producers, scripts, audiences — not a single hegemon |
