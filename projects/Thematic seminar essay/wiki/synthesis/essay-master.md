## 1. Thesis & argument logic

### 1.1 Working thesis

> **Indonesia's carbon-pricing architecture is not a decarbonisation mechanism — it is credit-export infrastructure, engineered to generate revenue and market access for politically connected interests while ensuring the price signal never reaches the broader economy.**

**Softer fallback (Framing 1)** if you want to be less critical of Indonesian policy: *"ETS-first sequencing is best explained by political economy rather than economic-design optimality, and Indonesia's binding constraint going forward is implementation, not policy design."*

### 1.2 The puzzle in three sentences

1. **Fact.** Law 7/2021 legislated both a carbon tax and an ETS simultaneously. The ETS launched Feb 2023; the tax has been postponed four times and remains unenforced as of early 2026.
2. **Paradox.** The developing-country literature broadly recommends a carbon tax over an ETS (van den Bergh & Drews 2026; Jia & Lin 2020). Indonesia reversed this preference — and what it built barely functions: ~USD 5M total IDX Carbon transactions since 2023 launch (IEEFA 2025).
3. **Question.** Why this sequencing? How has the ETS actually performed? What does the literature say Indonesia should do next?

### 1.3 The five cross-reading patterns

These are the **load-bearing claims** — patterns where 5+ independent sources converge. Cherry-picked citations can be challenged; pattern citations cannot.

#### Pattern A — "Policy on paper, hollow in practice"

The strongest pattern. Indonesia's carbon-pricing architecture is *legislated but not implemented*.

- **ICAP 2025 factsheet** — Phase 1 launched, Phase 2 2025 allocation postponed; no auctions; carbon tax legislated 2021, still pending Jan 2026
- **Sunanda et al. 2025 §4.1** — "carbon tax remains at $2/tCO2 with limited coverage and delayed enforcement"
- **IEEFA 2025** — IDR ~1B total transactions in H1 2025
- **Ball 2018 (Foreign Affairs)** — global "carbon pricing is failing in practice" line
- **Asadnabizadeh & Moe 2024** — "persistent failure of implementation" applied globally
- **Finch & van den Bergh 2022** — advertised vs realised carbon prices diverge across 31 countries; Indonesia among the most extreme
- **Climate Action Tracker (Dec 2024)** — "Critically Insufficient" rating

**Use:** the implementation gap is *not* Indonesia-specific weakness — it is a generic feature of carbon pricing in developing economies. Lets you cite global evidence to interpret a local phenomenon.

#### Pattern B — "Public supports the tax; political coalitions resist it"

The consistent gap between elite-level resistance and public-opinion support.

- **Yusuf & Resosudarmo 2015** — carbon tax NOT regressive at national level in Indonesia; progressive among rural lower-income households
- **Rahma, Hartono & Hastuti 2025** — SAM analysis confirms: regressive only in urban areas
- **Advani et al. 2021** (cited in lit review) — African evidence: tax is progressive at low income in developing economies
- **East Asia Forum Feb 2025** — 70%+ Southeast Asian public support for national carbon tax (ADB 2024 survey); 93% willing to bear personal costs
- **Stevens [87] in Sunanda et al. 2025** — *"Political resistance and lack of coordination hinder carbon tax implementation, despite its potential"*
- **Sunanda et al. 2025 §4.1** — bibliometric finding: 65% of Indonesia carbon-pricing studies are theoretical; implementation literature is small and mostly political-economy

**Use:** load-bearing counter to "ETS is necessary because tax is politically impossible." Data shows the tax is NOT politically impossible at the population level — it's blocked at the *coalition* level. Sharpens any "should Indonesia move on the tax now?" argument.

#### Pattern C — "Industrial-policy entanglement"

Indonesia's carbon-pricing design is woven into **downstreaming** industrial policy (nickel, steel, EV battery supply chain, captive coal).

- **ICAP 2025** — ETS free allocation in Phase 2 covers nickel, steel, cement, captive coal; "energy-intensive and trade-exposed" is a direct policy choice
- **Edianto, Trencher & Matsubae 2022** — Indonesia case study in coal-finance flows; bilateral coal agreements (China/Japan) shape the energy mix
- **Ordonez, Fritz & Eckstein 2022** — $5/tCO2 already makes coal unviable in Indonesia, yet political economy keeps coal alive
- **Tony Blair Institute 2025** — Indonesia coal-retirement abatement = $12–13/tCO2; the flagship cheap-abatement opportunity *globally*. The tax-rate gap ($2 vs $12) is the political-economy gap
- **Dutu 2016** — Indonesia's energy-sector political economy (subsidies, PLN, fuel-tax structure)
- **OECD ECR 2025 (Indonesia Feb 2026)** — Indonesia's *net* effective carbon rate is likely **negative** once fuel subsidies are netted

**Use:** Indonesia is not "behind" on carbon pricing for lack of capability — it is *deliberately* designing a price below the cost of changing its industrial trajectory. The ETS is doing exactly what it was designed to do: generate compliance activity without disrupting the downstreaming agenda.

#### Pattern D — "Credit-export economy, not decarbonisation economy"

Developing carbon-pricing systems can become credit-export operations rather than domestic decarbonisation.

- **Jakob & Steckel 2014** — "climate finance curse" parallel to resource curse
- **Pierucci et al. 2025** — "modern colonialism in carbon markets"; emergy accounting (Brazil)
- **Roberts & Parks 2009** — "ecologically unequal exchange" theoretical foundation
- **Rizzi 2025** — "subtle green grabbing" in forest-carbon offsetting (Colombian Amazon; transferable to Indonesia REDD+)
- **ICAP 2025** — Article 6 moratorium lifted Jan 2025; ~1.78 MtCO2e PLN-affiliated credits already authorised. Government ambition of 13.4 Bt credit sales
- **Gatto & Sadik-Zada 2025** + **Nofyanza et al. 2025** — REDD+ in Indonesia (mixed-to-negative findings)
- **Kerr & Hu 2025** — private climate finance (steelman counter-source)

**Use:** the sharpest critical line. If Indonesia's binding interest is becoming a *credit seller* rather than a *carbon-emitter taxpayer*, then ETS-first sequencing is *coherent with the wrong objective*. Generalises beyond Indonesia.

#### Pattern E — "Hybrid (tax + ETS) outperforms either alone"

The modelling literature converges on a combination design — and Indonesia has *legislated* exactly that, but not implemented it.

- **Lin & Jia 2020** — China: tax + ETS combination outperforms either alone
- **Jia & Lin 2020** — same authors, different paper: tax alone outperforms ETS alone
- **Sunanda et al. 2025 §4.2** — explicit graduated-tax + staged-ETS recommendation for Indonesia
- **Tvinnereim & Mehling 2018** — pricing alone won't deliver deep decarbonisation
- **Fay et al. 2015 (World Bank)** — "necessary but not sufficient" frame
- **van den Bergh & Drews 2026** — developing-country review converges on hybrid + complementary instruments

**Use:** Indonesia's *legislated* design (cap-tax-and-trade under PR 110/2025) matches the literature recommendation. The gap is implementation, not design. Indonesia doesn't need to choose between tax and ETS — it needs to enforce the hybrid it already has.

> **⚠️ Author-order note:** Jia & Lin (2020) and Lin & Jia (2020) are **two different papers**. Citing them in the same sentence is fine; conflating them in the reference list is a marking-deduction mistake. See §8 and `essay-bibliography.md` for the distinction.

### 1.4 Three framing options

#### Framing 1 — "Political economy, not economic design" *(softer)*

> ETS-first sequencing is best explained by political economy (concentrated burden, free allocation, captive-revenue logic), not by economic-design optimality. Indonesia has already committed to the literature-recommended hybrid on paper; the binding constraint is implementation.

- **Strengths:** balanced, citable, fits the literature, avoids polemic.
- **Weaknesses:** soft conclusion that doesn't sharply explain *why* implementation stalled.

#### Framing 2 — "Credit-export economy, not decarbonisation economy" *(sharper — recommended)*

> Indonesia's carbon-pricing architecture is engineered to generate fiscal revenue and credit exports without delivering domestic decarbonisation. The ETS-first sequencing protects industrial coalitions through free allocation; the Article 6 moratorium lift turns potential emissions reductions into exportable credits; and the inactive carbon tax means there is no price signal on the bulk of the economy. Indonesia is simultaneously a global leader in carbon-pricing *coverage* and a laggard in carbon-pricing *effect*.

- **Strengths:** single load-bearing claim, sharp, generalisable, connects to Pattern D, clear "what to do" prescription.
- **Weaknesses:** more critical of Indonesian policy; requires engaging "but Indonesia needs the revenue" steelman.
- **Best source anchors:** Pierucci et al. 2025; Jakob & Steckel 2014; ICAP 2025; Edianto et al. 2022; Sunanda et al. 2025; OECD ECR 2025.

#### Framing 3 — "The implementation gap is the policy" *(most academically distinctive)*

> Indonesia's "policy on paper, hollow in practice" pattern is not an implementation failure — it IS the policy. The legislated-but-unenforced carbon tax functions as a *credible non-threat*: enough on paper to claim climate credentials internationally, enough non-enforcement to reassure domestic incumbents. ETS-first sequencing is not delay; it is the equilibrium.

- **Strengths:** most theoretically interesting; introduces a "deliberate-incoherence" reading; generalisable (resource-curse / institutional-economics literature).
- **Weaknesses:** requires more theoretical scaffolding (Acemoglu/Robinson; Pritchett-Woolcock-Andrews on isomorphic mimicry); risks reading cynical; harder to anchor in current bibliography.

### 1.5 Why Framing 2 (and how to use Framing 3)

**Use Framing 2 as the primary thesis.** Reserve Framing 3 for one paragraph in the conclusion as a forward-looking research agenda. Framing 1 is fine as a fallback but reads as too cautious for a 3–4k word seminar essay; the literature review already builds the political-economy frame — the essay should *advance* it, not restate it.

### 2.2 Paragraph skeleton (one topic sentence per paragraph)

#### INTRODUCTION (~400 words, 3 paragraphs)

1. **Hook [~160 words].** The COP29 story — Indonesia's Special Presidential Envoy announced 557 Mt of credits and attracted EUR 1.2 billion in green financing without disclosing his own family conglomerate's carbon concession — is not an isolated irony but the operating logic of Indonesia's carbon-pricing architecture. *(Tempo/Pulitzer "Forest for Tycoons" 2025; see [essay-evidence.md §2](essay-evidence.md#evidence-2-hashim-djojohadikusumo--arsari-group))*

2. **Puzzle [~120 words].** Law 7/2021 legislated both tax and ETS; ETS launched Feb 2023; carbon tax postponed four times; total ETS trades ~USD 5M — despite a developing-country literature that broadly recommends the tax first. *(ICAP 2025; IEEFA 2025; van den Bergh & Drews 2026)*

3. **Thesis + roadmap [~120 words].** Argument: ETS-first sequencing reflects deliberate political-economy choices (concentrated burden, free allocation for industrial-policy priorities, credit-export revenue motive), and PR 110/2025 already contains the hybrid the literature recommends — making political will the binding constraint, not policy design. *(Sunanda et al. 2025; ICAP 2025)*

#### SECTION 1 — Why an ETS first? (~800 words, 5 paragraphs)

1. **Sequencing facts [~150 words].** Law 7/2021 legislated both; ETS launched Feb 2023 (Phase 1: ~99 coal plants; Phase 2 from 2025: 563 installations); carbon tax due April 2022, postponed four times, unenforced as of early 2026. Indonesia has not simply chosen ETS over tax — it has implemented only the ETS side of a legislated hybrid. *(ICAP 2025; Sunanda et al. 2025 Table 4)*

2. **What the literature prefers — the puzzle [~150 words].** Carbon tax dominates the LMIC literature for administrative simplicity, price certainty, revenue-recycling potential; Indonesia-specific CGE shows a revenue-recycled tax raises GDP + employment. Yet Indonesia launched ETS first. *(van den Bergh & Drews 2026; Hartono et al. 2023; Jia & Lin 2020)*

3. **PE reason 1 — burden visibility [~150 words].** ETS concentrates costs on ~563 regulated installations (mostly state-owned PLN); a tax would visibly raise energy prices for 270M people. Industry representatives have explicitly cited "political resistance" as the barrier to tax enforcement — the ETS is the politically safer option for managing industrial coalitions. *(Sunanda et al. 2025 [Stevens §87]; Finon 2019)*

4. **PE reason 2 — free allocation + credit-export revenue [~200 words].** 100% free allocation in Phase 1 functions as de facto industrial subsidy for downstreaming sectors (nickel, steel, cement, captive coal). Article 6 moratorium lift (Jan 2025) creates credit-export revenue without a domestic tax. USD 65B government target from credits by 2028 confirms the revenue motive. PR 110/2025 decouples international credits from NDC timeline — regulatory architecture that makes this possible. *(ICAP 2025; [essay-evidence.md §2](essay-evidence.md#evidence-2-hashim-djojohadikusumo--arsari-group); Pierucci et al. 2025)*

5. **Counter-evidence — "tax is political poison" overstates the case [~150 words].** Carbon tax not regressive nationally in Indonesia — progressive among lower-income rural; 70%+ SEA respondents support a national carbon tax. Resistance is elite-level, not mass-level — a distinction that matters for §3. *(Yusuf & Resosudarmo 2015; Rahma et al. 2025; East Asia Forum Feb 2025)*

#### SECTION 2 — How the ETS actually works (~500 words, 3 paragraphs)

1. **Coverage and phases [~150 words].** Phase 1 (2023–24): ~99 PLN-grid coal plants; Phase 2 (2025–27): 563 installations + captive coal/gas; Phase 3 (2028–30): all fossil-fuel power. Even at full power-sector completion, ETS covers only ~21% of national emissions (225.3 Mt ETS vs 1,053.4 Mt national), leaving forestry, transport, most industry outside scope. *(ICAP 2025)*

2. **Allocation and Article 6 [~100 words].** No auctions in any phase; allocation still 75–100% free; PR 110/2025 decouples voluntary credit trading from NDC timeline; Corresponding Adjustments (double-counting prevention) made optional rather than mandatory — structurally prioritises credit-export revenue over domestic accountability. *(ICAP 2025; [essay-evidence.md §2](essay-evidence.md#evidence-2-hashim-djojohadikusumo--arsari-group))*

3. **The hollow reality [~200 words].** Total IDX Carbon transactions since Sept 2023 launch: ~USD 5M (IDR 78B). Against IDR 713.5T (~USD 44B) in 2024 fossil-fuel subsidies — ratio 1:8,000. Indonesia's net effective carbon rate is likely *negative* once subsidies are netted. Climate Action Tracker rates Indonesia "Critically Insufficient" (Dec 2024). *(IEEFA 2025; [essay-evidence.md §1](essay-evidence.md#evidence-1-iisd-gsi--indonesian-fossil-fuel-subsidies); OECD ECR 2025; CAT Dec 2024)*

%%I really like bringing the subsidy debate and the comparison - this is a unique, good angle to take!%%

#### SECTION 3 — What Indonesia should do now (~1,000 words, 6 paragraphs)

1. **Opening frame — "already legislated, not yet enforced" [~50 words].** PR 110/2025 and the October 2025 NDC already commit Indonesia to all five recommendations the LMIC literature makes — political will, not policy design, is the binding constraint. *(Sunanda et al. 2025; van den Bergh & Drews 2026)*

2. **Recommendation 1 — Enforce the levy; remove the subsidy that cancels it [~200 words].** Commit to a graduated levy escalation path (e.g. $2 now → $10 by 2028 → $30 by 2032). But because fossil-fuel subsidies create a net-negative effective carbon rate, the prior step is removing the IDR 58.5T coal price cap. Indonesia has precedent — Jokowi cut the petrol subsidy in 2014. *(Sunanda et al. 2025; OECD ECR 2025; [essay-evidence.md §1](essay-evidence.md#evidence-1-iisd-gsi--indonesian-fossil-fuel-subsidies))*

3. **Recommendation 2 — Revenue recycling via PKH [~150 words].** Indonesia-specific CGE: recycling tax revenue into household transfers raises GDP and employment. PKH conditional cash-transfer is the ready-made vehicle. Replacing the regressive 3-kg LPG subsidy with targeted cash transfers would simultaneously be more progressive and free fiscal space. *(Hartono et al. 2023; Goulder 1995; [essay-evidence.md §1](essay-evidence.md#evidence-1-iisd-gsi--indonesian-fossil-fuel-subsidies))*

4. **Recommendations 3–5 — Fix the ETS, expand to peatland, coordinate regionally [~100 words].** Before expanding to industry/forestry: auctions, a price floor, stable annual rules are prerequisites. Peatland restoration is Indonesia's cheapest large-scale abatement. ASEAN price coordination weakens the competitiveness-leakage argument. *(ICAP 2025; Tan et al. 2022; Andriansyah & Hong 2022)*

5. **The CBAM clincher — problem + fiscal perversity [~150 words].** With CBAM in definitive application from 1 Jan 2026, Indonesian steel (USD 726M to EU in 2023) and aluminium face near-full CBAM certificate cost (~€70–75/tCO2) because Indonesia's domestic price is ~$2/tCO2. Indonesia's "low price protects competitiveness" argument has *inverted*: by keeping the price near zero, Indonesia is handing carbon tax revenue to Brussels. *([essay-evidence.md §3](essay-evidence.md#evidence-3-eu-cbam-exposure-for-indonesia); ICAP 2025)*

6. **The CBAM prescription [~150 words].** Raising the domestic price to the TBI USD 12–13/tCO2 benchmark (the MAC for Indonesian coal retirement) would simultaneously reduce CBAM liability AND unlock Indonesia's cheapest domestic abatement — converting climate ethics into fiscal self-interest. *(Fursman, Dace & Macgeoch 2025 [TBI]; [essay-evidence.md §3](essay-evidence.md#evidence-3-eu-cbam-exposure-for-indonesia))*

#### CONCLUSION (~300 words, 3 paragraphs)

1. **Restate the thesis [~100 words].** ETS-first sequencing and perpetually deferred tax are deliberate political-economy choices — free allocation protects industrial coalitions, credit exports generate state revenue without a domestic tax, price signal calibrated below the threat threshold. The Hashim/Arsari conflict of interest is the personal face of a structural argument. *(Sunanda et al. 2025; ICAP 2025; [essay-evidence.md §2](essay-evidence.md#evidence-2-hashim-djojohadikusumo--arsari-group))*

2. **Constructive close [~100 words].** Indonesia already has the right hybrid design on paper — PR 110/2025 and the 2025 NDC contain the cap-tax-and-trade combination the literature confirms outperforms either instrument alone. The five §3 recommendations are not proposals; they are Indonesia's own stated commitments waiting for enforcement. *(Lin & Jia 2020; Sunanda et al. 2025)*

3. **Forward gesture [~100 words].** CBAM is the external lever that may finally align fiscal self-interest with domestic decarbonisation. Open question for future research: whether "policy on paper, hollow in practice" is a generalisable middle-income country pattern, and whether external mechanisms (CBAM, conditional climate finance, Article 6 corresponding-adjustment requirements) are the only durable way to shift this equilibrium. *([essay-evidence.md §3](essay-evidence.md#evidence-3-eu-cbam-exposure-for-indonesia); Jakob & Steckel 2014)*

## 3. Drafted introduction — ready to edit

*Use as a starting point. Edit freely — the language should sound like yours.*

---

In November 2024, Indonesia's Special Presidential Envoy for Climate and Energy stood at the COP29 pavilion in Baku and announced that the country had 557 million verified tonnes of CO₂ credits ready to sell. He attracted EUR 1.2 billion in green financing. What he did not mention was that his own family conglomerate holds a licensed carbon-trading concession in the very market he was designing. This is not an isolated irony — it is the operating logic of Indonesia's carbon-pricing architecture. Read carefully, the system is not a decarbonisation mechanism. It is a credit-export infrastructure: an ETS that runs free allowances to the politically connected industries that matter; a carbon tax legislated in 2021 and postponed every year since; and a voluntary market now formally decoupled from Indonesia's own climate targets so that credits can be sold internationally without counting against the national carbon budget. Indonesia has built a sophisticated carbon accounting system while ensuring the price signal never reaches the economy that needs to change.

The sequencing is the puzzle. Indonesia's Tax Harmonization Law (Law 7/2021) legislated both a carbon tax and an Emissions Trading Scheme in the same legislation. The ETS launched in February 2023, covering coal-fired power plants on the state utility's grid. The carbon tax, originally due in April 2022, was postponed four times and as of early 2026 has still not been enforced (ICAP, 2025). The ETS, for its part, generated approximately USD 5 million in total transactions since launch — barely a functioning market (IEEFA, 2025). The academic literature broadly recommends a carbon tax over an ETS for developing-country contexts (van den Bergh & Drews, 2026; Jia & Lin, 2020), yet Indonesia has done the opposite of what the literature recommends, and what it has done is not working. Why?

This essay argues that Indonesia's carbon-pricing sequencing reflects deliberate political-economy choices rather than economic-design optimality. The ETS protects key industrial coalitions through free allowances; the perpetually postponed carbon tax ensures the price signal does not reach the broader economy; and the re-opened Article 6 international market turns potential emissions reductions into an export revenue stream. The result is a system that is simultaneously among the world's most developed in legal architecture and among the least effective in practice. The essay proceeds in three sections: Section 1 explains the political-economy logic of the ETS-first sequencing; Section 2 describes how the ETS has actually operated; Section 3 applies the developing-country policy literature to argue what Indonesia should — and credibly can — do next.

## 4. Section-by-section writing plan

> Use this for fuller paragraph-level guidance. The skeleton in §2.2 above is the topic-sentence version. This section adds source pointers, structural notes, and extension/cut markers.

### 4.1 Section 1: Why an ETS first? (800 words)

#### Move 1 — Document the sequencing [~150 words]
- Law 7/2021 legislated both
- Phase 1 (Feb 2023) → Phase 2 (2025) → Phase 3 (2028–30)
- Tax: April 2022 → July 2022 → 2025 → still pending
- PR 110/2025 reaffirmed hybrid but tied carbon levy to ETS price rather than fixing it independently
- End: *"Indonesia has not simply chosen ETS over tax — it has legislated a full hybrid, implemented only the ETS side, and repeatedly deferred the tax half."*

**Sources:** ICAP 2025 §"Regulatory Framework" + §"Year in Review"; Sunanda et al. 2025 Table 4.

#### Move 2 — Why the literature prefers a tax [~150 words]
- Carbon tax is simpler to administer: van den Bergh & Drews 2026
- Tax gives a certain price signal vs ETS volatility
- Indonesia-specific: revenue-recycled tax raises GDP + employment (Hartono et al. 2023)
- Jia & Lin 2020 on China: tax alone outperforms ETS alone
- End: *"If this is what the literature recommends, why did Indonesia launch the ETS and delay the tax?"*

#### Move 3 — The political-economy explanation [~350 words]

Three reasons:

**Reason 1 — Burden visibility.** A carbon tax raises fuel/electricity prices for 270M people; the ETS concentrates costs on ~563 regulated plants, mostly PLN. Concentrated costs in firms managed through industry dialogue are far easier than visible household price rises. Stevens in Sunanda et al. 2025 [§87]: *"Political resistance and lack of coordination hinder carbon tax implementation, despite its potential."* Resistance is from industries and coalition partners, not from voters.

**Reason 2 — Free allocation protects industrial-policy priorities.** ETS grants 100% free allowances Phase 1, 75–85% Phase 2 — de facto subsidy to nickel, steel, cement, captive coal. These are exactly the downstreaming sectors. A carbon tax would have taxed them from day one with no equivalent sweetener.

**Reason 3 — Revenue without a new tax.** Article 6 moratorium lifted Jan 2025 → foreign exchange from credit exports without a visible new domestic tax. Government target: USD 65 billion from carbon projects by 2028 (Hashim at COP29 Nov 2024). Creates incentive to maximise credit-export machine rather than enforce a domestic price.

▶ **Extension (4k version):** Reason 4 (AMRO "cap first, then tax" sequencing logic) + Reason 5 (MRV readiness — power sector has better emissions data).

### 4.2 Section 2: How the ETS actually works (500 words)

#### Paragraph 1 — Coverage and phases [~150 words]
- Phase 1 (2023–24): 99 coal plants ≥25 MW on PLN grid; ~42 entities
- Phase 2 (2025–27): 563 installations + captive coal + gas
- Phase 3 (2028–30): all fossil-fuel power
- Total coverage: ~21% of national GHG (225.3 Mt ETS vs 1,053.4 Mt national)
- Cap design: intensity-based (tCO2/MWh), NOT absolute; 2025 NDC introduced absolute 1.35 GtCO2e cap that future phases meant to align toward

#### Paragraph 2 — Allocation and Article 6 [~100 words]
- All allowances free Phase 1; 75–85% free Phase 2; **no auctions as of early 2026**
- Article 6 moratorium lifted Jan 2025; ~1.78 MtCO2e PLN-affiliated authorised
- Critical PR 110/2025 provision: voluntary market **decoupled from NDC timeline**; Corresponding Adjustments **optional**, not mandatory — credits exportable without deduction from Indonesia's climate account

#### Paragraph 3 — The hollow reality [~200 words]
- 2025 allowance allocation **postponed** due to regulatory revision
- No auctions; no allowance trading in 2025
- Total IDX Carbon transactions since Sept 2023: IDR 78B (~USD 4.9M)
- Comparison: Indonesia spent IDR 713.5T (~USD 44B) on energy subsidies in 2024, 89% to fossil fuels (IISD/GSI 2025). Carbon market is **~8,000× smaller** than the subsidy system it is supposed to offset
- Indonesia's **net effective carbon rate is likely negative** (OECD ECR 2025)
- Climate Action Tracker (Dec 2024): **"Critically Insufficient"**

✂ **If cutting:** merge into §1 as Pattern A evidence, skip standalone §2.

### 4.3 Section 3: What Indonesia should do now (1,000 words)

#### Opening frame [~50 words]
> *"The developing-country carbon-pricing literature converges on five recommendations. Crucially, Indonesia has already committed to all five on paper — in Presidential Regulation 110/2025 and the October 2025 NDC. The binding constraint is not policy design but political-economy implementation."*

#### Recommendation 1 — Enforce the levy + remove the canceling subsidy [~200 words]
- Levy in Law 7/2021 (IDR 30/kg CO2 ≈ USD 2/tCO2) exists but is enforced ~zero times
- Recommendation: graduated escalation — $2 → $10 (2028) → $30 (2032). Level matters less than trajectory
- BUT net effective carbon rate is likely **negative** once fuel subsidies netted. Coal price cap alone (IDR 58.5T) implicitly subsidises the sector ETS prices. Step one is removing the subsidy.
- Indonesia has reformed fuel subsidies before (Jokowi 2014, ~IDR 200T/year savings) — the "untouchable third rail" framing is partly dated.

#### Recommendation 2 — Revenue recycling [~150 words]
- Hartono et al. 2023: CGE shows revenue recycling raises GDP + employment (double dividend, Goulder 1995)
- PKH conditional cash-transfer is the ready-made vehicle
- 3-kg LPG subsidy (IDR 80.2T) is regressive — richest 10% consume nearly as much subsidised LPG as poorest 10% (IISD/GSI 2025)
- Frame: *"The political obstacle is not that it harms the poor — it is that beneficiaries of the current subsidy system have no interest in changing the system."*

#### Recommendation 3 — Fix the ETS before expanding it [~100 words]
- Auctions — none have happened
- Functioning price floor — embedded carbon levy should activate when ETS price drops
- Stable rules — 2025 postponement caused by mid-year regulatory revision; investors cannot plan

#### Recommendations 4–5 [~100 words, brief]
- **R4 — Expand to AFOLU/peatland:** power sector is only ~21% of emissions; deforestation and peat fires are larger and outside ETS. Peatland restoration: cheapest, highest-impact (Tan et al. 2022)
- **R5 — ASEAN coordination:** Singapore at ~USD 25/tCO2. If Indonesia + Singapore align, the "competitiveness leakage" argument weakens (Andriansyah & Hong 2022)

#### The CBAM clincher [~250 words] — essay's sharpest recommendation

Structure: problem → fiscal perversity → prescription.

- **Problem:** EU CBAM entered definitive regime 1 Jan 2026. Covers Indonesia's steel (USD 726M exported to EU 2023), aluminium (USD 29M), fertilisers. Indonesian domestic price (~$2/tCO2) is ~97% below EU ETS (~€70–75/tCO2) → Indonesian exporters face near-full CBAM liability.
- **Fiscal perversity:** Indonesia's stated reason for keeping the price low is competitiveness. But CBAM means the opposite: by keeping the price near zero, Indonesia is handing carbon tax revenue to the EU. Every tonne of CO₂ embedded in Indonesian steel exported to the EU is being priced — just by Brussels, not Jakarta. $30/tCO2 domestic would reduce CBAM liability *and* generate Indonesian state revenue. The "low price = competitive advantage" logic no longer holds for EU-facing sectors.
- **Prescription:** Raise the domestic price to TBI's USD 12–13/tCO2 benchmark (the MAC for Indonesian coal retirement). At that level, Indonesia simultaneously unlocks its cheapest domestic abatement AND reduces CBAM exposure. The argument that converts climate ethics into fiscal self-interest.

### 4.4 Conclusion (300 words)

#### Paragraph 1 — Restate the thesis [~100 words]
- ETS-first sequencing is political-economy, not economics-of-design
- The implementation-gap pattern (tax postponed 4×; $5M trades vs $44B subsidies) is not accidental — it reflects industrial coalitions' interest in free allocation and state actors' interest in credit-export revenue
- The Hashim/Arsari story is the human face of the structural argument

#### Paragraph 2 — Constructive close [~100 words]
- Indonesia doesn't need new policy — it needs to enforce what is already on paper
- PR 110/2025 + 2025 NDC already contain the hybrid (cap-tax-and-trade)
- §3 recommendations are not proposals — they are Indonesia's own commitments. Binding constraint is political will.

#### Paragraph 3 — Forward gesture [~100 words]
- CBAM is the external pressure that may finally align fiscal self-interest with climate action
- For future research: whether "policy on paper, hollow in practice" is a generalisable LMIC pattern; whether external mechanisms (CBAM, conditional climate finance, Article 6 CAs) are the only durable way to shift the equilibrium

## 5. Stress-test & counter-evidence

### 5.1 Five objections to Framing 2 — and responses

#### Objection 1: *"Revenue is what Indonesia legitimately needs — calling that 'engineered credit export' is rich-country moralising"*

**Steelman:** Indonesia is a middle-income country with massive transition needs. The literature recommends international climate finance as a development tool (Kerr & Hu 2025; Fay et al. 2015). Treating credit exports as failure applies a developed-country standard.

**Response:**
- Distinguish *credit exports as a finance tool* (legitimate) from *credit exports that crowd out domestic decarbonisation* (the actual concern)
- Cite Jakob & Steckel 2014's "climate finance curse" — the framing is *inside* the climate-finance literature
- Concede: right policy is a *floor* on domestic credit retirement (e.g. 50% retained for compliance). PR 110/2025 has provisions for this but doesn't enforce them
- Essay isn't arguing "no credit exports" — it's arguing "no credit exports without domestic-decarbonisation guardrails"

#### Objection 2: *"The ETS hasn't been running long enough — give it three more years"*

**Steelman:** Indonesia's ETS launched 2023. EU ETS Phase 1 (2005–07) was also widely judged a failure; mature designs take 10+ years.

**Response:**
- 2025 allowance allocation was *postponed* (not just imperfect); trading dropped to near-zero. This is regression, not maturation.
- Carbon tax postponed *three* times since 2022 — not "early-stage growing pains"
- Phase 1 EU ETS critics had visible plans being implemented. Indonesia's PR 110/2025 operational components keep slipping.

#### Objection 3: *"The ETS hasn't disrupted anything because the cap is intensity-based — once it shifts to absolute caps (2025 NDC), it will bind"*

**Steelman:** ICAP confirms a 2025 NDC absolute cap of 1.35 GtCO2e and planned transition.

**Response (the strongest objection — engage head-on):**
- The NDC absolute cap is real but currently unconnected to the ETS allocation methodology
- Transition from intensity to absolute requires substantial regulatory build-out that PR 110/2025 leaves to future regulations
- Climate Action Tracker (Dec 2024) rates Indonesia "Critically Insufficient" *even with* the 2025 NDC — meaning even the legislated cap is too loose
- Concede the transition could work *if implemented*, and pivot back to Framing 2: the binding constraint is implementation, and the political-economy logic of credit exports actively *disincentivises* the institutional build-out

#### Objection 4: *"$2 vs $30 ignores that every country starts low"*

**Steelman:** EU ETS started at ~€0–5/tCO2. Singapore launched at SGD 5. Starting low is normal.

**Response:**
- True for the *level*; the issue is the *trajectory* and the *gap to MAC*
- EU went €0 → €100 in 20 years through credible escalation (linear reduction factor, MSR)
- Indonesia has no credible escalation: rate hasn't moved since 2021, multiple deferrals, no published trajectory
- TBI benchmark ($12–13/tCO2 MAC for coal retirement) is the relevant threshold; $2 doesn't reach it

#### Objection 5: *"You're using the wrong baseline — Indonesia's ECR includes fuel subsidies that are politically untouchable"*

**Steelman:** OECD ECR shows net rate is negative because of subsidies. Removing them is the third rail (cf. May 1998 riots after Suharto cut subsidies).

**Response:**
- Engage the constraint honestly: yes, fuel subsidy reform has historically triggered protest
- BUT Indonesia *has* reformed fuel subsidies repeatedly since 2005 (Jokowi 2014); the third-rail framing is dated
- Distinguish *household* fuel subsidies (politically sensitive but reformable with right design — PKH) from *industrial* energy subsidies (captured by politically connected industries). The latter is where the binding pricing gap lives.
- See [essay-evidence.md §1](essay-evidence.md#evidence-1-iisd-gsi--indonesian-fossil-fuel-subsidies) for the data

### 5.2 Genuine counter-evidence — honest verdict

A counter-evidence search ran during the chain-task experiment to find evidence that Indonesia's ETS revenue *is* being deployed for domestic abatement, or that Article 6 has stronger domestic guardrails than Framing 2 assumes. Findings:

**BPDLH mechanism — exists, but scale is trivial.** The Environmental Fund Management Agency receives proceeds from Indonesia's carbon market. In May 2024 a BPDLH/UNDP program disbursed IDR 28.6B (~USD 1.8M) for domestic abatement. Against IDR 713.5T in fossil-fuel subsidies that's a ratio of ~1:25,000 — proves domestic recycling is *possible* but scale makes no material difference.

**PR 110/2025 SRUK registry — institutional infrastructure for decarbonisation, not evidence of decarbonisation.** The same regulation contains the provision that *weakens* domestic guardrails (decoupling VCM from NDC; making CAs optional).

**The strongest genuine counter — CBAM-driven price pressure.** This is real external pressure that *could* push Indonesia toward a higher domestic price independent of domestic politics. But it's prospective, not current; Indonesia's track record of responding to external price signals with compliance rather than evasion is mixed.

**Verdict:** no evidence overturns the thesis. BPDLH is the strongest counter-point; CBAM is the strongest *future* pressure. Acknowledge in a footnote or concession sentence to show the essay engaged the evidence seriously.

### 6.1 Overarching principles
