2.1 Project Goals, Design and Long-Term Viability  
2.1.1 Summary Description of the Project (VCS, 3.2, 3.6, 3.10, 3.11, 3.13, 3.14; CCB, 
G1.2) 
The Delta Kapuas Project area is located in West Kalimantan Province and comprises 25 village 
forests,  covering  a total  area  of approximately 122,119.88 Ha.  This  region  has met the 
requirements for emission reduction activities that will be implemented in the area as Reduced 
Emission    of    Deforestation    and    Degradation    (REDD), Afforestation Reforestation    and 
Rehabilitation (ARR) as  well  as Wetland Restoration and  Conservation  (WRC). The  project area 
encompasses 3 (three) main  ecosystem types in  the  area namely mineral  soil,  wetlands,  and 
mangroves.  These  ecosystem  types  are  spread  across  25  village  forest  areas within  West 
Kalimantan Province covering approximately 122,119.88 Ha. 
Based on the classification of forest area function, out of the total area, most  are categorized as 
protected  forest  (±101,693.71  Ha), which functions  as  conservation  zone  and  protects the 
ecological system, including hydrology and biodiversity. Furthermore, about ±19,721.01 Ha are 
categorized  as  production  forest,  with  the  potential  for  sustainable land  use activities  and 
restoration. The remaining small portion of the area is categorized as other land use areas (APL), 
along with bodies of water.  
Prior to project’s intervention,  the  area  still retained forest  cover across mineral  soil,  wetland, 
and mangrove areas. However, these areas were then under high pressure from deforestation. 
This pressure originates from high deforestation activities such as illegal logging, land clearing 
by  fire,  and  agriculture  activities  that  have  been  widespread,  further  exacerbated  by minimum 
monitoring and ineffective intervention.  
These activities led  to  a  reduction in  the  area’s  carbon  stock and an increase in  the  GHG 
emissions caused by biomass loss and wetland degradation. Furthermore, the lack of stable and 
sustainable alternative  livelihood  options  for the  local  communities contributed  to  the  ongoing 
environmental pressures in the area. In general, the local communities in the village forest areas 
of West Kalimantan are highly dependent on forest and land resources for their livelihoods, with 
limited access to other economic opportunities. Furthermore, the institutional capacity of  Village 
Forest Management Institutions (LPHD) still faces technical barriers, particularly in management, 
area protection, and access to financial support and training

Biodiversity  in  the  area, in  particular  within the  wetland  and  mangrove  ecosystem, faces 
significant pressure due to the absence of effective protection systems which are primarily driven 
by  land  conversion  and  habitat  disturbances.  Records  from  SAMPAN  Kalimantan  (2023-2024) 
show that Kubu Raya alone has an abundance of biodiversity with 67 mangrove species (33 true 
species  and  34  associated  species)  including  rare  species  such  as Bruguiera  hainesii (Tumuk 
Putih) and Heritiera  globosa (Mengkulang). These  ecosystems have  been  the vital  habitat  for 
endemic  fauna, However, 20%  of  the  mangrove  cover  has been  destroyed, mainly due  to land 
conversion, establishment of fish ponds, and unsustainable local community practices without 
effective  conservation  management consideration

Some areas of this project (in the Padang Tikar Landscape) have previously been registered with 
Verra under project ID 3226, titled “Padang Tikar Landscape,” as “under development” status, 
under the ownership of Swiss Carbon Value Ltd (South Pole). On 7 December 2023, through a 
letter  of  consent  from Swiss  Carbon Value,  the project  ID was transferred fully to  PT Belantara 
Sejahtera  Mandiri.  To prevent project  duplication  or  double  counting,  PT  Belantara  Sejahtera 
Mandiri is currently in the process of withdrawing Project ID 3226 from the Verra Registry. The 
process is expected to be completed prior to the final project registration process.  
Generally, the project aims to secure and protect the village forest areas within the project area, 
as  well  as to restore  and enhance ecosystem  services  and habitat  preservation  through 
conservation  activities.  The  project will manage  the  forest to  ensure  both  its preservation and 
the well-being of the local community.  
The  objectives  of  this  project  are  based  on three  key  aspects: climate,  community  and 
biodiversity. From  a  climate  perspective,  the  project  aims  to  reduce  GHG (Greenhouse  Gas) 
emissions from deforestation and forest degradation by protecting forest areas within the project 
region, enhancing the  institutional  capacity  of  LPHD,  and  restoring  mangrove  and  wetland 
ecosystems. From a community perspective, the project aims to improve the well-being of local 
community by strengthening local governance and increasing community awareness of the value 
of forests conservation and sustainable  resource management. From a biodiversity perspective, 
the  project  aims  to  protect  natural  habitats within  the  project  area that  support endemic  and 
protected   species   such  as   Orangutan   Kalimantan  (Pongo   pygmaeus),   Bekantan  (Nasalis 
larvatus), Trenggiling  (Manis  javanica),  Owa Janggut-putih  (Hylobates  albibarbis),  Rangkong 
Badak  (Buceros  rhinoceros),  Julang  Jambul-hitam  (Rhabdotorrhinus  corrugatus),  Kura-kura 
Matahari    (Heosemys    spinosa),    Ramin    (Gonystylus    bancanus),    Meranti    Lilin    (Shorea 
teysmanniana), and Tumu putih (Bruguiera hainesii).

the REDD+ Jurisdictional Program. However, the project is committed to coordinating with local

The selection of methodologies for the project has considered the ecosystem characteristics of 
the  project  area  and  the  types  of  interventions  to  be  implemented. This  ensures  that the 
projected emission reductions can be calculated accurately and are aligned with the current VCS 
Standard requirements. The  main  methodologies  used  to  estimate  the project’s emission 
reductions are: 
VM0048 Reducing  Emissions from Deforestation and  Forest  Degradation methodology  is  used 
for the Avoided Unplanned Deforestation and Degradation (AUDD) and Coastal Intact Wetlands 
Restoration  (CIW)  activities.  This  method  focuses  on  preventing  unplanned  deforestation  and 
forest degradation  in  the  area, as  well  as  restoring intact  tidal  ecosystems, including  tidal 
wetlands.  
VM0007 (version 1.8) REDD+ Methodology Framework for the AUDD and Restoration of Wetland 
Ecosystems (RWE) activities implemented across various ecosystems in the area (mineral soils, 
wetlands, and mangroves).  This approach  enables  the  calculation  of emission  reductions  from 
wetland protection and restoration efforts under diverse ecological conditions.  
VM0033  Methodology for Tidal  Wetland and  Seagrass  Restoration  is  used  specifically  for  the 
RWE  activities  in  mangrove  ecosystems.  The  activity  will  focus on  rehabilitation  of  mangrove 
ecosystems which play a crucial role in coastal protection and carbon sequestration.  
VM0047  Afforestation,  Reforestation and  Revegetation (ARR) methodology is  used  for ARR 
activities on  mineral  soils within  the  project  area.  The  methodology  calculates emission 
reductions based on revegetation and replanting in areas that were previously classified as non-
forests.  
The  project  crediting  period  will  span  64  years. It is  expected  to  generate  GHG  emission 
reductions averaging 2,438,715.98  tCO₂e annually. Over the full duration of the crediting period, 
the project is projected to achieve total emission reductions of 156,077,822.98 tCO₂e.

2.1.4 Project Eligibility (VCS, 3.1, 3.6, 3.8, 3.18, 4.1; CCB Program Rules, 4.2.4, 4.6.4) 
This  project  qualifies  for  registration  under both the  Verified  Carbon  Standard  (VCS) and the 
Climate,  Community, and  Biodiversity  (CCB) Standards  as  a  multi-category  AFOLU (Agriculture, 
Forestry, and Other Land Use) project. It encompasses the following categories: REDD (Reducing 
Emissions  from  Deforestation  and Forest Degradation),  ARR  (Afforestation,  Reforestation,  and 
Revegetation),  and  WRC  (Wetland  Restoration  and  Conservation).  These  categories  fall under 
VCS Scope 14: Agriculture, Forestry, and Other Land Use. 
The  project activities comprise four  main  components.  First,  Avoided  Unplanned  Deforestation 
and  Degradation  (AUDD)  that aimed  at preventing greenhouse  gas  emissions  by reducing 
unplanned  deforestation  and forest degradation in both  mineral  soils  and  peatlands.  Second, 
Afforestation, Reforestation, and Revegetation (ARR) that focused on restoring tree cover in open 
areas  to  enhance  carbon  sequestration.  Third,  Conservation  of  Intact  Wetlands  (CIW) that 
ensures the protection of intact peatland and mangrove ecosystems. And finally, Restoration of 
Wetland  Ecosystems  (RWE) that  involves rehabilitating  degraded  wetlands  including  peat, 
mineral soil, and mangrove areas through measures such as rewetting, revegetation, and canal 
blocking. 
The  main  methodologies  applied in this  project  include: VM0048,  which  is  used  for  activities 
related to Avoided Unplanned Deforestation and Degradation (AUDD) and Conservation of Intact 
Wetlands  (CIW);  VM0007,  which  supports  both  AUDD  and  Restoration  of  Wetland  Ecosystems 
(RWE) across mineral soils, peatlands, and mangrove ecosystems; VM0033, applied specifically 
for RWE in mangrove areas; and VM0047, which is employed for Afforestation, Reforestation, and 
Revegetation (ARR) activities on mineral lands. 
All applied methodologies are valid and recognized under the current VCS program framework. 
Each methodology is implemented in alignment with the biophysical characteristics and activity 
types  specific  to  each  project  site.  Supporting  modules  (VMDs)  are  applied  selectively,  in 
accordance  with methodological requirements, and will  be  detailed  in a separate annex  in  the 
relevant section.

a public  comment  period  through  the  Verra  Registry  prior  to  the  opening  meeting  with  the 
Validation/Verification  Body  (VVB).  The  project  will  ensure  that  the  validation  reports  and

The  Delta  Kapuas  Project  will  strive  to  deliver  measurable  climate  benefits across  the  entire 
project zone through comprehensive interventions. These include: 1) participatory forest patrols, 
remote  sensing,  and  early  warning  systems  to  safeguard  forest  areas  within  the  region; 2) 
Peatland management prioritized through canal blocking and rewetting techniques to maintain 
optimal moisture levels and prevent emissions; 3) Implementing restoration efforts for degraded 
non-forest  areas  within  forest  zones  using  reforestation  and  Assisted  Natural  Regeneration 
(ANR); 4) Introducing agroforestry systems on lands previously used by communities to enhance 
carbon sequestration while reducing fire risks; 5) Promoting the avoidance of chemical fertilizers 
and  other  substances use that  contribute  to  greenhouse  gas  emissions;  6)  Restoring  and 
protecting  mangrove  ecosystems  in  coastal  zones  through  silvofishery-based  replanting  on 
former aquaculture sites; 7) Conducting policy monitoring to track changes that may lead to forest 
degradation  or  land  conversions;  8)  Coordinating  with  stakeholders  to reinforce  collective 
commitments  to  forest  protection;  and  9)  Investing  in  climate  adaptation  training,  community-
based resource management, and participatory monitoring to build long-term resilience and local 
capacity.

2.1.17 Project Activities and Theory of Change (VCS, 3.6; CCB, G1.8) 
This  section  presents  a  sequence  linking  the  identified  problems  to  the  project  intervention 
activities, outputs, outcomes, and impacts that will all be aligned with the objectives of the Delta 
Kapuas Project. The framework is organized into three main components: Climate, Community, 
and Biodiversity. Each component is connected to the project’s Theory of Change (ToC) and 
project boundaries to ensure consistency with VCS 3.6, CCB v3.1, and G1.8 criterion. The table 
provides  measurable  indicators,  baseline–target  values,  and  monitoring  frequency  for  every 
activity. Through this structure, the claimed climate, biodiversity, and community benefits will be 
evidence-based and data driven while the project interventions will be positioned as measurable 
solutions to the identified problems. These will simultaneously serve as both the implementation 
roadmap and the adaptive management plan of the project.  
The Delta Kapuas Project has conducted an assessment of the key challenges within and around 
the project area, Leakage Belt, and Project Zone. This assessment is directly and indirectly linked 
to mapping the baseline activities that contribute to environmental degradation and loss of forest 
cover in the area. The assessment identifies both the underlying problems and the critical steps 
required  to  achieve  the  project’s  objectives,  which  are to  deliver  net  benefits  for  climate, 
communities,  and  biodiversity. The  project  activities  are  designed  to  ensure  conservation  and 
protection  of  biodiversity  and  natural  resources,  restrain  unplanned  deforestation,  reduce 
greenhouse   gas   emissions,   strengthen   social   inclusion,   improve   livelihoods   and   climate 
resilience, and safeguard species of High Conservation Value (HCV).

The project adopts a multi-dimensional approach built upon three pillars: ecological, economical, 
and social as conditions for project sustainability
15
. The ecological aspect is closely tied to the 
Delta  Kapuas  Project’s  efforts  in mitigating climate  change and conserving biodiversity. The 
socio-economic institutional aspect focuses on strengthening the capacity of human resources 
and  forest  management  institutions,  while  also  enhancing  community  livelihoods  through 
community-based  forest  management  training  and the development  of  alternative  income 
sources. Particular attention is given to vulnerable groups, ensuring their meaningful inclusion in 
the  decision-making  process  of  the  project.  This  strategy  aligns  with  the  national  ASTA  CITA 
development   agenda,   which   underscores    the   acceleration   of   poverty   reduction,   the 
enhancement of human resource quality, and the pursuit of inclusive and sustainable economic 
growth. 
The climate component focuses on reducing net emissions and enhancing carbon sequestration 
through a  combination of REDD-AUD, peatland rewetting,  tidal/mangrove  restoration, and  ARR 
on mineral soils. Activities and indicators implemented are aligned with relevant methodologies: 
VM0048  together  with  VMD0055  for  actual  deforestation  versus  baseline  allocated-risk; 
VM0007 for peatland interventions, including groundwater level rise, reduced fire incidence, and 
CH₄/N₂O  management;  VM0033  for  coastal  hydrology  restoration,  covering  soil  carbon  and 
biomass  recovery;  and  VM0047  for  ARR,  focusing  on  survival  rates  and  biomass  growth.  The 
climate  outcomes  are  supported  by  an  integrated  MRV  system  combining  remote  sensing, 
Permanent  Sample  Plots  (PSPs),  water  level data loggers,  fire incidence  monitoring,  and 
uncertainty  calculations.  Leakage  adjustments  are  applied  if  activity  displacement  is  detected 
within the leakage belt, as detailed in Section 3.3 on Monitoring and in the annexes. 
The  biodiversity  component  of  the  Delta  Kapuas  Project is  centered  on  efforts  to  protect  and 
conserve  the  rich  biodiversity  within  the  project  area.  Located  in  the  coastal  zone  of  West 
Kalimantan Province, the project area has been designated as an Important Bird Area (IBA) by 
BirdLife  (2025).  IBAs  are  recognized  as  sites  of  international  significance,  providing  diverse 
habitats  for  bird  species  and serving  as  migratory  corridors  for  numerous  species.  Within  the 
Delta  Kapuas  Project,  three  critical  ecosystems:  mangrove  forests,  peat  swamp  forests,  and 
dryland forests, form essential habitats not only for birds but also for a wide range of other fauna. 
Despite this ecological importance, the area is subject to various human activities that degrade 
ecosystems, diminish forest cover, and disrupt biodiversity conditions. The project interventions 
are  therefore  designed  to  address  these  threats  and  ensure  the  long-term  conservation  of 
species and habitats. 
Activities  that  threaten  and disrupt ecosystems  and  biodiversity  have  been identified in  the 
project area, including land encroachment for conversion into plantations and production forests, 
illegal logging for timber extraction, forest and land fires used as methods for land clearing, illegal 
hunting  for  wildlife  exploitation,  and  destructive  fishing  practices  that can damage  aquatic 
ecosystems. Furthermore, historically, based on land use planning documents, most notably the

In addressing the threats facing biodiversity, Delta Kapuas Project implements routine patrols to 
safeguard and monitor the project area. The patrols will document any disturbances and threats 
within  the  project  area  and  its surrounding areas.  The  data  collected  will  be  compiled  into  a 
database and shared  with  relevant  stakeholders  through  regular  reporting.  Alongside  these 
efforts, the project conducts in-situ monitoring of flora and fauna to observe biodiversity trends 
and track the condition of key species population. Specific monitoring techniques are detailed in 
Section 5.4.1. 
Other  activities  that  will  be  implemented include integrating  rehabilitation  and  reforestation 
efforts aimed at enhancing carbon sequestration while also conserving biodiversity. This includes 
planting native tree species in open areas, which is expected to create local corridors for fauna 
within the project area and expand overall forest cover. The project will also conduct Focus Group 
Discussions (FGDs) and training sessions in each village to identify instances of illegal hunting 
and raise community awareness of the ecological functions of forest and biodiversity. All activities 
will  involve surrounding communities,  with the expectations  that  these engagements  will  shift 
perceptions and encourage transitions toward alternative livelihoods facilitated by the project.  
The  community  component  ensures  social benefits are  inclusive and  equally  distributed,  while 
simultaneously reducing threats to forests through low-emission livelihood alternatives such as 
sustainable silvofishery, agroforestry, and Non-Timber Forest Products/NTFPs, capacity building, 
strengthening local governance, and safeguard mechanisms including FPIC, grievance redress, 
gender, and social inclusion. Main indicators include the number of beneficiary villages, changes 
in  household  income  and  economic  resilience,  100%  compliance  with  FPIC  for  relevant 
interventions,  and the rate  of  grievance  resolutions.  This  approach  ensures  social  license, 
mitigates activity-shifting  leakage,  and strengthens the long-term  sustainability  of  climate  and 
biodiversity outcomes. 
As  a  result,  the  Delta  Kapuas  Project  will  contribute  net  positive  benefits  to  climate,  local 
communities, and biodiversity. These outcomes will be achieved through diverse activities under 
each climate, community and biodiversity component, with the main interventions and expected 
impacts outlined in Table 2. 
The Theory  of  Change  for  the  Delta  Kapuas  Project  is  based  on  several  key  operational 
assumptions: (1) Communities will transition to low-emission livelihoods provided that benefits 
are delivered in a timely manner and associated risks are effectively managed; (2) Hydrological 
infrastructure such as canal blocks and embankment remains functional; (3) Local governance 
support is sustained; (4) Climate variability - including droughts, El Niño events, and fires - does 
not exceed the project’s capacity to respond; and (5) The integrity and quality of MRV data are 
preserved. In ensuring the assumptions are met, the project provides upfront support and market 
access  through  equitable  benefit-sharing  arrangements;  develops and  implements monitoring

2.1.19 Implementation Schedule (CCB, G1.9) 
The  project  implementation  was  initiated  in  response  to  the  condition  of  the  protected  forest 
within the project area, which had not been managed effectively. A lack of structured monitoring 
systems and sustainable use left the area’s potential underdeveloped for both conservation and 
economic purposes.  
Between  2011  and  2018,  efforts  to  address  this  challenge  focused  on  proposals  for  social 
forestry and facilitation of permits, pursued with support from donors and financial institutions. 
However, reflections conducted in 2020–2021 indicated that community assistance needed to 
shift  toward  a  multi-enterprise  approach  to  better  align  with  the  scale  of  the  permits  and  the 
needs of local communities. 
The repositioning of SAMPAN Kalimantan as a facilitator in 2021 marked a turning point, with a 
mandate from the LPHD to seek investment partners. This was followed by collaboration with PT 
BSM, involving feasibility studies, due diligence, and field visits, and was formalized in the signing 
of an MoU between the parties in mid-2022. 
Since  April  2023,  various  capacity-building  activities  for  the  LPHD  have  been  implemented, 
including  the  establishment  of  patrol  teams,  preparation  of  planning  documents,  training,  and 
the formation of KUPS. In September and October 2023, a cooperation agreement was signed 
between the LPHD, PT BSM, and SAMPAN Kalimantan, marking the transformation of protected 
forest management into a collaborative, participatory, and sustainable system.

2.2 Without-project Land Use Scenario and Additionality  
2.2.1 Conditions Prior to Project Initiation and Land Use Scenarios without the Project 
(VCS, 3.13; CCB, G2.1) 
The  Delta  Kapuas Project encompasses three  landscape areas:  Padang Tikar, Kubu Raya, and 
Pulau Maya. These landscapes contain distinct ecosystem types, namely mineral soils, peatland, 

      CCB & VCS Project Description Template  
                                                                                                CCB Version 3.0, VCS Version 4.4 
50 
CCB v3.0, VCS v4.4 
and  mangroves.  These  diverse  baseline  conditions  form  a  unique  characteristic  of  the  project 
area and constitute key focal point for the project’s design and implementation. 
The mineral soil ecosystem covers a large portion of the project area, which remains under forest 
cover.  As  outlined  in  Sub-chapter  2.5.10,  the  area  faces  threats  such  as  illegal  logging,  illegal 
wildlife hunting, land conversion into plantations and agriculture, as well as the risk of fire. Fires 
in  these  areas  have  the  potential  to  generate  significant  emissions  from  the  combustion  of 
biomass.  In  addition  to  these  illegal  activities,  there  are  also  legal  threats  arising  from 
contradictory government policies, such as permits for land clearing within protected forest areas 
as part of priority programs, which contribute to increased greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions. 
The peatland ecosystem is highly vulnerable to forest fire disturbances. Prolonged dry seasons 
and  the  construction  of  drainage  canals  increase  the  risk  of  land  fires  within  peatland 
landscapes. In addition, illegal logging poses a further threat by creating greater exposure and 
degradation of the area. 
The  mangrove  ecosystem  represents  a  rare  and  threatened  habitat.  Coastal  erosion  and 
seawater intrusion are driven by mangrove logging along the shoreline, where mangrove wood is 
often used for charcoal production, a source of income for coastal communities. Nevertheless, 
some mangrove areas remain in good condition. However, the conversion of coastal zones into 
fishponds poses an additional threat, as such areas can no longer be replanted with mangroves. 
The  livelihoods  of  communities  within  the  Delta  Kapuas  Project  area  are  diverse.  Sources  of 
income include farming, gardening, fishing, timber work, furniture making, boat building, hunting, 
livestock  raising,  daily  wage  labor,  trading,  civil  service,  and other  occupations.  One  notable 
source of income is charcoal production, which relies on mangrove wood harvested from coastal 
areas.

2.4 Management Capacity  
2.4.1 Project Governance Structures (CCB, G4.1) 
This project implements a comprehensive governance structure involving various stakeholders who have 
specific  roles  and  responsibilities  in  project  implementation  and  oversight.  The  governance  structure  is 
designed to ensure transparency, accountability, and compliance with applicable standards. 
PT  Belantara  Sejahtera  Mandiri,  together  with  the  25  Village  Forest  Management  Institutions  (LPHD)  of

and ownership. As the principal entities, both parties have the authority to make strategic decisions and 
ensure the project operates in accordance with the established vision and mission.

2.4.5 Financial Health of Implementing Organization(s) (CCB, G4.3) 
 
The Delta Kapuas Project is founded on a robust partnership between PT BSM and the LPHDs, featuring a 
clear, equitable, and sustainable financial mechanism. PT BSM's role as investor provides the investment 
acceleration required during the initial phase. This mechanism guarantees cash flow for project continuity 
in the early stages, prior to the realization of revenue from carbon credit sales. 
 
The investment is expected to be recovered proportionally as carbon credits are sold, thereby creating a

transparent  mechanisms,  and  strong  alignment  of  interests,  this  model  establishes  a  win-win  scenario 
wherein  the  LPHDs  obtain  access  to  capital,  entry  to  global  carbon  markets,  capacity  building,  and 
sustainable revenue, while PT BSM achieves fair investment returns, strategic positioning in the expanding 
carbon market, and meaningful contributions to the sustainability agenda.

2.5.5 Statutory and Customary Property Rights (VCS, 3.18, 3.19; CCB, G5.1) 
The  project  zone  is  located  within  state  forest  areas  managed  by  Village  Forest  Management 
Institutions  (LPHD),  where  the  forest  resources  are  utilized  to  support  community  welfare. 
Although  the  project  area  falls  under  LPHD  management  rights,  the  Delta  Kapuas  Project  will 
recognize, respect, and promote the rights of Indigenous Peoples (IPs) and Local Communities 
(LCs) present within the project zone. This commitment aligns with Law No. 39 of 1999 on Human 
Rights,  as   well  as   relevant  international  legal  frameworks,  including  the   United  Nations 
Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP), adopted by Indonesia in 2007, and 
ILO Convention No. 169 on Indigenous and Tribal Peoples. 
The  Delta  Kapuas  Project  has  conducted  participatory  mapping  based  on  Free,  Prior,  and 
Informed Consent (FPIC) principles to identify land tenure status and patterns of natural resource 
use within the project area. Participatory mapping was carried out jointly with IPs and LCs, local 
government authorities, and the LPHD. Land tenure types identified in the project zone include: 
•
 
Individual Ownership: Typically originating from transmigration programs, land purchase, or 
forest  clearing.  Such  land  is  used  for  settlement,  agriculture  (e.g.,  coconut  cultivation),  or

deforestation is no longer drawn from project-level historical data, but rather from jurisdictional 
risk maps that have been developed and subjected to independent evaluation.

3.1.3 Project Boundary (VCS, 3.12) 
The  Delta  Kapuas  Project  is  situated  within  a  complex  coastal–delta  landscape  unit,  where 
spatial  boundaries  are  defined  using  a  landscape  approach
17
.  This  approach  enables  the 
adaptive  balancing  of  conservation,  restoration,  and  community  livelihood  objectives  across 
multiple  land  uses  and  stakeholders.  The  principles  of  the  landscape  approach  emphasize 
multi‑purpose  management,  results‑based  monitoring,  and  recognition  of  trade‑offs  among 
landscape components—an especially relevant perspective for the mangrove–peatland–mineral 
soil mosaic of the Delta Kapuas Project.

interpretation of optical satellite imagery (Landsat, Sentinel), field verification based on selected 
samples,  and  the  use  of  high‑resolution  imagery  (PlanetScope),  further  adjusted  to  align  with 
official administrative boundaries. The Delta Kapuas Project covers a total area of 122,119.88 
Ha, located within Kubu Raya and Kayong Utara Regencies, West Kalimantan Province, Indonesia

dead wood/litter where required or deemed significant. In mangrove and peat ecosystems, soil 
organic carbon (SOC) is considered in line with methodological requirements. For peatland WRC 
activities  (VM0007  peat  modules),  the  primary  focus  is  CO₂ flux  from  peat  oxidation  due  to 
drainage and its reduction following rewetting, with CH₄ (potential increases post‑rewetting) and 
N₂O  assessed  where  relevant;  emissions  from  fire  are  recorded  if  they  occur.  For  tidal 
wetland/mangrove WRC activities (VM0033), accounting includes increases in AGB/BGB stocks, 
actual soil   carbon   following   hydrological   recovery,   and   CH₄/N₂O   in   accordance   with 
methodological guidance. For ARR activities (VM0047) on mineral soils, the main pools are AGB 
and BGB (and SOC where required or above thresholds), with additional non‑CO₂ sources from 
nitrogen fertilizer use or biomass burning if applicable.
