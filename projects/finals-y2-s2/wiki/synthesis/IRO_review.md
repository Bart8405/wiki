## asylum shopping

**Definition:** The practice (real or alleged) of asylum-seekers deliberately choosing between countries based on which offers the most favourable conditions — benefits, recognition rates, processing times. The term is politically charged: it implies strategic behaviour and is used to justify restrictive rules.

**Explained easily:** "Shopping" implies choice and calculation, which critics argue misrepresents refugees fleeing persecution who may have little say in which country they end up in. The Dublin Convention (1990) was specifically designed to end asylum shopping by requiring that you apply in the *first safe country* you arrive in — which in practice meant Greece and Italy bore most of the burden.

## Hobson's choice

**Definition:** A Hobson's choice is a seemingly free choice where in reality there is only one real option — take it or leave it. The name comes from Thomas Hobson (1544–1631), a Cambridge stable owner who required customers to take the horse nearest the stable door or none at all.

**Explained easily:** When the Week 6 summary describes illegal immigration and a black market for labour as "a Hobson's choice," it means states face a trap with no genuinely good option: either tolerate undocumented migration (and the black market that follows) or accept that legal guest worker schemes will fail and create the same outcome anyway. Either way, you end up with irregular migration.

## internal market spillover into internal security

**Definition:** Huysmans' argument that creating the EU's single internal market (via the Single European Act 1986) created functional pressure to harmonise internal security. Abolishing internal border controls required compensating measures — external border control, common visa policy, data sharing, police cooperation — which bundled migration with terrorism and crime in the same governance field.

**Explained easily:** Think of it like a domino chain: the SEA removed internal border checks → so states needed a common external border → so they needed to agree on who was a threat → migration policy got absorbed into the same institutions as anti-terrorism and policing. Migration became a "security issue" through bureaucratic integration logic, not only through political speeches about migrants being dangerous.

## liberal paradox

**Definition:** Hollifield's term for the structural contradiction liberal states face: their **economic logic** demands openness to trade, investment, and migration (closed borders hurt growth), while their **political and legal logic** demands closure (democracy, social contract, and public legitimacy require some territorial exclusion). States are permanently caught between these two pulls.

**Explained easily:** Germany invited Turkish guest workers in the 1960s → tried to send them home in 1973 after the oil shock → courts said no, family reunification is a right → a permanent immigrant population formed despite the policy reversal. Repeat for every liberal democracy. The paradox isn't a policy mistake — it's baked into what it means to be liberal.

## migration state

**Definition:** Hollifield's term for a third core function of the modern state, alongside the garrison state (military security) and the trading state (economic competitiveness). In a migration state, regulating who can enter, stay, and gain citizenship is as central to state purpose as defence or trade.

**Explained easily:** The classic state had two jobs: protect its people from external threats (garrison) and get rich through trade (trading state). Hollifield says there's now a third job that modern liberal states can't avoid: actively managing migration — deciding who can join the polity, for how long, and on what terms. The EU is the only experiment so far in doing this collectively across multiple states.

## security continuum

**Definition:** A concept from Bigo and Huysmans describing an institutionalised governance field that links border control, terrorism, transnational crime, and migration into a single domain. Once these issues share institutions (TREVI network, Schengen, Third Pillar), the security connotations of one (terrorism) transfer to the others (migration). It is a structural feature of EU policy, not just political rhetoric.

**Explained easily:** Think of it as a bureaucratic network effect: when police, customs, immigration, and counter-terrorism officials all attend the same committees, share the same databases (Eurodac, SIS), and operate under the same legal frameworks, they start treating all these problems as versions of the same problem. Migration gets coded as a security issue not because politicians say so on TV (that's the Copenhagen School), but because border guards, Frontex officers, and intelligence services all operate in the same institutional space (that's the Paris School / Bigo).

The Copenhagen School says securitisation happens through **speech acts** (a politician labels migration a security threat and that act creates the security frame).
