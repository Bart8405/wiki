# Lecture 2 — Knowledge, Truth, and Facts

*What does it actually mean to "know" something? Three foundational concepts: knowledge (as justified true belief), truth (as correspondence with facts), and facts (as state-of-the-world truthmakers).*

**Date:** 11 February 2026 · **Lecturer:** Prof. James W. McAllister · **Key thinker:** ==**Bertrand Russell**== (1872–1970) — for the correspondence theory; honourable mention to **Plato** (*Theaetetus*, ~370 BCE) for the JTB account.

## Lecture walkthrough

### What is knowledge? — the JTB account

Plato's analysis: "**A knows that P**" if and only if:
1. **P is true** (truth condition)
2. **A believes that P** (psychological condition)
3. **A is justified in believing that P** (justification condition)

Each condition does a specific job:
- **Condition 1** is about the world, not about A's awareness. You cannot *know* something false. You can *believe* it (e.g. "Milan is the capital of Italy") but not *know* it.
- **Condition 2** is about A's mental state. "I know P but I don't believe P" is self-contradictory.
- **Condition 3** rules out lucky guesses. You can guess the right answer without "knowing" it.

The famous diagram: **Knowledge = the intersection of Beliefs ∩ Truths ∩ Justified beliefs.** Outside that intersection sit: lucky guesses (true but unjustified), justified falsehoods (justified but untrue), and truths that are simply not part of our beliefs.

### What is truth? — the correspondence theory

Truth is a property *only* of propositions (and systems of propositions like theories). Russell's formulation:

> *"==A belief is true when there is a corresponding fact, and is false when there is no corresponding fact. … Minds do not create truth or falsehood. They create beliefs, but when once the beliefs are created, the mind cannot make them true or false."* — Bertrand Russell, *Problems of Philosophy* (1912)==

Classic example: *"Snow is white"* is true if and only if snow is white.

### What is a fact?

A fact is a **state of affairs in the world** — it makes a proposition true. Critical distinction:
- ==**Propositions** are **truth-bearers** (they can be true or false).==
- ==**Facts** are **truthmakers**== (they make propositions true or false). Facts themselves are *not* true or false; they simply *are*.

Permanence: facts don't change. "Dolphins are mammals" was true even when people believed them to be fish — only the belief changed, not the fact.

> **Explained easily — JTB:**
> Imagine your friend insists "the Eiffel Tower is in Berlin" because they read it on a sketchy blog. They *believe* it. But it's not true (so condition 1 fails) and even though they have a "reason" it's not a good reason (condition 3 also wobbles). So they don't *know* it. JTB is just a precise way of saying: knowing means *being right, believing what you're right about, and having good reasons for that belief.*

> **E==xplained easily — correspondence theory + facts:**==
> =="Snow is white" is a sentence in your head. Snow being white is a fact about the world. The sentence is **true** because the world *makes it true*. Facts are out there doing the work — your mind doesn't get a vote. Think of facts like planets: they're just there. Sentences about planets can be true or false; the planets themselves aren't true or false.==

> **Explained easily — belief vs knowledge being public:**
> Two people can sincerely *believe* opposite things. They cannot both *know* opposite things — because at most one of them can be right (condition 1: P must be true). This is why "we just have different opinions" works for matters of taste but doesn't dissolve disagreements about facts.

### Multiple Choice Questions

**Q1.** According to the JTB (Justified True Belief) account of knowledge, if a belief is justified but false, then:
- A) It still counts as knowledge because the person had good reasons
- B) It does not count as knowledge because the truth condition fails
- C) It counts as knowledge only if the person is an expert
- D) It does not count as knowledge because the justification condition fails

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B) It does not count as knowledge because the truth condition fails**

JTB requires all three conditions simultaneously: truth, belief, *and* justification. A justified but false belief fails condition 1 (P must be true). The justification does not "save" the belief from being false. Option A is the classic mistake students make — confusing justified belief with knowledge.

</details>

---

**Q2.** The correspondence theory of truth, as formulated by Bertrand Russell, holds that:
- A) A proposition is true if it coheres with other propositions in a system
- B) A proposition is true if it is useful to act on
- C) A proposition is true if and only if there is a fact corresponding to it
- D) A proposition is true if it has been verified by sensory experience

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C) A proposition is true if and only if there is a fact corresponding to it**

This is Russell's canonical formulation from *Problems of Philosophy* (1912). Option A describes a *coherence* theory; option B is closer to pragmatist theories of truth; option D is a verificationist position associated with logical positivism — none of these is the correspondence theory.

</details>

---

**Q3.** In the lecture's vocabulary, facts are described as:
- A) True propositions about the world
- B) Truthmakers — states of affairs that make propositions true or false
- C) Truthbearers — statements that can be assessed as true or false
- D) Mental representations of how the world is

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B) Truthmakers — states of affairs that make propositions true or false**

This is the key distinction: *propositions* are truth-bearers (they can be true or false); *facts* are truthmakers (they make propositions true or false but are not themselves true or false). Option C describes propositions, not facts. Facts are not mental representations (option D) — they are in the world, independent of minds.

</details>

---

**Q4.** The justification condition in JTB is needed primarily to:
- A) Ensure that beliefs are held sincerely
- B) Rule out lucky guesses from counting as knowledge
- C) Establish that the belief corresponds to reality
- D) Make knowledge public rather than private

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B) Rule out lucky guesses from counting as knowledge**

The lecture is explicit: the justification condition rules out lucky guesses. Without it, you could accidentally believe a true proposition and "know" it — which seems wrong. Option A (sincerity) is not part of JTB. Option C is the role of the truth condition, not justification. Option D is a feature of knowledge but not the purpose of the justification condition specifically.

</details>

---

**Q5.** "Two people can sincerely believe opposite things, but they cannot both *know* opposite things." This claim rests on which condition of JTB?
- A) The justification condition — only one can have good reasons
- B) The truth condition — at most one of the opposite beliefs can be true
- C) The belief condition — belief is subjective, knowledge is not
- D) The Gettier condition — one of them must have a defective fourth condition

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B) The truth condition — at most one of the opposite beliefs can be true**

If A knows that P and B knows that not-P, both beliefs would have to be true simultaneously — which is a contradiction. The truth condition (P must actually be true) is what makes knowledge the same for all, while belief can vary person to person. This is why "we just have different opinions" dissolves taste disputes but not factual ones.

</details>

---

**Q6.** The Gettier problem is best described as:
- A) The problem of proving that any belief corresponds to a fact
- B) The discovery of cases where justified true belief is not sufficient for knowledge
- C) The problem of explaining how justification can be objective
- D) The problem of circularity in using induction to justify beliefs

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B) The discovery of cases where justified true belief is not sufficient for knowledge**

Gettier (1963) produced counterexamples — scenarios where someone has a justified true belief but we intuitively deny they "know." These cases show that JTB as stated needs a fourth, stronger condition. Option A is a problem about correspondence theory, not Gettier. Option D is Hume's problem of induction (Lecture 3).

</details>

### Short Essay / Open Questions

**Essay Q1.** "Under what conditions may we say that Andrea knows that Bob was born in Paris?" Use the JTB framework to answer, explain why each condition is needed with an example, and note what JTB cannot handle.

<details>
<summary>Model answer outline</summary>

**Key points to hit:**
- State the three JTB conditions: (1) "Bob was born in Paris" is true; (2) Andrea believes it; (3) Andrea is justified in that belief (e.g. she has seen his birth certificate).
- Explain each: Condition 1 — you cannot *know* something false, even if you believe it sincerely. Condition 2 — you cannot know something you don't believe (self-contradiction). Condition 3 — lucky guessing doesn't count as knowledge.
- Illustrate a case where one condition fails: Andrea "knows" Bob was born in Paris because she dreamed it — condition 3 fails (no justification). Or: she believes it, has evidence, but Bob was actually born in Lyon — condition 1 fails.
- Note the Gettier problem: there are weird cases where all three conditions hold but we still wouldn't say someone "knows" — e.g. Andrea concludes correctly that Bob was born in Paris, but from a forged document. This motivates "JTB+" accounts.

**Good example to use:** The Eiffel Tower example from the "Explained easily" section — someone believes the Eiffel Tower is in Berlin because of a sketchy blog. True? No. Justified? Not really. So they fail conditions 1 and 3.

**Common mistake to avoid:** Treating justification as sufficient for truth. A belief can be fully justified (excellent reasons, best evidence available) and still be false. The lecture emphasises that justification "rises and falls with time" — what counts as well-justified changes.

</details>

**Essay Q2.** Explain the correspondence theory of truth and the distinction between facts (truthmakers) and propositions (truthbearers). Why does the lecture insist that facts are not themselves true or false?

<details>
<summary>Model answer outline</summary>

**Key points to hit:**
- Correspondence theory (Russell): a proposition is true if and only if there is a fact in the world that corresponds to it. "Snow is white" is true because snow *is* white — the world does the work.
- Truthbearers = propositions: things that can be evaluated as true or false (sentences, beliefs, theories).
- Truthmakers = facts: states of affairs in the world that make propositions true. Facts are not propositions — they don't have a truth value; they simply *are*.
- Key insight: minds create beliefs (propositions), but once created, minds cannot make them true or false — that is determined by the world (Russell quote from the lecture).
- Why facts can't be "true or false": asking whether a fact is true or false is a category error — like asking whether the planet Mars is grammatically correct.

**Good example to use:** "Dolphins are mammals" was true even when people believed them to be fish. The fact (dolphins are mammals) didn't change when beliefs changed — only the beliefs changed.

**Common mistake to avoid:** Confusing "facts" with "true statements." A fact is not a sentence — it is the state of affairs in the world that sentences are *about*. This is the key trap the lecture flags explicitly.

</details>

---

**Essay Q3.** Can a belief be justified but false? Explain your answer using the JTB framework and consider what this implies for the relationship between scientific evidence and scientific knowledge.

<details>
<summary>Model answer outline</summary>

**Key points to hit:**
- Yes — justified false belief is entirely possible under JTB. The three conditions are *independent*. You can have excellent reasons for a belief that turns out to be false.
- Historical science examples: Newtonian mechanics was fully justified by the evidence available for two centuries and turned out to be false (or at least incomplete) at relativistic speeds. This doesn't mean Newton's belief was *unjustified* — it means condition 1 eventually failed.
- Implication: scientific knowledge is provisional. We might today have the best available justification for a belief, and future evidence might show condition 1 fails. This is related to Popper's point (Lecture 5) — no amount of confirmation gives certainty.
- The lecture's point: justification "rises and falls with time." What was well-justified in 1700 may be unjustified (or differently justified) today.

**Good example to use:** Any historical "well-confirmed" scientific theory later replaced — e.g. caloric theory of heat, phlogiston theory of combustion. Both were well-justified given the evidence; both turned out to be false.

**Common mistake to avoid:** Concluding from "justified beliefs can be false" that we should stop trusting science. The argument shows only that science is fallible, not that all beliefs are equally (un)trustworthy.

</details>
