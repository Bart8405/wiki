# Gap 3 — EU CBAM Exposure for Indonesia

*Synthesis note for Task 9 evidence-chase. Relates to §4 Gap 3 in argument-logic.md and supports the "what Indonesia should do now" section of the essay.*

---

## Why CBAM matters for the essay in one paragraph

The EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) enters its **definitive application regime on 1 January 2026**. It creates an external revenue incentive that the essay should name explicitly: if Indonesia's domestic carbon price remains near zero, **EU importers of Indonesian steel and aluminium will pay the full CBAM certificate cost** — and that money goes to the EU budget, not to Indonesia. A genuine domestic carbon price allows Indonesian exporters to deduct it from CBAM obligations. Every dollar of domestic carbon revenue Indonesia fails to capture from these export sectors will instead be collected by Brussels. This is the sharpest "what Indonesia should do now" argument in the essay, and it requires no appeal to abstract climate ethics — it is pure fiscal self-interest.

---

## What CBAM covers

Products subject to CBAM from 2026:
- Cement
- Iron and steel (**biggest exposure for Indonesia**)
- Aluminium (significant exposure)
- Fertilisers (highest tariff rate exposure per tonne)
- Electricity
- Hydrogen

---

## Indonesia's quantified exposure

| Sector | 2023 Indonesian exports to EU | CBAM tariff exposure |
|---|---|---|
| Steel (iron & steel) | USD 726.84 million | High — carbon-intensive production methods |
| Aluminium | USD 29.17 million | 12.14% tariff rate cited |
| Fertilisers | USD 0.29 million | 138.72% tariff rate cited (highest) |

- EU ranks **5th** among Indonesia's export destinations (~7.4% of total exports)
- Approximately **6.2% of these exports** fall under CBAM coverage
- Indonesia faces higher CBAM costs than Japan and South Korea due to more carbon-intensive production processes

**Important caveat on nickel:** Indonesia is the world's largest nickel producer and is developing a downstream nickel-to-steel (and nickel-to-EV battery) sector under Jokowi/Prabowo's "downstreaming" policy. Steel from nickel-based Indonesian integrated mills could face significant CBAM exposure as EU imports from this sector grow. This is not yet captured in 2023 trade data but is strategically important.

---

## The carbon-price gap and its CBAM consequence

| Price | 2025 |
|---|---|
| EU ETS allowance (EUA) price | ~€70–75/tCO2 |
| Indonesia domestic carbon price (ETS) | ~USD 2/tCO2 |
| Gap | ~€68–73/tCO2 |

At current EU EUA prices, Indonesian steel exporters to the EU face a CBAM certificate cost close to the full EU carbon price (~€70/tonne of CO2 embedded in their products), because Indonesia's domestic price credits are near-zero.

**Rough fiscal consequence for Indonesian steel sector:**
- If Indonesian steel to EU embeds ~1.8 tCO2 per tonne of steel (typical for coal-based production), and USD 726 million worth of steel is exported annually, CBAM cost ≈ USD 726M × (tCO2/tonne steel) × (~€70–75/tCO2). This is a significant competitiveness cost.

---

## The incentive structure — why CBAM is a gift for the essay's argument

CBAM creates a **perverse irony** that the essay can exploit:
- Indonesia has resisted raising its domestic carbon price, citing competitiveness concerns (the "ETS should not disadvantage Indonesian industry" argument)
- CBAM means that *by not raising its domestic price*, Indonesia is actually **making its export industries less competitive** in EU markets — and transferring potential domestic fiscal revenue to Brussels
- A domestic price of €30/tCO2 would reduce CBAM liability while generating Indonesian state revenue — the opposite of what the "keep prices low to protect competitiveness" argument predicts

This is the cleanest "what should Indonesia do now" recommendation: raise the domestic carbon price to the level required to neutralise CBAM exposure, using the revenue to compensate affected workers and communities. The Tony Blair Institute's USD 12–13/tCO2 coal retirement MAC is the right domestic benchmark; CBAM is the external pressure lever.

---

## Indonesia's current policy response to CBAM

- Indonesia is "working closely with the European Commission to develop an ETS to offset any potential tariffs and duties imposed under CBAM" (per Argus Media / ICAP sources)
- This confirms that CBAM is explicitly motivating Indonesia's ETS development — but the ETS at current prices (~USD 2/tCO2) still leaves ~95% of CBAM exposure uncovered
- Ministry of Industry published an Industrial Decarbonisation Roadmap in 2025; Ministerial Regulation on Industrial Decarbonisation planned for 2026 — this suggests Indonesia is trying to manage the CBAM transition, but operationally is behind
