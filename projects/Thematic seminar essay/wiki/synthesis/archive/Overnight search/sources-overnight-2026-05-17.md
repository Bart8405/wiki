## Category A — Indonesia-specific: ETS, carbon tax, political economy

### 1. AMRO — "Cap First, and Then Tax: Carbon-Pricing in Indonesia"
**Publisher:** ASEAN+3 Macroeconomic Research Office (AMRO)  
**Link:** https://amro-asia.org/cap-first-and-then-tax-carbon-pricing-in-indonesia/  
**Free:** Yes (blog/analysis page)  
**Status tag suggestion:** `GREAT_` — directly addresses the essay's central puzzle

**Key points:**
- AMRO explicitly frames Indonesia's choice as "cap first, then tax" — not an abandonment of the tax but a deliberate sequencing.
- The voluntary ETS pilot (2021, 32 coal-fired power plants) generated more deficit firms than surplus, producing 28 transactions; this informed the mandatory ETS design.
- The carbon levy under Presidential Regulation 110/2025 is designed to float *with* the ETS market price, not as a separately set rate — which is why deferring the tax until the ETS develops a credible price signal is internally consistent as a policy design.

**Where to use:** Section 1c (political-economy case for ETS first); Section 2 (how the ETS works — backstop levy logic).

### 2. IEEFA — "Two years after launch, Indonesia's carbon market struggles to find momentum"
**Publisher:** Institute for Energy Economics and Financial Analysis (IEEFA)  
**Link:** https://ieefa.org/resources/two-years-after-launch-indonesias-carbon-market-struggles-find-momentum  
**Free:** Yes  
**Status tag suggestion:** `LOOK_`

**Key points:**
- From March to September 2025, total IDX Carbon transaction value: only IDR 1 billion (USD ~72,600). Total since the exchange launched: IDR 78 billion (~USD 4.9 million). For context, those are trivially small for a national carbon market.
- Average carbon price dropped from IDR 62,533 (USD 4.1/t) in 2023 to IDR 55,985 (USD 3.9/t) in December 2024 — the wrong direction.
- Main causes of low activity: regulatory instability (the 2025 allowance allocation was postponed, no allowances were issued and no trading occurred as of January 2026); companies waiting for firmer signals before committing.
- Institutional coordination: regulatory responsibilities split across sector-specific ministries, complicating harmonisation.

**Where to use:** Section 2 (critical view — "Indonesia has an ETS that doesn't yet trade"). This is a citable, named source for the operational hollowness of the current system.

### 4. Climate Action Tracker — Indonesia (December 2024 update)
**Publisher:** Climate Action Tracker (independent science group)  
**Link:** https://climateactiontracker.org/countries/indonesia/2024-12-10/policies-action/  
**Free:** Yes  
**Status tag suggestion:** `LOOK_`

**Key points:**
- Independent assessment of Indonesia's climate policies against Paris Agreement targets.
- Indonesia's October 2025 NDC introduced an absolute economy-wide cap (1.35 GtCO2e by 2030, unconditional; 1.27 GtCO2e conditional on international support) — a major shift from previous BAU-relative targets. The ETS expansion is meant to feed into this cap.
- Indonesia's current policies are rated "Critically Insufficient" — on track for 3°C+ warming.
- The gap between the NDC target and current policies is large; carbon pricing alone cannot close it.

**Where to use:** Section 3 (developing-country playbook) — use to set the stakes: why acceleration matters. Also supports the essay's point that Indonesia has committed to ambitious targets on paper while implementation lags.

## Category B — International / Comparative Carbon Pricing Reports

### 7. World Bank — State and Trends of Carbon Pricing 2025 (June 2025)
**Publisher:** World Bank Group  
**Link:** https://openknowledge.worldbank.org/entities/publication/e5f6e755-e6a6-4d2c-927a-23b5cc8a9b03  
**Free:** Yes (open access)  
**Status tag suggestion:** `LOOK_` (the 2024 version `LOOK_World Bank 2012` is already in the bibliography but from 2012; this is the 2025 flagship annual report — very different)

**Key points:**
- Carbon pricing revenues crossed **$100 billion globally in 2024** for the first time — a politically significant milestone demonstrating fiscal relevance.
- As of early 2025: **80 carbon pricing instruments** in operation worldwide (43 carbon taxes + 37 ETSs); up from 73 in 2023.
- **28% of global GHG emissions** are now covered by a direct carbon price.
- All large middle-income economies (Brazil, China, India, Indonesia, Türkiye) have implemented or are developing carbon pricing.
- Revenue earmarking: over half of revenues are directed to environment, infrastructure, and development — the revenue recycling recommendation the essay makes for Indonesia already has a global precedent.
- Indonesia specifically: The ETS's inclusion threshold was lowered from 100 MW to 25 MW in 2024, expanding coverage.

**Where to use:** Section 3 (developing-country playbook) — use to frame Indonesia's ETS as part of a global trend rather than an isolated experiment. The $100B revenue milestone is a compelling opening data point for a paragraph on revenue recycling potential.

### 8. OECD — Effective Carbon Rates 2025 (November 2025)
**Publisher:** OECD  
**Link:** https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2025/11/effective-carbon-rates-2025_a578dc11/a5a5d71f-en.pdf  
**Free:** Yes (PDF)  
**Status tag suggestion:** `LOOK_`

**Key points:**
- Covers **79 countries** accounting for 82% of global GHG emissions.
- Effective Carbon Rate (ECR) = the total price on carbon emissions from fuel combustion (carbon taxes + ETS permit prices + fuel excise taxes, net of fuel subsidies). More comprehensive than just the nominal carbon tax rate.
- Indonesia's ECR is dominated by fuel subsidies (which *negative* price carbon) and the tiny ETS price — meaning Indonesia's *net* carbon pricing signal is very weak or negative.
- Indonesia's ETS is "intensity-based" (tCO2 per MWh) rather than absolute-cap — OECD notes this is common among recently introduced ETSs and limits price signals.
- The country note for Indonesia is a separate free document (see source 9 below).

**Where to use:** Section 1b — to make the point that comparing Indonesia's "$2/t carbon tax" against the literature's "$30–60/t" is incomplete; the ECR framework shows Indonesia's *effective* net price is probably negative once fuel subsidies are factored in. This makes the puzzle even sharper.

### 10. UNDP — Carbon Tax in an Evolving Carbon Economy: Policy Design & Digital Innovations (March 2025)
**Publisher:** United Nations Development Programme  
**Link:** https://www.undp.org/sites/g/files/zskgke326/files/2025-03/undp-carbon-tax-in-an-evolving-carbon-economy-digital-version.pdf  
**Free:** Yes (PDF)  
**Status tag suggestion:** `LOOK_`

**Key points:**
- Practical guide for governments in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs) on designing and implementing carbon taxes.
- Recommends a **progressive carbon tax trajectory**: initial low tax rates, temporal rebates, adequate transition periods, with incremental adjustments built in upfront. This is the "graduated tax roadmap" recommendation Sunanda et al. 2025 also makes for Indonesia.
- Integrating **revenue redistribution** is central to social acceptability: protect vulnerable households, invest in green jobs and skills.
- **Digital technologies** (AI, blockchain, web data management) can improve the MRV and implementation efficiency — relevant given Indonesia's MRV readiness gap.
- Notes Indonesia explicitly as a key middle-income country grappling with tax design.

**Where to use:** Section 3, recommendation row "Phased/graduated carbon-tax roadmap" — UNDP is a high-authority source for developing-country recommendations, alongside Sunanda 2025 and van den Bergh & Drews 2026.

### 11. IISD — Implementing Carbon Pricing in Southeast Asia (policy brief, ~2024–25)
**Publisher:** International Institute for Sustainable Development  
**Link:** https://www.iisd.org/publications/brief/carbon-pricing-southeast-asia  
**Free:** Yes  
**Status tag suggestion:** `LOOK_`

**Key points:**
- Indonesia is the **first Southeast Asian country with an operational ETS** — first-mover advantage but also first-mover burden (no regional peers to learn from).
- Philippines: carbon pricing shifting from debate to legislation (20th Congress).
- Vietnam: carbon market trading postponed to end of 2026; regulatory progress continues.
- **IISD recommendation for all three:** improve MRV systems; finalize sector roadmaps; set appropriate rates; use revenues to support clean energy and protect vulnerable households.
- Indonesia's PR 110/2025 is noted as enabling international carbon trading under Article 6 — a regional first.

**Where to use:** Section 3, recommendation row "Regional/ASEAN coordination" — shows Indonesia is ahead of neighbours but cannot benefit from regional learning; makes the case for ASEAN coordination stronger.

### 12. ScienceDirect — "To tax or to trade? A global review of carbon emissions reduction strategies" (2024)
**Journal:** *Energy Strategy Reviews* (Elsevier)  
**Link:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2211467X24002177  
**Free:** Check institutional access  
**Status tag suggestion:** `LOOK_`

**Key points:**
- Meta-review of global carbon pricing strategies (over 70 carbon-based policies reviewed as of 2024).
- Finds that carbon taxes have been "extremely popular among developing economies," while ETSs were "widely adopted across developed nations."
- This is the opposite of Indonesia's sequencing choice — makes it unusual and worth explaining.
- Both instruments reduce emissions in the long run, but effectiveness varies by context; no consensus on comparative effectiveness yet.
- Political economy literature links carbon taxes to climate capitalism / narrow technocentric perspectives — relevant for the essay's critical-theory anchor.

**Where to use:** Section 1b (economic case for a tax) — use the "developing countries prefer tax" finding to highlight Indonesia's divergence from the global developing-country trend. This sharpens the puzzle.

### 13. East Asia Forum — "The case for carbon taxes in Southeast Asia" (February 2025)
**Publisher:** East Asia Forum (ANU Crawford School)  
**Link:** https://eastasiaforum.org/2025/02/22/the-case-for-carbon-taxes-in-southeast-asia/  
**Free:** Yes  
**Status tag suggestion:** `LOOK_`

**Key points:**
- ADB's 2024 climate change perception survey of 14 Asian economies: **over 70% of Southeast Asian respondents support a national carbon tax**; 93% willing to bear personal costs.
- This directly challenges the political-economy assumption that a carbon tax is "toxic" for public opinion in Indonesia — the essay should engage with this finding.
- Arguments for a carbon tax over ETS in SEA: simpler to administer, easier to layer onto existing fuel-tax infrastructure, lower MRV compliance cost for smaller firms and the informal sector.
- Notes Indonesia as the exception that chose ETS first.

**Where to use:** Section 1d (the conflicting fact: tax may not be as politically costly as assumed) — the 70%+ public support figure is a powerful counter-punch to the "tax is political poison" framing.
