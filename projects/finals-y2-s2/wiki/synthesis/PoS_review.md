## idiographic approach

**Definition:** The idiographic approach aims to understand the *particular* — the unique characteristics, meaning, and context of a specific case, event, or individual. It is associated with the human sciences (history, literary studies, philosophy) and contrasts with the nomothetic goal of discovering universal laws. Windelband coined both terms around 1894 to argue that the natural-vs-human-science divide is about a *choice of approach*, not just a choice of subject matter.

**Your question:** what is the difference or connection between nomothetic and idiographic and deduction/induction? Feels quite similar
**Answer:** They look similar but operate on different axes. **Nomothetic vs. idiographic** is about your *goal*: are you trying to find universal laws (nomothetic) or deeply understand one particular case (idiographic)? **Deduction vs. induction** is about your *method of reasoning*: deduction moves from general principles down to specific conclusions ("if all societies under trade pressure face more migration, and the EU faces trade pressure, then…"); induction moves from specific observations up to general patterns ("I've studied ten guest-worker schemes and they all ended the same way, so…"). The two dimensions can combine in any configuration: a physicist is nomothetic and deductive (deriving hypotheses from theory); a historian is idiographic and inductive (building an interpretation of this specific revolution from archival evidence). The practical tip for your PoS exam: when asked to classify a method, ask *two separate questions* — what knowledge type is the researcher after (law or particular?) AND what logical path do they use (from theory down, or from data up)?

## stikstofcrisis

**Definition:** The Dutch *stikstofcrisis* (nitrogen crisis) is an ongoing environmental and political crisis in the Netherlands triggered by court rulings in 2019–2021 that found Dutch nitrogen emissions — mostly ammonia from intensive livestock farming — violated EU Natura 2000 habitat protection laws. Farmers must dramatically cut livestock numbers, leading to large protests (tractors on highways), political upheaval, and the rise of a farmers' party (BoerBurgerBeweging).

**Explained easily:** The Netherlands has the world's most intensive farming per square metre, and decades of ammonia from cow and pig manure have been slowly poisoning protected nature reserves. Courts finally said "this is illegal" — and overnight, farmers were told to halve their herds. The crisis shows why one discipline isn't enough: you need chemistry (what ammonia does to soil pH), ecology (which protected species are dying), law and political science (which EU directives govern this and which coalitions can pass reform), economics (how to compensate farmers or transform the sector), and history (why Dutch farming got this intensive in the first place).
