# Week 11 — Globalization and Populism

*Why did globalization produce populism? Three stacked frameworks: sociological (thinning of social ties), political-economic (Rodrik's trilemma), psychological (Mishra's globalization of rage).*

**Date:** 11 May 2026 · **Lecturer:** Dr. Steven Denney · **Key thinker:** ==**Dani Rodrik**== — for the globalization trilemma and the demand/supply distinction in populism; Pankaj Mishra (rage) and Heywood & Whitham (thinning) as the other two layers.

**Lecturer's explicit exam focus:** the three frameworks and Rodrik's trilemma. The East Asia segment is *illustrative, not on the exam*.

## Key concepts — definitions

| Term | Definition | Source |
|---|---|---|
| **Thick / thin connectedness** | Thick = face-to-face, durable, localized social ties (village, parish, union); thin = mobile, individualized, digitally mediated (networks, platforms). Social life has been thinning for a century, fast in the last 25 years. | LEC11 / Heywood & Whitham ch.7 |
| **Precariat** | Guy Standing (2011): the new class of workers on insecure, low-wage, fixed-term labor without the protections their parents' generation had. A sizable fraction of the under-40 workforce — and they vote differently. | LEC11 / Standing |
| **Risk society** | Ulrich Beck (1992): a society whose politics is increasingly organized around the distribution of *bads* (climate, pandemics, financial crises) rather than *goods* (jobs, housing). Modern institutions produce the very risks they claim to manage. | LEC11 |
| **Liquid modernity** | Zygmunt Bauman (2000): old social forms (family, class, profession) once fixed are now fluid. Freedom and uncertainty arrive together — people are told they are free; many feel adrift. | LEC11 |
| **Network society** | Manuel Castells (1996): society organized through decentralized, code-mediated networks — horizontal in form, asymmetric in practice. The same networks that allowed cosmopolitan publics also enabled nationalist publics, often more efficiently because grievance moves faster than nuance. | LEC11 |
| **McWorld** | Benjamin Barber (1995): cultural homogenization — global brands, Hollywood, English-language media, a flattened aesthetic. | LEC11 |
| **Glocalization** | Roland Robertson (1995): global products taking local turns (kimchi burgers in Seoul, Bollywood adaptations). Hybridization rather than pure homogenization. Both happen at once. | LEC11 |
| ==**Globalization trilemma**== | ==Rodrik (2011, 2018): deep globalization, the nation-state, and democratic politics cannot all be fully maintained at once — pick any two. A structural constraint, not a moral claim.== | LEC11 / Rodrik |
| **Deep globalization** | Regulatory harmonization across jurisdictions, *not* aggregate trade volume. A country can trade a lot and still stay on the nation-state-plus-democracy corner. This is the depth that binds policy autonomy. | LEC11 |
| **Stolper–Samuelson (intuition)** | Trade liberalization makes some factors of production *absolutely* worse off, not just relatively. Late-stage globalization is more about distribution than expansion. (The China trade shock cost ~1 in 20 US manufacturing jobs in exposed regions.) | LEC11 |
| **Compensation problem** | Even when trade gains are large in aggregate, redistribution to losers fails. Two failure modes: it's *distortionary* (costly to redistribute) and *time-inconsistent* (winners promise compensation before liberalization, renege after). | LEC11 / Rodrik 2018 |
| ==**Globalization of rage**== | ==Pankaj Mishra (2016, 2017): globalization produced not only inequality but psychic dislocation. Modernity promised dignity and did not deliver; the political affect is resentment, organized around identity and belonging.== | LEC11 / Mishra |
| **Demand vs. supply (of populism)** | Rodrik's distinction. *Demand:* globalization shocks generate anti-establishment grievance. *Supply:* which cleavage gets activated depends on the type of shock — trade/finance shocks → class cleavage → left populism (Latin America); immigration shocks → ethno-cultural cleavage → right populism (Western Europe). | LEC11 |
| **Populism as repertoire** | A political style of opposition (people vs. elite) that can be picked up by parties of left or right — distinct from nationalism. Populism is portable; nationalism is structural. | LEC11 |
| **Left vs. right populism** | Left: class cleavage, targets are multinationals / IMF / financial elites (Chávez, Morales, Kirchner, Correa). Right: identity cleavage, targets are immigrants / supranational institutions / cosmopolitan elites (Le Pen, Orbán, Trump, Brexit, Meloni). | LEC11 / Rodrik 2018 |

## Lecture walkthrough

### The three-layer diagnosis

Today's argument is stacked: three authors, three layers, each needs the others to make sense.

| Author | Layer | Core claim |
|---|---|---|
| Heywood & Whitham (ch.7) | Sociological | Social life thinned — from face-to-face community to mobile, individualized, digitally mediated ties |
| Rodrik (2018) | Political-economic | Deep globalization, nation-state, and democracy cannot all be fully maintained at once |
| Mishra (2016) | Psychological / emotional | Modernity promised dignity and did not deliver — the political affect is rage |

The *conditions* (Heywood & Whitham) + the *constraint* (Rodrik) + the *affect* (Mishra) = populism.

### Layer 1 — Society in a global age (Heywood & Whitham)

**From thick to thin connectedness.** Industrial societies built stable jobs, unions, careers, social trust. Post-industrial economies built service work, gig work, short-term contracts → the **precariat** (Standing 2011).

Beck's **risk society** and Bauman's **liquid modernity** are two diagnoses of the same shift: when stable forms dissolve, politics changes shape. Anxiety, isolation, and meaninglessness fill the gap.

Castells's **network society** shows the architecture of thin connectedness. The same networks that allowed cosmopolitan publics also allowed nationalist publics — often more efficiently, because **grievance moves faster than nuance**.

Cultural globalization is *both* McWorld (homogenization) *and* glocalization (hybridization). They happen at once.

What this layer buys us analytically: the *conditions* under which a politics of resentment becomes possible — but not yet *why* those conditions produce *this* politics. For that, we need Rodrik.

### Layer 2 — Rodrik's globalization trilemma

The lecture's centerpiece. Three things cannot all be maximally pursued:

```
       Deep globalization
              /\
             /  \
            /    \
The nation-state — Democratic politics
            Pick any two
```

> *"We cannot simultaneously pursue democracy, national self-determination, and economic globalization."* — Rodrik (2011)

**What each combination costs:**

| You keep | You sacrifice | Empirical signature |
|---|---|---|
| Globalization + democracy | National policy autonomy | EU — technocratic delegation, supranational rules |
| Globalization + nation-state | Democracy | "Golden straitjacket": open economy + technocratic governance with thin democratic content |
| Nation-state + democracy | Depth of globalization | Tariffs, capital controls, withdrawal from supranational rules |

**Crucial:** "depth" is *regulatory harmonization across jurisdictions*, NOT *aggregate trade volume*. Brexit Britain still trades plenty; it just refuses to harmonize regulations. A country can trade a lot and stay on the nation-state-plus-democracy corner.

**Why trade generates losers (Stolper–Samuelson):** Trade liberalization makes some factors absolutely worse off, not just relatively. Late-stage globalization is more about distribution than expansion. The China trade shock cost roughly 1 in 20 US manufacturing jobs in exposed regions (Autor, Dorn & Hanson).

**Why compensation rarely comes:** (1) Distortionary — redistributing requires taxes/transfers that themselves cost efficiency. (2) Time-inconsistent — winners promise compensation *before* liberalization when losers' votes are needed; they renege *after* when the political moment has passed. Continental European welfare states are the partial exception: where compensation arrived, populist backlash arrived later and weaker.

**Five-country diagnosis (the in-class exercise):**

| Country | Corner | Sacrifice |
|---|---|---|
| **Greece (Eurozone, 2010s)** | Globalization + sovereignty (formal) | Substantive democracy — the 2015 referendum: voters said no, the policy didn't move |
| **Brexit UK (2016–)** | Sovereignty + democracy | Depth of globalization — "take back control" = give up regulatory harmonization |
| **China (post-2001)** | Globalization + nation-state | Democracy was never on the table; the combination is coherent, not paradoxical |
| **US under Trump** | Sovereignty + democracy | Depth of globalization rolled back — same corner as Brexit, sustained and electorally renewed |
| **Argentina (Milei era)** | Cycles between corners | A treadmill, not a fate — same country, different governments, different corners |

### Layer 3 — Mishra and the globalization of rage

Rodrik gives the constraint, not the affect. Mishra (2016, 2017) fills in the emotional layer: globalization produces **uprooting** — the loss of inherited frames that gave life shape. People told they are free feel abandoned. Abandonment breeds resentment. The result is rage looking for political form.

> *"Self-seeking individuals with very different pasts now find themselves herded together into a global marketplace ... where humiliating new hierarchies emerge."* — Mishra (2016)

**The long arc:** Today's reactionary movements have ancestors in 19th-century romantic nationalists, anarchists, revolutionaries. Same grievances, different content. The lineage matters — this is not a temporary pathology.

### Variations in populism — Rodrik's demand vs. supply

| | Left-wing populism | Right-wing populism |
|---|---|---|
| **Cleavage** | Class — the people vs. the rich | Identity — the people vs. outsiders |
| **Targets** | Multinationals, neoliberalism, IMF, financial elites | Immigrants, supranational institutions, cosmopolitan elites |
| **Geographic core** | Latin America, parts of Southern Europe | Western and Eastern Europe, North America |
| **Examples** | Chávez, Morales, Kirchner, Correa | Le Pen, Orbán, Trump, Brexit, Wilders, Meloni |

**Rodrik's answer to why the variation:** *Demand* (anti-establishment grievance) comes from globalization shocks; *supply* (which cleavage activates) depends on the *type* of shock. Trade/finance shocks → class cleavage → left. Immigration shocks → ethno-cultural cleavage → right. The US in the 2010s had both at once → Sanders and Trump on different cleavages of the same crisis.

**Important pedagogical caveat (Denney flagged this hard):** "Globalization explains right-wing populism" is the lazy reading. Rodrik's framework resists this. Globalization explains the *demand* for populism. It does *not* explain which form populism takes. Two countries hit by the same shock can produce opposite political responses.

### Populism vs. nationalism — the distinction

From the East Asia segment (not on the exam, but the take-home is exam-relevant): **populism is a repertoire; nationalism is a structure.** Populism is a political style that can be picked up and put down. Nationalism is the structural condition within which the repertoire either takes or does not.

## Key thinker — Dani Rodrik

Turkish-American economist at Harvard's Kennedy School (b. 1957). His *Globalization Paradox* (2011) introduced the trilemma; his 2018 article *Populism and the economics of globalization* applied the framework to explain why right-wing populism dominates in Western Europe and left-wing populism in Latin America.

His contribution is not pessimism about globalization. It is *clarity about the trade-offs*: any combination of two corners is coherent. The political question is which corner a society chooses and who pays the cost of the sacrifice.

*(Pankaj Mishra: Indian essayist and novelist. His* Age of Anger *(2017) argues that today's reactionary politics has 19th-century ancestors and is the predictable emotional response to the broken promises of modernity. The* Guardian/Foreign Affairs *article (2016) "Welcome to the age of anger" is the lecture's key text.)*

---

> **Explained easily — Rodrik's trilemma:**
> Imagine three things you want at full strength: (1) a fully open global economy where capital and goods flow freely, (2) your country making its own rules, (3) your country being democratic. You can pick any two. The EU picked open economy + democracy but gave up sovereignty (Brussels makes the rules). China picked open economy + sovereignty but skipped democracy. Brexit Britain picked sovereignty + democracy but is closing off the open economy. There's no fourth option where you get all three — somebody, somewhere, is paying for what you chose.

> **Explained easily — demand vs. supply of populism:**
> Globalization creates a lot of angry people (demand). But anger needs a story to attach itself to. In Latin America, the story available is "the rich are screwing the poor" — so anger becomes left-wing populism against multinationals and the IMF. In Western Europe, the story available is "outsiders are taking your jobs and culture" — so anger becomes right-wing populism against immigrants and Brussels. Same anger, different story, different politics.

> **Explained easily — populism is a repertoire, nationalism is a structure:**
> Think of nationalism like a building — it's a long-running structural form your political community lives in. Populism is more like a piece of music you can play inside that building. Different countries hit by the same globalization shock pick up the populism record at different volumes (or not at all), because their underlying nationalism-buildings have different acoustics. Korea and Japan have strong nationalisms but very little durable populism. The US has both at high volume right now.

### Multiple Choice Questions

**Q1.** According to Rodrik's globalization trilemma, which combination describes the contemporary EU?
- A) Deep globalization + nation-state (democracy sacrificed)
- B) Deep globalization + democracy (national policy autonomy sacrificed)
- C) Nation-state + democracy (depth of globalization sacrificed)
- D) All three corners are fully maintained

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B) Deep globalization + democracy (national policy autonomy sacrificed)**

The EU is the canonical case of trading sovereignty for integration + democracy. Member states agree to common rules made in Brussels; the Greek 2015 referendum (voters reject bailout terms; policy doesn't move) is the lecture's illustration. Option A is China; Option C is Brexit/Trump.

</details>

### Short Essay / Open Questions

**Essay Q1.** Explain Rodrik's globalization trilemma and use it to compare two of the following cases: Greece in the Eurozone, Brexit UK, China, the US under Trump.

<details>
<summary>Model answer outline</summary>

**Key points:**
- State the trilemma: deep globalization, nation-state, democracy — pick any two. Define "deep" as regulatory harmonization, not trade volume.
- Pick two cases and place each on its corner:
  - **Greece** = globalization + (formal) sovereignty, substantive democracy squeezed (2015 referendum case)
  - **Brexit UK / Trump US** = nation-state + democracy, depth of globalization sacrificed
  - **China** = globalization + nation-state, democracy was never on the table
- Compare: same constraint, different sacrifice. The trilemma is a *constraint, not a destination* — Argentina shows the corners can shift over time.
- Note Rodrik's broader point: each corner is *coherent*, not paradoxical. The trilemma is a constraint, not a moral judgment.

**Common mistake to avoid:** Treating "globalization" in the trilemma as trade volume. Brexit Britain trades plenty — it gave up *regulatory depth*, not volume.

</details>

---

**Essay Q2.** Why does globalization produce populism, and why does the form of populism differ between Western Europe and Latin America?

<details>
<summary>Model answer outline</summary>

**Key points:**
- Three-layer diagnosis: sociological (Heywood & Whitham — thinning of ties, precariat), political-economic (Rodrik — trilemma + Stolper-Samuelson losers + compensation failure), psychological (Mishra — rage from broken promises of modernity).
- Apply Rodrik's demand/supply distinction:
  - **Demand** = anti-establishment grievance generated by globalization shocks (universal).
  - **Supply** = depends on the *type* of shock. Trade/finance shocks → class cleavage → left populism. Immigration shocks → ethno-cultural cleavage → right populism.
- Latin America: trade and financial crises hit class cleavages → Chávez, Morales, Kirchner, Correa.
- Western Europe: immigration shocks (post-2015 refugee crisis) hit ethno-cultural cleavages → Le Pen, Orbán, AfD, Meloni.
- USA in 2010s: both shocks at once → Sanders (left) and Trump (right) on different cleavages of the same crisis.
- Caveat: "globalization explains right-wing populism" is the lazy reading. The variable doing the political work is the cleavage activated by the *type* of shock, not globalization itself.

**Good example:** The US case is the clearest illustration — same country, same period, two populisms (Sanders + Trump) along different cleavages of the same crisis.

</details>
