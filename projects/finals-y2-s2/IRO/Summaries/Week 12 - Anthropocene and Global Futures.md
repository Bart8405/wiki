## Lecture walkthrough

### The setup — twelve weeks of toolkit, one test case

The course built a toolkit: sovereignty, anarchy, balance of power, regimes, identity, security, world orders, regional integration, nationalism, populism. This final lecture asks: **is that toolkit equipped for the planetary problems we now face?**

The argument has two sections:
- **Section A:** The Anthropocene as a problem for IR (Mitchell's article).
- **Section B:** Four future challenges (the chapter on images of global futures).

### Section A — Is IR going extinct?

Mitchell's article (the title is a pun): a mass extinction event may already be under way; IR claims to be a "theory of survival" yet has no framework for this; the discipline keeps extinction in the footnotes; hence the question — is IR itself going extinct?

**The structural mismatch:**
- IR's basic unit is the *state* — bounded and territorial.
- Planetary processes (climate, biodiversity, oceans) do not respect borders or treaties and operate on *geological timescales*.
- Sovereignty was built for a different scale of problem. The mismatch is structural, not just a gap in policy.

**The ontological gap (Mitchell's deeper claim):**
- The standard response to environmental crisis is *better management* — better treaties, better data, better targets.
- This treats extinction as a problem of biopolitical management.
- But the gap is **ontological**: the field cannot yet think the *loss of life forms as such*. Counting species and grasping what is lost are different tasks.

**Anthropocentrism as the blind spot:**
- IR centers a particular image of "humanity" as the only subject worth political consideration.
- Nonhumans and inhuman forces are treated as backdrop — scenery for human politics.
- Survival becomes "our" survival on "our" terms.
- That premise rules questions about extinction out of bounds. The taboo is *structural, not evidential* — it's baked into how the field is set up, not a matter of insufficient evidence.

**What Mitchell proposes instead — post-humanist / planetary thinking:**
- Decentering the human as the only subject of ethics.
- Taking nonhuman and inhuman forces seriously as actors.
- Extending IR's temporal horizon to before and after humans.
- *Cosmopolitics* — politics rooted in a shared planet.
- An ethics of *gratitude and welcome* that goes beyond survival.

### Section B — Images of global futures

How we picture the future shapes what we do now — futures are political claims. The chapter walks through four future challenges.

**1. Technology and AI.**
- Capability moves faster than governance — rapid capability gains, slow governance response.
- *Dual-use problems*: the same tool secures and surveils.
- Autonomy in weapons, decisions, infrastructure.
- Who writes the rules, and for whom? Risk is distributed unevenly across regions and classes.

**2. The space frontier.**
- Great-power competition extended beyond Earth — dual-use satellites, anti-satellite weapons, lunar resources.
- A 1960s treaty regime for a very different industry.
- Private actors (SpaceX, etc.) now rival states in launch and orbit.
- A governance vacuum at altitude and on the ground.

**3. Ecology and planetary boundaries.**
- Biodiversity loss as a security problem; climate change as a *threat multiplier* across regions.
- *Tipping points* — nonlinear, hard to reverse, easy to ignore.
- The *nine planetary boundaries*: climate, biodiversity, biogeochemical, freshwater, land-use, ocean acidification, aerosols, ozone, novel entities. Several already in the high-risk zone.
- "Resilience" discourse can become a way of asking the vulnerable to absorb the cost. Equity shapes access to adaptation.
- For IR, the question is *not the science* — it's whose policies push the boundaries and who pays when they break.

**4. Migration as a future challenge.**
- Climate-driven displacement projected at unprecedented scale.
- Refugee law was written for a different kind of crisis.
- Internal displacement often exceeds cross-border movement.
- Border politics increasingly shaped by *anticipated* flows.

**Fertility decline (Denney's addition):** Almost everything is downstream of fertility. Below replacement (TFR<2.1), labor, pensions, growth all turn. Migration pressure rises mechanically — shrinking places need workers; growing places supply them. Geopolitical balance moves with population structure — Africa grows; East Asia and Europe shrink. The "global relationship recession" (Burn-Murdoch, FT 2025) is the deeper driver.

**The political work futures do:** Futures are *tools*. They can open or close political space. "Inevitable" futures often serve the interests of the present. Alternative futures are a resource for politics — picturing differently is itself political work.

### Course in review — three through-lines

The second half of the lecture is a course recap rather than new exam content. The three through-lines worth pulling forward:

1. **Constructivism and Global IR.** Sister critiques of mainstream theory — both ask who gets to define what counts as theory and refuse the realist claim that material power explains everything.
2. **Nationalism and populism.** Analytically separable, empirically often fused. Nationalism = structure; populism = repertoire. The 2010s–2020s show why: backlash mobilizes the nation. A favorite short-answer trap is collapsing the two.
3. **Rodrik's globalization trilemma.** One idea running through three lectures — previewed in Week 8 (world orders), carried Week 11 (populism), reappears Week 12 when the planet enters the picture. Economic, political, and ecological strain on the same constraint.

**Meta-lesson:** IR is a contested toolkit. Every tool answers a different question. The post-1989 settlement is fraying along several seams at once. The course gave you the language for those seams.

## Key thinker — Audra Mitchell

International relations scholar (Wilfrid Laurier University, Canada). Specialist in environmental politics, extinction, and posthumanism. Her 2017 article *Is IR going extinct?* (European Journal of International Relations) is the canonical statement of the argument that IR's state-centric, anthropocentric ontology cannot grasp the politics of planetary loss. Her follow-up with Aadita Chaudhury (2020) pushes the argument further, contrasting Western apocalyptic visions with BIPOC futurisms.

Her project is *not* environmental policy reform from within IR. It is an *ontological* critique — asking what the field cannot see because of how it is built.

*(Related thinkers worth flagging: Jairus Grove,* Savage Ecology *(2018) — the "Eurocene" thesis that the planetary crisis is a specifically Euro-American product, mentioned in Week 7. Mark Lacy on the futures of control. Pankaj Mishra on the affective register of crisis — Week 11.)*

---

> **Explained easily — IR's ontological problem with extinction:**
> Imagine you go to your doctor about a pain in your leg, and the doctor only owns measuring tools for the upper body. The doctor will keep measuring your arms and chest and finding nothing wrong, because the tools were built for a different part of the body. Mitchell says IR is like that doctor — its tools (state, sovereignty, anarchy) were built for one kind of problem (war and order between states). Planetary extinction is a different kind of problem entirely, and the tools don't reach it. You can't fix it by measuring more carefully with the wrong instrument.

> **Explained easily — anthropocentrism as a blind spot:**
> Think about how a city is designed only for cars. You can keep adding more roads and parking and it will still feel hostile to pedestrians, cyclists, and animals — because the design started from one user. IR was designed with one user in mind: humans, organized into states, pursuing their survival. Everything non-human (forests, oceans, species) appears only as resource or backdrop. To take extinction seriously, you would have to redesign the city — not just add more parking.

> **Explained easily — futures as political tools:**
> When a politician says "automation is inevitable" or "climate change is too late to stop," they're not just describing the future — they're using a future to do work in the present. If a future is "inevitable," then you don't need to fight about it. That's why the picture of the future is itself political: alternative pictures (e.g. that climate policy is still possible, that AI can be regulated) open up political space that "inevitable" futures close down.

### Short Essay / Open Questions

**Essay Q1.** Why does Mitchell argue that IR is "going extinct," and what does she propose instead? Distinguish ontology from management in your answer.

<details>
<summary>Model answer outline</summary>

**Key points:**
- State the title pun: extinction is now a stake in IR (mass extinction event may be under way); IR claims to be a "theory of survival" yet has no framework for it; the discipline keeps extinction in the footnotes; hence the question — is IR itself going extinct?
- The structural mismatch: state-centric ontology (bounded, territorial, short-timescale) vs. planetary processes (border-crossing, geological timescales). Sovereignty was built for a different scale of problem.
- The ontology/management distinction (this is the load-bearing move):
  - Management = better treaties, better targets, better data. Treats extinction as a biopolitical problem.
  - Ontology = the field cannot yet *think* the loss of life forms as such. Counting species and grasping what is lost are different tasks.
- The blind spot: anthropocentrism. IR centers a particular image of "humanity" as the only subject. The taboo is structural, not evidential.
- Mitchell's alternative: post-humanist / planetary thinking. Decenter the human as the only subject of ethics; take nonhuman forces as actors; extend the temporal horizon before and after humans; *cosmopolitics* rooted in a shared planet; ethics of gratitude and welcome beyond survival.

**Common mistake to avoid:** Treating this as a call for "better environmental policy." Mitchell's point is that better policy from within the existing ontology cannot reach the problem — the issue is what the field can *see* in the first place.

</details>

**Essay Q2.** Pick two of the four future challenges (technology/AI, space, ecology, migration) and explain why they test the limits of the IR toolkit.

<details>
<summary>Model answer outline</summary>

**Key points (pick any two; here's a sample using AI and ecology):**

**AI:**
- Capability outruns governance. Sovereign states are slow; AI capability gains are rapid.
- Dual-use problem: the same tool secures and surveils.
- Autonomy in weapons and decisions challenges the assumption that political authority sits with humans inside states.
- Who writes the rules, and for whom? Risk is distributed unevenly — most users of large AI systems are not in countries that make them.
- Toolkit limit: IR has no good account of non-state corporate actors (Anthropic, OpenAI, Microsoft) wielding strategic infrastructure.

**Ecology:**
- Biodiversity loss as a security problem; climate as a *threat multiplier*.
- Tipping points are nonlinear — IR's treaty-based gradualism is the wrong tempo.
- Planetary boundaries framework: nine earth-system processes, several already exceeded.
- "Resilience" discourse shifts costs onto the vulnerable.
- Toolkit limit: state-centric ontology can't think planetary timescales or non-human actors (Mitchell's argument).

**Cross-cutting point:** Both challenges expose the same structural mismatch — the IR toolkit was built for inter-state politics on human timescales, but planetary problems operate at different scales and involve actors (planetary systems, algorithms, non-humans) that the toolkit cannot easily admit.

**Good example for AI:** The space frontier point about a 1960s treaty regime for a 21st-century industry — same logic applies to AI governance: institutions designed for an earlier problem-set.

</details>
