## Key concepts — definitions

| Term | Definition | Source |
|---|---|---|
| **Nuclear proliferation** | The spread of nuclear weapons (and the materials, technology, and know-how to make them) to states that do not currently possess them. *Horizontal proliferation* = more states get the bomb; *vertical proliferation* = existing nuclear states build more / better weapons. | Heywood Ch.12 |
| **Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT, 1968/1970)** | The cornerstone treaty: (1) recognised nuclear-weapon states (US, USSR/Russia, UK, France, China) agree to pursue disarmament; (2) non-nuclear states agree NOT to acquire nukes; (3) all parties have the right to peaceful nuclear technology. 191 parties — the most widely subscribed arms-control treaty. | Heywood Ch.12 |
| **The "nuclear haves" / "have-nots"** | The NPT's central inequality — 5 official nuclear-weapon states are permitted; everyone else is permanently barred. Source of the legitimacy critique by Global South states and India/Pakistan/Israel. | Heywood Ch.12 |
| **Nuclear deterrence** | A state acquires/maintains nuclear weapons to deter attack — the threat of unacceptable retaliation makes aggression irrational. The realist-favoured rationale for proliferation. | LEC3 / Sagan |
| **MAD (Mutually Assured Destruction)** | The Cold War doctrine: if both sides have second-strike capability (can retaliate after being hit), neither can win a nuclear war → both refrain. Stabilising under tight conditions, terrifying under loose ones. | Heywood Ch.12 |
| **Second-strike capability** | The ability to retaliate after absorbing a first strike. Required for MAD to work. Usually means dispersed, hardened, or sea-based delivery systems. | Heywood Ch.12 |
| ==**Sagan's three models (1996)**== | ==Three explanations for why states build nuclear weapons: (1) **Security model** — to deter threats they can't meet otherwise; (2) **Domestic politics model** — bureaucratic and political coalitions push the bomb forward; (3) **Norms model** — nukes as symbols of modernity, prestige, identity.== | Sagan 1996 |
| ==**Security model (Sagan)**== | ==The "obvious" realist answer: states build nukes when facing a significant military threat that cannot be met through alternative means (alliances, conventional balance). If the threat goes away or alliance protection arrives, they refrain.== | Sagan 1996 |
| ==**Domestic politics model (Sagan)**== | ==Nuclear decisions are products of internal coalitions: military bureaucracies seeking missions and budgets; weapons-lab scientists seeking work; politicians seeking electoral gain or domestic legitimacy. States pursue bombs because *parochial interests* push for them.== | Sagan 1996 |
| ==**Norms model (Sagan)**== | ==Nuclear weapons are valued as **symbolic** — markers of modernity, sovereignty, civilisational status. States acquire (or refrain from) the bomb partly because of what it *signifies* to themselves and to others, not just what it *does* militarily.== | Sagan 1996 |
| **Iran case (typical exam case)** | Multiple drivers at once: SECURITY (Israeli + US threats; regional rivalries); DOMESTIC POLITICS (IRGC bureaucratic interests; nationalist mobilization); NORMS (post-Iraq sense of inequity; status). Hard to reduce to any single model. | Sagan 1996 / textbook |
| **South Africa (case for security model failing)** | Built nukes under apartheid; voluntarily dismantled in 1989–93. Sagan: shows that when security calculus changes (end of Cold War + regional détente), states can give up the bomb — but the timing + transparency suggests *norms* also mattered. | Sagan 1996 |
| **Ukraine (Budapest Memorandum 1994)** | Surrendered Soviet-era nuclear arsenal in exchange for US/UK/Russia security assurances. Russia's 2014 + 2022 invasions of Ukraine are widely read as the assurances having been broken — a hard test of whether states will EVER give up nukes again. | Heywood Ch.12 |
| **Vertical vs horizontal proliferation** | Horizontal = more states get nukes (the NPT's focus). Vertical = existing nuclear states build more / better weapons (the NPT's neglected half — covered by US-Russia bilateral treaties like New START, now unwinding). | Heywood Ch.12 |
| **Nuclear taboo (Tannenwald)** | The norm that nuclear weapons should never be used in combat. Holds since 1945 — Hiroshima/Nagasaki remain the only combat use. A strong empirical regularity, but its robustness is uncertain given current tensions. | Heywood Ch.12 |
| **Proliferation optimists vs pessimists** | Optimists (Waltz): more nuclear states → more deterrence → fewer wars. Pessimists (Sagan): more nuclear states → more accidents, theft, escalation, and decisions by parochial actors → higher risk. The canonical Waltz-Sagan debate. | Heywood Ch.12 / Sagan |
| **Disarmament** | The deliberate reduction or elimination of nuclear arsenals. NPT Article VI requires it of nuclear-weapon states; in practice, it has stalled. Treaty on the Prohibition of Nuclear Weapons (TPNW, 2017) is the recent Global South push. | Heywood Ch.12 |
| **TPNW (2017)** | Treaty on the Prohibition of Nuclear Weapons — bans nuclear weapons outright. Driven by non-nuclear states (Austria, Mexico, South Africa) frustrated with NPT stagnation. Entered into force 2021. NO nuclear-weapon state has signed. | Heywood Ch.12 |

## Lecture walkthrough

### The puzzle Sagan opens with

> *"Why do states build nuclear weapons? Having an accurate answer to this question is critically important both for predicting the long-term future of international security and for current foreign policy efforts to prevent the spread of nuclear weapons."* — Sagan (1996)

Sagan's complaint: there's a **near-consensus** among US policymakers and IR scholars that the answer is "obvious" — states pursue nukes when they face a military threat they can't meet otherwise. He shows this consensus is **insufficient** — at least two other models do significant explanatory work. Treating nuclear acquisition as *just* a security problem leads to bad non-proliferation policy.

### The three models

#### 1. Security model — the realist default

**The argument:**
- Under anarchy, states must self-help.
- When a state faces a significant military threat — especially a nuclear threat — that cannot be deterred through alliances or conventional balance, it builds the bomb.
- When threats disappear or credible alliance protection arrives, states refrain or disarm.

**Cases that fit:**
- **USSR** built the bomb in response to US Hiroshima/Nagasaki.
- **China** built in response to US nuclear pressure (Korean War, Taiwan crises).
- **India** built in response to Chinese nukes (1964 → 1974 PNE → 1998 tests).
- **Pakistan** built in response to Indian tests.

**Policy implication:** to stop proliferation, *reduce the threats* states face — extended deterrence (US nuclear umbrella for allies), arms control, security guarantees, conflict resolution.

**Cases that strain the model:**
- **Ukraine** (1994) gave up Soviet-era nukes despite facing a still-powerful Russia. Why? Trust in Budapest Memorandum assurances (which Russia later violated).
- **Brazil and Argentina** abandoned competing programs in the 1980s–90s without dramatic security changes.
- **South Africa** dismantled in 1989–93 — security calculus changed, but the timing was also about international legitimacy.

#### 2. Domestic politics model

**The argument:**
- Nuclear weapons programs require BIG bureaucracies (military, weapons labs, energy ministries).
- These bureaucracies have parochial interests — missions to justify, budgets to defend, careers to advance.
- Once established, they *push* for the bomb regardless of strict security needs.
- Politicians may also pursue nukes for domestic legitimacy, electoral signalling, or to placate nationalist coalitions.

**Cases that fit:**
- **India 1974 ("peaceful nuclear explosion")** — military and Atomic Energy Commission pushed for the test against civilian opposition; Indira Gandhi authorised partly for domestic political reasons.
- **France** under de Gaulle — *force de frappe* served Gaullist sovereignty narrative as much as concrete security needs.
- **South Africa apartheid regime** — internal politics of a besieged regime mattered as much as external threats.

**Policy implication:** non-proliferation needs to address INTERNAL drivers — strengthen civilian control, deny bureaucratic monopolies, support anti-proliferation domestic coalitions.

#### 3. Norms model

**The argument:**
- Nuclear weapons are not just military instruments — they are SYMBOLS.
- States acquire or renounce them partly because of what nukes *mean*: modernity, sovereignty, prestige, civilisational status.
- The NPT itself created a norm: "responsible modern states don't pursue the bomb" — making proliferation a status-LOSING move for most states.

**Cases that fit:**
- **France** in the 1960s — nukes as symbol of *grandeur* and resistance to US dominance.
- **India 1998** — Hindu nationalist (BJP) framing of tests as assertion of civilisational status.
- **Germany, Japan, South Korea** — could easily build but DON'T, partly because of post-WWII anti-nuclear identity (Japan especially).
- **South Africa, Brazil, Argentina** — giving up the bomb fit the "joining the responsible international community" norm.

**Policy implication:** strengthen the *norm* against proliferation (e.g. via the NPT, TPNW, public diplomacy, civil society). Sanction states that defy the norm to keep the norm strong.

### Why this matters — the three models in combination

Sagan's deepest point: **no single model is right for every case.** Most actual proliferation decisions involve some mix of all three.

**Iran is the classic exam case:**
- *Security:* US + Israeli threats; regional balance against Saudi Arabia.
- *Domestic politics:* IRGC bureaucratic interests; nationalist coalitions; post-2018 (Trump's JCPOA withdrawal) hardliners empowered.
- *Norms:* sense of post-Iraq inequity ("if the US can invade Iraq because it lacks WMD, the lesson is to build them"); status assertion.

Policy implication: a non-proliferation strategy that addresses only security (offering Iran a guarantee) will fail if domestic politics and norms-based motivations remain. And vice versa.

### The Waltz-Sagan debate — optimists vs pessimists

A famous companion debate. Kenneth Waltz argued that **more nukes = more deterrence = more stability** ("nuclear weapons may be a blessing rather than a curse"). Sagan disagreed: more nukes = more accidents, more theft, more decisions by parochial domestic actors, and higher risk of escalation.

This debate is mostly NOT in Sagan's three-models paper, but it's the broader frame for thinking about whether proliferation is *bad in itself*. Mainstream IR has tilted toward Sagan's pessimism since the early 2000s.

### Proliferation today — the contemporary picture

- **9 nuclear-weapon states:** US, Russia, UK, France, China (NPT-recognised) + India, Pakistan, Israel (de facto, non-NPT) + North Korea (acquired since 2006).
- **Iran:** the most-watched threshold state; JCPOA collapsed; current trajectory uncertain.
- **End of US-Russia arms control:** New START (2010) expires February 2026 — the LAST major bilateral cap. (Note this links directly to Week 1's "end of arms control" trend.)
- **TPNW (2017):** treaty banning nukes outright, entered into force 2021. No nuclear state has signed.
- **Nuclear taboo (Tannenwald):** still holding, but tested by Russia's nuclear rhetoric over Ukraine.

The Sagan framework forces you to ask, for any case: *which* of the three drivers (security, domestic politics, norms) is doing the work? Different answers → different policy responses.

## Key thinker — Scott D. Sagan

Stanford political scientist, leading scholar of nuclear strategy, arms control, and proliferation. Faculty associate of the Center for International Security and Cooperation (CISAC).

His 1996 *International Security* article *Why Do States Build Nuclear Weapons? Three Models in Search of a Bomb* is the canonical reference for proliferation theory. The paper's strength is its **pluralism** — it pushes back against the realist monopoly on proliferation analysis and shows that domestic politics and norms can do as much explanatory work as security.

Sagan's other major contribution is the **organisational** theory of nuclear safety — *The Limits of Safety* (1993) argued that even disciplined nuclear-armed states (like the US) had repeated close-call accidents during the Cold War. This anchors the "pessimist" side of the Sagan-Waltz debate on whether nuclear proliferation is destabilising.

In policy terms, Sagan has been a consistent advocate for **deepening the nuclear taboo** and for **multi-pronged non-proliferation** (not just security guarantees, but also institutional, normative, and domestic interventions).

*(Kenneth Waltz: leading neorealist, "proliferation optimist." Their 2013 book* The Spread of Nuclear Weapons: An Enduring Debate *is the canonical pro/contra. Nina Tannenwald: nuclear taboo theorist. T.V. Paul, Etel Solingen: scholars of regional proliferation dynamics.)*

---

> **Explained easily — the three models in one paragraph each:**
> **SECURITY:** "I'm scared of my neighbour with the bigger army. If I get the bomb, they won't dare attack me." This is the answer most people start with and the only answer realists usually give.
>
> **DOMESTIC POLITICS:** "Our military has a Nuclear Bureau, our weapons labs need funding, our ruling party wants to look strong, and once you have all those interests aligned, the bomb gets built whether or not the security situation actually requires it." This is the answer if you watch *how* nuclear decisions actually get made inside states.
>
> **NORMS:** "A modern, serious nation has nukes — they're a sign of being grown-up, sovereign, civilised. We need them not so much to USE them, but to BE the kind of country that has them." This is why France, India, and apartheid South Africa pursued them — partly for status, partly for security.

> **Explained easily — why Sagan's pluralism matters for policy:**
> If you think Iran wants nukes for *security*, you offer a guarantee → done. If you think Iran wants nukes because the *IRGC* needs missions and Iranian nationalism needs a totem, a guarantee won't help — you'd need to weaken those domestic actors. If you think Iran wants nukes because of post-Iraq *norms* about how powerful states get treated, you'd need to fix the broader Middle East order. Most actual cases mix all three — which is why non-proliferation is hard.

> **Explained easily — proliferation optimists vs pessimists:**
> Waltz (optimist): "Nuclear weapons are scary. Scary things deter people. More scary things → more deterrence → less war. Let the spread continue, it'll stabilise." Sagan (pessimist): "Yes, deterrence works WHEN the systems are reliable, the leadership is rational, command-and-control is tight, and there are no accidents. None of these conditions reliably hold. More nukes = more accidents, more theft, more close calls — eventually a catastrophe." Mainstream IR has sided with Sagan since the 2000s.

> **Explained easily — Ukraine and the lesson of 1994:**
> When Ukraine gave up its Soviet-era nukes in 1994 (Budapest Memorandum), the US/UK/Russia promised security assurances. Russia invaded in 2014 and again in 2022. Many countries now look at that and conclude: "If you give up your bomb, your guarantor may abandon you." This is BAD news for future disarmament — and a reason why North Korea will not give up its weapons under any plausible deal.

## Likely exam angles

### Multiple Choice Questions

**Q1.** Sagan's central claim in "Why Do States Build Nuclear Weapons?" (1996) is that:
- A) States build nuclear weapons primarily for security reasons
- B) Nuclear proliferation is best explained by three distinct models — security, domestic politics, and norms — often operating together
- C) Proliferation is best explained by the breakdown of international institutions
- D) Domestic political pressures are the single most important driver of proliferation

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B) Nuclear proliferation is best explained by three distinct models — security, domestic politics, and norms — often operating together**

Sagan's whole pluralist point: the security model (Option A) is the dominant *consensus* he's pushing back against. Domestic politics (Option D) is one of his three, not the headline. Most actual cases mix the three.

</details>

**Q2.** Which of the following is a textbook example of the NORMS model?
- A) Pakistan acquiring nuclear weapons after India's 1998 tests
- B) North Korea's nuclear program in response to perceived US threats
- C) France's *force de frappe* as a symbol of Gaullist sovereignty and grandeur
- D) The Soviet Union's nuclear program after Hiroshima

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C) France's *force de frappe* as a symbol of Gaullist sovereignty and grandeur**

France's nukes did have a security rationale, but the SYMBOLIC content (national independence, resistance to US dominance, restored greatness after WWII) was central. A, B, D are textbook security-model cases.

</details>

**Q3.** Why does Sagan think the security model alone is insufficient for explaining proliferation?
- A) States never actually face genuine security threats
- B) Some cases (like France or India 1974) involve significant domestic and normative drivers beyond pure security
- C) International institutions always prevent security-driven proliferation
- D) Nuclear weapons don't actually provide effective deterrence

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B) Some cases (like France or India 1974) involve significant domestic and normative drivers beyond pure security**

Sagan's empirical claim: even when security pressures exist, domestic-political and normative drivers do significant work. And in some cases (Ukraine's 1994 disarmament, South Africa's), security threats persisted but states still gave up the bomb — pointing to non-security drivers.

</details>

**Q4.** The Waltz-Sagan debate is best characterised as:
- A) A disagreement about whether nukes exist
- B) Optimists (Waltz): proliferation stabilises through deterrence; Pessimists (Sagan): proliferation destabilises through accidents, theft, parochial actors
- C) A debate over whether the NPT should be reformed
- D) A debate over whether arms control treaties are legally binding

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B) Optimists (Waltz): proliferation stabilises through deterrence; Pessimists (Sagan): proliferation destabilises through accidents, theft, parochial actors**

Waltz argued more nuclear states = more deterrence = more stability. Sagan countered: deterrence requires reliable systems and rational decision-making, neither of which we can assume — so more proliferation = more risk of accidents, escalation, theft.

### Short Essay / Open Questions

**Essay Q1.** Apply Sagan's three models to ONE case of proliferation (e.g. Iran, North Korea, India 1998, Israel) or non-proliferation (Ukraine 1994, South Africa, Brazil/Argentina, Germany/Japan). Which model best fits, and what does that imply for non-proliferation policy?

<details>
<summary>Model answer outline</summary>

**Structure:**
- State the three models briefly (security, domestic politics, norms).
- Apply each lens to the chosen case.
- Take a position on which dominates.
- Draw the policy implication.

**Worked example — Iran:**
- **Security:** US + Israeli threats; regional balance against Saudi Arabia + nuclear Israel; post-Iraq War lesson that lacking WMD invites invasion.
- **Domestic politics:** IRGC has bureaucratic interests in keeping options open; nationalist political coalitions (especially after the JCPOA collapse in 2018) push hard for the program; weapons-lab and military scientific establishment self-perpetuates.
- **Norms:** sense of *inequity* in the NPT system (why is the US allowed to have nukes but Iran is not?); status-seeking against the West.
- **Verdict:** all three operate. Pure security guarantees (Option 1) won't suffice — Iran's hardliners benefit politically from confrontation regardless of US posture. Sanctions strengthen domestic-political drivers (rally-round-the-flag). The norms problem requires reforming the inequity at the heart of the NPT (slow, structural).
- **Policy implication:** non-proliferation needs to address security + domestic + normative pathways simultaneously. JCPOA-style deals are necessary but not sufficient.

**Common mistake:** Picking just ONE model and ignoring others. Sagan's whole point is the *interaction*.

</details>

---

**Essay Q2.** Are proliferation optimists (Waltz) or pessimists (Sagan) more persuasive in 2026? Discuss using the contemporary nuclear landscape.

<details>
<summary>Model answer outline</summary>

**Structure:**
- State the Waltz position: more proliferation → more deterrence → fewer wars.
- State the Sagan position: more proliferation → more accidents, theft, parochial actors → higher risk.
- Apply to 2026 evidence.

**Empirical updates favouring Sagan (pessimist):**
- **End of New START (2026):** vertical proliferation likely to accelerate as US-Russia caps end. More warheads = more accident risk + tighter alert postures.
- **Russian nuclear rhetoric over Ukraine (2022–):** strong test of the nuclear taboo; multiple ambiguous threats have not yet broken it, but the risk has been higher than at any point since the early 1980s.
- **AI + cyber + autonomous weapons:** new failure modes for command-and-control that weren't on Waltz's mental map in the 1980s.
- **North Korea's continued buildup:** more warheads + missile capability + uncertain command + active hostility = a pessimist's case study.
- **Iran on the threshold + Saudi statements** about getting nukes if Iran does → potential cascade.

**Empirical updates favouring Waltz (optimist):**
- The nuclear taboo HAS held since 1945, including through multiple acute crises (Cuban Missile, Able Archer 83, Kargil 1999, Russia-Ukraine 2022–).
- India-Pakistan have maintained mutual deterrence for ~25 years despite multiple crises (Kargil, Mumbai 2008, Pulwama-Balakot 2019).
- The number of new nuclear states has been LIMITED (NPT regime has worked better than 1960s forecasts).

**Verdict:** the contemporary balance tilts toward Sagan. The optimist's case depends on reliable command-and-control + rational leadership + secure technology — and all three look more fragile in 2026 than at any time since the late 1980s. End-of-arms-control, AI, cyber, and a higher-tension great-power environment compound the risks.

**Tie-in to Week 1:** Carney's "rupture, not transition" framing applies here too. The institutional architecture restraining vertical and horizontal proliferation is fraying simultaneously.

**Common mistake:** Treating this as a values debate ("nukes are bad / nukes are good"). It's an empirical debate about whether deterrence or accidents dominate over time.

</details>
