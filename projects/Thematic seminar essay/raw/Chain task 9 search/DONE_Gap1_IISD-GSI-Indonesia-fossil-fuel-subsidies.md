# Gap 1 — IISD-GSI: Indonesia Fossil-Fuel Subsidy Political Economy

*Synthesis note for Task 9 evidence-chase. Relates to Objection 5 in argument-logic.md §3.*

---

## Key finding in one sentence

Indonesia spent **IDR 713.5 trillion (~USD 44 billion)** on energy subsidies in 2024, with ~89% (IDR ~634 trillion) supporting fossil fuels — approximately **8,000 times** the total value traded on Indonesia's carbon market since its 2023 launch.

---

## The scale gap (most important for the essay)

| Measure | Value |
|---|---|
| Total energy subsidies (2024) | IDR 713.5 trillion (~USD 44 billion) |
| Share supporting fossil fuels | 88.9% |
| PLN electricity compensation | >IDR 100 trillion |
| 3-kg LPG subsidy | IDR 80.2 trillion |
| Pertamina fuel compensation (Pertalite + Premium) | IDR 116.1 trillion |
| Coal price cap (implicit subsidy) | IDR 58.5 trillion |
| Transport fuel subsidy | IDR 17.1 trillion |
| **Total IDX Carbon transactions since Sept 2023 launch** | **IDR 78 billion (~USD 4.9 million)** |

The ratio is not a rounding error — it is the structural argument. Indonesia simultaneously operates the world's largest fossil fuel subsidy system relative to its carbon market and claims to price carbon.

**For Objection 5 in the essay (§3):** The OECD ECR finding that Indonesia's *net* effective carbon rate is negative is confirmed here. The fossil fuel subsidy system cancels out — and then some — every signal the ETS and carbon tax try to send.

---

## Who benefits — distributional finding (for Objection 5 response)

IISD analysis shows the **3-kg LPG subsidy** (the most expensive line item at IDR 80.2 trillion) is highly regressive: the **richest 10% consume almost as much subsidized LPG as the poorest 10%**. The World Bank CCDR 2023 recommendation: replace the LPG subsidy with targeted cash transfers (per PKH/BPNT design). This is politically viable — Indonesia has a proven conditional cash-transfer system (PKH).

**Implication for the essay's argument:** the political impossibility of subsidy reform is overstated for the household component. The genuinely hard-to-reform part is the industrial component (PLN compensation + Pertamina industrial fuel allocation), which benefits the politically connected utilities and national oil company, not the urban poor.

---

## Reform history (key point for essay: the third-rail framing is dated)

Indonesia has reformed fuel subsidies before:
- **2014 (Jokowi, first year):** Removed Rp 6,500/litre premium petrol subsidy, saving ~IDR 200 trillion/year. Redirected to infrastructure and social transfers. Public backlash was manageable.
- **2022:** Cut some fuel subsidies as global prices spiked. Pertalite price rose from IDR 7,650 to 10,000/litre.
- **Regression (2023-24):** Under Prabowo, some subsidy protections expanded, particularly for PLN compensation.

The political cost of subsidy reform is real but not prohibitive — the 1998 riots were an extreme case (Suharto crisis context, 70%+ price rises, no cash-transfer cushion). Modern targeted-compensation programs can blunt household-level impact. The binding constraint is **industrial energy subsidies captured by PLN, Pertamina, and captive-coal operators** — these are harder to reform because the beneficiaries are large corporations with political connections, not vulnerable households.

---

## Fiscal arithmetic for the essay

- Fossil fuel subsidy bill 2024: ~IDR 634 trillion
- Tony Blair Institute's MAC for coal retirement in Indonesia: USD 12-13/tCO2
- Annual coal retirement cost at ~50 Mt abatement: ~USD 600 million–650 million
- **Savings from even partial subsidy reform would dwarf the cost of coal retirement abatement** — the fiscal logic runs directly against the "Indonesia can't afford to decarbonise" argument

The coal price cap alone (IDR 58.5 trillion = ~USD 3.6 billion annually) subsidises the coal sector the ETS is supposed to price. Removing the coal price cap would immediately raise the effective carbon rate on power generation more than any realistic ETS price escalation could.
