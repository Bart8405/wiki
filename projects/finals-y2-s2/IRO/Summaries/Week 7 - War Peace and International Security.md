## Lecture walkthrough

### What is war? — definitions and problems

The lecture opens with a deliberately unsettled question: "we all think we know what war is, but the term has a history and cannot simply be taken for granted."

The UN's threshold of ≥1,000 deaths/year is the most common working definition, but it's *arbitrary* — it excludes the Falklands War of 1982, which nobody would deny was a war. Different types — Blitzkrieg (speed/shock), Total War (societal mobilisation), Guerrilla war (asymmetric, non-state), Revolutionary war (political transformation as goal) — already hint that war is not a single phenomenon.

### IR Theories and War

Each major IR tradition gives a different answer to why wars happen and what they mean:

==**Realism (Clausewitz):**==
> ==*"War is simply a continuation of political intercourse, with the addition of other means."*==

War is not an aberration — it is a tool of statecraft. States pursue power, and force is one instrument. Peace and war are not opposites but points on a spectrum of political relations.

**Idealism/Liberalism:**
- Idealists (drawing on Kant) push for *perpetual peace* through international cooperation, internationalism, and collective security.
- Karl Deutsch (1957) coined *security communities* — regions where war is unthinkable because states are integrated by shared identity and institutions.
- Liberals see war as a problem solvable by spreading democracy (*democratic peace theory*), free trade, and international law (Deudney & Ikenberry 1999).
- But they face a tension: how to protect citizens from outside threats while preserving individual freedom?

**Post-colonialism:**
- Where mainstream IR sees the world as anarchic (no government above states), postcolonialism sees *hierarchy* — some states dominate others.
- Brings in race, ethnicity, gender, and class.
- "Intervention is a distinctive technology of colonial-modern rule" (Shilliam, 2013) — connecting foreign intervention to the history of empire.

### Just War Theory

Just War is *not* realism (which says war is always potentially legitimate as a tool of power) and *not* pacifism (which says war is never legitimate). It is a middle-ground tradition:

**Jus ad bellum** — is it right to go to war?
1. Last resort (all other options exhausted)
2. Just cause (e.g. self-defence, stopping atrocities)
3. Legitimate authority (declared by proper authority)
4. Right intention (no hidden motives)
5. Reasonable prospect of success (don't start a losing war)
6. Proportionality (expected benefits outweigh expected harm)

**Jus in bello** — are you fighting it rightly?
1. Discrimination (target combatants, not civilians)
2. Proportionality (don't cause more harm than necessary)
3. Humanity (no cruelty)

### Decolonising War (Barkawi)

The lecture devotes significant attention to Tarak Barkawi's 2016 article — the key reading for this week.

Barkawi's argument: the concept of "war" as used in IR and war studies is **Eurocentric**. It was built from the experience of European nation-state wars and implicitly treats these as the template for "real war." Everything else — colonial uprisings, police actions, counterinsurgencies — gets relegated to lesser categories ("Small Wars").

**Three Eurocentric building blocks he attacks:**
1. **War and peace are opposites** — in Eurocentric thought, history is periodised into "war" and "peace." But this erases the continuous use of force in colonial settings. As the Marine quote on the slide puts it: "America is not at war. The Marine Corps is at war. America is at the mall."
2. **War happens between sovereign states** — this excludes the vast colonial experience, where empires fought non-state entities, insurgents, and colonies without any of it being coded as "war."
3. **War is either international or civil** — this two-category system cannot capture colonial wars, which crossed all these lines.

**His alternative:** conceive force as an *ordinary* dimension of politics (not an exception), situate war in transnational/imperial context, and attend to how war and society are *co-constitutive* — war shapes society; society shapes how wars are fought.

### New Wars and Contemporary Conflict

Kilcullen (2020): the US military model of high-tech, high-precision warfare that "won" the Gulf War in 1991 is **no longer working**. Today's threats come from:
- "Dragon" states (Russia, China) that refused to orbit the US-led order and developed alternative strategies — including "three warfares" (psychological, public opinion, "lawfare")
- "Snakes" — non-state actors and threats from state collapse
- The War on Terror: turned into "a threat without enemies" — the idea of terrorism as a unified opponent with overarching objectives was an illusion.

**Private Military Security Industry (PMSI):**
PMSCs (Private Military and Security Companies) now perform critical security tasks historically reserved for state militaries. By 2016, 1 in 4 US armed personnel in Iraq/Afghanistan was a private contractor. This raises serious questions about accountability, sovereignty, and democratic control of force (the Montreux Document, 2008, tried to establish some legal framework).

### Re-Arm Europe (contemporary relevance)

The lecture closes with a live policy issue: the EU's €800 billion ReArm Europe plan (2025), suspending strict budget rules, pooling procurement, and creating a new lending instrument (SAFE). This is a direct consequence of the Ukraine war and represents a historic shift in European defence posture.

## Key thinker — Tarak Barkawi

British-Canadian scholar at the LSE. His 2016 article "Decolonising War" argues that IR's concept of "war" is a Eurocentric construct built from European nation-state wars, which systematically excludes colonial and small wars — the wars that shaped most of the world. His prescription is not to study a different *geography* of war but to change the *conceptual framework* itself.

*(Clausewitz: Prussian military theorist, wrote **On War** (1833 — posthumous). His key formula — war as the continuation of politics — became the foundational realist view of conflict. He also stressed the "fog of war" and friction as inherent to military operations.)*

---

> **Explained easily — Clausewitz:**
> Imagine two people negotiating a business deal. When the negotiation breaks down and they sue each other, that's not a new situation — it's the same conflict, just using different tools. Clausewitz says war is exactly like that: it's states continuing their political dispute, just switching from diplomacy to bullets. War doesn't replace politics; it *is* politics by violent means.

> **Explained easily — Just War Theory:**
> Think of just war like the conditions for a legitimate fistfight: you can only punch someone if they hit you first (just cause), you tried talking it out (last resort), you don't hit harder than necessary (proportionality), and you don't go for the eyes (discrimination). The theory tries to distinguish "tragically necessary" wars from aggression.

> **Explained easily — Decolonising War:**
> Imagine if historians only defined "music" as classical orchestral music, and put hip-hop, jazz, and folk into separate lesser categories called "sounds" or "noise." That's what Eurocentric war studies did with colonial conflicts — they defined "real war" as European interstate war, and called everything else (colonial uprisings, counterinsurgency, occupations) something other than war. Barkawi says: the definition is the problem. Fix the concept, not just the geography.

> **Explained easily — Democratic Peace Theory:**
> The observation that liberal democracies almost never fight *each other*, though they fight plenty of non-democracies. The explanation: shared norms (respect for rights, rule of law) and economic interdependence make war too costly and morally illegitimate between democracies. This is a key liberal argument for spreading democracy.

## Likely exam angles

### Multiple Choice Questions

**Q1.** According to Clausewitz, what is the relationship between war and politics?
- A) War is the breakdown of politics — it begins where politics ends
- B) War is a continuation of political intercourse by other means
- C) War is fundamentally irrational and cannot be understood politically
- D) War and politics are separate domains that only interact in crises

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B) War is a continuation of political intercourse by other means**

Clausewitz's formula is the lecture's defining realist statement on war. War does not replace politics — it is a different instrument of the same political pursuit. This is why the slide says "there is a co-constitution of war and politics." Option A is the common intuition that Clausewitz explicitly rejects.

</details>

**Q2.** Barkawi's "decolonising war" argument holds that:
- A) IR should shift its geographic focus from Europe to the Global South
- B) Colonial wars were fundamentally different from "real" wars and belong in a separate category
- C) The Eurocentric concept of war excludes colonial and small wars from its core definition and must be rethought
- D) Postcolonial states should develop their own separate war studies disciplines

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: C) The Eurocentric concept of war excludes colonial and small wars from its core definition and must be rethought**

Barkawi explicitly says it is *not* sufficient to just add Global South conflicts to existing frameworks — the problem is the Eurocentric concept itself. Option A is what he explicitly rules out as insufficient. Option B is the Eurocentric assumption he *critiques*, not his own position.

</details>

**Q3.** The principle of *jus ad bellum* concerns:
- A) The just conduct of soldiers during battle
- B) The conditions under which it is morally justified to go to war
- C) The responsibilities of states after a war ends
- D) The proportionality of weapons used in conflict

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B) The conditions under which it is morally justified to go to war**

*Jus ad bellum* = the right to war. *Jus in bello* = right conduct *in* war. *Jus post bellum* = justice *after* war. The lecture lists six *ad bellum* conditions: last resort, just cause, legitimate authority, right intention, reasonable prospect of success, and proportionality.

</details>

**Q4.** Karl Deutsch's concept of "security communities" (1957) suggests:
- A) Military alliances are more stable than trade agreements
- B) Groups of states can develop shared identities that make war between them unthinkable
- C) States form communities to pursue collective security against shared threats
- D) Security is primarily a domestic rather than international concern

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B) Groups of states can develop shared identities that make war between them unthinkable**

Security communities go beyond mere alliances — they are zones where war is not just unlikely but *unthinkable* because of integrated values, institutions, and identity. The EU/NATO area is the canonical example. Option C describes collective security (like the UN), which is different.

### Short Essay / Open Questions

**Essay Q1.** "War is simply a continuation of political intercourse by other means." Discuss this claim using the perspectives of at least two IR theories.

<details>
<summary>Model answer outline</summary>

**Key points to hit:**
- Start with Clausewitz and the realist foundation: war is not an aberration but a political instrument. The co-constitution of war, politics, economy, and society.
- Contrast with liberalism: liberals are *disturbed* by war because they see it as a failure. Liberal remedies — democratic peace, international law, trade, IOs — are all attempts to make political disputes resolvable *without* war.
- Add a postcolonial twist (Barkawi): even the distinction "war vs. peace" is politically constructed. Colonial empires maintained permanent low-level force as "peace" while calling it something other than war — revealing how naming war is itself a political act.
- Conclusion: Clausewitz is right that war and politics are intertwined, but postcolonialism shows that *who gets to call something war* is also political.

**Good example:** The slide's Marine Corps quote — "America is not at war. The Marine Corps is at war. America is at the mall" — shows how war and peace coexist differently for different populations within the same state.

</details>

---

**Essay Q2.** What are the three Eurocentric building blocks of the concept of war that Barkawi challenges, and what does he propose instead?

<details>
<summary>Model answer outline</summary>

**Key points to hit:**
1. **War/peace binary:** Barkawi replaces this with a battle/repression schema where force is an *ordinary* dimension of politics, not an exception. Colonial empires used continuous force labelled as "police actions," not war.
2. **War between sovereign states:** The nation-state is Eurocentric. Colonial wars involved empires fighting non-state entities, peoples, tribes — none of which fit the sovereign-state template.
3. **International vs. civil war:** This two-category system cannot capture colonial wars that crossed all these lines simultaneously.

**Proposed alternative:** Conceive of force as ordinary (not exceptional), situate war in transnational/imperial hierarchies, and study how war and society are *co-constitutive* (they shape each other).

**Common mistake:** Saying Barkawi just wants to study wars in the Global South — he explicitly rejects this. The point is to change the *concept*, not just the geography.

</details>
