## 1. What the Study Guide tells you to know — with definitions

The Baisir guide's "Migration and mobility" glossary is the **complete short-answer ammunition** for this week. Every term below is fair game. Definitions are drawn from the lecture, Huysmans, Hollifield, or — where the readings don't define a term — from the UNHCR / IOM glossaries (the canonical international references).

| Term | Definition | Source |
|---|---|---|
| **Refugee** | A person who, owing to a **well-founded fear of being persecuted** for reasons of race, religion, nationality, membership of a particular social group, or political opinion, is outside the country of their nationality and is unable — or owing to such fear, unwilling — to avail themselves of its protection. | Art. 1, 1951 Geneva Convention (in lecture) |
| **Asylum-seeker** | Someone seeking international protection whose request for refugee status has **not yet been processed** (or who has not yet formally applied but intends to). Asylum-seeker is the pending-status version of refugee. | UNHCR (in lecture) |
| **Internally Displaced Person (IDP)** | Persons forced to flee their homes — particularly due to armed conflict, generalised violence, human rights violations, or natural/human-made disasters — **who have not crossed an internationally recognized state border**. The border is what distinguishes IDPs from refugees. | UN Guiding Principles on Internal Displacement (in lecture) |
| **Stateless person** | A person not considered as a national by any state under the operation of its law. Statelessness can be inherited (born to stateless parents), arise from gaps in nationality laws, state succession, or discriminatory denial of citizenship. The 1954 Convention Relating to the Status of Stateless Persons is the relevant international instrument. | UNHCR (not defined in lecture — added from canonical source) |
| **Voluntary vs forced migration** | **Voluntary** migration is motivated by choice (work, study, family, lifestyle). **Forced** migration is compelled by external pressures — persecution, conflict, disaster, or development displacement — that leave the person with no meaningful alternative. In practice the line blurs (economic desperation, environmental degradation). | IOM (lecture references the distinction; definition added) |
| **Push and pull factors** | **Push factors** are conditions in the country of origin that drive people to leave: war, persecution, poverty, environmental degradation, political repression. **Pull factors** are conditions in the destination country that attract: jobs, family, safety, freedoms, welfare. Hollifield uses *supply-push* and *demand-pull* as economic shorthand for the same idea. | Standard migration-studies framework (used throughout Hollifield) |
| **Securitization of migration** | The political process by which migration is **constructed as a security issue** through speech acts and institutional practices, framing migrants as an existential threat to public order, identity, or welfare — which justifies extraordinary measures that bypass normal democratic politics. | Huysmans 2000; Wæver / Copenhagen School |
| **The liberal paradox** | Hollifield's central concept: liberal states are trapped between the **economic logic** of liberalism (which pushes toward openness to trade, investment, and migration to maintain competitiveness) and the **political and legal logic** of liberalism (which pushes toward closure — borders, the demos, the social contract). They want both and can fully have neither. | Hollifield 1992, 2004 |
| **The migration state** | Hollifield's name for the **third function** of the modern state, alongside the garrison state (security) and Rosecrance's trading state (economy). In the migration state, regulating international migration is as central a function as providing security and economic well-being. | Hollifield 2004 |
| **1951 Refugee Convention** | The Geneva Convention Relating to the Status of Refugees. Sets out the **definition** of a refugee, their rights, and the legal obligations of signatory states. Together with its **1967 Protocol** (which removed the original geographic and temporal restrictions to "European events before 1951"), it is the legal backbone of the global refugee regime. | Lecture; Hollifield |
| **Non-refoulement** | The rule that a state party to the 1951 Convention **cannot return** an individual to a country where they face threats of persecution. It is the single most important principle of refugee law and is widely regarded as customary international law (binding even on non-signatories). | Hollifield; 1951 Convention Art. 33 |
| **Border externalization** | Pushing border control **beyond your own territory** — by funding, training, and equipping third countries to police migration before migrants reach your borders. Examples: the EU-Tunisia 2023 deal, the EU-Turkey 2016 deal, the US "safe third country" arrangements. | Lecture; Huysmans |
| **Migration diplomacy** | The use of migration as a **bargaining chip** in interstate relations. Can be coercive (blackmail, "let us in or we'll release the migrants") or cooperative (backscratching). Yields material gains (economic, institutional, aid) and symbolic gains (status, reputation, moral standing). | Lecture; Tolay 2022 |
| **Diaspora** | A community of people who have settled outside their country of origin but maintain ties (sentimental, cultural, economic, political) with the homeland. Diasporas can be transnational political actors — lobbying host-state policy, sending remittances, shaping homeland politics. | Standard usage (not formally defined in week materials; included as a Baisir glossary term) |

From the lecture you should also be ready on:
- The **Copenhagen School** (Buzan, Wæver) — security as **speech act**.
- The **Paris School** (Bigo) — security as **everyday practice** of professionals.
- The **Global Refugee Regime** (Betts) and its deficiencies.

Likely short-answer prompt: *"How and why has migration been securitised in liberal states, and what are the consequences?"* Or: *"What is the 'liberal paradox' Hollifield identifies, and how have liberal states tried to escape it?"*

> **Explained easily — the whole week in one paragraph:**
> Migration crosses borders, and borders are where states matter most. Two readings give you the two big stories: **Huysmans** says EU politics has *made* migration into a security problem (it didn't have to be one — politicians, police and bureaucrats framed it that way). **Hollifield** says liberal democracies are stuck in a contradiction — their economies need open borders, their politics demand closed borders. The whole week is about how those two stories interact, and how the EU has built institutions (Schengen, Dublin, Frontex, deals with Tunisia/Turkey) that try to manage both.

## 2. The lecture — Wolff's framing (LEC6)

### 2.1 The three driving questions

1. What drives international migration nowadays? Who counts as a migrant?
2. What dilemmas are liberal states facing when it comes to migration?
3. Why was the global refugee regime set up and what are its deficiencies?

### 2.2 Drivers of migration — debunking the myths

- Migration **is not for everyone**: "the poors remained trapped at home" (Parkes). The very poorest cannot afford to move.
- **Most migration is intra-regional** — not the dramatic South-North flows imagined in European debates.
- South-North flows are **declining**; South-South migration is **rising**.
- We are in the "**age of migration**" (Castles et al.) — likely to remain the "new normal."

### 2.3 The essential definitions (memorise verbatim where possible)

| Term | Source | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| **Refugee** | Art. 1 of the 1951 Geneva Convention | A person who, owing to a well-founded fear of being persecuted for reasons of **race, religion, nationality, membership of a particular social group or political opinion**, is outside the country of their nationality and is unable or — owing to such fear — unwilling to avail themselves of the protection of that country |
| **Asylum-seeker** | UNHCR | Someone seeking international protection. Their request for refugee status has yet to be processed (or they intend to apply) |
| **Migrant** | IOM | A person who moves away from their place of usual residence, whether within a country or across an international border, temporarily or permanently, and for a variety of reasons |
| **Internally Displaced Person (IDP)** | UN Guiding Principles | Persons forced or obliged to flee or leave their homes — particularly as a result of armed conflict, generalised violence, human rights violations, or natural/human-made disasters — **who have not crossed an internationally recognized state border** |

The IDP/refugee distinction is exam-relevant: the difference is the **international border**.

### 2.4 The Liberal State and migration — historical arc

- For a long time, migration policies were tackled from the perspective of **race**. Immigration helped **build the nation-state**, especially in settler states wanting to populate their lands:
  - **1901 Commonwealth Immigration Restriction Act** — the "White Australia" policy.
  - **1924 Reed-Johnson Immigration Act (US)** — excluded Asian immigrants.
- In post-WWII Europe, migration was linked to **colonial policy** — colonial workers could settle and were recruited.
- By the 1970s, most European countries **restricted labour migration**.

### 2.5 The **Liberal Paradox** of the migration state (Hollifield 1992)

- Since the 1970s, liberal states do **not** solicit legal migration as before (no more big quota or guest-worker programmes). Reason: politicians' short-termism and re-election logic.
- But migration is still **accepted** for:
  - Humanitarian reasons
  - Recognition of migrant and refugee rights
  - **Family reunification**
  - The state's sheer **incapacity to keep migrants out** (illegal immigration)
- Hence Joppke's famous question: **"Why do liberal states accept unwanted immigration?"** (1998).

### 2.6 Regulating migration — the policy toolkit

Migration is a transnational challenge for states and their borders — challenges governance at global, regional, national and local levels. After WWII, new international organisations emerge: **UNHCR, IOM, ILO**.

- **Before 1914** migration was not seen as a challenge to state sovereignty. This changes after WWI (Castles 2014).
- Policy instruments are **not neutral** (Wolff and Trauner 2014):
  - Readmission agreements
  - Visas
  - Work permits
  - Residence permits
  - Regularization, employer or carrier sanctions
  - Points programmes for high-skilled migrants (Blue Card, Green Card)
- General trend: **restrictive policies, attracting high-skilled, criminalising migrants/helpers, rise of human smuggling/trafficking** (in spite of more security).

### 2.7 Why is migration so prominent on policy agendas?

Four theoretical explanations:

1. **Securitization of migration** (Bigo, Wæver) — is migration a security issue? Role of **street-level bureaucrats** and Ministries of Interior in setting agendas; role of border guards (Guiraudon, Lavenex).
2. **Identity and failure of multicultural policies** (Joppke) — are migrants challenging societies' identities? **Ontological insecurity** and fear of "the Other," Islam, Migrants (Kinvall).
3. **Globalization** — acceleration of mobility; countries of emigration become countries of transit and destination (e.g. Morocco). Neoliberal reforms + need for cheap labour.
4. **Eurocentric / Western-centric approach to migration** — ignores local circumstances; lots of migration is intra-regional. Need to **decentre** approaches to migration (El Qadim).

> **Explained easily — the four "why is migration political" answers:**
> Wolff offers four theories for why migration dominates headlines: (1) **securitization** — politicians and bureaucrats keep *calling* it a security threat; (2) **identity / failed multiculturalism** — voters feel their society's culture is being diluted ("ontological insecurity"); (3) **globalization** — neoliberal economies need cheap labour, mobility has accelerated, and old "emigration countries" like Morocco are now also "transit" and "destination" countries; (4) **Eurocentrism** — most migration is actually intra-regional (Africans moving to other African countries, Asians to other Asian countries), but European debates pretend it's all heading north. You can apply any of these four lenses on the exam.

### 2.8 Securitization theory — the Copenhagen School (Buzan, Wæver)

- Conflict and Peace Research Institute of Copenhagen.
- **Security = survival** (Buzan et al. 1998: 21).
- **Security is a speech act**: *"It is by labelling something a security issue that it becomes one"* (Wæver 2004: 13).
- **Securitization is the move that takes politics beyond established rules of the game**, framing the issue as either a special sort of politics or *above* politics (Buzan et al. 1998: 23-26).
- Result: **extraordinary measures**, removed from normal politics, becoming emergency politics — **circumventing democratic rules**.
- Beyond military security, they add **four categories**: political, economic, **societal**, environmental.

### 2.9 Critical theory — the Paris School (Bigo)

A different angle on the same problem:

- Control practices are *"routinely embedded in a practical sense that informs what controlling borders does and means."*
- Use of "**sociological intervention method**" — letting actors themselves access certain forms of consciousness.
- Look at *"everyday practices that enact a governmentality of fear and unease"* (Bigo 2014: 211).
- **Militarisation of border controls is in fact limited** in the central justification of border personnel (EU NavFor Med-Brief example).
- European border guards, even if of military background, are **socialised** into the idea of "Europeanised border control" and "see themselves as **managers of flows**" and as "**the ultimate judges of the true behaviour of an individual**" (Bigo 2014: 215).
- **Digitalisation of borders, preventive work, biometrics** (e.g. Eurodac database). They don't see borders as barriers but **"as a series of disconnected geographical points, linked through speed of information and data sharing"** (Bigo 2014: 217).

**The key contrast:** Copenhagen School emphasises **discourse / speech act** (top-down political construction); Paris School emphasises **everyday practice** (bottom-up bureaucratic routine). Both are critical / post-positivist.

### 2.10 The Global Refugee Regime

Definition (Betts 2015): *"A set of norms and institutions relating to protection and solutions for Refugees,"* or *"the architecture of reciprocal support that confers a set of global public goods in the field of asylum."*

The architecture rests on the **1951 Geneva Convention** + UNHCR.

### 2.11 Migration Diplomacy

Diplomatic practices around migration:

- **Coercive vs. cooperative** practices
- **Blackmailing vs. backscratching** practices
- **Externalization** (only one type of migration diplomacy practice — moving border control beyond your territory)

Material gains in migration deals:
- Economic gain — trade agreements/grants (e.g. tomato deals)
- Institutional benefits — membership in an organisation (e.g. Turkey, EU accession)
- Aid — military or humanitarian

**But not just a bargain** — also **symbolic gains**: soft power, cultural/symbolic, status, reputation, moral standing. Tolay (2022) argues we should think about **interactions** rather than just bargains.

### 2.12 EU Migration Diplomacy — case: EU-Tunisia deal

- 16 July 2023 Memorandum of Understanding with five pillars.
- Complements the EU-Tunisia Association Agreement.
- Priority: irregular migration, avoiding loss of human life, developing legal pathways; combating smuggling and human trafficking.
- **Budget:** €105 million for Tunisian coastguards and border control; €900 million in macroeconomic support; €150 million in direct budgetary support.
- **Wider picture**: asylum and migration perceived increasingly as a **geopolitical risk**. Countries **blackmail the EU**. Strategy to return migrants home.

### 2.13 Demographic challenge

Europe needs migration — demographic decline means labour shortages. Restrictive politics and demographic reality are in tension.

## 3. Required reading 1 — Huysmans, "The European Union and the Securitization of Migration" (2000)

*Journal of Common Market Studies 38(5), pp. 751–777. The foundational application of Copenhagen School / securitization theory to EU migration policy.*

### 3.1 The big claim

Since the 1980s, the political construction of migration has increasingly referred to:
- The **destabilising effects** of migration on domestic integration.
- The **dangers for public order** it implied.

The **EU integration process** is **implicated** in this securitisation — most visibly through the **Third Pillar on Justice and Home Affairs, the Schengen Agreements, and the Dublin Convention**.

But this isn't isolated: it's connected to a **wider politicisation** that portrays immigrants and asylum-seekers as a **challenge to national identity and welfare provisions**. It also impinges on the **"politics of belonging"** — the contest over criteria for legitimate membership of West European societies.

### 3.2 Quick history of EU migration policy

| Period | Policy character | Key dynamic |
|---|---|---|
| **1950s–60s** | Permissive / promotional. France, Germany, Netherlands actively recruited guest workers (cheap, flexible labour) | Immigrants seen as workforce, not as a security issue |
| **Late 1960s–70s** | Shift to control-oriented, restrictive | Labour market changes; protect domestic workers. Family reunification kept immigrant population growing |
| **1980s onwards** | Increasing politicisation through **conflation of immigration and asylum** | Asylum politicised as alternative route for economic immigration (den Boer 1995). The Eurodac database explicitly links illegal immigrants and asylum-seekers |
| **1986 onwards** | Significant Europeanisation: SEA, Schengen group, Trevi, Ad Hoc Group on Immigration | Intergovernmental fora pre-structured EU migration policy |
| **1992 TEU** | Third Pillar (Justice and Home Affairs) | Migration treated as intergovernmental Justice/Home Affairs issue |
| **1997 Amsterdam** | Third Pillar migration sections communitarised | Moved from Third to First Pillar |

### 3.3 What is securitisation? — Huysmans' argument

The standard view: there's a security *problem* → states adopt security *policies*. The problem comes first, policy is an instrumental reaction.

Huysmans rejects this as **too narrow**. Security practices are also **defining practices** that *turn an issue like migration into a security problem* by mobilising specific institutions and expectations (Wæver 1995, Buzan et al. 1998, Bigo 1996a). When the **police** and Ministry of Home Affairs take a prominent role in migration regulation, they bring their professional disposition to **represent and categorise policy concerns in a security discourse**. As a result, immigrants are framed as a security problem — different from approaching asylum as a human rights question.

> *"Security policy is a specific policy of mediating belonging. It conserves or transforms political integration and criteria of membership through the identification of existential threats. In security practices the political and social identification of a community and its way of life develop in response to an existential threat."*

The political myth: a **homogenous national community or Western civilisation** existed in the past and can be re-established today through the **exclusion of migrants identified as cultural aliens**.

### 3.4 Securitization is not a few actors — it's structural

You might be tempted to reduce securitisation to "extreme right parties" or governments. But the process involves national governments, grassroots groups, European transnational police networks, the media. **Securitisation is a structural effect of a multiplicity of practices.**

Huysmans focuses on **three relating themes** through which securitisation of migration in the EU has developed:

1. **Internal security**
2. **Cultural security**
3. **Crisis of the welfare state**

### 3.5 Theme 1 — **Internal Security and Migration**

- The key dynamic: **securitisation of the internal market**. With the abolition of internal border controls under the Single European Act, transnational flows of goods, capital, services and people are presumed to challenge public order and rule of law.
- The reasoning: **fewer internal borders → strengthen external borders** to control who legitimately enters the free-movement space.
- This logic created **"Fortress Europe"** (Ireland 1991; Bigo 1998).
- Critique: most illegal immigrants are not smuggled — they overstay visas (Salt 1989). Border control isn't even the main instrument — work permits, residency permits, welfare access are more important.
- The **Security Continuum**: police and customs cooperation produced a connection between **border control, terrorism, international crime, and migration**. Bigo (1994): the issue was no longer terrorism *on one hand* and asylum *on the other* — they came to be **treated together** as an "interrelation between problems and free movement."
- The **security continuum is an institutionalised mode of policy-making** that allows the **transfer of security connotations** of terrorism, drug trafficking and money-laundering to the area of migration.
- More recently extended to **EU enlargement** to Central and Eastern Europe (Tampere 1999: candidate states must take on the **Schengen acquis**).
- The continuum first emerged from professional and political cooperation in internal security: Bern Club, **TREVI**, Police Working Group on Terrorism. These networks of security professionals produce and distribute internal security knowledge that articulates the continuum between borders, terrorism, crime and migration.

> *"Migration is a nodal point in the internal security field… 'Migration' and related labels such as 'foreigner' and 'asylum-seeker' are politically powerful signifiers in contemporary Europe."*

Migration has become a **meta-issue** — a phenomenon that can be referenced as the cause of many problems (Faist 1994).

### 3.6 Theme 2 — **Cultural Security and Migration**

- Migration policy must address that European countries have become **countries of immigration**. Immigrants/asylum-seekers/refugees are present and **challenge the myth of national cultural homogeneity**.
- Migration is politicised because "cultural mixing… challenges the desire for coinciding cultural and political frontiers."
- Even **without a radical "cultural war" discourse**, framing migration as a vague challenge to social/political integration has **strong securitising effects** (Heisler and Layton-Henry 1993).
- Three EU-level themes:
  - **Border control has cultural dimensions.** External borders are more "real" for non-OECD nationals than OECD nationals. The Schengen visa list singles out Third World countries.
  - **Integration policies are ambivalent.** Calling for migrants to "integrate" can confirm a **nationalist desire for cultural homogeneity** and position migrants as outside the national social formation — late arrivers who disrupted a culturally homogenous space.
  - **Multicultural and non-racist projects.** EU's politicisation of multiculturalism articulates a specific **fear of (a possible revival of) the European past** — extreme nationalism, racism, xenophobia of the early 20th century.
- The EU is **ambivalent**: simultaneously sustains nationalist/racist/xenophobic reactions (through restrictive policy and negative portrayals) **and** campaigns against revival of these.

### 3.7 Theme 3 — **Welfare, Security and Migration**

- **Belonging** is mediated not only by cultural identity and border policing, but by **access to social and economic rights**.
- Successive economic recessions + rising unemployment since the 1970s made the struggle over distribution of social goods (housing, healthcare, unemployment benefits, jobs) more competitive.
- Result: **welfare chauvinism** — privileging national citizens in distribution of social goods. For welfare chauvinists, immigrants are not just rivals but **illegitimate** claimants of socio-economic rights.
- Welfare provisions are presented as a **magnet pulling migrants** into the EU. Curtailing social rights is then justified as limiting applications.
- The EU mirrors this in **transferability of social entitlements for nationals of Member States** vs. exclusion of third-country nationals.
- Two forms of welfare chauvinism:
  - **Radical** — migrants as "profiteers," "free-loaders," "committing welfare fraud."
  - **Moderate** — community should first and foremost provide benefits for its "own" people; recession justifies curtailment.
- Metaphors like **"invasion" or "flood"** of asylum-seekers dramatise the threat.
- Securitisation here is also embedded in a **struggle for political legitimacy** of the post-war political order in Europe. The welfare-state crisis is in essence a **political crisis** about the decline of the post-war technology of social integration. Migrants become **scapegoats** for declining political legitimacy.

> **Explained easily — what Huysmans is doing:**
> Imagine the EU said in the 1980s, "OK, we'll let goods, services, money, and people move freely between us." Fine for goods. But the moment you remove internal borders, governments panic about who is moving where. So they reinforce **external borders** and start treating immigration and asylum the same way they treat **terrorism, drug trafficking, and organised crime** — same departments, same databases, same logic. Huysmans says: that wasn't inevitable. Police, politicians and media *chose* to lump migration into the security basket. Once that's done, immigrants and asylum-seekers are framed as a threat to (1) public order, (2) national identity/culture, and (3) the welfare state. Even moderate voices end up speaking the security language. **The EU didn't just respond to a "migration crisis" — its institutions helped manufacture one.**

### 3.8 Conclusion — Huysmans' bottom line

- Migration has become a **meta-issue in the political spectacle** — connecting identity controls, visa policy, asylum, integration, social entitlements, cultural diversity.
- Discourses and governmental technologies reifying migrants/asylum-seekers/refugees/foreigners as a **dangerous challenge to societal stability** play a prominent role.
- The **Europeanisation of migration policy has made a distinct contribution** — directly (integrating migration into an internal security framework) and indirectly (the construction of the internal security field; restrictive migration policy; privileging Member State nationals; supporting welfare chauvinism and cultural homogeneity).
- This **bolsters domestic political spectacles** in which migration is easily connected to crime, urban riots, transnational crime, welfare fraud.
- The wider consequence: it sustains a **radical political strategy aimed at excluding particular categories of people by reifying them as a danger** — making inclusion of immigrants, asylum-seekers and refugees more difficult and shaping what kind of solidarity, social integration, cultural identity, civility and public order is promoted in the community.

## 4. Required reading 2 — Hollifield, "The Emerging Migration State" (2004)

*International Migration Review 38(3), pp. 885–912. The founding statement of the "migration state" concept and the canonical formulation of the "liberal paradox."*

### 4.1 The framing — three state functions

Historically in IR theory, the **Westphalian state** is above all a **garrison state** (security). The industrial revolution added an economic function → Rosecrance's **trading state**. The 20th–21st centuries see the rise of the **migration state**, where regulation of international migration is as important as providing security and economic well-being.

Three state functions today:
1. Maintaining national security
2. Building trade and investment regimes
3. **Regulating migration**

### 4.2 The "Liberal Paradox" — central concept

> *"States are trapped in a 'liberal paradox' — in order to maintain a competitive advantage, governments must keep their economies and societies open to trade, investment, and migration. But unlike goods, capital, and services, the movement of people involves greater political risks."*

- The **economic logic** of liberalism is **openness**.
- The **political and legal logic** is **closure**.
- Why? Because:
  - Borders are sacrosanct in the international system; sovereignty requires territorial closure.
  - Unlike trade or finance, migration **changes the ethnic composition of societies** and disrupts what Koslowski calls the "**demographic maintenance regime**."
  - The **demos** (the citizenry) may be transformed in ways that "violate the social contract and undermine the legitimacy of the government" (Walzer 1983).

### 4.3 The German example (anchor case)

- May 2000: Schroder launches **"green card"** programme to recruit 20,000 high-tech workers from outside the EU.
- "We Germans must make sure that in these times of globalization we don't suffer from a lack of cosmopolitanism."
- Coincides with **new German nationality law** (1999) injecting birthright citizenship (**jus soli**) — moving from blood-based to more inclusive citizenship.
- Initially announced **no family reunification** for high-tech workers — quickly reversed after criticism. Classic illustration of the liberal paradox in action.

### 4.4 The migration-trade link — historical

Migration and trade are **two sides of the same coin** — the rise of the trading state necessarily entails the rise of the migration state.

| Pre-1914 | Open migration regimes. Driven by colonisation, push/pull, demographics. Sending states (Germany, Italy) had jus sanguinis (ethnic) citizenship; receiving states (US, France) had jus soli (birthright) |
| WWI–WWII | Closure. War, decolonisation → nationalism → passport/visa systems. 1924 US National Origins Quota system; 1882 Chinese Exclusion Act |
| Post-1945 | New international refugee + human rights regime — **1951 Geneva Convention, principle of non-refoulement, 1948 UDHR**. Postwar order "created new legal spaces (rights) for individuals and groups" |
| 1950s–60s Europe | Guest worker programmes (Germany-Turkey, France-North Africa, US-Mexico bracero) |
| 1973–74 onwards | First oil shock → big shift. End of guest worker recruitment. "We asked for workers, but human beings came" (Max Frisch) |

### 4.5 Why rights matter — Hollifield's central legal-political insight

> *"Individuals, unlike goods, services or capital, have a will of their own and can become subjects of the law and members of the societies in which they reside."*

Liberal democracies have a **"rights-based liberalism"** in jurisprudence (Cornelius, Martin and Hollifield 1994; Joppke 2001). International + municipal law feed off each other, creating new legal spaces for aliens at both levels.

This is why deportation and mass expulsion are not viable in liberal states — they would undermine the state's own legitimacy and *raison d'être*.

### 4.6 The family reunification phase — the canonical illustration

After the 1973–74 suspension of recruitment, Turkish migration to Germany and North African migration to France **continued well into the 1980s** — as **family rather than worker migration**.

What made this possible: the **intervention of courts**, extending rights of residence to guest workers and families. Executive and administrative authorities were **hampered by legal/constitutional constraints**. States with universalistic, republican traditions (US, France, to a lesser extent Germany) + strong independent judiciary had much greater difficulty cutting migration flows. **Britain is the "deviant case"** — parliamentary supremacy, no universalistic republican tradition → easier to restrict.

### 4.7 The 1990s — the shift to control

In response to mass asylum seeking and crisis:

- **External border control** — Operations Gatekeeper and Hold the Line on US-Mexican border; **Schengen system** in Europe; "safe third country" rule.
- **Internal regulation** of labour markets (employer sanctions).
- **Integration** of large established foreign populations.
- **Dublin Convention 1990** — asylum seekers must apply in first "safe country" of arrival. Prevents "asylum shopping" and turns CEE into buffer states.
- **Schengen Agreement 1985** — eliminate border checks in exchange for common visa requirements to control TCNs.
- **Maastricht 1993** — European citizenship.

### 4.8 The 1990s asylum crisis

- End of Cold War + collapse of USSR → easier to leave and seek asylum in the West.
- By the mid-1990s, more than **16 million refugees** worldwide (two-thirds in Africa and Middle East).
- **UNHCR** transformed virtually overnight into one of the most important international institutions.
- Most asylum claims rejected → Western governments concluded most were "**economic refugees**." Human rights advocates feared genuine refugees would be submerged.

### 4.9 The restrictionist response of the 1990s

- **Germany 1993**: amended its constitution to eliminate the blanket right of asylum that was in Article 16 of the old Basic Law.
- **France 1995–96**: **Pasqua and Debré Laws** — roll back rights of foreign residents, make naturalisation harder.
- **US 1996**: Illegal Immigration Reform and Immigrant Responsibility Act — curtailed welfare rights for legal *and* illegal immigrants; limited due-process rights.
- **California Proposition 187** — struck down by courts.
- Many draconian laws were either struck down by courts or substantially modified to conform with liberal-constitutional principles.

But simultaneously: **expansion** of legal immigration for **highly skilled** (H-1B in US, German green card). And liberalisation of citizenship law (France 1997, Germany 1999).

### 4.10 The "migration state" defined

> *"The migration state is almost by definition a liberal state inasmuch as it creates a legal and regulatory environment in which migrants can pursue individual strategies of accumulation. But regulating international migration requires liberal states to be attentive to the (human or civil) rights of the individual."*

Why? Because *"if rights are ignored or trampled upon, then the liberal state risks undermining its own legitimacy and raison d'être."*

The way out (or at least the way to **finesse** the paradox):
- **Multilateral / regional migration regimes** — the EU model.
- The EU has built **a regional migration regime + supra-national authority** that allows member states to "finesse, if not escape, the liberal paradox" (Geddes 2000, 2003).
- Playing **good cop / bad cop** between national and EU level + symbolic politics maintains the **illusion of border control**, fending off forces of closure in the short run.

### 4.11 Why economic substitutes don't fully work

- Some hope **trade and FDI** will substitute for migration (bring capital and jobs to people).
- In the long term: **factor-price equalisation** (Stolper-Samuelson, Mundell). But in the **short/medium term**, exposing LDCs to market forces **often increases migration** (NAFTA → more Mexican migration).
- Trade in services often **stimulates "high-end" migration** because services require movement of the people who make and market them (Bhagwati 1998).

> *"In short, the global integration of markets for goods, services and capital entails higher levels of international migration. Therefore, if states want to promote freer trade and investment, they must be prepared to manage higher levels of migration."*

### 4.12 The political economy of migration policy

- Mass migration of unskilled workers meets greater political resistance (even where there is high demand in sectors like construction or healthcare).
- Tendency: go back to **guest worker models** with strict contracts limiting stay and prohibiting settlement/family reunification.
- **Alternative**: illegal immigration and a growing black market for labour — *"a ==Hobson's choice==."*

### 4.13 Regional integration as midwife to the migration state

- Regional integration **blurs lines of territoriality**, lessening problems of integration and national identity.
- The disjuncture between people and place — which might have provoked a crisis of national identity in a single state — is less of a problem when the state is tied to a regional regime like the EU.
- **Vicente Fox (Mexico) sought a similar approach with NAFTA** — but the US (especially post-9/11) is reluctant.
- In Asia, more divided, often authoritarian, little prospect of rights-granting to migrants. The exceptions (Japan, Taiwan, South Korea) are only beginning. In Africa and the Middle East — high migration but unstable, fluid states with little institutional capacity.

> **Explained easily — what Hollifield is doing:**
> A liberal country can't fully control migration without abandoning its own values. To stay rich, it needs trade, investment, and workers — that means letting people in. But it's also a *liberal* state, meaning courts protect migrants once they're inside (you can't just deport everyone), and the public expects the government to police the borders. So Germany invites Turkish guest workers in the 1960s, tries to send them home in the 1970s, fails because courts protect family reunification, and ends up with a permanent immigrant population. Repeat for every liberal democracy. Max Frisch's line nails it: **"We asked for workers, but human beings came."** Hollifield's contribution is naming this trap — the ==**liberal paradox**== — and saying it's now a *third function* of the state (alongside security and trade): managing the contradiction. The EU is the only experiment so far at doing this regionally.

### 4.14 Conclusion — Hollifield's bottom line

Migration is both a **cause** and a **consequence** of political and economic change. International migration, like trade, is a **fundamental feature of the postwar liberal order**. As states and societies become more liberal and open, migration has increased.

- Will the increase be virtuous (greater openness, wealth, human development) or vicious (anarchy, disorder, war)?
- Depends on how migration is managed by the more powerful liberal states.
- **First halting steps toward an international migration regime have been taken in Europe**; North America likely to follow.
- Pessimistic note: the **asymmetry of interests between developed and developing world** makes a truly UN-led international regime unlikely. *"States are likely to remain trapped in a liberal paradox for decades to come."*

## 5. Cross-readings and connections

### 5.1 Huysmans ↔ Hollifield

| | Huysmans (2000) | Hollifield (2004) |
|---|---|---|
| **Disciplinary lens** | Critical / constructivist; Copenhagen School (securitization) | More mainstream / liberal IR + political economy |
| **What drives migration politics** | **Discourse + practice** — political construction of migration as security threat by police, governments, media | **Economic + legal logic** — interaction of supply-push, demand-pull, and rights-based liberal jurisprudence |
| **The state** | Site of "politics of belonging" — security policy mediates membership | The "migration state" — a third state function alongside garrison and trading |
| **Liberal contradiction** | EU policy is **ambivalent**: supports nationalism/xenophobia + multicultural project at once | The **liberal paradox**: open economically, closed politically |
| **EU's role** | Part of the problem — directly and indirectly securitises migration | Part of the solution — regional regime that allows states to **finesse** the paradox |

They are **complementary** rather than rival accounts. Huysmans tells you about the **politics** of how migration becomes a security issue; Hollifield tells you about the **legal/economic structure** that produces the paradox in the first place.

> **Explained easily — Huysmans vs. Hollifield in one sentence:**
> Huysmans tells you *how* migration becomes a political problem (discourse + practice — it's constructed). Hollifield tells you *why states are stuck* with it (an economic-vs-political contradiction baked into liberalism). You usually need both to write a strong essay.

### 5.2 The Paris vs. Copenhagen split (from lecture, important for full marks)

| | Copenhagen School (Wæver, Buzan) | Paris School (Bigo) |
|---|---|---|
| Securitisation is… | A **speech act** — labelling something a security issue makes it one | A set of **everyday practices** of professionals — surveillance, biometrics, risk management |
| Where to look | Political discourse, exceptional measures | Bureaucratic routines, "managers of flows" |
| Where the threat comes from | Existential threats articulated by political elites | "Governmentality of fear and unease" enacted by border guards, police, customs, datasharing systems |
| Solution to securitisation | **Desecuritisation** — return to normal politics | Critique of the entire bureaucratic field |

> **Explained easily — Copenhagen vs. Paris:**
> Copenhagen looks at **politicians on TV** saying "migrants are a threat" — that *speech act* is what creates the security problem. Paris looks at **the border guard at the airport** running biometric checks, the customs officer profiling travellers, the database engineer building Eurodac — those *everyday practices* are what really create the regime of suspicion. Same phenomenon, two levels of analysis. On the exam, name both and you look like you've read the lecture properly.

Huysmans synthesises elements of both — he's more on the Copenhagen side but draws on Bigo's "internal security field" idea.

### 5.3 The Eurocentric blind spot (link to Weeks 4–5)

Wolff's lecture flags **N. El Qadim** — a postcolonial scholar arguing that EU migration approaches **ignore local circumstances**. Migration scholarship is itself Eurocentric. This is the Acharya/Seth critique applied to migration:
- Most migration is intra-regional, not the dramatic South-North flows that dominate European political imagination.
- French-Moroccan cooperation on forced returns (one of the recommended Week 6 readings — El Qadim 2014) shows how postcolonial dynamics shape contemporary migration control.
- The **EU-Tunisia deal** can be read as a contemporary form of **migration diplomacy** with deep postcolonial roots — Europe externalising border control to former colonial spaces.

## 6. Likely exam questions and how to answer

### 6.1 Concept-level (MCQs / short definitions)

- ==**Refugee** — Art. 1 of the 1951 Convention: person outside their country of nationality with a "well-founded fear of persecution" on grounds of race, religion, nationality, social group, or political opinion.==
- **Asylum-seeker** — has applied for international protection but case not yet decided.
- **IDP** — displaced but has not crossed an internationally recognised state border.
- ==**Non-refoulement** — a signatory of the 1951 Convention cannot legally send an individual back to a country where they face persecution==.
- **Liberal paradox** (Hollifield) — economic logic of liberalism = openness; political/legal logic = closure. States are trapped between.
- **Migration state** (Hollifield) — the third state function (alongside garrison and trading), in which migration regulation is central to state purpose.
- **Securitization of migration** (Huysmans / Copenhagen School) — process by which migration is politically constructed as a security issue through speech acts and institutional practices that move it beyond normal politics. - ==is the paris school also under securitization of migration, just another approach of obtaining it or what is the relation?
- **Speech act** (Wæver) — security is not a property of an issue but is created by labelling something as a security issue.
- ==**Security continuum** (Bigo / Huysmans) — institutionalised mode of policy-making linking border control, terrorism, crime and migration. Allows transfer of security connotations between these areas.==
- **Border externalization** — pushing migration control beyond your territory (e.g., EU-Tunisia, EU-Turkey deals).
- **Migration diplomacy** — using migration as a bargaining chip in interstate relations; can be coercive (blackmail) or cooperative (backscratching). Material gains (economic, institutional, aid) + symbolic gains (status, reputation, moral standing).
- **Welfare chauvinism** — privileging national citizens in distribution of social goods; framing migrants as illegitimate claimants.
- **Fortress Europe** — popular shorthand for the restrictive, externalised, high-walled EU migration regime.
- **Schengen Agreement (1985)** — eliminate internal border checks among signatories in exchange for common external visa requirements.
- **Dublin Convention (1990)** — asylum seekers must apply in first "safe country" of arrival; prevents "asylum shopping."

### 6.2 Short-answer template — "Securitization of migration"

**Define:** Securitization (Wæver, Buzan and the Copenhagen School) is the process by which an issue is politically constructed as a security threat through speech acts — "it is by labelling something a security issue that it becomes one." The securitisation move pushes an issue beyond normal politics into emergency politics, justifying extraordinary measures that circumvent democratic rules. Applied to migration, this means immigrants, asylum-seekers and refugees are reified as a danger to societal stability.

**Apply (a case):** **Huysmans' analysis of EU migration policy** shows how the **spillover of the internal market into an internal security project** securitised migration in the EU. The abolition of internal border controls under the SEA was paired with reinforced external borders, and the Third Pillar on Justice and Home Affairs, Schengen and Dublin institutionalised migration alongside terrorism, transnational crime and policing. Police and security professionals — via networks like TREVI and Bern Club — produced a **security continuum** linking borders, terrorism, crime and migration. Three themes anchor this: (1) **internal security** (Fortress Europe), (2) **cultural security** (homogeneity vs. multicultural reality), and (3) **welfare chauvinism** (migrants as illegitimate claimants on welfare). Wolff's lecture cases (e.g. **EU-Tunisia deal 2023**) extend this into the present: €105 million for Tunisian coastguards plus institutional + macroeconomic carrots, while framing asylum as a "geopolitical risk."

**Assess (explains/overlooks):**
- *Explains:* Why migration becomes politically central even when the actual numbers don't justify the alarm; why "extraordinary" anti-migration measures get democratically accepted; the role of bureaucratic professionals (Paris School: Bigo's "managers of flows") in shaping the meaning of borders; why securitisation persists across different governing coalitions (it's **structural**, not the act of a few far-right parties).
- *Overlooks:* (1) the **liberal paradox** Hollifield identifies — economic forces still drive openness and rights-based jurisprudence still protects migrants; (2) the Copenhagen School can over-emphasise discourse and under-emphasise everyday practice (Paris School corrective); (3) **postcolonial blind spot** — most migration is intra-regional, not South-North, and the EU-Tunisia/EU-Turkey deals carry colonial echoes (El Qadim); (4) it can essentialise "security" itself — risk of just *describing* securitisation without offering a route to **desecuritisation**.

### 6.3 Short-answer template — "The liberal paradox and the migration state"

**Define:** The **liberal paradox** (Hollifield 1992, 2004) holds that liberal states are caught between two opposing logics — the **economic logic** of liberalism, which pushes toward openness to trade, investment and migration to maintain competitiveness; and the **political and legal logic** of liberalism, which pushes toward closure because borders, the demos and the social contract require some degree of territorial closure. The **migration state** is Hollifield's name for the third function of the modern state (after the garrison state and Rosecrance's trading state), where regulation of migration is now central to state purpose.

**Apply (a case):** **Postwar Germany.** Initially recruited Turkish guest workers; **1973 oil shock** → recruitment suspension; courts intervened to extend residence rights → family reunification phase continued migration despite halt. Schroder's **2000 "green card"** for high-tech workers + 1999 jus soli citizenship reforms show the liberal direction. Yet attempts to ban family reunification for green card workers were quickly reversed under rights pressure. The "We asked for workers, but human beings came" (Frisch) line captures the paradox exactly. **The EU as regional regime** lets Germany finesse (not escape) the paradox: free movement within, externalised control outside; symbolic politics, good cop / bad cop between EU and member state, and shared border governance (Schengen, Frontex).

**Assess (explains/overlooks):**
- *Explains:* Why deportations are sparing and largely symbolic; why mass expulsions are not viable in liberal democracies; why courts repeatedly strike down draconian laws (California Proposition 187, French Pasqua/Debré Laws); why high-skilled migration is expanded while low-skilled is curtailed; why guest worker schemes are politically attractive but legally unsustainable.
- *Overlooks:* (1) Huysmans' securitisation angle — Hollifield is light on the **politics of cultural anxiety**; (2) the **welfare/labour-market** restructuring under neoliberalism shapes who counts as "desirable" migration; (3) **postcolonial dimensions** of policy (border externalisation to former colonies; rights stratification by origin); (4) the model is implicitly **Western/OECD-centric** — most of the world's migration occurs in regions (Africa, Middle East, Asia) without the rights-based liberal infrastructure Hollifield assumes.

### 6.4 Cross-week threads

- **Week 5 (critical theories)**: Huysmans' securitisation is a **constructivist/critical** approach; the Paris School (Bigo) is poststructuralist (Foucauldian "governmentality"). The wider critique of EU migration governance is a postcolonial critique (El Qadim — links to Seth Week 5 reading).
- **Week 4 (NWIRT)**: the Eurocentric framing of migration scholarship is what Acharya/Seth would call a failure of deprovincialization.
- **Week 7 (war, peace, security)**: securitisation theory connects to broader debates about how things become "security issues" — Barkawi's "decolonising war" extends similar logic.
- **Week 8 (world orders)**: the EU as a regional migration regime is part of the wider debate about regional integration as a response to the crisis of the liberal international order.
- **Week 9 (EU regional integration)**: a deep dive into the EU as global actor; migration is a key test case for "Normative Power Europe" and EU strategic autonomy.
- **Week 11 (globalization and populism)**: Rodrik on populism and economics of globalization links to migration politics; right-wing populist mobilisation around migration is the political consequence of Huysmans' "politics of belonging."
