# Lecture 5 — Falsificationism

*Karl Popper's model of science as trial and error: scientists can only ever *refute* hypotheses, never *confirm* them. The asymmetry of confirmation and refutation, the demarcation criterion, the rejection of induction — and four criticisms.*

**Date:** 4 March 2026 · **Lecturer:** Prof. James W. McAllister · **Key thinker:** **Karl R. Popper** (1902–1994)

---

## Key concepts — definitions

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **To falsify** | To refute; to establish empirically that a hypothesis is untrue. |
| **Falsifiability** | The susceptibility of a hypothesis to being falsified. Popper's hallmark of scientific status. |
| **Falsification** | The act of falsifying a hypothesis. |
| **Falsificationism** | Popper's thesis: **scientific method consists in falsification**. Scientists invent hypotheses and try to break them; what survives counts as (provisional) scientific knowledge. |
| **Asymmetry of confirmation and refutation** | Popper's key logical insight. Observing that hypothesis H entails observation O, then seeing O happen, does *not* prove H (logically invalid: "affirming the consequent"). But seeing *not-O* happen *does* prove not-H (logically valid: *modus tollens*). So we can only ever refute, never confirm. |
| **Demarcation problem** | How to distinguish science from non-science. Popper's answer: **falsifiability**. If a discipline's practitioners can say what would refute their claims, the discipline is scientific. If they can't, it's pseudo-science (e.g. astrology). |
| **Context of discovery** | The phase in which scientists *freely conjecture* hypotheses. No rules, just creativity. Inspiration can come from dreams, observations, anything — none of which justifies the hypothesis. |
| **Context of justification** | The phase in which scientists *test* hypotheses. Phase of logic, rigour, objectivity. The actual locus of "scientific method." |
| **Trial and error** | Popper's image of science. Conjectures (trials) are subjected to refutation attempts (error-elimination). What survives goes forward to the next test. |
| **Corroboration** | Popper's late-career concession: a weak form of confirmation. Hypotheses that have survived many genuine refutation attempts are "corroborated" — not proved, but tentatively more trustworthy. |
| **Ad hoc modification** | Tinkering with a hypothesis to save it from refutation. Popper disapproved; critics say scientists do this productively all the time. |
| **Pseudo-science** | A discipline whose claims are *unfalsifiable* (e.g. astrology's vague predictions, some forms of psychoanalysis on Popper's view). |

## Lecture walkthrough

### Popper's two questions

1. **What is a sound way to test scientific hypotheses?** → falsification, not confirmation.
2. **How does science differ from non-science?** → falsifiability is the criterion.

His overriding priority: **eliminate uncertainty from science**. Hume's problem of induction told him you can never *confirm* with certainty — but you *can* refute with certainty (one black swan kills "all swans are white"). Build the whole picture of science on that asymmetry.

### How to test a hypothesis

You can't test the entire hypothesis directly (too general). But you can test its **observational consequences**.

> Hypothesis H entails observational claim O. Check whether O is true or false. Infer something about H.

Two possible outcomes:
- **O is true.** Does *not* prove H — many other hypotheses might also entail O. *Logically invalid:* "H entails O. O. Therefore H." (Affirming the consequent.)
- **O is false.** Proves H is false. *Logically valid:* "H entails O. Not O. Therefore not H." (*Modus tollens*.)

→ Only refutation gives certainty. Stop trying to confirm.

### Popper's image of science

- Scientific knowledge = a set of **not-yet-refuted hypotheses**.
- Scientific progress = **elimination of errors**, not accumulation of truths.
- Scientific practice characterised in **negative** terms — what we've ruled out.
- Attractive picture: scientists as **creative, risk-taking, undogmatic**.

### Demarcation criterion

A discipline is scientific if its practitioners can say **what observations would falsify their claims**. Astrology's "you will meet a tall, mysterious stranger next month" is unfalsifiable (anyone tall-ish counts, "stranger" is vague, no time limit) → pseudo-science. Many researchers still use Popper's question reflexively: *"Interesting theory — but is it falsifiable?"*

### Four criticisms

1. **Refutation is less straightforward than Popper assumed.** Most hypotheses yield predictions only when combined with **auxiliary assumptions**. A failed prediction could be the fault of the hypothesis *or* of an auxiliary — you can't always tell which.
2. **Lack of accord with scientific practice.** Even Popper's heroes (Einstein) weren't strict falsificationists. Scientists make *ad hoc* modifications all the time, and this is often fruitful — "methodological value of dogmatism."
3. **Popper neglects ways to make confirmation more rigorous.** Pragmatic warrant (Lecture 3), **Bayesian confirmation theory**. Even Popper conceded "corroboration" near the end of his career.
4. **Some valuable scientific hypotheses are not strictly falsifiable.** Probabilistic / statistical hypotheses ("A is correlated with B," e.g. income & health) can't be killed by a single counter-instance. Falsifiability ends up ruling out a lot of good *social* science.

## Key thinker — Karl R. Popper

Born Vienna 1902; PhD 1928; taught in New Zealand during WWII; Professor at London School of Economics from 1945. Died 1994. Founder of the school of **critical rationalism**. Five major books:
- *The Open Society and Its Enemies* (1945) — liberal polemic against Plato, Marx, and "historicism."
- ***The Logic of Scientific Discovery* (1959)** — the treatise that introduced falsificationism.
- *Conjectures and Refutations* (1963) — essays on science as trial and error.
- *Objective Knowledge* (1972) — links science with Darwinian evolution.
- *Unended Quest* (1982) — autobiography.

His influence outside professional philosophy of science remains enormous — the phrase "but is it falsifiable?" is one of the most common philosophical demands in working science.

> **Explained easily — the asymmetry, in one analogy:**
> Imagine you guess: "all swans are white." Every white swan you see is *consistent* with your guess but doesn't prove it (a black swan could still be hiding somewhere). A *single* black swan, however, *kills* your guess outright. So evidence is asymmetric: it can refute, but it can't confirm. Popper's whole philosophy of science is built on that one observation.

> **Explained easily — what science actually does, per Popper:**
> Scientists don't accumulate truths — they accumulate "things we've successfully *not* refuted." Your best current scientific theory is just the one that's survived the hardest beating so far. That can sound deflating, but Popper meant it as a *liberating* picture: science is creative, risk-taking, never dogmatic, always one experiment away from being wrong.

> **Explained easily — the astrology test:**
> The reason astrology fails the science test isn't that horoscopes are wrong — it's that they're *too vague to be wrong*. "You will meet a stranger" can match any week. If a discipline's claims can't be pinned down enough to be refuted by some specific observation, they're not scientific claims, just suggestive ones.

> **Explained easily — why "but is it falsifiable?" doesn't always work:**
> Try applying it to "income correlates with health." A single rich sick person doesn't refute that — it's a statistical claim. So Popper's test ends up *too strict*, ruling out a lot of useful social science. Most working scientists today think falsifiability is *one* virtue among several, not the single criterion of science.

## Likely exam angles

- **Define:** falsificationism, falsifiability, the asymmetry of confirmation and refutation.
- **Apply:** Why is astrology a pseudo-science on Popper's view? Find your own example.
- **Critical:** Name two criticisms of falsificationism and explain why each matters.
- **Compare:** Popper's image of science vs Kuhn's (Lecture 6) — Popper sees science as continuous trial-and-error refinement; Kuhn sees long periods of paradigm-bound puzzle-solving punctuated by revolutionary breaks.
- **Cross-lecture:** Popper accepted Hume's problem of induction (Lecture 3) and *built around it* — falsificationism is his attempt to do science *without* induction.
