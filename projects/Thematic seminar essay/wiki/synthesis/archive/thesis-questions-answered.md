# Thesis Questions Answered

*Synthesis of answers to the two core questions driving the thematic seminar essay, based on existing sources in `raw/` and the overnight web search.*

---

## Question 1 — Why does Indonesia pursue an ETS first rather than a carbon tax?

> *Why did Indonesia choose to sequence an ETS first while indefinitely postponing the carbon tax, when (a) the literature broadly prefers a tax, (b) Indonesia's own design is a hybrid, and (c) academic models recommend a tax rate of $30–60/tCO₂ versus Indonesia's planned $2/tCO₂?*

### Short answer (essay-ready paragraph)

Indonesia's ETS-first sequencing is best explained by political economy rather than policy design. The carbon tax — mandated by Law 7/2021 and set at IDR 30,000/tCO₂ (~$2) — was postponed from April 2022 to July 2022, then again to 2025, and remains unenforced as of January 2026. Meanwhile, the NEK ETS launched in February 2023. The gap between legal mandate and actual implementation reflects three overlapping political constraints:

1. **Burden visibility.** A carbon tax visibly raises fuel and electricity prices for households and would be associated in the public mind with the government imposing a cost. An ETS, by contrast, places obligations on ~563 power-sector facilities — mostly the state-owned utility PLN and large energy-intensive industries. The burden is concentrated, invisible to the general public, and manageable through bilateral negotiation between government and industry.

2. **Free allocation as a subsidy to connected industries.** The NEK ETS provided 100% free allocation in Phase 1 and 75–85% free thereafter. This means the politically powerful nickel processing, steel, cement, and captive-coal sectors — exactly the industries at the centre of Indonesia's "downstreaming" industrial-policy ambitions (the EV battery supply chain) — received a carbon-pricing obligation that costs them almost nothing in the short run. A carbon tax would not have allowed this kind of targeted subsidy.

3. **Regulatory complexity as cover for delay.** Indonesia's Minister of Finance cited the need for a "carbon tax roadmap" and cross-ministry coordination as reasons for each postponement. The legal analysis (Pena Justisia 2024, from the overnight search) confirms that the delay is partly genuine (MRV infrastructure was not ready) and partly strategic: without a roadmap, there is no enforceable obligation. Industry lobbying — including from the oil and gas sector (Saputra & Haryono 2025) — has exploited this complexity to prevent the tax from taking effect.

4. **Article 6 and IDXCarbon revenue without new taxation.** The ETS generates credit-trading revenue and enables international transfers under Article 6 of the Paris Agreement (moratorium lifted January 2025). This revenue stream reaches the government and covered entities without requiring a new line item in the national tax code — important in a context where the government is sensitive about perceptions of new taxes.

5. **Financial-market vs. tax-authority governance.** The ETS is administered by OJK (the Financial Services Authority) and routed through the IDXCarbon exchange. This keeps the mechanism within the financial-market infrastructure rather than the tax authority — which matters politically because it avoids the Ministry of Finance directly raising a "tax" on energy.

### What the literature says about the sequencing choice

The AMRO "Cap First, and Then Tax" analysis (Andriansyah & Hong) describes the hybrid design as a deliberate sequencing strategy: the ETS was intended to generate a market price signal before the carbon tax kicks in as a compliance backstop. The carbon levy under Presidential Regulation 110/2025 is explicitly tied to the prevailing ETS market price — so in theory, once the ETS produces a credible price, the tax follows automatically. The problem is that the ETS currently produces almost no price signal (average price ~$4/tCO₂e in 2025; total transaction value March–September 2025 = ~$73,000 per IEEFA). So the tax keeps being deferred because the price signal it was supposed to follow never materialised.

The IMF (Parry, Black & Roaf 2022) notes that ETSs can be politically attractive because they "hide the cost" from public view — the emission constraint is built into the market, and prices are set by supply and demand rather than by a visible government decision. This is directly relevant: Indonesia's government chose the mechanism that least requires them to announce a price.

Finon 2019 provides the theoretical anchor: developing economies face political-economy constraints that make standard carbon-pricing prescriptions (i.e., "set a high tax") a poor fit. The constraint is not ignorance — Indonesian policymakers know what the literature recommends — but politics.

**Counter-evidence to acknowledge:** Yusuf & Resosudarmo 2015 and Rahma, Hartono & Hastuti 2025 show that the carbon tax is *not* as regressive as feared at the national level — it is progressive among rural lower-income households (who use less fossil energy) and only regressive in urban areas. So the political-cost argument partly rests on a distributional misperception. This is worth a paragraph in the essay: the tax's political toxicity is partly perception, not fact — which implies that with the right communication and revenue-recycling design, the tax *could* be made politically viable.

## Question 2 — What are the most crucial things Indonesia should do now to enhance the environmental and economic framework of carbon pricing?

### Short answer (essay-ready paragraph)

The developing-country recommendation literature converges on five moves. Crucially, Indonesia has already committed to all five on paper (Presidential Regulation 110/2025, the 2025 NDC, Law 7/2021). The binding constraint is not policy design — it is implementation pace. Here are the five moves, with the Indonesia-specific implication of each:

---

### Move 1 — Enforce the carbon tax with a credible escalator path

**The recommendation (from Sunanda et al. 2025; van den Bergh & Drews 2026; Parry et al. 2022):** Start with even a low carbon tax but commit to a predictable annual escalation — e.g., $2 → $10 → $30 over 2026–2035. Price certainty matters more for investment than the starting level.

**Indonesia-specific implication:** This means actually enforcing Law 7/2021's IDR 30,000/tCO₂ ($2) rate with a legislated escalator. The tax has been postponed four times; the next deferral should require cabinet-level political commitment, not just regulatory revision. As long as investors cannot predict when or whether the carbon tax will apply, no green investment decision can be made on that basis.

**Literature anchor:** Hartono et al. 2023 shows that even at a low rate, carbon-tax revenue recycling raises Indonesian GDP and employment. The economic case for enforcement is strong. Sembiring et al. 2025 quantify the emissions impact: at $2, reductions to 2050 are marginal; at $63, they are substantial. The gap between law and reality is the central policy failure.

---

### Move 2 — Revenue recycling toward households and green investment

**The recommendation (UNCTAD 2022; Hartono et al. 2023; Ojha et al. 2020):** Carbon pricing revenues must be visibly recycled toward: (a) household transfers to offset regressive effects on urban energy consumers, and (b) green investment (renewable energy, grid modernisation). This is the "double dividend" (Goulder 1995): reduced emissions AND improved economic welfare.

**Indonesia-specific implication:** Indonesia has an existing conditional cash-transfer infrastructure (PKH programme) that could serve as the recycling vehicle. Hartono et al. 2023 modelled exactly this for Indonesia — the results are positive: GDP and employment rise when revenue is recycled productively. The government should publish a clear revenue-use commitment alongside any enforcement of the carbon tax, so that the political narrative shifts from "a new tax" to "a tax that pays for X."

**Why this is the crucial unlock:** The distributional fear (that the tax hurts the poor) is the political cover for industry resistance. Revenue recycling removes that cover. Once voters can see what the tax money is doing, the political economy changes.

---

### Move 3 — Shift ETS allocation toward auctions

**The recommendation (Sunanda et al. 2025; ICAP design standards):** Auction-based allocation, not free allocation, is the standard for a mature ETS. Free allocation subsidises incumbents and prevents new entrants; auction revenue can be recycled (see Move 2); auctions generate a transparent price signal.

**Indonesia-specific implication:** The NEK ETS has given free allocation since launch. No auctions have occurred as of January 2026. The transition to partial auctions (e.g., 25% by 2027, 50% by 2030) would simultaneously generate revenue, improve the price signal, and push covered entities to actually reduce emissions rather than game the allocation.

**Caution:** Moving too fast to auctions without price-floor mechanisms risks a price crash (as happened in Europe's early ETS phases). Presidential Regulation 110/2025 provides for a carbon levy as a backstop — this is the price floor. Enforcing the levy at the same time as introducing auctions would protect the price signal.

---

### Move 4 — Expand ETS coverage to industry, transport, and land use

**The recommendation (Sunanda et al. 2025; van den Bergh & Drews 2026):** Power-sector coverage alone is insufficient. Indonesia's power sector covers only ~21% of national emissions (225 MtCO₂e of 1,053 MtCO₂e, per ICAP 2025). Forestry (deforestation), transport, and palm oil are the major unpriced sources.

**Indonesia-specific implication:** Phase 3 of the NEK ETS (2028–30) is supposed to extend to all fossil fuel power. Beyond that, industrial expansion and AFOLU (Agriculture, Forestry and Other Land Use) coverage require separate regulatory tracks. The MRV infrastructure for forestry is partially in place via REDD+ (Gatto & Sadik-Zada 2025); peatland restoration is the priority (Tan et al. 2022 — marginal abatement cost is low). Indonesia should treat the ETS expansion and the REDD+/peatland track as complementary decarbonisation pillars.

---

### Move 5 — Use Article 6 to finance the transition, not just to export credits

**The recommendation (TBI 2025; Kerr & Hu 2025; ICAP 2025):** Indonesia's coal retirement abatement cost is $12–13/tCO₂ — among the cheapest large-scale mitigation opportunities globally. International finance (via Article 6 transfers) could fund this coal retirement *if* the deals are structured to ensure domestic decarbonisation, not just credit export.

**Indonesia-specific implication:** The risk is what Jakob & Steckel 2014 call the "climate finance curse" — becoming a credit-export economy without domestic structural change. The 1.78 MtCO₂e of PLN-affiliated credits already authorised for international transfer under Article 6 (January 2025) shows the mechanism works; the question is whether the underlying abatement is real and additional. Indonesia needs a high-integrity domestic registry (the SRN-PPI system exists on paper) and a domestic-use floor on credits to prevent all abatement from being exported.

---

### Summary table — five moves

| Move | What the literature says | Indonesia has committed to it? | What's missing |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Enforce carbon tax with escalator | Essential; start low, raise predictably | Law 7/2021 — yes | Enforcement; no escalator announced |
| 2. Revenue recycling | Doubles political feasibility + economic gains | Not explicit in current law | Public commitment to specific recycling use |
| 3. Shift to auctions | Generates revenue, improves price signal | No auctions yet | Timetable for first auction |
| 4. Expand to industry/land use | Power sector = 21% of emissions | Phase 3 planned for 2028–30 | AFOLU, transport still not covered |
| 5. Article 6 with domestic floor | Coal retirement at $12–13/tCO₂ | Moratorium lifted Jan 2025 | Integrity safeguards; domestic-use floor |

**The essay's punchline:** Indonesia has committed to all five moves on paper. Presidential Regulation 110/2025 contains moves 1, 2 (implicitly), 3, and 5. The NDC contains moves 4 and 5. **The binding constraint is not policy design — it is political will to implement the commitments already made.**
