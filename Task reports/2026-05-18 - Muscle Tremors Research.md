## Key findings

### Most likely culprits for this specific pattern

**1. Post-exercise adrenaline surge + CNS fatigue (explains the post-bench-press shaking)**
- Heavy lifting triggers a large adrenaline release. Adrenaline directly stimulates motor neurons and enhances physiological tremor — this is well-documented and expected. After a maximal effort like a 100 kg bench, motor units are temporarily depleted and adrenaline is still elevated → fingers shake.
- This is **not a sign of weakness or illness** — it is a sign of a hard effort. The fact that you benched 100 kg confirms your strength is fine.

**2. Magnesium deficiency (explains the day-to-day shakiness)**
- A peer-reviewed systematic review of 60 patients found that postural tremor (shaking while holding a position — e.g. a plate) is the most common tremor type caused by low magnesium (23.3% of cases). Magnesium deficiency is extremely common in young adults eating a typical Western diet.
- The mechanism: magnesium blocks calcium from over-exciting nerve cells. Without it, nerves fire too easily.

**3. Vitamin D deficiency (a plausible background factor)**
- A 40-study systematic review found up to 83–89% of people with movement disorders have low vitamin D. Living in the Netherlands as a student with limited outdoor time in spring = high risk group.

**4. Pre-workout fasting + caffeine (amplifier)**
- Training on an empty stomach or after only coffee is a known triple-stacker for post-exercise tremor: low glucose, caffeine's tremor-amplifying effect, and adrenaline. Any one of these alone is manageable; combined they can produce noticeably shaky hands after a hard set.

## Practical recommendations (short version)

1. **Get a blood panel:** magnesium, vitamin D (25-OH), B12, potassium. Inexpensive, conclusive.
2. **Eat before training:** carbs + protein 60–90 min before; snack immediately after.
3. **Hydrate properly** and add electrolytes on hard training days.
4. **Try magnesium supplementation:** 300–400 mg magnesium glycinate/citrate daily.
5. **Vitamin D in winter/spring:** 1,000–2,000 IU daily is standard in Northern Europe.
6. **Don't train fasted + caffeinated:** food first, then coffee if needed.
7. **Sleep:** even one night of poor sleep measurably increases tremor the next day.

## Limitations

- Could not download full PDFs (sandbox restriction) — relied on PMC open-access HTML versions and abstracts.
- Caffeine research is slightly mixed (single dose of 325 mg in a lab showed no effect; but 3 mg/kg dose in another study did show significant tremor increase). Both effects are real but dose-dependent.
- Vitamin D link to tremor is associative, not yet proven causal in clinical trials.
- None of this replaces a doctor's assessment if the shakiness worsens or appears at rest without exercise/stress context.
