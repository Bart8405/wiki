# Task Report — Cognitive Independence Guide (Task 7)

**Date:** 2026-05-19  
**Task:** "I don't want to offload my cognitive tasks here so I don't just lose them — write a guide on what should I not be using Claude Code for not to become dumb and unable to think on my own."

---

## What the task asked for

A guide identifying which tasks Bart should keep doing himself rather than delegating to Claude, specifically to protect his intellectual development and critical thinking capacity.

---

## Assumptions made

- This guide belongs in the `personal` project (alongside the task 5 Claude Code humanities guide, which covers what Claude *is* good for — this guide is its complement).
- "Not becoming dumb" translates to protecting specific cognitive skills: original argumentation, reading comprehension, source evaluation, opinion-formation, language acquisition, memory.
- Bart's research context (political economy, Indonesia, SE Asia, humanities) should shape the examples throughout.
- The guide should be practical and direct, not abstract — rules Bart can actually follow.

---

## What I did

Created `projects/personal/wiki/synthesis/cognitive-independence-guide.md`.

The guide is organised into five parts:

**Part 1 — Tasks that belong entirely to you.** Five cognitive acts where the process is the point: forming your research question, reading a source for the first time, writing the first attempt at an argument, making connections between ideas, and evaluating a source's credibility. Each has a concrete "the rule" section.

**Part 2 — Skills that atrophy fastest.** Three slower-burning risks: language learning (Bahasa Indonesia specifically), post-lecture recall, and opinion-formation about news and events.

**Part 3 — Warning signs.** Five signals that dependency has crept in — feeling anxious without Claude, not being able to summarise reading, writing that sounds like Claude, etc.

**Part 4 — The "you first" rule.** A single practical rule that covers most cases: form your own view first, write it down, then bring it to Claude. Takes two minutes and prevents the worst of the cognitive offloading.

**Part 5 — What Claude is actually for.** A table of good uses — finding sources, formatting, generating Anki cards from readings you've already done — so the guide doesn't feel anti-AI. It ends on a positive reframe: Claude amplifies what you've already done.

---

## Key judgment call

The framing could have been "use Claude less" but that would be useless advice. Instead I framed it around *sequencing*: do the intellectual work yourself first, then bring Claude in. This means Bart can still use Claude extensively while protecting the specific moments where his own cognition needs to fire.

The core principle: **Do not use Claude to skip the friction. Friction is where growth happens.**

---

## What Bart should review

- The "you first" rule in Part 4 is the practical core of the whole guide. If Bart only remembers one thing, that's it.
- Part 3 (warning signs) is the most personally useful section — worth checking against Bart's current habits every few months.
- The guide cross-references the task 5 humanities guide (`claude-code-humanities-student-guide.md`) as a complement. Together they form a paired picture of what Claude is for and what it isn't for.
