# Week 8 — Power, Liberalism, and World Order

*What is a "world order," how does power shape it, and is the liberal world order in crisis? The lecture traces world orders from bipolarity through the post-Cold War liberal moment to today's contested multipolar landscape.*

## Lecture walkthrough

### What is world order and why does it matter?

==The lecture opens with two definitions of world order:==
1. ==**Heywood:** the actual or perceived distribution of power, giving rise to stable patterns of relationships and behaviours.==
2. ==**Roach & O'Callaghan:** a stable pattern of relations that sustains common goals — *not to be confused with peace or justice.*== ^auto-power-liberalism-and-world-ord

The second definition matters: world order is about stability and power, not necessarily fairness. A stable world order can be deeply unjust.

**Why it matters — two nexuses:**
- **Order and power:** order follows power, especially the balance of power. The post-Cold War liberal order was the product of US dominance.
- **Order and diversity:** the central question today — how do you sustain a stable order when the world's values, interests, and power are increasingly diverse?

### Varieties of world order: from bipolarity to multiplexity

| Order type | Period / description                                               |
| ---------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Bipolar    | Cold War: US vs USSR, two blocs                                    |
| Unipolar   | Post-1991: US hegemony, "unipolar moment"                          |
| Multipolar | Today: multiple great powers (US, China, Russia, India...)         |
| Multiplex  | Acharya's concept: multiple distinct shows, audiences have choices |
|            |                                                                    |

==Acharya's *multiplex* is importantly different from simple *multipolarity*. Multipolarity describes *how many* powers there are. Multiplexity describes the *character* of the system: diverse, with no single actor able to set the rules or impose its vision. It is a cinema with multiple screens, not just a bigger screen.== ^auto-power-liberalism-and-world-ord-3

**Historical note:** the lecture draws on scholarship (Zarakol) showing that sovereignty is not uniquely a Westphalian/European invention. The Chingissid order (Mongol Empire, 13th–18th century) also had concepts of centralised authority, territorial reach, and external recognition as a legitimising force. World orders predate and extend beyond the European tradition.

### Power — what is it?

**Definition (Dahl, 1961):** "The ability to get others to do something that they would not otherwise do."

==The lecture distinguishes two types of power:== ^auto-power-liberalism-and-world-ord-6
1. ==**Power as resource (absolute/quantitative):** about what you *possess* — military might, economic size, population. Associated with realism.==
2. ==**Power as outcome (relational):** about your ability to *use* what you have, including through skills, strategies, and perceptions. Even small states can be influential this way.==

**Hard vs. Soft power (Nye):**
- Hard power = military + economic coercion (force, sanctions, payments) — realist tool
- Soft power = attraction through culture, values, foreign policy — liberal tool
- Smart power = combining both

==**Institutional power:** Power is embedded in institutions (Thelen 2004). Institutions are "the rules of the game" (North 1990) — whoever shapes the rules shapes outcomes. The US shaped post-WWII institutions (UN, WTO, IMF) in its own image, giving it structural/institutional power long after its material dominance peaked.== ^auto-power-liberalism-and-world-ord-7

**Dani Rodrik's globalization trilemma:** You cannot simultaneously have:
- Deep global economic integration
- Full national sovereignty
- Democracy

You can pick two. The EU has traded sovereignty for integration + democracy (supranationalism). China has deep integration + sovereignty but limited democracy. This "impossibility theorem" frames the political backlash against globalization (populism, "precariat," rising inequality) as inevitable.

### The crisis of the Liberal World Order

**Post-Cold War new world (dis)order:**
- After 1989: de-ideologization (Gorbachev), strengthened UN peacekeeping, rule-of-law-based order, US-Soviet cooperation.
- But also: collapse of bipolarity that had given unity → fragmentation, ethnic and regional conflicts.

**The War on Terror (9/11):**
- Robert Kagan (2004): "America did not change on September 11. It only became more itself." → neoconservatism, unilateralism.
- ==The US assertion of primacy challenged the multilateral rules-based order it had built.==

**Acharya's alternative: the multiplex world order:**
> *"Future world order will not be dominated by one nation or civilization. Neither the West nor the East will get to shape the future world order. What matters is cultural and political diversity."*

Multiplex = multiple sources of authority, legitimacy, and norms — not a return to Western hegemony, not a Chinese century, but genuine diversity.

### China and the world order

China's evolving posture (Deng → Xi):
1. **Deng Xiaoping (1980s):** "Bide time, hide brightness" — low profile, focus on economic growth.
2. **Hu Jintao:** "Peaceful rise" / "Peaceful development."
3. **Xi Jinping (2010s–):** "Chinese Dream," "Major Country Diplomacy," "China will never seek hegemony" — but accompanied by assertive territorial claims, BRI, and growing military capability.

**Belt and Road Initiative (BRI, 2013):** Not a multilateral institution but a series of bilateral infrastructure deals linking China to neighbours (Asia → Europe via land; South China Sea → Indian Ocean → Mediterranean via sea). Funded via the China Development Bank. Criticised as "debt trap diplomacy" — terms that leave borrowing countries vulnerable to Chinese influence.

**China and the Global South:**
- China frames itself as a developing, post-colonial nation — a "second identity" alongside communist state.
- Historical lineage: Bandung Conference (1955), Non-Aligned Movement (observer).
- Relations with Global South are *asymmetric* (China accounts for 66% of the GDP growth in emerging/developing countries 2000–2019) and *comprehensive* (political + economic goals inseparable).

## Key thinker — Amitav Acharya

Singaporean-American IR scholar (American University, Washington DC). Founding figure of *Global IR* — the agenda to make IR theory genuinely global by recovering non-Western traditions and perspectives. Key works: *The End of American World Order* (2014); *A World of Regions* (2007).

His **multiplex world order** concept (developed through the 2010s–2020s) offers an alternative both to unipolarity and to simple multipolarity. The metaphor is a multiplex cinema: many shows run simultaneously, audiences choose, no single director controls what's on. Distinct from Huntington's "clash of civilizations" — Acharya sees diversity as a source of stability, not conflict.

*(Dani Rodrik: Turkish-American economist at Harvard. His globalization trilemma — also called "the political trilemma of the world economy" — appeared in a 2007 paper and became one of the most-cited frameworks for understanding the political backlash against globalization.)*

---

> **Explained easily — world orders:**
> Think of the international system like the music industry. In the Cold War (bipolar), there were only two major labels (US and USSR) and everyone had to sign with one of them. After 1991 (unipolar), one label (US/Western liberal order) dominated. Now (multipolar/multiplex), streaming has disrupted everything — there are many labels, many genres, audiences can choose, and no single company controls what gets played. Acharya's "multiplex" is the streaming era of world politics.

> **Explained easily — hard vs. soft power:**
> Hard power is getting what you want by threatening or paying someone — like a bouncer. Soft power is getting what you want because people admire you and want to be like you — like a musician whose fans copy their style. The US has both: its military (hard) and Hollywood/universities/democracy (soft). China has been building its hard power for decades and is now actively trying to develop soft power (BRI, cultural diplomacy).

> **Explained easily — globalization trilemma:**
> ==Imagine three things you want: a unified global economy, your country's ability to make its own rules, and democratic accountability. You can combine any two, but not all three. If you fully open your economy (global integration) and want democracy, your voters will demand protection from global competition — undermining global integration. The EU picked integration + democracy and gave up some sovereignty. Brexit was a vote to get sovereignty back, at the cost of integration.== ^auto-power-liberalism-and-world-ord-11

> **Explained easily — the multiplex world order:**
> In the Cold War, the world had two TV channels. After 1991, one channel (US/liberal order) dominated. Acharya says we're moving to Netflix: many competing producers, many genres, no single show dominates, audiences pick what they watch. Neither the US nor China will run the next world order. It'll be genuinely messy and diverse.

### Multiple Choice Questions

**Q1.** Acharya's "multiplex world order" differs from simple multipolarity in that:
- A) It describes a world where military power is distributed among many states
- B) It describes a world of diverse actors, norms, and sources of authority — not just multiple great powers
- C) It refers to the period immediately following the Cold War
- D) It describes a world order managed by regional organisations rather than states

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B) It describes a world of diverse actors, norms, and sources of authority — not just multiple great powers**

Multipolarity is about the *number* of great powers; multiplexity is about the *character* of the system — diverse, with no single actor able to set universal rules. Acharya explicitly says "multiple shows on offer, giving the audience a choice of producers, directors, actors and plots." Option A is multipolarity, not multiplexity.

</details>

**Q2.** Dani Rodrik's "globalization trilemma" holds that:
- A) Democracy, national sovereignty, and deep global economic integration cannot all coexist simultaneously
- B) Global governance, regional governance, and national governance are mutually reinforcing
- C) Economic integration leads inevitably to political integration (spillover)
- D) Hard power, soft power, and smart power must be balanced for effective foreign policy

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A) Democracy, national sovereignty, and deep global economic integration cannot all coexist simultaneously**

Rodrik's "impossibility theorem" says any two of these three can be combined, but not all three. Option C describes neofunctionalism's "spillover" (a different theory). Options B and D are unrelated.

</details>

**Q3.** Which of the following best describes "soft power" as used by Joseph Nye?
- A) The use of economic sanctions to coerce other states
- B) The ability to attract and co-opt others through culture, values, and foreign policy
- C) The use of secret intelligence operations to shape foreign governments
- D) Military force used through proxy states

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B) The ability to attract and co-opt others through culture, values, and foreign policy**

Nye coined "soft power" to describe co-optive rather than command power — you attract others rather than force them. Hard power uses carrots (payments) or sticks (force/sanctions). Option A is hard power (economic sanctions). The others are neither.

</details>

**Q4.** Robert Kagan's remark about 9/11 — "America did not change on September 11. It only became more itself" — best reflects which foreign policy tradition?
- A) Liberal institutionalism — the US doubled down on international law
- B) ==Neoconservatism — the US asserted unilateral primacy== ^auto-power-liberalism-and-world-ord-4
- C) Realism — the US pursued balance-of-power calculations

**Essay Q2.** How does Rodrik's globalization trilemma help explain contemporary political tensions in democracies?

<details>
<summary>Model answer outline</summary>

**Key points:**
- State the trilemma: democracy + national sovereignty + deep global economic integration — pick any two, not all three.
- Application: Western democracies in the 1990s–2000s chose integration + democracy but ceded sovereignty (EU, WTO disciplines, capital mobility). The result: governments lost tools to manage economic dislocation; voters faced inequality and insecurity but had no political channel to address it.
- ==This produced the "precariat" — populations feeling powerless — and fuelled populism, Brexit, Trump, and anti-globalization movements.== ^auto-power-liberalism-and-world-ord-17
- Note the EU as a case: it chose integration + democracy but created a legitimacy gap (the "democratic deficit") by transferring authority to non-elected EU institutions.
- China: chose integration + sovereignty but limited democracy. Shows the trilemma is real — China maintains capital controls and industrial policy that WTO-compliant states cannot.

**Common mistake:** Treating the trilemma as pessimistic about globalization. Rodrik's point is not that globalization is bad but that *governing* it requires trade-offs that need to be made consciously and democratically.

</details>
