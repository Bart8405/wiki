## Answers to the thesis questions (summary)

### Q1 — Why ETS first?

The short answer is **political economy**, not policy design. Three overlapping causes:

1. **Burden visibility**: ETS hides the cost inside a market mechanism; tax visibly raises fuel/electricity prices.
2. **Free allocation**: 100% free in Phase 1 subsidises politically connected industries (nickel, steel, cement, PLN). A tax would not allow this.
3. **Regulatory complexity as cover**: The lack of a carbon-tax roadmap gave industry lobbyists grounds to demand more preparation time. Each delay is technically justified; collectively they amount to indefinite postponement.

The AMRO paper frames this as a deliberate "cap first, then tax" sequencing — the carbon levy under Presidential Reg. 110/2025 is designed to activate once the ETS generates a credible price. The problem: the ETS price is ~$4/tCO₂e and volumes are minimal. So the tax trigger never fires.

### Q2 — What should Indonesia do now?

Five moves from the developing-country playbook (all already committed to on paper by Indonesia):

1. Enforce the carbon tax with a credible escalator ($2 → $10 → $30 by 2035)
2. Revenue recycling toward households (via PKH cash-transfer infrastructure) and green investment
3. Shift ETS from free allocation toward auctions
4. Expand ETS coverage to industry, transport, and AFOLU (forestry, peatland)
5. Use Article 6 to finance coal retirement — but with integrity safeguards to avoid becoming a credit-export economy without domestic decarbonisation

The punchline for the essay: Indonesia has committed to all five moves. Presidential Regulation 110/2025 + the 2025 NDC contain all of them. The binding constraint is not policy design — it is political will to implement.
