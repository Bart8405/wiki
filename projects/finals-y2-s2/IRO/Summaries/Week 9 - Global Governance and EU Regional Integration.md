*What is a region, why do states integrate, and what kind of power is the EU? The lecture moves from the concept of regionalism through the EU's institutional architecture and integration theories to the question of the EU as a global actor.*

## Lecture walkthrough

### What is a region? Regionalism: why and how

A region is more than a geographical fact — it is *constructed* through interaction, shared identity, and external recognition. Cantori & Spiegel (1970) define it as a space with geographical proximity, sustained international interaction, common bonds (ethnic / cultural / social / historical), and a sense of regional identity.

Two main theoretical approaches:
- **Political economy:** regions are *by-products* of globalization — driven by trade flows, production networks, and capital movements.
- **Constructivism (Katzenstein):** regional identities are *imagined* and socially constructed. They complement, rather than replace, evolving state and national identities.

There has been an **explosion** of regional organisations since the 1990s — from ASEAN to AU to Mercosur to the EU. The lecture's point: regionalism is no longer just a European story.

### The European Union — key figures and the Pan-European idea

**EU in numbers:** 27 member states, 447 million people (3rd globally after China and India), €199B budget (2025), $20.29T GDP (2nd in the world), 21 Eurozone members.

**Diversity:** Luxembourg has 47% non-nationals; Malta has the highest EU-trust (71%); France the lowest (34%); 64.6% of EU citizens speak more than one language.

**Pan-Europeanism (lecture frame, picked up by Zwolski 2025):** *"Europe is uniting without the majority of Europeans being ready for it"* — *"Europe becomes one on the level of parliaments and state chancelleries but not within the hearts of Europeans"* — the legitimacy gap is structural, not new.

### The EU — more than an IO, less than a state

**More than an international organisation because of:**
- Supranational decision-making (Commission, qualified majority voting in Council, directly elected European Parliament)
- The EU is a *legal entity* — has its own legal order with **supremacy** and **direct effect**
- Elected European Parliament
- Scope and volume of common policies (*Europeanisation*)
- Internal Market + Monetary Union (Eurozone)

**Less than a state because of:**
- Limited sovereignty — only insofar as the Treaty allows
- No clear-cut territory (Schengen ≠ EU borders; UK left)
- Not one population/language but many nationalities — *weak European identity*
- No power monopoly (no army, no taxes, no police)

### The institutional triangle and how the EU works

Three core bodies:
- **European Commission** — proposes legislation (right of initiative), implements policy. Technocratic, supranational.
- **European Parliament** — co-decision with Council, democratic control, advisory. Directly elected (last: 2024).
- **Council** (of the EU + European Council) — represents member states, takes decisions (by QMV or unanimity depending on policy area).

**Four types of competence:**
1. **Exclusive** — trade, internal market, agriculture, fisheries (only EU acts)
2. **Shared** — environment, transport, consumer protection (both levels)
3. **Complementary** — education, culture, health (EU supports member action)
4. **Intergovernmental** — CFSP (states keep control)

All on the basis of *subsidiarity* (EU only when more effective than national) and *proportionality* (no excessive burden).

### Theories of European integration

The lecture stresses that *no single theory* captures the EU — Puchala's famous analogy: scholars studying the EU are like blind men trying to describe an elephant.

**Neofunctionalism (Haas, 1958):**
- Economic integration leads to political integration (functional + political *spillover*).
- The "Community method" of policy-making is a procedural code conditioning actors' expectations.
- Technocratic process — driven by the Commission and policy elites.

**Liberal intergovernmentalism (Moravcsik, 1993, 1998):**
- The nation-state is "obstinate" (Hoffmann 1966) — but actively *strengthened* by EU integration (Milward 1993: *The European Rescue of the Nation-State*).
- Three-step model: (1) national preference formation rooted in *economic* interests; (2) interstate bargaining at the EU level; (3) institutional choice to lock in *credible commitments*.
- Empirical claim: major integration steps (Single European Act, EMU, Amsterdam) were *Franco-German bargains*. EMU was driven by German economic interest — a high Deutschemark was hurting German exports.
- Actors have rational, fixed preferences.

**Empty Chair Crisis (1965–66):** de Gaulle's France boycotted the Council for 7 months over QMV in agriculture. Resolved by the *Luxembourg Compromise* — preserving the national veto for "vital interests." Classic test of supranational vs intergovernmental logic.

**Crisis-driven Europeanisation (Wolff & Ladi, 2021):** Covid-19 forged *coordinative reflexes* — agility, inter-crisis learning, more ownership by member states (Greek NRRP example). New angle: integration through crisis.

### The EU as a global power — what kind of power?

The lecture asks: what *kind* of international actor is the EU?

| Label | Thinker | Claim |
|---|---|---|
| Civilian power | Duchêne (1972) | Achieves goals through diplomacy, trade, persuasion — not arms |
| Soft power | Nye | Attraction via culture and norms |
| Hard power? | — | Limited — no army, fragmented foreign policy |
| ==Normative power== | Manners (2002) | EU shapes what counts as "normal" via norms, standards, rules |
| Ethical power | Aggestam (2008) | EU as a force for ethical global order |
| Regulatory power | — | EU sets global standards (data privacy → GDPR; chemicals → REACH) |
| Soft imperialism | Critical view | The "universal" norms are themselves a Eurocentric imposition |

**Internal challenge:** the EU's external action is fragmented across exclusive / shared / intergovernmental competences. *Who* represents the EU abroad depends on the policy area — Commission for trade, HR/EEAS for CFSP, rotating presidency for some others. This makes coherent global action hard.

### Decentring EU studies — the added value of an IR perspective

Borg & Diez (2016): decentring exposes the Eurocentrism and *geopolitical othering* baked into EU studies. So far this critique has stayed mostly in postcolonial theory, political theory, and sociology — *not* in European studies proper.

Nicolaïdis (2020): decentring = mutual engagement + self-reflexivity ("acknowledging one always speaks from somewhere") + *radical* decentring ("acknowledging the centrality of those speaking from elsewhere").

The provocation: why should Europe be the core referent if you study constitutionalism? Why not start with South African or Indian constitutionalism and ask what they tell us about European constitutionalism?

### Zwolski 2025 — Pan-Europe revisited (the key reading)

Zwolski's argument: the EU's contemporary push for "geopolitical power" (sovereignty, strategic autonomy, ReArm Europe, defence integration) echoes *inter-war* debates about a pan-European federation as a response to American and Soviet hegemony. The intellectual lineage runs from Coudenhove-Kalergi (*Pan-Europa*, 1923) through to today's calls for "European sovereignty."

**Why it matters:** the geopolitical EU is not a 21st-century novelty. It revives debates the EU was specifically designed to *avoid* (military integration, geopolitical assertiveness). Understanding the inter-war intellectual lineage helps clarify what kind of actor the EU is now becoming.

## Key thinker — Andrew Moravcsik

American political scientist (Princeton). His *Choice for Europe* (1998) is the canonical statement of **liberal intergovernmentalism** — the theory that EU integration is best explained as bargains between major member states (especially France, Germany, UK) whose national preferences are rooted in domestic economic interests, particularly those of major export industries.

His three-step model:
1. **National preference formation** — domestic economic actors shape state preferences.
2. **Interstate bargaining** — the resulting national positions clash and combine in Council negotiations.
3. **Institutional choice** — states pool sovereignty when they need *credible commitments* (e.g. EMU locked in by an independent ECB).

LI is the main rival to neofunctionalism (Haas) — and the EU literature is largely a dialogue between these two schools, with comparative-politics, multilevel-governance, and constructivist alternatives later joining the conversation.

*(Ernst Haas: father of neofunctionalism. Stanley Hoffmann: "obstinate nation-state." Alan Milward: the EU as *rescuing* (not transcending) the nation-state. Ian Manners: normative power. François Duchêne: civilian power.)*

---

> **Explained easily — neofunctionalism vs liberal intergovernmentalism:**
> Think of EU integration like a relationship. **Neofunctionalists** say couples drift into deeper commitment one shared problem at a time — you share a flat, then a bank account, then kids, then a mortgage; nobody plans the destination, you just keep solving the next problem (*spillover*). **Liberal intergovernmentalists** say no — every step is a calculated negotiation where both partners weigh what they get out of it and lock in the deal. Neofunctionalism explains the *momentum* of integration; LI explains the *grand bargains* (Maastricht, the euro, enlargement). Both capture something real; neither captures everything.

> **Explained easily — normative power Europe:**
> The EU can't send aircraft carriers, but it can write rules. When the EU bans something (chlorinated chicken, lithium in mining waste, harvested children's data), everyone selling into the EU market has to comply — and often it becomes the *de facto* global standard. That's normative power: shaping what counts as "normal" through regulation. Critics ("soft imperialism") say it's still imposition — just dressed up as universalism.

> **Explained easily — subsidiarity (two different meanings):**
> In the EU, *subsidiarity* means decisions should be made at the lowest level that can effectively handle them — only kick it up to Brussels if a national parliament can't handle it. In Acharya's Global IR (Week 4), *subsidiarity* means the opposite: local norms in the Global South diffuse *outward* into global governance (e.g. human security from Pakistan → UNDP). Same word, opposite direction. On the exam, be explicit which one you mean.

> **Explained easily — Pan-Europe revisited (Zwolski):**
> In the 1920s, Europeans (Coudenhove-Kalergi) said: "we need to unite, or America and Russia will run the world." That vision faded after WWII because the EU was built explicitly to be a *civilian* project — no army, no geopolitics, just trade and rules. Now in the 2020s — with Ukraine, ReArm Europe, talk of "strategic autonomy" — the EU is rediscovering the inter-war geopolitical impulse. Zwolski's point: this is not new, it's the resurrection of a pre-EU idea.

### Multiple Choice Questions

**Q1.** Moravcsik's liberal intergovernmentalism explains EU integration primarily as:
- A) A series of supranational moves driven by the European Commission and the European Court of Justice
- B) Bargains between member states whose preferences are rooted in domestic economic interests
- C) The unintended consequence of functional spillover from one sector to another
- D) A normative project to spread liberal democracy across Europe

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B) Bargains between member states whose preferences are rooted in domestic economic interests**

LI's three-step model: national preference formation (economic) → interstate bargaining → institutional choice to lock in credible commitments. Option A is supranational/neofunctionalist; option C is neofunctionalist spillover; option D is more like normative-power EU.

</details>

**Q2.** "Spillover" in neofunctionalist theory refers to:
- A) The migration of policy specialists between EU institutions
- B) The dynamic by which integration in one sector creates pressure to integrate adjacent sectors
- C) The diffusion of EU norms to non-member states
- D) The accidental side-effects of common policies on national budgets

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B) The dynamic by which integration in one sector creates pressure to integrate adjacent sectors**

Functional spillover: single market → common competition rules → currency union. Political spillover: technocrats and interest groups form transnational coalitions that push for further integration. This is the engine Haas proposed for the EU's quasi-automatic deepening.

</details>

**Q3.** The principle of *subsidiarity* in the EU (Article 5 TEU) means:
- A) Decisions should be taken at the lowest level of government able to handle them effectively
- B) Local norms should diffuse outward to global governance
- C) EU institutions are subordinate to member-state governments
- D) The European Court of Justice has the final word on competence disputes

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: A) Decisions should be taken at the lowest level of government able to handle them effectively**

The EU only acts where action at EU level is more effective than at national level. Option B is Acharya's *subsidiarity* (Global IR / Week 4) — same word, opposite direction. Don't confuse the two.

</details>

**Q4.** Ian Manners' concept of *normative power Europe* claims that the EU's main international influence comes from:
- A) Its military and economic coercive capacity
- B) Its ability to shape what is considered "normal" through norms, standards, and rules
- C) The personal diplomacy of the High Representative and member-state leaders
- D) The volume of development aid it provides to the Global South

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B) Its ability to shape what is considered "normal" through norms, standards, and rules**

Manners (2002) argues the EU exports norms (human rights, rule of law, environmental standards) and shapes the *rules of the game* of international politics. Different from hard power (A), diplomatic agency (C), or aid (D). Critics call this "soft imperialism" when the normative imposition is Eurocentric.

</details>

### Short Essay / Open Questions

**Essay Q1.** Compare and contrast neofunctionalism and liberal intergovernmentalism as theories of European integration. Use one historical episode to illustrate.

<details>
<summary>Model answer outline</summary>

**Key points:**
- **Neofunctionalism (Haas):** integration is driven by *spillover* — functional (one sector → adjacent sectors) and political (transnational alliances pressuring for deeper integration). The Commission and technocratic elites are central actors. Quasi-automatic momentum.
- **Liberal intergovernmentalism (Moravcsik):** integration is driven by *grand bargains* between member states whose preferences reflect domestic economic interests. Three-step model: preference formation → interstate bargaining → institutional choice to lock in commitments. Nation-states remain "obstinate" (Hoffmann).
- **Empirical illustration — EMU (the euro):**
  - *Neofunctionalist reading:* spillover from the Single European Act (1986) — completing the internal market required addressing currency volatility, which led to monetary integration.
  - *LI reading:* the euro was a Franco-German bargain. Germany wanted to lock in low inflation via an independent ECB; France wanted to constrain a unified Germany. EMU is a classic credible-commitment mechanism.
- Both readings contain truth. Spillover explains *momentum and timing*; LI explains *the structure of the deal* and which countries' preferences shaped it.

**Good example:** the Empty Chair Crisis (1965–66) shows that the nation-state is genuinely "obstinate" — de Gaulle could halt the supranational project for 7 months. But it also shows the limits: the EU continued and deepened after Luxembourg Compromise.

**Common mistake:** treating these theories as rivals where one must be right. Most contemporary EU scholarship is *eclectic* — using LI for big bargains, neofunctionalism for incremental policy spillover, constructivism for identity formation.

</details>

---

**Essay Q2.** Is the EU a normative power, soft imperialist, or both? Use examples to evaluate.

<details>
<summary>Model answer outline</summary>

**Key points:**
- Define **normative power** (Manners 2002): the EU shapes what is considered normal through norms, standards, and rules, not coercion.
- Define **soft imperialism**: the critical reading — normative diffusion is itself a form of Eurocentric imposition.
- **Examples supporting normative power:**
  - GDPR shaping global data privacy standards (Brussels effect)
  - Death penalty abolition as accession condition
  - Climate policy norm diffusion (EU ETS as template)
- **Examples supporting soft imperialism:**
  - The neighbourhood policy's conditionality on Mediterranean and Eastern partners imposes EU-defined "good governance" without their input
  - Trade agreements with developing countries embed EU-defined labour and environmental standards that may not fit local conditions
  - Postcolonial critiques (Borg & Diez 2016): the very claim to universalism is itself geopolitical *othering*
- The two readings are *not* mutually exclusive — the EU genuinely shapes norms, AND that shaping has the structural features of imperial diffusion. Nicolaïdis's *decentring* offers a third position: take responsibility for the partial perspective from which norms are exported.

**Good example:** the Cotonou Agreement (EU-ACP) — promotes good governance and human rights, but the conditionality runs one-way, and the African Union's own normative framework (e.g. AU Constitutive Act) gets ignored in EU framing.

</details>
