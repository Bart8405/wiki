## Key concepts — definitions

| **Arms race** | An action-reaction cycle of military build-up driven by the security dilemma. State A arms → State B feels threatened → State B arms → State A feels threatened → spiral. The US-Soviet nuclear race (handful of warheads in 1945 → ~70,000 at Cold War peak) is the canonical case. | LEC3 |
| **Chain reaction (in proliferation)** | "Proliferation begets proliferation" (Sagan 1996, p.57): one state's bomb triggers neighbours to seek their own — the security dilemma applied to nuclear weapons specifically. | LEC3 / Sagan |
| **Horizontal vs vertical proliferation** | *Horizontal* = the spread of nuclear weapons to **new** states (the NPT's main focus). *Vertical* = increasing the size and sophistication of **existing** arsenals (the focus of bilateral US-Russia treaties like New START — now expired). | LEC3 / Heywood Ch.12 |
| **Nuclear deterrence** | Preventing attack by threatening nuclear retaliation. The realist-favoured rationale for proliferation. | LEC3 |
| **Extended deterrence** | Protecting allies under a "nuclear umbrella" — e.g. US extended deterrence over NATO, South Korea, Japan. A key reason why non-nuclear allies refrain from acquiring. | LEC3 |

| **Stability-instability paradox** | Nuclear stability at the *strategic* level may *enable* conventional conflict at lower levels. When mutual annihilation is off the table, the cost of conventional adventurism falls. Explains India-Pakistan limited wars under nuclear umbrella. | LEC3 |

Cornerstone of the non-proliferation regime. | LEC3 / Heywood Ch.12 |

## Lecture walkthrough

### Section 1 — Review: IR theories applied to proliferation

Denney opens by mapping Week 2's three traditions onto the proliferation question — every key concept reappears here.

**From realism:** anarchy, self-help, balance of power, internal vs external balancing, security dilemma. **From liberalism:** international institutions, regimes, interdependence, arms control. **From constructivism:** norms, identity, social construction, taboos and stigma.

Each tradition gives a *different lens* on why states build (or don't build) nukes — and that's the setup for Sagan's three models in Section 4.

### Section 2 — The security dilemma

The conceptual core of the lecture. Definition: *one state's efforts to increase its own security make other states less secure, triggering a spiral of competition.*

**Three key dynamics:**
- Defensive measures get perceived as offensive threats.
- Uncertainty about intentions in an anarchic system amplifies fear.
- Arms races emerge **even when no state desires conflict.**

**Applied to nuclear weapons:** "proliferation begets proliferation" (Sagan 1996, p.57). One state's bomb triggers neighbours to seek their own — a chain reaction.

**Historical illustration:** US-Soviet arms race from ~handful of warheads in 1945 to ~70,000 at the late-1980s peak. This is the security dilemma at maximum intensity.

| France | 1960 | NPT nuclear-weapon state |
| China | 1964 | NPT nuclear-weapon state |
| Israel | undeclared | Not a signatory |

**States that gave up weapons or programs:** South Africa, Ukraine, Kazakhstan, Belarus, Libya.

**Arsenals over time:**

**Three perspectives on proliferation (mapping the IR traditions):**

| Approach | Core logic | Policy implication |

| **Realist** | States acquire nukes to survive under anarchy; proliferation driven by security threats | Balance of power, deterrence |

| **Constructivist** | Norms, identity, and prestige shape proliferation; nuclear taboo restrains use | Norm-building, stigmatization, socialization |

### Section 4 — Why do states build the bomb? Sagan's three models

The lecture's intellectual centrepiece.

> *"Nuclear weapons ... are more than tools of national security; they are political objects of considerable importance in domestic debates and internal bureaucratic struggles and can also serve as international normative symbols of modernity and identity."* — Sagan (1996, p.55)

**Model 1 — Security model (realist):**
- States in an anarchic system respond to external threats.
- "Chain reaction" — one state's bomb triggers neighbours.
- Nukes as the ultimate deterrent and guarantor of survival.
- **Lecturer's case: USSR (1949).** Stalin to his scientists after Hiroshima: *"Provide us with atomic weapons in the shortest possible time. You know that Hiroshima has shaken the whole world. The balance has been destroyed. Provide the bomb — it will remove a great danger from us."* (Sagan, p.58)
- Also fits Britain, France, China (all built in response to perceived threats).

**Model 2 — Domestic politics model:**
- Nuclear weapons serve bureaucratic and political interests *within* the state.
- Three key domestic actors:
  - **Nuclear energy establishment** — scientists and labs seeking funding and prestige
  - **Military** — services and units that gain resources and mission from a nuclear arsenal
  - **Politicians** — leaders who use nuclear programs for domestic political advantage
- **Lecturer's case: India (1974).** India's first nuclear test was ordered by Indira Gandhi *"with the advice of a very small circle of personal advisers and scientists from the nuclear establishment"* (Sagan, p.67). **The key piece of evidence:** despite China testing in 1964, India waited a *decade* — a delay the security model alone cannot explain. So why 1974 specifically? Domestic political calculations (post-Bangladesh-war confidence, Gandhi's domestic position) explain the timing.

**Model 3 — Norms model:**
> *"State behavior is determined not by leaders' cold calculations about the national security interests ... but rather by deeper norms and shared beliefs."* — Sagan, p.73

- Nukes as symbols of **modernity, technological achievement, great-power status**.
- States may pursue the bomb to *join the club* — OR refrain because of normative commitments.
- The **nuclear taboo** works in both directions: stigmatizing use AND shaping the identity of non-nuclear states.
- **Lecturer's case: France (1960).** De Gaulle to Eisenhower in 1959: *"A France without world responsibility would be unworthy of herself ... it is for this reason too that she intends to provide herself with an atomic armament."* (Sagan, p.79)

**Summary table:**

| Model | Core logic | Key actors | Lecturer's case |
|---|---|---|---|
| Security | External threats in anarchy | State leaders responding to threats | USSR (1949) |
| Domestic Politics | Bureaucratic + political interests | Scientists, military, politicians | India (1974) |
| Norms | Identity, prestige, status | State identity, international norms | France (1960) |

**Sagan's conclusion:** *"The evidence ... strongly suggests that **multicausality**, rather than measurement error, lies at the heart of the nuclear proliferation problem."* (p.85)

### Section 4b — Beyond Sagan: additional explanations

Subsequent scholars have extended Sagan's three-model framework:

- **Hymans (2006) — leader psychology:** personal identity and "oppositional nationalism" of state leaders can drive proliferation decisions. Individual-level addition to Sagan's state-level models.
- **Solingen (2007) — political economy:** domestic coalitions shape nuclear choices; **outward-oriented economies are less likely to proliferate** (they have more to lose from sanctions and isolation). Helps explain why Japan / South Korea / Taiwan refrain despite technical capacity.
- **Kartchner & Larsen (2009) — strategic culture:** how states think about the role of force and the meaning of nuclear weapons. Culturally specific frameworks shape doctrine and acquisition.
- **Leverage and bargaining:** using a nuclear *program* (not the weapon itself) as a diplomatic tool to extract concessions. North Korea is the textbook case.

Greitens (2022) is the lecturer's synthesis reference for these post-Sagan additions.

**Lecturer's prompt:** *"Which combination of factors best explains North Korea's nuclear program?"* — likely an essay/discussion angle.

### Section 5 — The proliferation debate (Waltz vs Sagan)

The canonical optimist-vs-pessimist debate, also covered in Heywood Ch.12.

**"More may be better" — the optimist view (Waltz):**
- If nuclear deterrence works, spreading nukes could *increase* stability.
- Rational states will avoid war when both face annihilation.
- Evidence: no nuclear war since 1945; nuclear-armed rivals (US-USSR, India-Pakistan) have avoided direct conflict; Cold War as "the long peace."
- Core logic: deterrence produces caution. The spread of nukes extends that caution to more states.

**"More will be worse" — the pessimist view (Sagan):**
- Organizations are NOT perfectly rational — they have biases, routines, and pathologies.
- Key risks:
  - **Preventive war** — states may attack *before* a rival completes its program.
  - **Accidents and unauthorized use** — Cold War near-misses (1983 Soviet false alarm — Stanislav Petrov; 1961 Goldsboro incident — US B-52 crash with two thermonuclear bombs).
  - **Civil-military dysfunction** — militaries may prioritize offensive doctrines, undermining stable deterrence.
  - **Irresponsible nuclear powers** — not all states have robust command-and-control systems.
- The more states with nukes, the greater the probability of catastrophic failure.

**Summary:**

| "More may be better" (Waltz) | "More will be worse" (Sagan) |
|---|---|
| Absence of nuclear war since 1945 | Fallibility of deterrent systems |
| Effective deterrence | Danger of nuclear imbalances |
| International stability | "Useable" nuclear weapons (low-yield) |
| Nuclear statesmanship | Irresponsible nuclear powers |

**The unresolved question:** does nuclear deterrence make the world safer — or does each new nuclear state increase the risk of catastrophe?

### Section 6 — Controlling proliferation: the regime and its unravelling

**The non-proliferation regime:**
- **NPT (1968)** — three pillars (non-proliferation, disarmament, peaceful use). 191 parties.
- **IAEA** — verification, safeguards, inspections.
- **CTBT (1996)** — comprehensive ban on testing; **not yet in force**.
- **Nuclear-weapon-free zones** — Latin America, SE Asia, Africa, Central Asia, South Pacific.

**Arms control timeline (Heywood Ch.12 + LEC3):**

| Year | Agreement |
|---|---|
| 1963 | **LTBT** (Limited Test Ban) — banned atmospheric, underwater, outer-space tests |
| 1968 | **NPT** — cornerstone treaty |
| 1972 | **SALT I / ABM Treaty** — first US-Soviet limits on strategic weapons |
| 1987 | **INF Treaty** (Intermediate-Range Nuclear Forces) — eliminated an entire class of missiles |
| 1991 | **START I** — major reductions in US and Soviet strategic arsenals |
| 1996 | **CTBT** — opened for signature; not yet in force |
| 2010 | **New START** — limited each side to 1,550 deployed strategic warheads |
| 2017 | **TPNW** (Treaty on the Prohibition of Nuclear Weapons) — adopted at UN; no nuclear powers signed |

**The unravelling (contemporary picture, as of February 2026):**
- **2019:** US withdraws from INF Treaty (citing Russian violations).
- **2023:** Russia suspends participation in New START.
- ==**5 February 2026:** New START **expires without renewal** — the first time in over 50 years with NO binding limits on US and Russian nuclear arsenals.==
- **Iran nuclear deal (JCPOA)** — collapsed after US withdrawal in 2018.
- **North Korea's program** — has tested ICBMs capable of reaching the continental US.
- **China's rapid nuclear expansion** — arsenal may triple by 2035.
- **Modernization** programs continue across all nine nuclear-armed states.

This is the live policy backdrop for everything else in the course (cf. Week 1's "end of arms control" trend; Week 8 on world orders; Week 12 on planetary futures).

### Key takeaways (lecturer's wrap-up)

1. The **security dilemma** helps explain why states arm — including with nuclear weapons.
2. **Nine states** currently possess nuclear weapons; global arsenal peaked at ~70,000 and now stands at ~12,500.
3. **Sagan's three models** show proliferation is driven by **security, domestic politics, AND norms** — not just one factor (multicausality).
4. The **Waltz-Sagan debate** on whether proliferation increases or decreases stability remains unresolved.
5. The **arms control regime** that reduced arsenals after the Cold War is under serious strain — most acutely with New START's February 2026 expiry.

## Key thinker — Scott D. Sagan

Stanford political scientist, leading scholar of nuclear strategy, arms control, and proliferation. Faculty associate of the Center for International Security and Cooperation (CISAC).

His 1996 *International Security* article *Why Do States Build Nuclear Weapons? Three Models in Search of a Bomb* is the canonical reference. Its strength is **pluralism** — pushing back against the realist monopoly on proliferation analysis and showing that domestic politics and norms can do as much explanatory work as security.

Sagan's other major contribution is the **organisational** theory of nuclear safety — *The Limits of Safety* (1993) argued that even disciplined nuclear-armed states (like the US) had repeated close-call accidents during the Cold War. This anchors the "pessimist" side of the Sagan-Waltz debate on whether nuclear proliferation is destabilising.

*(Kenneth Waltz — leading neorealist, proliferation optimist. Their 2013 co-authored book* The Spread of Nuclear Weapons: An Enduring Debate *is the canonical pro/contra. Jacques Hymans — leader psychology / oppositional nationalism. Etel Solingen — political-economy of proliferation; outward-oriented economies refrain. Sheena Greitens (2022) — syntheses the post-Sagan literature.)*

> **Explained easily — the security dilemma:**
> Imagine you and a neighbour both live on a quiet street. You install a security camera because you're worried about thieves. Your neighbour sees the camera and thinks: "is he watching me? Maybe he's planning something." So your neighbour installs cameras too, plus motion sensors. You see those and think: "what's he up to?" So you install bigger cameras and a fence. Neither of you wanted conflict; both of you ended up with a fortified street and a tense relationship. That's the security dilemma. Apply it to nuclear weapons and you get an arms race.

> **Explained easily — Sagan's three models in one paragraph each:**
> **SECURITY:** "I'm scared of my neighbour with the bigger army. If I get the bomb, they won't dare attack me." The realist starting point.
> **DOMESTIC POLITICS:** "Our military has a Nuclear Bureau, our weapons labs need funding, our ruling party wants to look strong. Once those interests align, the bomb gets built whether or not the security situation actually requires it." This is the model that best explains *timing* — why India 1974, not 1965.
> **NORMS:** "A modern, serious nation has nukes — they're a sign of being grown-up, sovereign, civilised. We need them not so much to USE them, but to BE the kind of country that has them." Explains de Gaulle's France better than security alone does.

> **Explained easily — why India 1974 is the load-bearing case:**
> China tested its bomb in 1964. If India was building purely out of security (Sagan Model 1), it should have raced to build in 1964–65. Instead India waited *ten years*. The security threat didn't change in 1974; what changed was Indira Gandhi's domestic political position after the Bangladesh war. So security explains *whether* India built; domestic politics explains *when*. The case Sagan keeps coming back to because it shows you can't reduce proliferation to security alone.

> **Explained easily — Waltz vs Sagan:**
> Waltz (optimist): "Nuclear weapons are terrifying. Terrifying things make people careful. More terrifying things → more careful behaviour → less war. Let the spread continue, it'll stabilise." Sagan (pessimist): "Yes, deterrence works WHEN systems are reliable, leaders are rational, command-and-control is tight, and no one has accidents. None of those reliably hold. More nukes = more accidents (Goldsboro, Petrov), more theft, more close calls — eventually a catastrophe." Mainstream IR has sided with Sagan since the 2000s.

> **Explained easily — why Feb 5, 2026 matters:**
> For the first time in over 50 years, there are NO binding limits on US and Russian nuclear arsenals. Both sides can — and probably will — start building more warheads, with no required disclosure, no inspections, no cap. Combine that with China's rapid expansion and North Korea's growing capability, and the next decade looks more dangerous in nuclear terms than any since the early 1980s. The arms control architecture that reduced arsenals from ~70,000 to ~12,500 is dismantling itself in real time.

### Short Essay / Open Questions

**Essay Q1.** Apply Sagan's three models to ONE proliferation case (e.g. Iran, North Korea, India 1998, Israel) OR non-proliferation case (Ukraine 1994, South Africa, Brazil/Argentina, Japan/South Korea). Which model best fits, and what does that imply for non-proliferation policy?

<details>
<summary>Model answer outline</summary>

**Structure:**
- State the three models briefly (security / domestic politics / norms).
- Apply each lens to your chosen case.
- Take a position on which dominates.
- Draw the policy implication.

**Worked example — North Korea:**
- **Security:** Korean War legacy + US extended deterrence over the South + sanctions / regime-survival fears (Libya 2011 = the lesson never to give up nukes once you have them).
- **Domestic politics:** Kim regime survival; Korean People's Army has a dedicated nuclear command structure; weapons program is central to regime legitimacy ("Songun" military-first ideology).
- **Norms:** prestige in defying the international system; status symbol; "join the club" logic. But anti-imperial framing matters more than modernity framing here.
- **Beyond Sagan:** *Hymans* (oppositional nationalism — fits Kim ideology perfectly). *Solingen* (closed, autarkic economy → exactly the type her model predicts is more likely to proliferate). *Leverage* angle — using the program as bargaining chip.
- **Verdict:** all three Sagan models operate; the *security* model dominates given the regime-survival stakes, but domestic politics + Solingen's closed-economy lens add critical analytic value.
- **Policy implication:** pure security guarantees won't work without addressing regime-survival fears (closer to the Iran deal model than to the Libya outcome). Sanctions alone fail because they reinforce both the security AND domestic-politics drivers.

**Common mistake:** Treating Sagan's models as *competing* hypotheses to be tested separately. Sagan's whole point is *multicausality* — they operate together.

</details>

**Essay Q2.** Is Waltz's optimist or Sagan's pessimist position on proliferation more persuasive in 2026? Discuss using the contemporary nuclear landscape.

<details>
<summary>Model answer outline</summary>

**Structure:**
- State Waltz's position: more proliferation → more deterrence → fewer wars.
- State Sagan's position: more proliferation → more accidents / parochial actors / catastrophic-failure risk.
- Apply to 2026 evidence.

**Empirical updates favouring Sagan (pessimist):**
- **5 February 2026: New START expires without renewal.** No binding US-Russia limits → vertical proliferation accelerates → higher alert postures → higher accident risk.
- **Russian nuclear rhetoric over Ukraine (2022–):** strongest test of the nuclear taboo since the early 1980s. Multiple ambiguous threats; close calls.
- **AI + cyber + autonomous weapons:** new failure modes for command-and-control that didn't exist in Waltz's mental model.
- **North Korea's continued buildup:** more warheads + ICBMs + uncertain command + active hostility = a pessimist's case study.
- **China's rapid expansion:** arsenal may triple by 2035 — first time the US faces TWO peer nuclear adversaries.
- **JCPOA collapse + Iran on the threshold** + Saudi statements about acquiring → potential cascade.

**Empirical updates favouring Waltz (optimist):**
- The **nuclear taboo HAS held** since 1945 — through Cuban Missile, Able Archer 83, Kargil 1999, Russia-Ukraine 2022–.
- **India-Pakistan** have maintained mutual deterrence for ~25 years despite multiple crises (Kargil, Mumbai 2008, Pulwama-Balakot 2019).
- The **NPT regime** has limited the number of new nuclear states better than 1960s forecasts predicted (Kennedy expected 15–25 by 1975; we have 9 today).

**Verdict:** 2026 balance tilts toward Sagan. The optimist case depends on reliable command-and-control + rational leadership + secure technology — all three look more fragile in 2026 than at any time since the late 1980s. End of arms control + AI/cyber + higher-tension great-power environment compound the risks.

**Common mistake:** Treating this as a values debate ("nukes are bad / nukes are good"). It's an empirical debate about whether deterrence or accidents dominate over time.

</details>

==**Security dilemma:**== one state's efforts to increase its security make others *less* secure → spiral. Defensive measures perceived as offensive. Arms races emerge even when no one wants conflict. Applied to nukes: "proliferation begets proliferation."

**Nuclear landscape today:**
- **9 nuclear states:** NPT-recognised — {US, Russia, UK, France, China}; outside NPT — {India, Pakistan, Israel}; withdrew + acquired — {North Korea (2006)}.
- **Arsenals:** peaked ~70,000 (late 1980s) → ~12,500 today. Russia ~6,375 + US ~5,800 dominate; China ~320 expanding rapidly.
- **Gave up:** South Africa, Ukraine, Kazakhstan, Belarus, Libya.

**Key nuclear concepts:** deterrence · extended deterrence (nuclear umbrella) · MAD · first-strike vs second-strike · nuclear taboo · stability-instability paradox.

**Three perspectives on proliferation (mapping IR traditions):**

| Tradition | Logic | Policy |
|---|---|---|
| Realist | Survival in anarchy; security drives proliferation | Balance of power, deterrence |
| Liberal | Institutions can constrain proliferation | NPT, IAEA, CTBT |
| Constructivist | Norms + identity + nuclear taboo | Norm-building, stigmatization |

==**Sagan's three models (1996):**==

| Model | Logic | Lecturer's case |
|---|---|---|
| **Security** | External threats → bomb to deter | USSR 1949 (Stalin: "Provide the bomb — it will remove a great danger from us") |
| **Domestic politics** | Bureaucracies + politicians push for parochial gains | India 1974 (waited 10 years after China 1964 — security alone can't explain timing; Gandhi's domestic position can) |
| **Norms** | Bomb as symbol of modernity / prestige / status | France 1960 (de Gaulle: "A France without world responsibility would be unworthy of herself") |

Sagan's bottom line: **"multicausality, rather than measurement error, lies at the heart of the nuclear proliferation problem"** (p.85).

**Beyond Sagan:**
- **Hymans (2006):** leader psychology + oppositional nationalism.
- **Solingen (2007):** outward-oriented economies less likely to proliferate (have more to lose from sanctions).
- **Kartchner & Larsen (2009):** strategic culture.
- **Leverage / bargaining:** the program itself as diplomatic tool (North Korea).

==**Waltz-Sagan proliferation debate:**==
- **"More may be better" (Waltz):** rational states under deterrence avoid war → spreading nukes extends caution. Evidence: no nuclear war since 1945; Cold War "long peace"; India-Pakistan avoided direct war.
- **"More will be worse" (Sagan):** organisations have pathologies — preventive war, accidents (Petrov 1983, Goldsboro 1961), civil-military dysfunction, irresponsible powers. More nukes = higher catastrophic-failure risk.
- Mainstream IR has tilted toward Sagan since early 2000s.

**Stability-instability paradox:** nuclear stability at strategic level may *enable* conventional conflict at lower levels (India-Pakistan limited wars under nuclear umbrella).

**Arms control regime + key dates:**
- **NPT (1968)** — three pillars (non-proliferation, disarmament, peaceful use); 191 parties; cornerstone treaty.
- **IAEA** — verification + safeguards.
- **CTBT (1996)** — NOT in force.
- **NWFZs** — Latin America, SE Asia, Africa, Central Asia, South Pacific.
- Timeline: LTBT 1963 · NPT 1968 · SALT I / ABM 1972 · INF 1987 · START I 1991 · CTBT 1996 · New START 2010 · TPNW 2017.

==**The unravelling (as of Feb 2026):**==
- 2019: US withdraws from INF.
- 2023: Russia suspends New START participation.
- ==**5 February 2026: New START expires without renewal — the first time in 50+ years with NO binding limits on US/Russian nuclear arsenals.**==
- JCPOA collapsed 2018; North Korea ICBMs reach continental US; China expanding fast.
