# Week 1 — Old Debates in a New World

*Three post-Cold War visions of where world politics is heading — Fukuyama's liberal endpoint, Huntington's civilizational clash, Kagan's return of great-power competition — set up the whole course's interpretive grid.*

**Date:** 2 February 2026 · **Lecturer:** Prof. Sarah Wolff · **Key thinkers:** ==**Francis Fukuyama**== (End of History) · **Samuel Huntington** (Clash of Civilizations) · **Robert Kagan** (Return of History). Amitav Acharya is flagged as the contemporary critic of all three.

## Lecture walkthrough

### IR vs International Studies — the disciplinary frame

Wolff opens with a methodological point: IR and International Studies are distinct disciplines, even if often conflated.

| | IR | International Studies |
|---|---|---|
| Focus | Interstate relations, the international system | Global issues, cultural + social dimensions |
| Themes | Power, security, war | Globalization, development, human rights, migration |
| Theory | Realism, liberalism, constructivism | Critical, postcolonial, identity, feminism |
| Units | States (mainly) + IOs + non-state actors | Wide range of actors |
| Roots | Political science + economics | Interdisciplinary |

The BAIS programme sits on the *International Studies* side — explaining why critical, postcolonial, and identity-focused theories feature throughout the course alongside the traditional IR canon.

### Trends in world politics (where we actually are)

Before the theoretical debates, Wolff anchors the lecture in *empirical trends*:

**1. Wave of autocratisation (V-Dem Report 2025):**
- Average citizen's democracy level is back to **1985**.
- 88 democracies, 91 autocracies — a full reversal.
- **Liberal democracies = least common regime type** (29 in 2024).
- **72% of humans live in autocracies.**
- Worsening attacks on freedom of expression in 44 countries (2024) vs 35 (2023).

**2. End of arms control:** New START (the last major US-Russia arms reduction treaty) is winding down.

**3. Weaponised interdependence:** Carney's Davos speech (20 Jan 2026): great powers are using "economic integration as weapons, tariffs as leverage, financial infrastructure as coercion, supply chains as vulnerabilities."

This is the context in which we re-read the three great post-Cold War visions.

### The three old debates

#### Fukuyama — The End of History (1989)

> *"What we may be witnessing is not just the end of the Cold War, or the passing of a particular period of post-war history, but the end of history as such: that is, the end point of mankind's ideological evolution and the universalization of Western liberal democracy as the final form of human government."* — Fukuyama (1989)

**Core claim:**
- The American-led world order is here to stay.
- Liberalism has defeated communism (and earlier fascism) — no serious ideological alternatives remain.
- All parts of the world will *irresistibly gravitate* towards a single model: liberal democracy + market economy.
- "End of history" ≠ end of events; it means the end of the *contest of ideas* about how to organise society.

**Influence on IR theory:**
- **Democratic peace theory** revived — if all states converge to democracy, war between major powers becomes structurally improbable.
- **Institutionalism** — international institutions (UN, WTO, Bretton Woods) built on liberal principles guide global governance.
- **Economic interdependence** — market-driven economies reduce the likelihood of war via mutual benefits.

#### Huntington — The Clash of Civilizations (1993)

**Core claim:** *Civilization* and *culture* will become the drivers of global conflict, NOT ideology or economics.
- Ideological / economic conflicts are over.
- **Identity matters** at local, national, regional levels.
- Huntington's 7–8 major civilizations: **Western, Confucian, Japanese, Islamic, Hindu, Slavic-Orthodox, Latin American**, and *possibly* African.
- The most violent conflicts will be along *civilizational fault lines* — especially West vs Islam.

**Problems with Huntington's thesis (lecture critique):**
- **Civilizations don't inevitably clash** — security ties between India and the US contradict the simple culturalist forecast.
- **Too many intra-civilizational clashes** for the thesis to hold: Sunni–Shia, intra-"Confucian" tensions, Russia–Ukraine (both "Slavic-Orthodox").
- **Other variables** explain conflict (geopolitics, regime type, resource competition).
- **Essentialization / racialization** of cultures — treats them as monolithic and static.
- **Too simplistic** a model of a complex world.

The lecture quotes Acharya: "The rise of civilizations does not inevitably lead to conflict" (2025: 360). What we're witnessing is *fragmented multipolarity*, not a clash of civilizations.

#### Kagan — Return of History (2007)

**Core claim:** The world has *not* been transformed by 1989.
- Traditional power struggles between nations — driven by ambition and competition — still shape global politics.
- The ideological struggle between **liberalism and autocracy has re-emerged** as a defining feature.
- There's a lack of common political principles or shared morality among great powers, other than *power itself*.

**Key tension Kagan flags** (from the textbook, p. 342):
> *"Tensions between democratic states (e.g. US and EU) and between authoritarian states (notably China and Russia) may be just as significant as those across the democracy/authoritarian divide."*

This complicates the simple "democracy vs autocracy" framing.

### Comparison table — the lecture's centrepiece

| | **Fukuyama** | **Huntington** | **Kagan** |
|---|---|---|---|
| Main driver of world politics | Ideology + political systems | Culture + civilization | Power, force, geopolitical rivalry |
| Image of post-Cold War world | Convergence to liberal democracy | Fragmentation into civilizational blocs | Return of great-power competition |
| Main argument | Liberal democracy vs residual authoritarianism | Civilizational boundaries (West vs Islam, Sinic…) | Liberal order vs revisionist powers |
| Role of the West | Model + vanguard of human development | One civilization among others | Order-enforcer (especially US) |
| IR theoretical affinity | Liberalism / Hegelian idealism | Constructivism (culturalist) + realist undertones | Realism / Neoconservatism |
| Main critique | Underestimates nationalism, religion, power | Essentializes cultures; self-fulfilling | Normalizes militarism; underplays norms |

### Beyond the three — Acharya's multiplex world

Acharya (2025): the future world order will **not** be dominated by one nation OR civilization. Neither the West nor the East will get to shape it. What matters is **cultural and political diversity** — a "multiplex world" with "multiple shows on offer, giving the audience a choice of producers, directors, actors and plots."

This is the constructive alternative that bridges to Week 4 (NWIRT) and Week 8 (world orders).

## Key thinker — Francis Fukuyama

American political scientist (Stanford), originally a State Department official and RAND analyst. His 1989 essay *The End of History?* (later expanded into the 1992 book *The End of History and the Last Man*) is the canonical post-Cold War thesis: liberal democracy as the endpoint of human ideological evolution.

Fukuyama's frame is **Hegelian** — history as a directional process driven by the unfolding of ideas. Liberalism wins not because it's accidentally dominant, but because no rival ideology offers a stable solution to humans' demand for *recognition* (Hegel's *thymos*).

His thesis was widely misread as "end of events." His actual claim was narrower: the end of fundamental *contestation* over how societies should be organised — even if specific conflicts (terrorism, ethnic violence, autocratic backlash) continue.

Fukuyama himself has substantially revised his thesis in later work (especially after 9/11 and the 2008 financial crisis) — he now sees liberal democracy as fragile and reversible.

*(Huntington: Harvard political scientist. His clash thesis was a deliberate counter to Fukuyama's optimism. Kagan: neoconservative public intellectual; his "return of history" updated realist gloom for the post-9/11, post-Iraq era. Acharya: Singaporean-American, the contemporary critic who proposes *multiplex* as the alternative to all three.)*

---

> **Explained easily — End of History:**
> Fukuyama isn't claiming nothing will happen after 1989. He's claiming the BIG DEBATE about how societies should be organised is settled — liberal democracy + markets won. Wars will happen, terrorism will happen, but no one will seriously argue any more that fascism or communism is a better way to organise a country. (He was wrong about that confidence — and has himself walked it back. But the thesis is the canonical liberal-optimist statement of the 1990s.)

> **Explained easily — Clash of Civilizations:**
> Huntington says: forget ideology, forget economics — what really drives conflict from now on is *culture*. The West will fight Islam; China will fight the West; the deep fault lines run along civilizational borders. The problem: many of the bloodiest conflicts since 1993 have been WITHIN civilizations (Sunni vs Shia, Russia vs Ukraine, Hindu vs Hindu in India). So Huntington's grid maps badly onto actual events.

> **Explained easily — Return of History:**
> Kagan is the buzz-killer: "Sorry, nothing fundamental changed in 1989. Great powers still compete for territory, influence, and prestige; ideology is back; democracy isn't winning automatically — it has to be defended." If Fukuyama is the 1990s optimist and Huntington the 1990s pessimist, Kagan is the 2000s realist saying both got distracted by the wrong story.

> **Explained easily — why this matters for the rest of the course:**
> Every week of this course is a re-reading of one of these three frames. Migration (Week 6) tests Fukuyama's institutionalism. Nationalism (Week 10) tests Huntington's culturalism. World order (Week 8) and war (Week 7) test Kagan's great-power realism. Globalization and populism (Week 11) revisits all three at once. Acharya's *multiplex* sits in the background as the contemporary alternative.

**Q1.** Fukuyama's "End of History" thesis (1989) claims that:
- A) Major historical events will no longer occur
- B) Liberal democracy and market capitalism are the endpoint of human ideological evolution
- C) The Cold War's end means democracies have already universally triumphed
- D) The 20th century saw the end of war between democracies

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Correct: B) Liberal democracy and market capitalism are the endpoint of human ideological evolution**

Fukuyama's claim is about the end of fundamental *ideological* contestation, not events (Option A is the common misreading). Option C overstates — Fukuyama says states will *gravitate* towards democracy, not that they have already arrived. Option D is democratic peace theory (a descendant claim), not Fukuyama's main thesis.

### Short Essay / Open Questions

**Essay Q1.** Compare Fukuyama, Huntington, and Kagan's accounts of post-Cold War world politics. Which best explains events since 2014?

<details>
<summary>Model answer outline</summary>

**Key points:**
- **Fukuyama:** liberal democracy as endpoint; ideological convergence; democratic peace, IOs, market integration. *Empirical predictions for 2014–2026:* further democratisation, deeper multilateralism, declining great-power rivalry.
- **Huntington:** civilizational fault lines as the main conflict driver. *Empirical predictions:* West vs Islam (war on terror), West vs Sinic (US-China rivalry), Russia-West tensions along Orthodox/Western fault.
- **Kagan:** return of great-power competition; liberal order requires US enforcement. *Empirical predictions:* Russia revanchism, China assertiveness, US-Russia confrontation, possible US-China war.

- **What fits 2014–2026 best:**
  - **Kagan's lens:** Russia's annexation of Crimea (2014) and full invasion (2022); US-China trade war and tech rivalry; NATO's revival and ReArm Europe (2025). Great-power competition IS back.
  - **Huntington's lens:** *partly* — China-West tensions could be read culturally; Russia's invasion has been framed civilizationally (Putin's "spiritual" / "Slavic" rhetoric). BUT the intra-civilizational counter-evidence is overwhelming (Russia attacking another Slavic-Orthodox state, India-US ties).
  - **Fukuyama's lens:** poorly. The V-Dem 2025 data (autocracies > democracies for the first time, liberal democracy the least common regime) is a direct refutation of the convergence thesis.

- **Verdict:** Kagan wins on the BIG picture (great-power competition). Huntington captures *some* rhetoric but the empirical fit is patchy. Fukuyama looks worst — but his deeper claim (the *normative* superiority of liberal democracy as a model) is harder to falsify and is independent of whether it spreads.
- **Acharya's multiplex** offers a 4th framing that's arguably the most accurate empirically — neither US nor China dominates; the system is genuinely diverse.

**Common mistake:** Reading Fukuyama as *predicting* universal democratisation by date X. His claim is about ideological endpoint, not timeline.

</details>

**Essay Q2.** "The rules-based international order has reached its limits." Discuss using examples from contemporary world politics.

<details>
<summary>Model answer outline</summary>

**Key points:**
- Define **rules-based order**: the post-1945 multilateral system — UN, Bretton Woods (IMF, World Bank, WTO), international law, shared norms (human rights, sovereignty, non-aggression).
- Carney (Davos 2026): "the story of the international rules-based order was partially false ... this bargain no longer works ... we are in the midst of a rupture, not a transition."
- **Symptoms of the rupture:**
  - **Weaponised interdependence:** tariffs (Trump), financial sanctions (SWIFT cut-off of Russia), supply chain coercion (China rare earths, semiconductor chokepoints).
  - **End of arms control:** New START winding down.
  - **Wave of autocratisation** (V-Dem 2025): liberal democracy now least common regime.
  - **Multilateral institutions paralysed:** UN Security Council vetoes (Russia on Ukraine, US/Russia/China cross-vetoes); WTO appellate body blocked since 2019.
  - **Middle powers rebuilding sovereignty** (Carney) via collective investments in resilience.
- **Counter-points (the order isn't dead):**
  - Most trade still happens under WTO rules.
  - Climate diplomacy (Paris, COP) continues.
  - Regional integration deepens (ASEAN, AU, EU's ReArm).
  - Norms (human rights, R2P) still shape rhetoric even when violated.
- **Synthesis:** The order is in *partial rupture*. The fiction of universal applicability has collapsed; the institutional architecture continues but with reduced legitimacy. Whether this is "the end" or a transition depends on whether middle powers + emerging powers build alternatives — Acharya's multiplex world is one possible future.

**Good example:** China-Russia ties + BRICS expansion (2024) as evidence of an alternative architecture forming alongside (not replacing) the post-1945 system.

</details>
