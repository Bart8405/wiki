### Key institutions — what they say

**World Bank (State and Trends of Carbon Pricing 2025, June 2025):** Carbon pricing revenues exceeded $100 billion globally in 2024 for the first time. 80 instruments now operate worldwide (43 carbon taxes + 37 ETSs). 28% of global emissions covered. Indonesia's ETS expanded its coverage threshold in 2024. World Bank frames the $100B milestone as proof that carbon pricing is generating meaningful fiscal revenue — the revenue recycling argument for Indonesia is now backed by global precedent.

**OECD (Effective Carbon Rates 2025, November 2025 + Indonesia country note, February 2026):** The OECD calculates "effective carbon rates" that net out fuel subsidies. Indonesia's *net* effective carbon rate — once its fuel subsidies are subtracted from the ETS price and carbon levy — is likely negative or near-zero. This is a stronger way to frame the "Indonesia's $2/tCO2 is too low" argument: it's not just that the nominal price is low, the net price is effectively a subsidy to fossil fuels.

**ADB (Carbon Pricing for Energy Transition and Decarbonization):** Recommends graduated escalation of carbon prices; prioritise revenue for low-income households and clean energy. Notes that Indonesia's coal retirement cost ($12–13/tCO2) is among the cheapest decarbonisation opportunities globally. Consistent with the Tony Blair Institute 2025 source already in the bibliography.

**UNDP (Carbon Tax in an Evolving Carbon Economy, March 2025):** Practical guide for low- and middle-income country governments. Recommends a progressive trajectory with initial low rates, built-in escalation, revenue redistribution, and digital tools for MRV. This is the LMIC-specific counterpart to the academic literature (Sunanda et al. 2025, van den Bergh & Drews 2026) already in the wiki.

**IISD (Implementing Carbon Pricing in Southeast Asia):** Indonesia is the first Southeast Asian country with an operational ETS. Philippines drafting legislation; Vietnam postponed trading to 2026. This makes Indonesia a regional first-mover — with both the advantages (lead experience) and the burdens (no regional peers to learn from or coordinate with).

**AMRO ("Cap First, and Then Tax"):** The single most directly relevant new source. AMRO (the ASEAN+3 Macroeconomic Research Office — the same institution that produced the Andriansyah & Hong 2022 paper already in the bibliography) explicitly analyses Indonesia's sequencing as a "cap first, then tax" design. It explains that the carbon levy under PR 110/2025 is meant to float *with* the ETS market price — so the "tax" is not being abandoned, it is being deferred until the ETS produces a credible price benchmark. This is a citable institutional source for the political-economy argument in Section 1c of the essay.

**IEEFA (Two years after launch, Indonesia's carbon market struggles):** The most critical new source. Provides hard numbers: total IDX Carbon transactions from March–September 2025 = IDR 1 billion (~$72,600). Total since launch = IDR 78 billion (~$4.9 million). Average price dropped from $4.1/t in 2023 to $3.9/t in 2024. No allowances were issued and no trading occurred in 2025 due to regulatory revisions. This is the data source for the essay's claim that "Indonesia has an ETS that doesn't yet trade."
