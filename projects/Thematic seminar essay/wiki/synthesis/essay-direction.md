# Essay direction — Indonesian carbon pricing

*Scratchpad for the thematic seminar essay. Holds the working thesis, a draft outline, source pointers, open questions, and a free-text section at the bottom for the user. Everything here is provisional — overwrite freely.*

> **Working title (placeholder):** *"Why an ETS first? Indonesia's carbon-pricing sequencing and what the developing-country playbook says about the road ahead."*

---

## 1. The puzzle (the user's starting intuition, refined)

The user's instinct from reading the literature: most scholars prefer **carbon tax** over ETS, yet Indonesia launched an **ETS first**. Why?

**Important refinement before writing:** Indonesia did not "skip" the tax. Law 7/2021 ("Harmonization of Tax Regulations") legislated a carbon tax in tandem with the ETS, and Presidential Regulation 110/2025 reaffirms a hybrid **"cap-tax-and-trade"** design. The carbon tax was originally scheduled for April 2022, then postponed to July 2022, then 2025, and **as of January 2026 has not been enforced.** Meanwhile the NEK ETS launched in early 2023 (coal-fired power, Phase 1) and expanded in 2025 (Phase 2 — captive coal + gas) [raw/icap-etsmap-factsheet-104.pdf](../../raw/icap-etsmap-factsheet-104.pdf).

So the real puzzle is sharper and more interesting:

> **Why did Indonesia choose to *sequence* an ETS first while indefinitely postponing the carbon tax, when (a) the literature broadly prefers a tax, (b) Indonesia's own design is a hybrid, and (c) academic models recommend a tax rate of $30–60/tCO2 versus Indonesia's planned $2/tCO2?**

This is a cleaner puzzle to drive an essay because it's grounded in fact (the postponement is documented), it sets up a political-economy explanation (the user's hypothesis), and it opens directly into a forward-looking "what now" section using developing-country playbooks.

## 2. The user's working hypothesis

The user's hypothesis, paraphrased:

> *"It may be easier to get money — for the state and for individuals — through carbon trading than through taxing the population, which can be politically costly as well."*

This holds up in the literature. Specifically:

- **Burden visibility.** A tax visibly raises fuel and electricity prices for households; an ETS imposes costs on ~563 covered facilities (mostly the state-owned utility PLN and a small set of energy-intensive industries) [raw/icap-etsmap-factsheet-104.pdf](../../raw/icap-etsmap-factsheet-104.pdf). The political cost is concentrated and easier to manage.
- **Free allocation as soft launch.** Indonesia's ETS gives **100% free allocation in year one**, then 75–85% free, with the discount tied to compliance. This subsidises the transition for politically connected industries — nickel processing, steel, cement, captive coal — exactly the sectors the ICAP factsheet says the government wants to protect as "energy-intensive and trade-exposed."
- **Captive revenue, no new tax.** The ETS plus Article 6 international transfers (re-opened January 2025 after a four-year moratorium) bring in revenue *without* a visible new tax on the population.
- **Stevens [87] in Sunanda et al. 2025:** "Political resistance and lack of coordination hinder carbon tax implementation in Indonesia, despite its potential to reduce emissions and fund climate programs." This is a direct, citable validation of the hypothesis.
- **Finon 2019** (in [raw/LOOK_Finon - anti carbon pricing in developing countries.pdf](../../raw/LOOK_Finon%20-%20anti%20carbon%20pricing%20in%20developing%20countries.pdf)): developing economies face political-economy constraints that make standard carbon-pricing prescriptions a poor fit — useful theoretical anchor for the political-cost argument.

**Counter-pressure the user should engage with:** the literature also suggests that carbon tax in Indonesia is **not as politically costly as assumed** because (Yusuf & Resosudarmo 2015; Rahma, Hartono & Hastuti 2025) the tax is **not regressive at the national level** — it is regressive only in urban areas and *progressive* among lower-income rural households, who use less fossil energy. So the "tax-as-political-poison" framing is partly a perception problem, not a hard distributional fact. That tension is worth foregrounding in the essay.

## 3. Draft essay outline

A skeleton to fill in. Three sections, plus intro/conclusion. Tight enough for a seminar essay (~3–4k words).

### Intro (≈400 words)
- Set up the puzzle: carbon pricing is the consensus policy tool; literature prefers carbon tax; Indonesia legislated both but only implemented the ETS.
- State the question: why this sequencing, how does the ETS actually work, and what should Indonesia do next according to the developing-country playbook?
- Position: the essay argues that the ETS-first sequencing is best explained by **political economy** (concentrated burden, free allocation, captive-revenue logic), not by economic-design optimality — and that the developing-country recommendation literature now points to a clear next move (graduated tax + ETS expansion + revenue recycling) that Indonesia is on paper committed to but has stalled on.

### Section 1 — Why an ETS first? (≈1000 words)
- **1a. Document the sequencing.** Law 7/2021 → ETS launch 2023 → repeated tax postponements → Presidential Reg 110/2025 reaffirming hybrid design. Use [raw/icap-etsmap-factsheet-104.pdf](../../raw/icap-etsmap-factsheet-104.pdf) as primary source; cross-check with [raw/expert/GREAT_Advancing the carbon market framework in Indonesia.pdf](../../raw/expert/GREAT_Advancing%20the%20carbon%20market%20framework%20in%20Indonesia.pdf) (Sunanda et al. 2025).
- **1b. The economic-design case for a tax.** Brief: tax has lower admin cost (Metcalf 2021), simpler to layer onto existing fuel taxes (van den Bergh & Drews 2026), stronger incentive for low-carbon investment, easier revenue recycling, better emissions outcome in Jia & Lin 2020. This is the "weird because…" the user noted.
- **1c. The political-economy case for an ETS first.** Concentrated burden (~563 facilities vs. ~270M people); free allocation softens cost to nickel/steel/cement (the very sectors driving Indonesia's industrial-policy ambitions — downstreaming, EV battery supply chain); ETS administered by financial-markets infrastructure (IDXCarbon, OJK) rather than tax authority; Article 6 export revenue. Anchor with Finon 2019, Stevens [87] (in Sunanda), Jakob & Steckel 2014 ("climate finance curse" parallel — useful for the *risk* of relying too heavily on credit-export revenue).
- **1d. The conflicting fact: the tax may not actually be that regressive.** Yusuf & Resosudarmo 2015; Rahma et al. 2025. Conclude this section by noting the gap between *perceived* political cost and *measured* distributional impact — which sets up the recommendation in Section 3 that the tax can be unblocked with the right communications and revenue-recycling design.

### Section 2 — How the Indonesian ETS actually works (≈800 words)
- Scope: power sector only in Phase 1 (2023–24, coal plants ≥25 MW on PLN grid); Phase 2 (2025–27) adds captive coal + gas; Phase 3 (2028–30) extends to all fossil-fuel power.
- Cap design: **intensity-based** allowances (tCO2 per MWh), not absolute cap; the 2025 NDC introduces an absolute economy-wide cap that future ETS phases are meant to align with.
- Allocation: free in Phase 1, 75–85% free thereafter, with non-compliance triggering a reduction — *but* no auctions have happened as of January 2026.
- Compliance backstop: the carbon levy under Pres. Reg. 110/2025 is supposed to function as a price floor tied to market price — this *is* the embedded "tax" in the hybrid model.
- Offsets: SPE-GRK domestic credits allowed; Article 6 international transfers re-opened January 2025 (~1.78 MtCO2e PLN-affiliated already authorised).
- **Critical view:** as of January 2026, 2025 allowance allocation was *postponed* due to regulatory revisions, and no auctions or trading have happened in the current phase. So the system is in force on paper but not yet generating a price signal or revenue. (This is a key fact to land — Indonesia has an ETS that doesn't yet trade.)

### Section 3 — Applying the developing-country playbook to Indonesia (≈1200 words)
This is the section that aligns most closely with the user's expressed interest: take the major developing-country recommendation reports and apply them to Indonesia.

The recommendation literature converges on roughly five moves. For each, take the recommendation and ask: *what would it look like in Indonesia, and what would the implications be?*

| Recommendation (from the literature) | Indonesia-specific implication | Source anchors |
|---|---|---|
| **Phased / graduated carbon-tax roadmap** — start low, raise predictably | Means actually enforcing Law 7/2021 with a clear escalator (e.g. $2 → $10 → $30 over 2026–2035). Implies confronting the political coalition that keeps postponing it. | Sunanda et al. 2025 §4.2; Parry et al. 2021 (in user lit review); van den Bergh & Drews 2026 |
| **Revenue recycling toward households + green investment** — neutralise distributional + macro hit | Hartono et al. 2023 shows recycling can raise GDP & employment in Indonesia specifically. Implies a politically visible household-transfer program (cf. Indonesia's existing PKH cash transfer infrastructure — could re-purpose) | Hartono et al. 2023; Ojha et al. 2020 (India CGE); UNCTAD 2022 |
| **Staged ETS expansion** to industry, transport, AFOLU | Power sector is only ~21% of national emissions coverage in 2024 per ICAP. Major emissions sources (forestry, transport, palm-oil sector) untouched. Sunanda's "basic themes" quadrant (deforestation, fossil fuels) flags this gap. Implies institutional build-out in Ministry of Industry MRV. | Sunanda et al. 2025; ICAP factsheet 104; Tan et al. 2022 (peatland) |
| **Regional / ASEAN coordination** to limit competitiveness leakage | Nurdianto & Resosudarmo 2016 modelled a uniform ASEAN carbon tax with Indonesia gaining due to its heavy energy subsidies. Andriansyah & Hong 2022 (AMRO) maps the regional landscape — only Indonesia, Singapore, Japan, Korea, China have priced carbon in the region. | Andriansyah & Hong 2022; Nurdianto & Resosudarmo 2016 |
| **International alignment via Article 6 + integrity standards** | Indonesia lifted its Article 6 moratorium in January 2025. TBI 2025 cites Indonesia coal-retirement abatement at $12–13/tCO2 as a flagship opportunity. Risk: "climate finance curse" if Indonesia becomes a credit-export economy without domestic decarbonisation. Implies high-integrity MRV (the existing SRUK / SRN-PPI infrastructure) and a domestic-use floor on credits. | TBI 2025; Kerr & Hu 2025; Jakob & Steckel 2014; Pierucci et al. 2025 (modern-colonialism critique) |

**Strong closing for this section:** these five moves are not the user's invention — Indonesia's own 2025 Presidential Regulation 110 contains four of them on paper. The real essay argument is therefore not "Indonesia should do X" but "**Indonesia has already committed to X on paper; the binding constraint is political-economy implementation, not policy design.**"

### Conclusion (≈300 words)
- Restate: ETS-first sequencing is a political-economy choice, not an economics-of-pricing-design choice.
- The hybrid is the right model on paper (Lin & Jia 2020 confirms tax + ETS dominates either alone).
- Indonesia's binding constraint going forward is implementation pace, not policy menu.
- Open the door for the policy question worth flagging: should development partners (ADB, World Bank, TBI's network) condition climate finance on tax-enforcement milestones, given that the tax is the politically hardest piece?

## 7. Things I noticed while reading that surprised me

(Free notes — keep or delete.)

- The Indonesian ETS has been *in force* since 2023 but had **no auctions and no allowance trading at all in 2025** because the regulatory framework was being revised. So "Indonesia has an ETS" is technically true and operationally hollow at the moment of writing.
- Sunanda et al. 2025's bibliometric finding: **over 65% of Indonesia carbon-pricing studies are theoretical/CGE simulations**, mostly in the energy sector. So when the user says "the literature mostly prefers a carbon tax" — that's largely a *model-based* preference. The pilot/legislated literature is much smaller. Worth flagging in the intro: there's a genre mismatch between what scholars *model* and what the government *does*.
- The most recent NDC (October 2025) introduced an **absolute economy-wide cap (1.35 GtCO2e by 2030, unconditional)** — a major shift from the previous BAU-relative targets. This is the policy frame the ETS expansion is meant to feed into. Mention briefly in §2 or §3.
- The carbon levy under Pres. Reg. 110/2025 is **explicitly tied to the prevailing ETS price** as a backstop. So the design isn't "tax then trade" — it's "trade and let the tax float to the market price." This is unusual and worth ~150 words of analysis. It also means the *level* of the eventual carbon tax depends on how Indonesia's ETS price evolves, not on a separate political decision — which may explain why the tax is being deferred until the ETS is producing a credible price.
