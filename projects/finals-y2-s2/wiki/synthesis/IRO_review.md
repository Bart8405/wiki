### Hobson's choice

**Definition:** A Hobson's choice is an apparent free choice where in reality there is only one option — take it or leave it. The phrase comes from Thomas Hobson (1544–1631), a Cambridge stable owner who required customers to take the horse nearest the door or none at all.

**In Week 6 context:** Hollifield uses it to describe the state's trap around irregular migration: tolerate illegal immigration and a black-market labour economy, or try to enforce strict controls and watch courts repeatedly block you. Neither option "works" — hence Hobson's choice.

**Connected to:** → [liberal paradox](#liberal-paradox--migration-state) — Hobson's choice is the street-level symptom; the liberal paradox is the structural explanation for why the trap exists.

### liberal paradox + migration state
*(Hollifield — these two concepts form a single argument)*

**liberal paradox:** Liberal states face a structural contradiction between their **economic logic** (openness to trade, investment, and migration is necessary for growth and competitiveness) and their **political/legal logic** (territorial closure is necessary for democracy, social contract, and public legitimacy). Courts protect migrants once they're inside; public opinion demands border control. States are permanently caught between both pulls and cannot fully satisfy either.

**migration state:** Hollifield's name for the third core function of the modern state, alongside the garrison state (security) and the trading state (economic growth). In the migration state, deciding who can enter, stay, and gain citizenship becomes as central to state purpose as defence or trade policy.

**How they connect:** The migration state is Hollifield's proposed *solution* (or at least, his diagnosis of what states are already doing) to the liberal paradox. Once you accept that migration cannot simply be stopped or fully opened, you accept that managing it is a permanent core function of government — that's the migration state. The EU is the only experiment so far in doing this *collectively* across multiple liberal states, which lets member states "finesse" (not escape) the paradox by offloading some of it to the regional level.

**Explained easily:** Germany invited Turkish guest workers in the 1960s → tried to send them home after 1973 → courts said family reunification is a right → permanent immigrant population formed despite the policy reversal. That's the paradox. The 1999 *jus soli* reform and the 2000 Green Card for tech workers both showed the same pattern: you can't import workers without importing humans. A government that acknowledges this and builds institutions around managing it is a migration state.

**Connected to:** → [Hobson's choice](#hobsons-choice) for the street-level expression of the paradox; → [security continuum](#security-continuum--internal-market-spillover) for the rival (Huysmans) explanation of why migration becomes political.

### security continuum + internal market spillover
*(Huysmans — these two concepts explain the same phenomenon from different angles)*

**security continuum:** Bigo and Huysmans' term for the institutionalised governance field that links border control, terrorism, transnational crime, and migration into a single domain. Once these issues share institutions — the TREVI network, Schengen, the Third Pillar — the security connotations of terrorism transfer to migration. This is a structural feature of EU policy, produced by bureaucratic integration, not merely by political rhetoric.

**internal market spillover into internal security:** Huysmans' specific mechanism explaining *how* the security continuum formed in the EU. Creating the single internal market (Single European Act 1986) required abolishing internal border checks → which required compensating external border measures → which required common visa policy, data sharing (Eurodac, SIS), and police cooperation → which bundled migration with terrorism and crime in the same institutions. Migration became a security issue through integration logic, not just political choices.

**How they connect:** "Internal market spillover" is the *historical explanation* for how the security continuum came to exist in Europe. The SEA created functional pressure that led, step by step, to migration and terrorism sharing the same governance structures. The security continuum is the *resulting structure* — the ongoing institutional reality where migration is permanently coded as a security issue because it's governed alongside terrorism and crime.

**Explained easily (the key difference from Copenhagen School):** The Copenhagen School (Wæver) says securitisation happens when a politician makes a speech declaring something a security threat. Huysmans/Paris School says: look at the *border guard at the airport*, the *customs officer profiling travellers*, the *Frontex database*. These everyday professional practices build the security frame from the bottom up, through shared institutions — not through speeches. The security continuum is that institutional reality.

**Connected to:** → [liberal paradox + migration state](#liberal-paradox--migration-state) — Huysmans and Hollifield are **complementary**: Hollifield tells you *why states are economically stuck* with migration (the paradox); Huysmans tells you *how migration gets politically constructed as a security threat* (the continuum). You need both for a strong essay answer.

### asylum shopping

**Definition:** The practice (alleged or real) of asylum-seekers choosing between countries based on which offers the most favourable conditions — higher recognition rates, better benefits, faster processing. The term is politically loaded because it implies migrants are strategic actors rather than people fleeing persecution.

**The Dublin fix (and its problems):** The Dublin Convention (1990) was designed to end asylum shopping by requiring asylum seekers to apply in the *first safe country* of arrival. In practice this shifted the burden onto southern border states (Greece, Italy) that first-arrivals land in, and was largely suspended after 2015. Critics argue "asylum shopping" misrepresents refugees' actual situation — most have little choice in which country they reach first.

**Connected to:** → [security continuum](#security-continuum--internal-market-spillover) — Dublin is one of the legal instruments produced by the Schengen/Third Pillar integration chain that Huysmans analyses. The "asylum shopping" discourse also feeds into the security continuum by framing refugees as strategic and therefore suspect actors.

**Line 447** — Question about Paris School and securitisation:
> *"is the paris school also under securitization of migration, just another approach of obtaining it or what is the relation?"*

**Answer:** Yes — the Paris School is a *variant* of securitisation thinking, not a separate theory. The Copenhagen School (Wæver) says securitisation happens through **speech acts**: a politician labels migration a security threat and that declaration creates the security frame. The Paris School (Bigo) says securitisation happens through **everyday practices** of bureaucratic professionals — border guards, customs officers, database engineers — who build a surveillance infrastructure through routine work. Huysmans synthesises both. For your exam: name Copenhagen for "discourse and speech acts," Paris/Bigo for "bureaucratic practice and the internal security field," note they're complementary, and Huysmans draws on both.

**Line 471** — Question about the Germany / liberal paradox example:
> *"Example to the liberal paradox is germany that was inviting turkish refugees to work until 1973 when they wanted to stop but not really successfully. Then with the citizenship reform 1999 jus soli and 2000 green card, they wanted only tech workers but again found out that they cant have just the workers and not their families, children etc - is this right?"*

**Answer:** Yes, that's essentially correct. Germany recruited Turkish *Gastarbeiter* (guest workers) from 1955 under a bilateral agreement. After the 1973 oil shock Germany suspended recruitment — but courts upheld family reunification rights, so the population stayed and grew. The 1999 *jus soli* reform gave citizenship by birth on German soil (a first), and the 2000 Green Card tried to attract only IT specialists — but ran into the same wall: once workers settle, families arrive, children are born German, and you can't treat them as temporary. That's the paradox in action: each attempt to manage migration cleanly (only workers, only skilled, only temporary) gets overridden by the legal rights liberal courts protect. Small correction: they were recruited as *workers*, not refugees — but the dynamic you described is exactly right.
