# Lecture 8 — Quantitative and Qualitative Approaches

*Two families of social-science research methods. Not just "numbers vs words" — distinguished by the direction of causal inference and their place in the empirical cycle. Case study research as the canonical qualitative method; internal vs external validity as the key trade-off.*

**Date:** 8 April 2026 · **Lecturer:** Prof. James W. McAllister · **Key thinkers:** **Gary Goertz & James Mahoney** (reading authors); **Theda Skocpol** (canonical case-study example)

---

## Key concepts — definitions

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Quantitative methods** | Methods that perform **causal inference via cross-case analysis**. Collect (quasi-)numerical data on relatively few variables from many instances ("**Large N**"). Analyse with statistical tools. Typical forms: experimental, quasi-experimental, interventional studies. |
| **Qualitative methods** | Methods that reconstruct **causal pathways via within-case analysis**. In-depth study of many variables in few instances ("**Small N**"). Typical forms: observational studies, **case studies**. |
| **Forward causal inference** | Reasoning *from causes to effects.* The native direction of quantitative methods. "We know intervention T occurred — what was its effect?" |
| **Reverse causal inference** | Reasoning *from effects to causes.* The native direction of qualitative methods. "We see outcome E — what caused it?" |
| **Hypothesis generation** | The exploratory phase of the empirical cycle — *generating* candidate hypotheses. Qualitative methods are strong here (in-depth knowledge → conjectures about causal pathways). |
| **Hypothesis test** | The confirmation/disconfirmation phase. Quantitative methods are strong here (statistical tests of predicted correlations). |
| **Case study** | Intensive study of a single case, with the purpose of shedding light on a *larger class* of cases. Instance of qualitative method. Procedure: note an outcome, try to identify the causal factor responsible. |
| **Internal validity** | Degree to which a study's findings are *warranted for the case studied*. Qualitative methods tend to score high here. |
| **External validity** | Degree to which a study's findings *generalise* beyond the case studied. Quantitative methods tend to score high here. |
| **Trade-off** | Internal vs external validity are usually inversely related. Measures that strengthen warrant (internal) tend to limit generalisability (external). |
| **Mixed methods / triangulation** | Modern practice — combining quantitative and qualitative methods to compensate for each one's weaknesses. |
| **Important caution** | Quantitative/qualitative ≠ nomothetic/idiographic. Don't conflate them. Quant/qual is about *data and case count*; nom/idi is about *what you're trying to achieve epistemically* (Lecture 1). |

## Lecture walkthrough

### Why split methods this way?

The social world is complex (many instances × many variables). Researchers simplify either by *limiting variables* (quant: many instances, few variables — Large N) or by *limiting instances* (qual: few instances, many variables — Small N). The decision drives everything else.

### Comparison on two axes

#### Axis 1 — Direction of causal inference

| | Direction | Native question |
|---|---|---|
| **Quantitative** | Forward (causes → effects) | "What does intervention T do to subjects?" |
| **Qualitative** | Reverse (effects → causes) | "We see this outcome — what caused it?" |

*Example: a clinical drug trial* compares outcomes for subjects who got drug T vs a control group. Gauges the *average effect* of T. Not suited to confirm T was *the* cause for any particular subject — quantitative methods reason from cause to population-effect, not the reverse.

#### Axis 2 — Place in the empirical cycle

| | Best for | Worst for |
|---|---|---|
| **Quantitative** | Hypothesis **test** | Hypothesis **generation** |
| **Qualitative** | Hypothesis **generation** | Hypothesis **test** |

Why? Quant studies typically collect few variables from many cases — fine for testing a specific predicted correlation, poor for sketching out causal pathways you didn't already suspect. Qual studies do the opposite. Single cases are a *poor basis* for judging the validity of a *general* hypothesis (vulnerable to cherry-picking critique); even falsification barely applies (works only for "A is a necessary or sufficient condition for B" — rare in social science).

### Summary trade-offs

| | Qualitative | Quantitative |
|---|---|---|
| **Strengths** | Reverse causal inference; reconstructing causal pathways; generating hypotheses | Forward causal inference; testing generalisations |
| **Weaknesses** | Selection bias; hard to generalise | Poor at reconstructing pathways; can't tell *which* cause produced an effect |

> The labels "qual"/"quant" are **misleading**. Whether you use numbers isn't the deep difference. The two axes above (causal direction + cycle place) capture the distinction better. There's no hierarchy: both play essential and irreducible roles in science.

### Case study research

A **case study** is the intensive study of a single case with the aim of illuminating a *larger class of cases*. Procedure: note an outcome → try to identify the causal factor responsible. Instance of qualitative method. Reverse causal inference.

**Functions of case studies:** good for hypothesis generation and exploratory research; poor for hypothesis test/confirmation. A single-unit study lets you ventilate multiple hypotheses but rarely adopts or rejects one decisively.

**Disciplines that rely heavily on case studies:** history, sociology, political science, law, anthropology, psychology, economics. Some are *almost entirely* case-based: psychoanalysis (Freud's clinical histories), business studies (MBA casework). (Possible reason: theoretical weakness — when you don't have strong theory, you fall back on detailed cases.)

**Three canonical illustrations:**
- **Frédéric Le Play** (1806–1882) — French sociologist, pioneer; sent fieldworkers to *live with families* during industrialisation.
- **Robert & Helen Lynd** — *Middletown* (1929), study of Muncie, Indiana; showed how the working/business class division explained "what one does all day long throughout one's life."
- **Theda Skocpol** — *States and Social Revolutions* (1979), comparative analysis of France, Russia, and China. Aim: understand revolutions *in general* through deep study of three cases.

### Internal vs external validity

The big trade-off: measures that enhance warrant of findings (**internal validity**) tend to limit their generalisability (**external validity**), and vice versa.

- **Qualitative:** high internal, lower external. Easy to establish a finding for a single case; harder to claim it generalises. Risk: using a France case study to "understand nation-states in general."
- **Quantitative:** lower internal, high external. The reverse trade-off.

### Modern practice

Most contemporary writers emphasise *integrating* the two — **mixed methods, triangulation**. Don't pick a team; combine them.

## Key thinkers — Gary Goertz & James Mahoney

Political scientists; co-authors of *A Tale of Two Cultures: Contrasting Qualitative and Quantitative Paradigms* (Princeton 2012), the prescribed reading. They are the leading contemporary theorists of the quant/qual distinction *as a methodological choice with its own logic* — rather than as a dispute about which is "more scientific."

*(Theda Skocpol — Harvard sociologist — is the canonical case-study exemplar mentioned in lecture: her three-revolution comparison is the textbook example of using small-N to illuminate a large class.)*

---

> **Explained easily — the two axes in plain words:**
> Quant says: "Take a lot of people, measure a few things about each, then crunch the stats — what works on average?" Qual says: "Take a few cases, dig into everything about them, then reconstruct the story — *how* and *why* did this happen?" Quant is good at saying *whether* something worked (and how much, on average). Qual is good at saying *how* and *why* it worked (or didn't).

> **Explained easily — internal vs external validity:**
> *Internal validity* = "I trust this finding *for the case I studied*." *External validity* = "I trust this finding *for cases I didn't study*." There's a built-in tension. A super-controlled lab experiment has great internal validity (you really did show X causes Y *in the lab*) but iffy external validity (does that hold in messy real-world conditions?). A rich ethnographic case study has great internal validity for *this village* but you can't say much about other villages. There's no method that wins on both.

> **Explained easily — when to use which:**
> *Early in research, when you don't know what's going on yet:* qualitative. Dig deep into a few cases, generate hypotheses about what matters. *Later, when you have a clear hypothesis to test:* quantitative. Take that hypothesis to a large sample and see if the predicted pattern holds. Mature research programs cycle between the two. The hostility some scholars feel toward one side or the other is mostly tribal — both are essential.

> **Explained easily — case studies aren't "less scientific":**
> A common put-down: "that's just one case, it's not scientific." But Skocpol's three-revolution book made claims about *revolutions in general* — that's exactly the goal. Single cases generate the ideas that quantitative work then tests. Excluding case studies would impoverish science. The legitimate worry is *over-generalising* from one case — not the method itself.

## Likely exam angles

- **Define:** quantitative vs qualitative methods (Large N vs Small N; cross-case vs within-case).
- **Define:** forward vs reverse causal inference. Internal vs external validity.
- **Compare:** strengths and weaknesses of quantitative and qualitative on the two axes.
- **Apply:** Given a research scenario, identify whether quant or qual is more appropriate and explain why.
- **Avoid trap:** Don't confuse quant/qual with nomothetic/idiographic.
- **Cross-lecture:** Forward causal inference (cause → effect) maps roughly onto Hempel's DN model (Lecture 4); reverse causal inference (effect → cause) maps onto causal-mechanical and Verstehen-style (Lecture 10) reconstruction.
