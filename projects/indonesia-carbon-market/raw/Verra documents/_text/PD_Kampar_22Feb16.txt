1. PROJECT DETAILS 
1.1 Summary Description of the Project 
The  statistics  of  the  fiscal  year  2005 provided  that  a  total  of  1.79  GtCO
2
eof 
greenhouse gas was emitted by the Republic of Indonesia, with emissions from Land 
Use   Change   and   Forestry   (LUCF)   and   peat   fire   comprising   37%   and   25% 
respectively  (SNC,  2010).  Consequently,  the  government  of  Indonesia  suggested  a 
goal of 26% emission reduction based on BAU (Business as Usual) by the year 2020 
through   unilateral   actions.   In   doing   so,   prevention   of   deforestation   and   peat 
protection are crucial elements. 
Kampar peninsula is located in the Riau province of Sumatra island and is one of the 
major  peat lands  in Indonesia  with the total area  of 671,125 ha (Tropenbos,  2011). 
Due  to  the  formation  of  industrial  plantation  aimed  for  pulp  production  starting  from 
2002, most of the area that was once mostly peat swamp forest has been converted 
(RPHJP  KPHP  TBS,  2014).  Conversion of  peat  swamp  forest  into  plantation  has 
estimated  to  have  increased  the  risks  of  peat  subsidence  and  fire,  which  ultimately 
lead to the continued increase of greenhouse gas emission. 
Production  Forest  Management  Unit  (KPHP  in  Indonesian)  Tasik  Besar  Serkap 
(hereafter  referred  as  “KPHP  TBS”)  is  located  in  Riau  Province  and  manages  an 
area  of  513,276  ha  within  the  Kampar  Peninsula.  The  organization  is  under  the 
government  of  the  Riau  province  and  is  authorized  of  establishment  of  forest 
management  plans,  management/supervision  of  concession-issued  areas,  forest 
protection,  and  profitable  activities  in  designated  areas  including timber  plantation. 
Area it manages is classified as national forest with production purpose.  
Implementation  of REDD+  activities  (Reducing  Emissions  from  Deforestation  and 
Forest    Degradation,    and    role    of    Conservation    Forest,    Sustainable    forest 
management,  and  Enhancement  of  Forest  Carbon  Stock)  play  pivotal  roles  in 
prevention  of  deforestation  and  peat land  preservation  in  Indonesia. As  mentioned 
above, emissions from deforestation and peat decomposition is the major source of 
national  emissions.  Therefore, a  REDD  project  is  being  developed  to  avoid  the 
conversion of some areas of the Kampar Peninsula. 
This  project  aims  to  reduce  greenhouse  gas  through  preventing 14,722.9 ha 
managed by KPHP  TBS from being  converted  into  a  timber  plantation.  The  project 
area consists of swamp forest on top of a thick layer (5-12m) of peat that is currently 
maintained  under  a favourable condition.  Nevertheless,  the  issuance  of  17  timber 
plantation concession over the 355,848 ha out of the 531,276 ha, and an investment 
analysis  result indicate that conversion of  the  remaining  national  forest  into  a 
plantation  would  be  the  best  investment. These  show that  the  project  area  is 
exposed to great risk of conversion into plantation. 
Hence,  KPHP  TBS  intends  to  protect  peat  swamp  forest  through  this  project  using 
REDD+  Avoided  Planned  Deforestation  (APD)  mechanism  provided  by  the  Verified 
Carbon Standard (VCS). In particular, the area also stores more than of 24.8 MtCO

1.8 Description of the Project Activity 
Main  objective of the proposed  project is to avoid conversion of the 14,722.9 ha of 
the proposed  project area into  timber  plantation by  assign  it  as  a  REDD  area  thus 
change its function from production to protection.  
The  area  is part  of “wilayah tertentu”  (“specific area”) of  the  KPHP  TBS.  By 
regulation, the “specific area” is defined as area of KPHP in which no concessional 
rights have  been awarded  to  any  party. Within  the  KPHP  TBS,  there  are  about 
40,000  ha  “specific area”. The  KPHP has  the  rights  to  manage  this  area  for 
production of timber and/or non-timber forest products.  
In  line  with  the  function  of  the  KPHP  as  defined  in  Minister  of  Forestry  Decree  no. 
509/Menhut-VII/2010,  this  area  is  planned  to  be  managed  for timber  production,

1.10. Conditions Prior to Project Initiation 
a. Physical and climate  
Peat  swamp  forest  in  the  project site is  made  up  of  young  and  old  alluvium 
sediments consisting of sand sediment, lake, clay, peat and plant remains. Based on 
the national soil classification, the project site is dominated by Organosol / Peat soil 
which contains  a  lot  of  organic  material, so  that  development  of  the  land will  be 
affected   by   the   level   of  maturity,   and   the   decomposition   of   organic   material 
properties. Morphologically, the peat ecosystem is characterized by formation of soil 
horizons with dark brown to black colour,  high  water levels  and  very  sour in  acidity 
(pH  3-5). Based  on  the  interpolation  technique  of  field  measurement  data  by  using 
GIS, Peat depth varies, ranging from 4 to more than 12 m (see Figure 3) 
Based on Digital Elevation Model (DEM) data from SRTM, the average altitude is 20 
meters  above  sea  level  with  slope ranging from 0 to 8% (flat  area). Overall  type  of 
climate in the area by classification Schmidt - Fergusson is included as climate type 
A  (wet  climate) with  average  of annual  rainfall  in  Siak  (2011) is 2,443.80  mm.  
Temperatures ranging from 26 °C to 30 °C.

b. Landcover  
Land  cover of  the  project  area  was  developed  through  Landsat  8/Operational  Land 
Imager  (OLI)  classification. Overall  accuracy  of  the  classification  was  90.79  %  and

The forest average  is  dominated  by  dipterocarpaceae  trees.  Other  tree  species 
found in the area are Kelat (Syzygium aqueum Burm.), Bintangur (Callophylum spp), 
Gelam (Melaleuca cajaputi Powell), Jerejet, Kelincan (Cinnamomum spp), Mahakai, 
Pelintai (Syzygium spp), and Sepahit
4
. 
Based on the Simpson diversity  index, the  proposed  location falls  into the category 
of  complex  and  stable  community. While  based  on  the  calculation  of  the  Shannon-
Wiener  diversity  index,  level  of  diversity  in  the  project  area  are  classified  as 
moderate category. 
For  the  purpose  of  forest  stratification  with  regard  to  above  ground  carbon  stock 
measurement, the forest was differentiated further  into four level of density, namely 
very low density, low density, medium density and dense forest. This will ensure the 
homogeneity of plot during measurement (Subedi et al., 2010). For that reason then 
the  object  based  stratification  method  was performed  based  on  field  observation. 
Meanwhile  for  forest  density  differentiation, the  stem  count was  selected  as  key

communities.

2.4 Baseline Scenario 
Baseline  scenario  is  conversion  of  the  proposed  project  area  to  timber  plantation 
forest. Most  likely  commodity developed under  the  timber  plantation  is Acacia 
crassicarpa. Detail on the steps for selection of baseline scenario is described in the 
below Section 2.5 (Additionality). 
Following  the  national  policy  on  REDD,  the  objective  of  development  of  REDD  in 
Indonesia  is  to  save  the  country’s  natural  forests  and  the  flora  and  fauna  within 
them.  Thus  conversion  of  natural  forest  into  non-natural  forest  into  for  example, 
timber   plantation,   rubber   plantation,   and   sago   plantation   is   considered   as 
deforestation. Since the proposed area is legally authorized for conversion to a man-
made  plantation,  the  activity  proposed  under  this  project  is  an  avoided  planned 
deforestation. 
KPHP TBS is the agent of deforestation and it is estimated that deforestation under 
baseline scenario will occur at the rate of 1,037.94 ha/yr.  Detail on the estimation of 
deforestation rate is presented in Section 3.1.1.

3.2 Project Emissions 
The  proposed  project  is  continuation  of  existing  land  use  of  natural  forest so there  will  be  no 
deforestation activity expected to occur. In terms of emissions from peat, the emission from peat 
decomposition is assumed zero since the area is still in its natural state, while areas surrounding 
the  proposed  project  have  been  converted  into  timber  plantation. Thus  the  change  of  condition 
outside of project area in the future might not be significant.  
The  potential  factor  that  may  affect  the  water  condition within  the  proposed  project  area  is 
development  of  road  which  is  now  still  underway  (see  Figure 8).  However,  based  on  expert 
judgement (see Appendix 2), this may not have significant influence on water table in the project 
area. This is because construction of the road will involve the process of compaction of peat, so 
that the  permeability of the peat  will be very  low. The construction of the road  will have positive 
impact on water retention. Water drain from the area will be channelled to existing river networks.

3.3 Leakage 
According  to  VCS  Methodology  VM0007,  mandatory  source of leakage  to  be  considered  under 
avoid planned deforestation project is leakage related to shifting of activity.  
Deforestation  agent  in  this  project  is  the  KPHP  TBS,  therefore  leakage  analysis  is  analyse  on 
possible  timber  plantation  activity  the  KPHP  TBS  might  do  to  replace  the  one  that  will  be 
conducted in the proposed project area in the absence of the REDD project. 
In this regard, potential leakage from displacement of activities is considered to be zero since (a) 
all areas under the KPHP-TBS aside from the proposed project area is already being designated 
as  production  area  and  will  remain  the  same, there  will  be  no  area  under  the  KPHP  TBS  that 
function  will  be  changed  (e.g.  from  protection  to  production); and  (b)  the  KPHP TBS have  no 
authority  to  develop  timber  plantation  outside  of  its assigned area according  to  the  Minister  of 
Forestry decree no. 509/Menhut-VII/2010 regarding the establishment of the Tasik Besar Serkap 
Production Forest Management Unit.

4.3 Monitoring Plan 
Monitoring  on carbon  stock  changes  will  be  conducted  for  aboveground  and  below  ground 
biomass. The proposed project will use both ground measurement and satellite imagery analysis 
to first estimate above ground biomass stock is the project area.  
4.3.1 Forest cover change monitoring 
The primary data used in forest cover change monitoring is high to medium data with 10 to 15 m 
spatial resolution. Landsat 8 imagery is the minimum imagery standard in combination with SPOT 
or higher resolution imagery.  
A GPS device will be used to navigate the location of selected samples during ground verification. 
Digital camera will be used to photograph the land cover segments at visited sites for each class 
as reference data in the accuracy assessment.  
The digital image processing will be carried out using computer based Remote sensing software 
for image processing, classification and analysis of the imagery. 
Satellite  image  acquisition,  pre-processing,  secondary  data  collection,  image  classification, 
ground verification, accuracy assessment and output  derivation are the main components in the 
monitoring  process.  Pre-processing  should  be  performed  to  improve  qualitative  judgements 
concerning the data.  
The  classification  system  will  be  referred  to  the  land  cover  map  issued  in  the  2014  land  cover 
mapping,  which  covers  at  least  the  following  forest  class:  High  density,  medium  density,  low 
density, and very low density forest. 
The data collected and posterior analysis shall cover:  
 The  entire  reference  region:  at  least  at  the  beginning  and  at  the  end  of  the  crediting 
period (for periodical revision of the baseline).  Unit of analysis is ha yr
-1
. 
 The entire project area: at least at the beginning and at the end of each monitoring period 
(for periodical verifications). Unit of analysis is ha yr
-1
.

Two main change detection techniques can be be used:  
 Post-classification  change  detection:  Two  maps  are  generated  for  two  different  time 
points and then compared to detect changes in forest cover.  
 Pre-classification  change  detection  (direct  classification  of  change):  Two  data  sets 
acquired at different dates are analyzed together to detect the locations where a change 
has occurred 
Automated classification methods should be used as the method of interpretation is more efficient 
and  repeatable  than  a  visual  interpretation.  Interpretation  should  also  focuses  on  change 
detection with interdependent assessment of two multi-temporal images together 
An  accuracy  of  the  outcome  of  the  mapping  will  be  assessed  by  estimating  the  following  three 
types  of  errors:  (1)  Overall  classification  accuracy  (2)  Error  of  omission  of  each  category  of 
change  (error  of  excluding  an  area  from  a  category  to  which  it  does truly  belongs,  i.e.  area 
underestimation) (3)  Error of commission of each category of change (error of including an area 
in  a  category  to  which  it  does  not  truly  belong,  i.e.  area  overestimation)  for  each  category  of 
change, using statistical sampling 
4.3.2 Analysis and Frequency  
From forest cover change mapping, the following will also be covered in the analysis: 
- Monitoring of areas undergoing degradation 
- Monitoring of areas undergoing carbon stock enhancement 
As  stated  in  2014  landcover  map,  the  project  contains  forest  areas  that  are  assumed  to  be 
accumulating carbon and these areas are included in the baseline (Very low density, low density 
and medium denity). Forest cover monitoring will be used for monitoring any change in the area 
of those mentioned strata.  
A consistent time-series of land  use-change  will  be  used for  for  periodical monitoring. Following 
standard  of  landcover  monitoring  in  Indonesia,  three  years  monitoring  interval  will  be  applied  to 
achieve optimum result.

5. ENVIRONMENTAL IMPACT 
Proposed project activity is continuation of existing land use, i.e as natural forest, which does not require 
an   environmental   impact   assessment   according   to   the Minister   of   Environment   No.5/2012   that 
Environmental Impact Assessments (EIAs) are required if the following conditions exist: 
1. Concession Forest, all area scale 
2. Timber Plantation, area is ≥ 5,000 Ha 
Therefore, EIAs is not required for the proposed activity as the proposed project activity is not developing 
a concession forest and timber plantation. 
In  addition, implementation  of the  project  is expected to  assist  in  conservation  of  biodiversity  in  the 
Kampar Bay area. As mentioned in the Section 1.10c regarding biodiversity, the proposed project area is 
home to 84 plant species, 23 herpetofauna, 75 avifauna, and 16 large mammals, some of it are endemic 
species  such  as Sumatran  tiger (Panthera  tigris  sumatrae), avifauna  (perenjak  jawa), herpetofauna 
(hylarana  rawa). There  were  also  some  endangered species  such  as  agile  gibbon  (owa  ungko),  sunda 
pangolin (trenggiling peusing). 
In terms of biophysical impact of the proposed project, no significant change is planned on the landscape 
as  well  as  the  composition  of  plants  and  peat  since  the  project  maintains  current  state  of  the  land,  i.e 
natural  forest.  Located  in  a  peat  land,  the  proposed  project  will  avoid  decomposition  of  peat  that  will 
otherwise occur if the area is converted into timber plantation.
