## 1. World Bank & Multilateral Institution Reports

### 1.1 World Bank — *State and Trends of Carbon Pricing 2025*
**Type:** Annual flagship report | **Released:** June 2025  
**Key stats:** 80 carbon pricing instruments now operating worldwide (43 taxes + 37 ETSs); revenues exceeded $100 billion in 2024 for the first time; coverage = ~28% of global emissions. Indonesia mentioned as one of the major middle-income ETS implementers.  
**Why useful:** Up-to-date global benchmark; confirms Indonesia's ETS is in a broader wave of emerging-economy ETS adoption (India, Turkey, Brazil also referenced). The revenue milestone ($100B) is a strong opener for your essay.  
**URL:** https://openknowledge.worldbank.org/entities/publication/e5f6e755-e6a6-4d2c-927a-23b5cc8a9b03  
⬇ Direct PDF: https://alliedoffsets.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/State-and-Trends-of-Carbon-Pricing-2025-2.pdf

### 1.5 OECD — *Effective Carbon Rates 2025*
**Type:** OECD biennial publication | **Released:** 2025  
**Key content:** Measures "effective carbon rates" (explicit taxes + ETS prices + implicit pricing via fuel taxes/subsidies) across 72 countries. Tracks whether stated carbon prices are real.  
**Why useful:** Directly complements Finch & van den Bergh 2022 (authenticity of carbon prices) and Agnolucci et al. 2024 (total carbon pricing). The OECD methodology captures Indonesia's implicit fossil fuel subsidies as a *negative* effective carbon rate — important for your argument that Indonesia's effective price is much lower than advertised.  
**URL:** https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/effective-carbon-rates-2025_a5a5d71f-en.html  
**Suggested prefix:** `LOOK_`

### 1.6 IMF — *Carbon Taxes or Emissions Trading Systems? Instrument Choice and Design* (2022)
**Type:** IMF Staff Climate Note | **Authors:** Parry, Black, Roaf | **Published:** July 2022  
**Key content:** Systematic comparison of carbon tax vs. ETS for the IMF's member countries. Key finding: carbon taxes have significant political economy advantages when governments want revenue; ETSs have political economy advantages when governments want to obscure the cost from the public. Also covers the case for hybrids.  
**Why useful:** This is probably the most policy-authoritative "why ETS vs tax" treatment. Directly answers your first thesis question from the perspective of the IMF. The finding that ETSs can "hide the cost" within a market mechanism (away from public view) is a textbook version of your political economy hypothesis.  
**URL:** https://www.elibrary.imf.org/view/journals/066/2022/006/article-A001-en.xml  
**Suggested prefix:** `LOOK_`

## 2. Indonesia-Specific Academic Sources

### 2.1 AMRO — *Cap First, and Then Tax: Carbon-Pricing in Indonesia*
**Type:** Policy analysis / working paper | **Authors:** Andriansyah & Seung Hyun Hong | **Institution:** ASEAN+3 Macroeconomic Research Office (AMRO) | **Date:** 2023/2024  
**Key content:** Directly addresses why Indonesia chose to implement a cap-and-trade (ETS) before the carbon tax. Describes the "cap-and-tax" hybrid model, timelines, and postponement history.  
**Why useful:** This is the single most directly relevant source to your first thesis question. AMRO is a credible multilateral; Andriansyah is the same author as the ASEAN+3 comparison paper already in your `raw/`. The title literally answers your question.  
**URL (free):** https://amro-asia.org/cap-first-and-then-tax-carbon-pricing-in-indonesia/  
**Suggested prefix:** `DONE_` after reading

### 2.2 Sembiring et al. (2025) — *Carbon Tax and Trading Mechanisms for Emission Reduction in the Indonesian Power Sector*
**Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article | **Published:** 2025, *Clean Energy and Environment Transitions* (Elsevier/ScienceDirect) | **DOI:** S2666790825001478  
**Key content:** Models three carbon tax scenarios ($2, $63, $127/tCO₂) and two ETS cap designs for Indonesia's power sector to 2050. The $2 scenario (current law) produces minimal emissions reduction; the $63–127 range matches Paris-consistent pathways but requires massive capital ($962B cumulative for the highest scenario).  
**Why useful:** Quantitative modelling of exactly the gap between Indonesia's current policy ($2) and what the science says is needed ($60+). Perfect for Section 2 of the essay (critical view of what the ETS actually achieves) and Section 3 (how far policy falls short of the playbook).  
**URL:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666790825001478  
*(Also in DOAJ — may be open access)*  
**Suggested prefix:** `LOOK_`

### 2.3 "Delaying the Implementation of Carbon Tax in Indonesia: A Critical Review of the Harmonization of Tax Regulations"
**Type:** Law review article | **Journal:** *Pena Justisia: Media Komunikasi dan Kajian Hukum* | **Year:** 2024 (ResearchGate upload)  
**Key content:** Legal analysis of why the carbon tax has been postponed. Traces the legal pathway from Law 7/2021 to the repeated deferrals. Identifies three causes: (1) absence of a carbon-tax roadmap, (2) lack of MRV readiness, (3) political pressure from industrial sectors.  
**Why useful:** A clear, citable legal-academic treatment of the postponement — fills the gap between the ICAP factsheet (which documents *what* happened) and the political economy explanation (which explains *why*). Good for Section 1b-1c.  
**URL:** https://jurnal.unikal.ac.id/index.php/hk/article/view/4140  
Also on ResearchGate: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/384719831  
**Suggested prefix:** `LOOK_`

### 2.4 Saputra & Haryono (2025) — *Carbon Tax Implementation Urgency in Indonesia's Oil and Gas Sector: Fiscal Policy Agency and Industry Perspectives*
**Type:** Peer-reviewed article | **Journal:** *International Journal of Energy and Infrastructure* (IJEI), Acadlore | **Year:** 2025  
**Key content:** Interviews with Fiscal Policy Agency officials and industry reps. Shows that the delay in the carbon tax is not just regulatory — it reflects active resistance from the oil and gas sector, which fears competitiveness losses. Provides the "industry perspectives" side of the political economy story.  
**Why useful:** Primary-source evidence of industry resistance — exactly the kind of political-economy grounding Finon 2019 and Stevens [87] (in Sunanda) call for theoretically. Rare qualitative angle in an otherwise quantitative literature.  
**URL:** https://www.acadlore.com/article/IJEI/2025_8_6/ijei080606

### 2.5 Pratama et al. (2025) — *Political Economy of Energy Policy in Indonesia towards Net Zero Emissions by 2060*
**Type:** Peer-reviewed article | **Journal:** *Energy for Sustainable Development* (ScienceDirect) | **Year:** 2025  
**Key content:** Political economy analysis of Indonesia's broader energy policy — why fossil-fuel subsidies, coal interests, and industrial policy ambitions (nickel/battery supply chain) constrain the energy transition. Carbon pricing treated as one constraint among many.  
**Why useful:** Big-picture political economy context for why Indonesia moves slowly. Supports the argument that the ETS-first sequencing is embedded in a broader pattern of industrial-policy protection — not just about the tax vs. ETS design choice.

### 2.6 IEEFA (2025) — *Two Years After Launch, Indonesia's Carbon Market Struggles to Find Momentum*
**Type:** Think-tank report | **Institution:** Institute for Energy Economics and Financial Analysis (IEEFA) | **Date:** September 2025  
**Key content:** After two years of operation, IDX Carbon trading volume is far below comparable schemes. From March–September 2025, total transaction value fell to IDR 1 billion (~$73,000). Average price ~$4/tCO₂e. Eight projects listed, 132 registered participants.  
**Why useful:** The most current independent assessment of the ETS's practical failure to generate a price signal. Critical evidence for the essay's argument that "Indonesia has an ETS that doesn't yet trade" (essay-direction.md §7). Good for the "critical view" closing of Section 2.  
**URL:** https://ieefa.org/resources/two-years-after-launch-indonesias-carbon-market-struggles-find-momentum

## 3. Broader Carbon Pricing Theory & Comparative

### 3.1 Tanaka et al. (2024) — *To Tax or to Trade? A Global Review of Carbon Emissions Reduction Strategies*
**Type:** Peer-reviewed article | **Journal:** *Energy Strategy Reviews* (Elsevier) | **Year:** 2024  
**Key content:** Global comparative review asking whether carbon taxes or ETSs reduce emissions more effectively. Finding: context-dependent, but ETS tends to dominate in large economies with strong monitoring; carbon taxes tend to outperform in countries with weak institutional capacity and large informal sectors.  
**Why useful:** Up-to-date complement to Jia & Lin 2020 and van den Bergh & Drews 2026. Directly relevant to your argument that Indonesia's institutional capacity might favour a carbon tax over ETS — yet Indonesia chose ETS.  
**URL:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2211467X24002177  
**Suggested prefix:** `LOOK_`

---

### 3.2 Irawan et al. (2025) — *Carbon Pricing in ASEAN and East Asia Summit Countries: Are Consumers Willing to Pay?*
**Type:** Peer-reviewed article | **Journal:** *Journal of Economic Analysis* (Anser Press) | **Year:** 2025  
**Key content:** Survey of 520 stakeholders across ASEAN + EAS countries (Sep 2023 – May 2024). Maximum willingness to pay: $10/tCO₂. Perceived barriers: economic competitiveness, energy affordability, institutional capacity.  
**Why useful:** Empirical demand-side data on carbon pricing acceptance in the region. The $10 willingness-to-pay ceiling confirms that current ETS prices (~$4) are within public tolerance but well below what economists recommend ($30–60). Useful for Section 3 on political feasibility.  
**URL:** https://www.anserpress.org/journal/jea/4/4/127  
**Suggested prefix:** `LOOK_`

---

### 3.3 IFS — *What is the Case for Carbon Taxes in Developing Countries?* (2023)
**Type:** Policy brief | **Institution:** Institute for Fiscal Studies (IFS, London) | **Year:** 2023  
**Key content:** Makes the administrative, distributional, and fiscal case for carbon taxes over ETS in low-income and lower-middle-income countries. Emphasises that carbon taxes are implementable via existing fuel-tax infrastructure — a key advantage Indonesia's essay overlooks.  
**Why useful:** Authoritative UK academic-policy voice for Section 1b (the economic case for a tax). The IFS is the gold standard for fiscal policy analysis — citing it alongside IMF 2022 signals you know the policy literature.  
**URL:** https://ifs.org.uk/articles/what-case-carbon-taxes-developing-countries  
**Suggested prefix:** `LOOK_`
