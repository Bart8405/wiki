# Lecture 6 — Paradigms and Revolutions

*Thomas Kuhn's picture of science: theory-laden observation, paradigm-bound "normal science," disruptive scientific revolutions, and the famously controversial idea of incommensurability between paradigms.*

**Date:** 11 March 2026 · **Lecturer:** Prof. James W. McAllister · **Key thinker:** **Thomas S. Kuhn** (1922–1996)

---

## Key concepts — definitions

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Theory-ladenness of observation** | All sense experience is made and interpreted in light of concepts and theories. We can't "just look" independently of conceptual frames. Even "I see a duck" requires the *concept* of duck. |
| **Paradigm** | A conceptual framework that shapes scientists' thought and work, dominating a branch of science for a period. Consists of: (a) **assumptions** about the world; (b) **exemplars** for problem-solving; (c) **style of theorising**. *Example:* Newtonianism in physics, 1700–1900. |
| **Normal science** | The long, productive periods when scientists work within a paradigm — solving "puzzles" by imitating exemplars. Assumptions remain beyond dispute. Brings career structure, coordination, clear standards of progress; in return, strict bounds on creativity. |
| **Anomaly** | Radically new data that can't be explained within the paradigm. Triggers a sequence: initially dismissed → eventually accepted as needing explanation → leads to criticism of the paradigm. |
| **Scientific revolution** | Paradigm switch in a discipline. Scientific community splits into conservative and progressive factions; both can claim good reasons. *Crucially: the switch is not fully rationally justifiable.* |
| **Incommensurability** | Lack of common measure between successive paradigms. Originally a geometry term (no integers m, n such that √2 = m/n). For Kuhn: paradigms can't be directly compared, translated, or ranked. |
| **Semantic incommensurability** | Scientific terms get their meaning *from* a paradigm. *Example:* "motion" means goal-directed qualitative change for Aristotle but quantitative position-change for Newton. Cross-paradigm communication breaks down. |
| **Observational incommensurability** | Strong form of theory-ladenness — your paradigm determines what you *see*. Different paradigms see different things. Visual analogy: Gestalt switches (duck/rabbit, vase/faces). |
| **Methodological incommensurability** | Each paradigm has its own rationality and standards of progress. No paradigm-independent criteria for theory choice. Is quantum physics "better" than Newtonian? Difficult to establish. |
| **Five epistemic values** (late Kuhn, 1977) | Partial retraction in *The Essential Tension*: **accuracy, consistency, scope, simplicity, fruitfulness** have validity across paradigms. They give continuity to paradigm succession — though *applying* them still requires interpretation. |

## Lecture walkthrough

### Image of science *before* Kuhn

- Theoretical terms have clear, stable meanings (e.g. "atom," "gene").
- Empirical data give **univocal, objective** verdicts on theories.
- Growth of science is **continuous, cumulative**.
- All scientists in history share the same standards of rationality.

(Popper's falsificationism is the canonical "before-Kuhn" view.)

### Image of science *after* Kuhn

- Theoretical terms shift meaning between theories.
- Observations are **partly shaped by theories** — empirical tests aren't independent of theory.
- Development of science is **discontinuous and non-cumulative**.
- Standards of rationality **change over time**.

### Four key concepts

#### 1. Theory-ladenness of observation
"Pure observation" doesn't exist. Even the simplest perception ("I see a duck") involves conceptual classification. In science, observations also depend on **theory-laden experimental apparatus** (e.g. what you take an MRI scan to show depends on your assumptions about how the scanner works). Implication: testing a theory is tricky because the jury (observations) is partly contaminated by the very theory under test — risk of circularity.

#### 2. Paradigm
A package — not a single theory, but a **whole framework**. Newtonianism (1700–1900) is the example: metaphysics (particles + forces), exemplars (Newton's own solved problems), style of theorising (mathematical laws of forces). Within a paradigm, scientists **solve puzzles by imitating exemplars**; underlying assumptions go unchallenged.

#### 3. Scientific revolution
**How paradigms end:** anomalies pile up → first dismissed → eventually accepted → eventually paradigm is criticised → scientists split into conservative vs progressive factions. The new paradigm is **sketched**, not rationally derived.
*Example:* transition from classical to quantum physics, 1925–1930. Conservatives (Einstein, Schrödinger) wanted to keep determinism and visualisability; progressives (Bohr, Heisenberg) prioritised empirical performance with minimal metaphysics. Both had good reasons — the switch wasn't strictly forced by logic.

#### 4. Incommensurability
Three forms (above): **semantic, observational, methodological**. Successive paradigms aren't just "different" or "incompatible" — they lack a *common measure* for comparison.

### Kuhn's later partial retraction

In *The Essential Tension* (1977), Kuhn conceded **five epistemic values** that hold *across* paradigms: accuracy, consistency, scope, simplicity, fruitfulness. They restore some continuity. But: *applying* these values still requires interpretation, so the verdict on paradigm-succession is never fully algorithmic.

### Kuhn's legacy

- Awareness that observation depends on **concepts and frameworks** (rediscovery of Kantian themes).
- Idea of a **plurality of scientific styles**.
- Problematising the concepts of scientific **rationality and progress**.
- More attention to science's **historical periodisation** and **social dynamics**.

## Key thinker — Thomas S. Kuhn

Born Cincinnati 1922; PhD in physics, Harvard 1949; developed a "General Education in Science" programme at Harvard from 1950; Professor of philosophy at MIT from 1979; died 1996.

His one book that mattered: ***The Structure of Scientific Revolutions*** (1962; 2nd ed. with postscript 1970). Short, readable, only 173 pages. *Most cited publication in humanities and social science between 1976 and 1983.* The phrase "paradigm shift" is now everyday English because of him.

---

> **Explained easily — theory-laden observation:**
> You think "looking" is innocent — eyes open, see what's there. Kuhn says no. You can't see "a duck" unless you already have the concept *duck*. In science, every measurement is interpreted through assumptions: an MRI scan isn't a direct picture of the brain, it's a picture *given* a theory of how the scanner works. So when you "test" a theory with observations, the observations themselves are already coloured by other theories. There's no innocent jury.

> **Explained easily — what a paradigm is:**
> Think of a paradigm as the *rulebook plus the playbook plus the dress code* of a scientific community. It's not just a theory — it's everything that goes unspoken when scientists work: what questions count, what answers count, what methods are respectable, what counts as a successful career move. As long as the paradigm holds, life is good: you have puzzles to solve, training is clear, careers are stable. The price is that you almost cannot think outside it.

> **Explained easily — scientific revolution:**
> When enough weird data piles up that the paradigm can't absorb, the field cracks. Half the community holds onto the old framework (with reasons), half jumps to a sketchy new one (with reasons). The switch isn't a *proof*; it's more like a religious conversion mixed with a generational change. After it, scientists look back at the old paradigm and feel they were practically speaking a different language. That last bit — "different language" — is **incommensurability**.

> **Explained easily — Popper vs Kuhn in one line:**
> Popper: science is constant critical testing. Kuhn: most of the time, science is *not* critical at all — it's puzzle-solving inside an unquestioned framework, *occasionally* interrupted by upheaval.

## Likely exam angles

- **Define:** paradigm, normal science, scientific revolution, incommensurability (with at least one of its three forms).
- **Apply:** Identify Newtonianism, classical-to-quantum, or any other case as Kuhn would describe it.
- **Critical:** What is Kuhn's challenge to the cumulative view of scientific progress? What did his late retraction (five epistemic values) try to recover?
- **Compare:** Popper (Lecture 5) vs Kuhn — continuous critical testing vs paradigm-bound normal science with revolutionary breaks.
- **Cross-lecture:** Kuhn's idea that "each paradigm has its own rationality" sets up Lecture 11 directly — relativism about rationality.
