2 PROJECT DETAILS 
2.1 Project Goals, Design and Long-Term Viability  
2.1.1 Summary Description of the Project (VCS, 3.2, 3.6, 3.10, 3.11, 3.13, 3.14; CCB, 
G1.2) 
The Lestari Project, located within Malinau Regency, North Kalimantan Province, Indonesia, is 
focused on the conservation of forest resources. The project involves the conservation of three 
plots of forest area originally designated for logging and conversion into oil palm plantations.  
 
The Project Proponent is PT. Agra Inti Investama, which is the landowner of the three plots of land 
under three of its subsidiaries: PT. Agra Beverindo, PT. Agra Cahaya Keumala, and PT. Agra Water 
Front Indonesia. The project aims to maintain the primary land use as forest, with a small portion 
reserved for village expansion and improved social services.  
 
Project Proponent is committed to preventing deforestation in the project area with 60,000 
hectares of land in the Malinau Regency, North Kalimantan. 
 
The project’s mitigation measures include enhancing local surveillance, mapping deforestation 
patterns, collaborating with educational institutions, and providing alternative income and social 
benefits to neighboring communities to minimize deforestation. The ultimate goal is to protect 
the natural forest from being cleared for forestry development. 
 
The project operates to safeguard the forest from planned deforestation, with the primary climate 
objective being to reduce emissions from deforestation and forest degradation. It also aims to 
promote health and well-being among local populations and protect biodiversity. Monitoring 
efforts are crucial to ensure the integrity of the forest, with a portion of carbon credits allocated 
to improving social and environmental conditions in the project region.

The Project Lestari (AB) area is generally made up of plains (alluvial systems), hilly areas and 
mountainous areas. Plains are found around the narrow areas along the Kayan River stream, 
around the Kayan Selatan district. A majority (72%) of the AB land is made up of hilly areas with

The Project Lestari area features Neogene volcanic cones (NVC), Neogene volcanic rocks (NVR), 
and Early Miocene rocks (EM). In the northern region, the landscape is characterized by 
oceanic crustal bedrock and sedimentary formations, including slate, shale, mudstone, phyllite, 
siltstone, metamorphic siltstone, quartz sandstone, metamorphic sandstone lithologies, and 
metamorphic conglomerate (KYx). Central and eastern sections of the area comprise younger 
volcanic rock formations, characterized by a diverse lithological composition that includes lava, 
breccia, agglomerate, and basalt to andesite lava breccia, alongside igneous features such as 
dikes, plugs, and stocks consisting of diorites, metadiorites, andesites, basalt, and plateau 
basalt. In the southwestern region, particularly along the Sarawak area of Malaysia, the 
lithological composition includes serpentinite, peridotite, gabbro, metamorphic gabbro, basalt, 
metamorphic basalt, metadiorite, and various metamorphic metadiorites. 
 
Soil 
 
The types of soil found in the area are cambisol, podzol, and alluvial soils,  
 Alluvial soils (Entisols) are formed from alluvium, which includes clay and sand 
deposits, along with claystone, phyllite, and slate. These soils are typically found in 
meandering areas and are characterized by a deep solum, a fine to slightly coarse 
texture, stratification, and excellent drainage. The soil reaction is acidic, and it exhibits 
low nutrient levels, base saturation, and cation-exchange capacity, Within this category, 
only the Tropofluvents great-group is present in the AB area. 
 Cambisols (Inceptisols) originate from a diverse range of parent materials, including 
aluminum, sedimentary rocks (such as claystone and sandstone), and metamorphic 
rocks (like phyllite and slate). This results in a varied soil nature and pattern. Cambisols 
can be found across all landforms, exhibiting drainage that ranges from obstructed to 
good. They are characterized by a deep solum, moderate to fine texture, and an acidic 
to slightly acidic soil reaction. Base saturation and cation-exchange capacity range from 
low to medium, and nutrient levels are generally low. Organic carbon content varies 
significantly, from low to high. In this area, only the Dystropepts great-group is identified 
within the Cambisol category. 
 Podzols (Ultisols) primarily originate from claystone, slate, and phyllite. They have a 
broad distribution, commonly found in hilly and mountainous regions with complex 
slopes at altitudes ranging from 50 to 1270 meters above sea level, often exhibiting 
good drainage. These soils are characterized by a deep to very deep solum (greater 
than 75 cm), a fine to medium texture, a lumpy structure, and a firm consistency. The 
soil reaction is acidic, and like the others, it has low nutrient levels, base saturation,

and cation-exchange capacity. In the AB area, two great-groups are identified under

Climate 
 
The Project Lestari (AB) area is classified under the Tropical Rainforest climate (type Af). 
Average monthly temperatures of the area range from 25.8°C in January to 26.4°C in October. 
The lowest average temperature in January can be attributed to shortest duration of solar 
radiation (5 hours per day) throughout the year, while higher average temperature in October 
can be attributed to the longest duration of solar radiation (6.2 hours per day). The relative 
humidity of the area ranges from 95% (October) to 97% (February). This is due to high levels of 
water vapor in the area, which is attributed to the evaporation of water sources around the 
area, i.e., vegetation and water bodies (e.g., Kayan River). 
 
A study of rainfall patterns in the area found the maximum rainfall to be 446mm in November 
and the minimum to be 96mm in August. The high rainfall in November is because the air 
masses blowing past the area are warm and humid (west trade winds), resulting in an 
abundance of water vapor in the air of the area. The topography of the area also plays a role in 
the high rainfall in November; therefore, the type of rainfall in question is orographic rainfall. On 
the other hand, the low rainfall in August is due to the hot and dry air masses that blow past 
the area, causing a lower water vapor content in the air. In a year, the average total rainfall in 
ranges from 1,982 to 3,895 mm. The rainfall pattern of the area is the bimodal rainfall pattern, 
which is commonly found in tropical regions. In a year, the amount of rainfall occurrences range 
between 88 to 168 days, with 24-46% of probability of rainfall.

2. PT. AGRA CAHAYA KEUMALA ("ACK") 
 
Topography 
 
The Project Lestari (ACK) area has an altitude of 150-1,000m above sea level. It is generally 
made up of hilly to mountainous areas. Plains are only found on terraces along river banks and 
narrow valleys between hills. A majority (54.65%) of the land is made up of hilly areas with 
gentle slopes (8-15% gradient and 5-50m local relief) and moderate slopes (15-25% gradient 
and 50-100m local relief). The remaining area is made up of mountainous areas with steep 
slopes (25-45% gradient and 100-300m local relief) and extremely sleep slopes (>45% 
gradient and >300m local relief). 
 
The ACK area is included in the Malinau formation that forms the Tarakan basin, which consist 
of late Cretaceous rocks. This includes neogene sedimentary rocks which are folded rocks 
under very great pressure. Sedimentary rocks, the most dominant rocks in the area, consist of 
sandstone, rock dust and claystone from the Pliocene and Miocene periods. 
 
Soil 
 
Soil formation in the ACK area is significantly influenced by parent materials, topography, 
climate, vegetation, and time. As the main influencing factor, parent material serves as a 
reference for soil classification. Soil type mapping indicates that Tropodults dominate the area, 
comprising Typic Tropodults (chromic podzols) and Dystropepts-Tropodults (haplic podzols), with 
the latter accounting for 88.28% of the land. The soil is characterized by a deep solum (over 
100 cm), a loam to clay loam texture, and a subangular to angular blocky structure. 
Permeability ranges from 0 to 19.6 cm/hour, classifying it as slow to medium.

Soil pH is acidic, ranging from 4.95 to 5.85, consistent with podzol characteristics. Organic 
content varies with depth, typically higher in the 0-20 cm layer compared to the 20-40 cm layer,

Climate 
 
The Project Lestari (ACK) area is classified under the Tropical Rainforest climate (type Af). 
Temperatures in the overall land area range from 26°C to 28.6°C. The relative humidity of the 
area ranges from 62.8% to 77%. Specifically, the forest area has an average temperature of 
26°C and a relative humidity of 77%. 
 
A study of rainfall patterns in the area found the maximum rainfall to be 400mm in August and 
the minimum to be 238mm in February. Number of rain days range from 4 to 13 days per 
month, with an average of 9.5 days of rain per month. 
 
Hydrology 
 
The overall area is situated within the Kayan River basin. The flow pattern of the rivers in this 
region, influenced by the steep to very steep topography, results in a rapid time of 
concentration. The upstream area is predominantly rocky, characterized by strong currents and 
numerous rapids, which contribute to a heightened potential for landslides and riverbank 
erosion. 
 
A groundwater balance study has revealed that the area consistently experiences a water

3. PT. AGRA WATER FRONT INDONESIA ("AWI") 
 
Topography 
 
The Project Lestari (AWI) area has an altitude of 150-1,000m above sea level. It is generally 
made up of plains, hilly areas and mountainous areas. A majority (62.5%) of the land is made 
up of hilly areas with gentle slopes (8-15% gradient and 5-50m local relief) and moderate 
slopes (15-25% gradient and 50-100m local relief). 26.4% of the area is made of plains (0-8% 
gradient and <5m local relief). The remaining area (12.1%) is made up of mountainous areas 
with steep slopes (25-45% gradient and 100-300m local relief). 
 
The AWI area is included in the Malinau formation that forms the Tarakan basin, which consist 
of late Cretaceous rocks. This includes neogene sedimentary rocks which are folded rocks 
under very great pressure. Sedimentary rocks, the most dominant rocks in the area, consist of 
sandstone, rock dust and claystone from the Pliocene and Miocene periods. 
 
Soil 
 
Soil formation in the AWI area is significantly influenced by parent materials, topography, 
climate, vegetation, and time. As the main influencing factor, parent material serves as a 
reference for soil classification. Soil type mapping indicates that Tropodults dominate the area, 
comprising Typic Tropodults (chromic podzols) and Dystropepts-Tropodults (haplic podzols), with 
the latter accounting for 88.28% of the land. The soil is characterized by a deep solum (over 
100 cm), a loam to clay loam texture, and a subangular to angular blocky structure. 
Permeability ranges from 0 to 19.6 cm/hour, classifying it as slow to medium.

Soil pH is acidic, ranging from 4.95 to 5.85, consistent with podzol characteristics. Organic 
content varies with depth, typically higher in the 0-20 cm layer compared to the 20-40 cm layer, 
while humus depth ranges from 5 to 10 cm. Cation-exchange capacity is between 6.74 and

higher in the upper layer (0.1-0.2%) than in the lower layer (0.02-0.14%), attributed to organic 
matter weathering. Phosphorus (P) levels are very low, averaging under 1 ppm, likely due to 
absorption by abundant aluminum and iron in the area. 
 
Climate 
 
The Project Lestari (AWI) area is classified under the Tropical Rainforest climate (type Af). 
Temperatures in the overall land area range from 26°C to 28.6°C. The relative humidity of the 
area ranges from 62.8% to 77%. Specifically, the forest area has an average temperature of 
26°C and a relative humidity of 77%. 
 
A study of rainfall patterns in the area found the maximum rainfall to be 400mm in August and 
the minimum to be 238mm in February. Number of rain days range from 4 to 13 days per 
month, with an average of 9.5 days of rain per month. 
 
Hydrology 
 
The overall area is located in the Kayan River basin. Based on the flow pattern of rivers in the 
area, which is also influenced by steep topography, the river has a fast time of concentration. 
The upstream area of the review is generally rocky with a strong current pattern and many 
rapids are found, resulting in a high potential for landslides and riverbank erosion. 
 
A groundwater balance study has revealed that the area consistently experiences a water 
surplus, enabling the river to flow year-round. This condition is largely attributed to the healthy 
and natural state of the land cover. Geologically, the presence of tuff rocks in the area 
facilitates effective flow absorption, allowing the aquifer to maintain a high water content. Soil 
analysis indicates that the constituent rocks possess a significant clay content, ensuring that 
the soil beneath the forest stands remains perpetually saturated with water. 
 
The river's flow rate remains relatively stable throughout the year, thanks to continuous rainfall 
and the natural vegetation cover, which prevents disruption of the hydrological cycle.

2.1.17 Project Activities and Theory of Change (VCS, 3.6; CCB, G1.8) 
The Lestari Project Area encompasses a total expanse of 60,000 hectares which were initially 
slated for logging operations and subsequent conversion to oil palm plantation. Presently, 
however, the intention is to conserve and safeguard the entire Project Area. 
The Lestari Project entails the execution of conservation strategies to prevent the planned 
deforestation within the Project Area. Since there will be no felling of trees as per the land-use 
change authorizations, the forest will be preserved in its natural state. The region will not be 
transformed into agricultural land, thus ensuring a consistent habitat for flora and fauna. 
The project incorporates forest protection measures such as the recruitment and oversight of 
surveillance teams to monitor any suspicious activities, as well as the deterrence of 
unauthorized entry into the project area. Additional mitigation efforts proposed by the project to 
avert planned deforestation include enhanced monitoring of the area, mapping local 
deforestation trends, forging partnerships with educational and research institutions, and 
offering benefits to adjacent communities. The aim is to reduce deforestation by providing 
alternative sources of income, educational opportunities, and vocational training. Through the 
execution of these project activities, Project Proponent is working in conjunction with the local 
community to halt deforestation in their forested regions.

2.2 Without-project Land Use Scenario and Additionality  
2.2.1 Conditions Prior to Project Initiation and Land Use Scenarios without the Project 
(VCS, 3.13; CCB, G2.1) 
The Lestari Project, located within Malinau Regency, North Kalimantan Province, Indonesia, is 
renowned for its extensive tropical rainforests, which are characterized by high biodiversity and 
complex ecological networks. The region's ecosystem primarily consists of lowland and 
montane rainforests, supporting a wide array of flora and fauna, including endangered species 
such as the orangutan, clouded leopard, and various bird and amphibian species. Malinau 
Regency experiences a tropical rainforest climate (Köppen Af), marked by warm temperatures 
(20°C to 32°C), high humidity (75% to 85%), and substantial annual precipitation (over 2000 
mm). 
The Lestari Project area has historically been a natural forest, maintaining its forested state 
over time with minimal human disturbance. Currently, the area continues to be predominantly 
forested, serving as a critical habitat for local biodiversity and playing a vital role in carbon 
sequestration and watershed protection.  
 
Ecosystem type of the project area: 
The ecosystem of Malinau Regency is primarily composed of lowland and montane tropical 
rainforests, characterized by high biodiversity and complex ecological networks that support a 
wide array of flora and fauna, including numerous endangered species. 
 
Current and historical land-use of the project area: 
A formal approval, Logging concessions, to the working plan of logging activities under the

stepping up local surveillance, mapping local deforestation patterns, forming collaborations

social services and facilities, education, and professional training.  For more details, please

2.4.6 Avoidance of Corruption and Other Unethical Behavior (VCS, 3.19; CCB, G4.3)

involved in the project’s execution maintain the highest levels of integrity and ethical conduct.

misappropriation of funds, fraud, favoritism, cronyism, nepotism, extortion, and collusive 
practices. To guarantee this standard, we have instituted the following measures and 
processes:

accompanied by a series of compliance policies that delineate our ethical expectations and 
standards for everyone associated with the project. These documents explicitly forbid any 
corrupt or unethical actions and offer clear guidance on acceptable behavior. All members

policies.

Policy and provide regular training sessions and awareness initiatives to inform all 
stakeholders about the necessity of eschewing corruption and unethical practices. These

reporting procedures, and the consequences of non-compliance. Through fostering a 
culture of integrity and awareness, we intend to ensure that all participants comprehend 
their duties and actively work to prevent corruption. 
3. Transparent Procurement and Vendor Selection: In an effort to promote transparency and 
equity, we have developed an internal Procurement Policy and instigated stringent 
procurement and vendor selection procedures. These processes include definitive 
evaluation criteria, competitive tendering, and rigorous due diligence checks. We 
meticulously document all procurement proceedings, including proposal requests, bid 
appraisals, and contract awards. By adhering to these transparent methods, we reduce the 
risk of favoritism, cronyism, and collusion.

whistleblower mechanism that enables individuals to report suspected instances of 
corruption or unethical behavior. This mechanism guarantees that reports can be made 
without fear of retribution and establishes a pathway for thorough investigation and 
suitable response. The confidentiality and protection of whistleblowers are stringently

2.5.4 Indigenous Peoples and Cultural Heritage (VCS, 3.18, 3.19) 
The project marks a significant advancement in tackling the global issues of climate change 
and biodiversity loss. However, its success is not solely defined by its environmental impact. 
Equally important is the recognition and protection of indigenous peoples' cultural heritage as a 
core component of the project's activities. The project honors the cultural heritage of 
Indigenous Peoples, acknowledging their deep and enduring connections to the forests and 
ecosystems they inhabit. Indigenous communities possess invaluable traditional knowledge, 
practices, and spiritual beliefs that have sustained their cultures for generations. The project 
recognizes the importance of safeguarding and respecting these cultural assets, which are 
integral to their identity and way of life. 
The indigenous population in the Project area is mainly made up of the Dayak Apokayan people 
group, which includes three sub-ethnic Dayak people, namely Kayan People, Kenyah People, 
and Bahau people. The dominant religion of the region is Christianity (Protestant and Catholic), 
which contribute to over 90% of the population of the Kayan Selatan, Kayan Hulu and Kayan 
Hilir districts. In general, Malinau Regency is rich in cultural heritage, with 11 traditional 
institutions and 14 communities.  
The Project respects the rights of indigenous peoples to free, prior, and informed consent (FPIC) 
and strives to form meaningful partnerships with these communities. By involving indigenous 
peoples in the planning, implementation, and monitoring of the project, it ensures that cultural 
heritage concerns are integrated from the beginning. The project takes proactive steps to 
protect these sites from deforestation, degradation, and encroachment, recognizing their 
significance not only to Indigenous Peoples but to humanity as a whole. By safeguarding these 
sites, the project contributes to the preservation of cultural heritage and the continuity of 
traditional practices. 
In addition, the Project acknowledges the importance of building the capacity of Indigenous 
Peoples to actively participate in and benefit from project activities. It supports initiatives that 
enhance the skills, knowledge, and capabilities of Indigenous communities in cultural heritage 
preservation, documentation, and transmission. By empowering indigenous peoples, the 
project ensures that they are committed to protecting, revitalizing, and passing on their cultural 
heritage to future generations.

illegal activities within the project area. Community members are encouraged to report 
any illegal actions through a secure and confidential feedback mechanism.

education empowers community members to recognize and reject illegal practices, 
fostering a culture of legality and sustainability.

trafficking, forced labor, and child labor in all its operations. This commitment is clearly 
communicated through contracts and agreements with stakeholders, ensuring that all 
project participants adhere to ethical labor practices. Regular audits and assessments 
are conducted to verify compliance, and any violations are addressed promptly. 
 Community Empowerment: By promoting alternative livelihood opportunities, the 
Project reduces the economic incentives for engaging in illegal activities. Empowering 
local communities to derive income from legal and sustainable sources is essential for 
the long-term success of the Project. 
Through these measures, the project aims to minimize the risk of illegal activities impacting its 
goals, ensuring that all benefits are derived from lawful practices while safeguarding human 
rights and community well-being.
